# Exam 2 Focused Reading Guide - Filled-In Study Notes

This guide turns the reading outline into quick-study notes. It is organized by topic and focuses on definitions, distinctions, clinical cues, and nursing implications that are commonly tested.

## Skin Integrity, Inflammation, and Wound Healing

### Functions of the integumentary system

- Protects the body from injury, infection, fluid loss, and UV exposure.
- Helps regulate temperature through sweating and vasodilation/vasoconstriction.
- Provides sensation for pain, pressure, touch, and temperature.
- Contributes to vitamin D synthesis.
- Supports immune defense through the skin barrier and immune cells.
- Helps maintain fluid and electrolyte balance.

### Factors that affect integumentary function

- Age: skin becomes thinner, drier, and less elastic; healing slows.
- Nutrition: protein, calories, vitamins, and minerals are needed for tissue repair.
- Hydration: poor hydration leads to dry, fragile skin.
- Circulation and oxygenation: poor blood flow reduces nutrient and oxygen delivery.
- Mobility: immobility increases pressure injury risk.
- Sensory impairment: reduced ability to feel pain or pressure increases injury risk.
- Chronic illness: diabetes, vascular disease, renal disease, and immunosuppression impair skin integrity.
- Medications: corticosteroids and some chemotherapy agents slow healing.
- Moisture/incontinence: causes maceration and skin breakdown.
- Friction and shear: damage superficial and deeper tissues.

### Normal phases of wound healing

#### 1. Hemostasis

- Occurs immediately after injury.
- Blood vessels constrict and clotting begins.
- Platelets form a clot to stop bleeding.

#### 2. Inflammatory phase

- Usually lasts several days.
- Vasodilation increases blood flow.
- WBCs move to the area to remove bacteria and debris.
- Expected findings: redness, warmth, swelling, pain.

#### 3. Proliferative phase

- Granulation tissue forms.
- Fibroblasts produce collagen.
- New blood vessels grow.
- Wound begins to contract and epithelial tissue forms.

#### 4. Maturation/remodeling phase

- Collagen reorganizes and strengthens.
- Scar tissue forms.
- May continue for months to a year or longer.

### How specific factors affect wound healing

#### Nutrition

- Protein is needed for collagen formation and tissue repair.
- Vitamin C supports collagen synthesis.
- Vitamin A supports epithelialization and immune function.
- Zinc helps cellular repair.
- Inadequate calories or protein delay healing and increase infection risk.

#### Circulation and oxygen delivery

- Oxygen is required for fibroblast function, collagen synthesis, and immune defense.
- Poor perfusion from vascular disease, edema, anemia, or hypotension delays healing.
- Smoking reduces oxygen delivery and constricts blood vessels.

#### Impaired inflammatory and immune responses

- Immunosuppression reduces the body's ability to remove bacteria and debris.
- Diabetes can impair leukocyte function and circulation.
- Delayed or ineffective inflammation makes infection more likely and healing slower.

#### Obesity

- Adipose tissue has poorer blood supply.
- Increased tension on wound edges raises risk of dehiscence.
- Skin folds and moisture increase infection and breakdown risk.

#### Age

- Older adults often have thinner skin, lower collagen production, and slower cell turnover.
- They may also have poorer circulation and more chronic disease.

#### Corticosteroids

- Suppress inflammation and immune response.
- Delay collagen synthesis and wound contraction.
- Increase infection risk and slow tissue repair.

### Complications that delay wound healing

#### Hemorrhage

- Causes loss of blood volume and oxygen-carrying capacity.
- Hematoma formation can separate wound edges and promote infection.

#### Infection

- Bacteria compete for nutrients and trigger prolonged inflammation.
- Infection destroys healthy tissue and delays granulation and epithelialization.

#### Dehiscence and evisceration

- **Dehiscence:** partial or total separation of wound layers.
- **Evisceration:** protrusion of internal organs through the wound.
- Risk factors: obesity, poor nutrition, infection, coughing, vomiting, straining, steroid use.

### Interventions to prevent dehiscence and evisceration

- Support incision during coughing, moving, or deep breathing (splinting).
- Control nausea, vomiting, and constipation to reduce strain.
- Encourage proper nutrition and hydration.
- Monitor for infection and report increased drainage, separation, or bulging.
- Teach patients to avoid heavy lifting.

### If evisceration occurs

- Stay with the patient and call for help.
- Cover exposed organs with sterile dressings moistened with sterile normal saline.
- Place the patient in low Fowler's position with knees bent if tolerated.
- Do **not** try to push organs back in.
- Prepare for urgent surgical management.

### Inflammatory response

- Injury or infection triggers release of chemical mediators such as histamine and prostaglandins.
- Blood vessels dilate and become more permeable.
- Fluid, proteins, and WBCs move into tissues.
- Purpose: isolate damage, destroy pathogens, remove debris, and begin healing.

### Localized vs systemic inflammation

#### Localized inflammation

- Limited to one area.
- Findings: redness, heat, swelling, pain, and sometimes loss of function.

#### Systemic inflammation

- Involves the whole body.
- Findings may include fever, fatigue, malaise, elevated WBC count, tachycardia, and increased inflammatory markers.

### Acute vs chronic inflammation

#### Acute inflammation

- Rapid onset and usually short duration.
- Often follows injury or infection.
- Dominated by neutrophils and obvious signs like redness, pain, and swelling.

#### Chronic inflammation

- Longer duration.
- May result from persistent infection, autoimmune disease, or repeated injury.
- Can lead to tissue destruction and fibrosis.

### Purpose of RICE

- **R - Rest:** prevents further injury.
- **I - Ice:** reduces pain, swelling, and local blood flow.
- **C - Compression:** limits edema.
- **E - Elevation:** improves venous return and decreases swelling.

### Contraindications for heat and cold therapies

#### Heat: avoid or use cautiously with

- Active bleeding
- Acute injury with swelling in the first phase
- Impaired sensation
- Impaired circulation
- Severe edema
- Areas with malignancy unless specifically ordered

#### Cold: avoid or use cautiously with

- Impaired circulation
- Peripheral vascular disease
- Raynaud's phenomenon
- Cold hypersensitivity
- Areas of nerve damage or poor sensation

### Medication classes used to treat inflammation

#### Corticosteroids

- Strong anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive drugs.
- Reduce capillary permeability and inflammatory mediator activity.
- Risks: hyperglycemia, infection, poor wound healing, GI irritation, osteoporosis, adrenal suppression.

#### NSAIDs

- Reduce inflammation, pain, and fever by inhibiting prostaglandin synthesis.
- Risks: GI bleeding, kidney injury, fluid retention, increased bleeding tendency.

## Pressure Injury and Wound Care

### Types of wounds

- **Open wound:** break in skin or mucous membrane.
- **Closed wound:** tissue damage without skin opening, such as a hematoma.
- **Acute wound:** heals in expected time with orderly repair.
- **Chronic wound:** delayed healing, often due to poor perfusion, pressure, or disease.
- **Intentional wound:** created deliberately, such as a surgical incision.
- **Unintentional wound:** accidental trauma.
- **Clean wound:** low microbial contamination.
- **Contaminated/dirty wound:** higher infection risk.

### How pressure injuries develop

- Prolonged pressure compresses capillaries.
- Tissue ischemia develops when blood flow is reduced.
- Friction and shear worsen tissue damage.
- Moisture and poor nutrition increase breakdown risk.
- Pressure injuries commonly occur over bony prominences.

### Blanching

- Blanching is temporary whitening of skin when pressure is applied.
- It indicates that capillary blood flow can still return.
- **Nonblanchable erythema** is a hallmark of Stage 1 pressure injury.

### Braden Scale interpretation

The Braden Scale estimates pressure injury risk by assessing:

- Sensory perception
- Moisture
- Activity
- Mobility
- Nutrition
- Friction and shear

General rule:

- Lower score = higher risk.
- Patients with limited mobility, poor nutrition, moisture exposure, or decreased sensation need more aggressive prevention.

### Pressure injury prevention interventions

- Reposition regularly.
- Use pressure-relieving surfaces.
- Keep skin clean and dry.
- Manage incontinence promptly.
- Inspect skin daily, especially bony prominences.
- Support adequate protein, calories, and hydration.
- Reduce friction and shear during transfers and repositioning.
- Encourage mobility when possible.

### Pressure injury stages

#### Stage 1

- Intact skin with nonblanchable redness.

#### Stage 2

- Partial-thickness skin loss involving epidermis and/or dermis.
- May appear as a shallow open ulcer or blister.

#### Stage 3

- Full-thickness skin loss into subcutaneous tissue.
- Fat may be visible.
- **Undermining and tunneling are commonly seen here.**

#### Stage 4

- Full-thickness tissue loss with exposed muscle, tendon, or bone.
- **Undermining and tunneling are also commonly seen here.**

#### Unstageable

- Base is covered by slough or eschar, so true depth cannot be determined.

#### Deep tissue pressure injury

- Persistent nonblanchable deep red, maroon, or purple discoloration.
- Indicates damage beneath the surface.

### Basic wound cleansing principles

- Clean from least contaminated area to most contaminated area.
- Usually clean from the center outward for an open wound.
- Use enough pressure to remove debris but not damage healthy tissue.
- Use sterile technique when required by wound type and agency policy.
- Avoid harsh scrubbing of healing tissue.

### Cytotoxic vs non-cytotoxic cleansing agents

#### Non-cytotoxic

- Normal saline is the standard choice for routine wound cleansing.
- It does not damage healthy granulation tissue.

#### Cytotoxic

- Hydrogen peroxide, povidone-iodine, and similar agents can damage fibroblasts and healthy tissue.
- They are generally avoided for routine healing wounds unless specifically indicated.

### Purpose of debridement

- Removes necrotic tissue, slough, bacteria, and debris.
- Reduces infection risk.
- Promotes formation of healthy granulation tissue.
- Helps the wound progress through normal healing.

### Methods of debridement

#### Mechanical debridement

- Removes dead tissue physically.
- Example: wet-to-dry dressing or wound irrigation.
- Can also remove healthy tissue and may be painful.

#### Autolytic debridement

- Uses the body's own enzymes and moisture under an occlusive dressing.
- Example: hydrocolloid dressing over a wound with light to moderate drainage.
- Slower but selective and less painful.

#### Chemical/enzymatic debridement

- Uses topical agents to digest necrotic tissue.
- Example: collagenase ointment.

#### Sharp/surgical debridement

- Fast removal using instruments by a trained provider.
- Used when there is extensive necrosis or infection.

## Infection and Infection Prevention

### Relationship between WBCs and infection

- WBC count often rises with infection.
- Neutrophils usually increase in bacterial infections.
- A "left shift" suggests increased immature neutrophils during acute infection.
- A normal WBC count does **not** always rule out infection, especially in older or immunocompromised patients.

### Measures to prevent transmission of infection

- Perform hand hygiene consistently.
- Use standard precautions with all patients.
- Use transmission-based precautions when indicated.
- Clean equipment and environment.
- Use proper PPE.
- Handle specimens safely.
- Maintain aseptic technique during invasive procedures.

### Specific actions for a client with C. diff

- Use **contact precautions**.
- Wash hands with **soap and water** after care because alcohol sanitizer is less effective against spores.
- Use gloves and gown.
- Dedicate equipment when possible.
- Clean environment with a sporicidal agent.
- Monitor stool output and dehydration risk.

### Factors that affect normal resistance to infection

- Age extremes
- Poor nutrition
- Stress
- Fatigue
- Chronic disease
- Impaired skin or mucous membranes
- Immunosuppressive therapy
- Invasive devices such as urinary catheters or IV lines

### Chain of infection

The six links are:

1. Infectious agent
2. Reservoir
3. Portal of exit
4. Mode of transmission
5. Portal of entry
6. Susceptible host

Breaking any link helps prevent infection.

### Lactate and infection

- Elevated lactate suggests tissue hypoperfusion and anaerobic metabolism.
- In sepsis, rising lactate can indicate worsening perfusion and severity.
- A high lactate level is a warning sign for sepsis and shock.

### Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and infection

- ESR is a nonspecific marker of inflammation.
- It may rise with infection, autoimmune disease, or chronic inflammation.
- It supports the overall picture but does not diagnose infection by itself.

### How specimen cultures are used

- Identify the causative organism.
- Help determine which antimicrobial is effective through sensitivity testing.
- Ideally obtain cultures **before** starting antibiotics when possible.

### Localized vs systemic infection

#### Localized infection

- Redness
- Warmth
- Swelling
- Pain
- Purulent drainage
- Delayed healing

#### Systemic infection

- Fever or hypothermia
- Chills
- Tachycardia
- Tachypnea
- Fatigue or malaise
- Confusion
- Elevated or low WBC count
- Hypotension in severe infection/sepsis

### Early signs and symptoms of sepsis

- Fever or low temperature
- Tachycardia
- Tachypnea
- Altered mental status
- Hypotension or a downward blood pressure trend
- Elevated lactate
- Decreased urine output

### Phases of a febrile episode

#### Chill phase

- Temperature rising
- Patient feels cold and may shiver
- Skin may be pale

#### Fever phase

- Temperature remains elevated
- Skin is warm
- Patient may have thirst, malaise, headache, and weakness

#### Flush/crisis phase

- Temperature begins to fall
- Sweating increases
- Skin becomes warm and flushed
- Patient may be weak or dehydrated

### Surgical (sterile) vs medical asepsis

#### Medical asepsis

- "Clean technique"
- Reduces number and spread of microorganisms.
- Used for routine care, standard precautions, and many bedside tasks.

#### Surgical asepsis

- "Sterile technique"
- Eliminates all microorganisms and spores from a field.
- Used for sterile procedures, invasive care, and many dressing changes for deep or surgical wounds.

## Respiratory Assessment and Oxygenation

### Significance of common breath sounds

- **Crackles:** fluid in alveoli or collapsed airways popping open; seen with heart failure, pneumonia, atelectasis.
- **Rhonchi:** coarse, low-pitched sounds from secretions in large airways; may improve after coughing.
- **Wheezing:** high-pitched musical sound from narrowed airways; common in asthma or bronchospasm.
- **Pleural friction rub:** grating sound from inflamed pleural surfaces rubbing together.

### Effects of dehydration on the respiratory system

- Secretions become thicker and harder to clear.
- Mucociliary clearance worsens.
- Risk of mucus plugging and atelectasis increases.

### Why ambulation, positioning, incentive spirometry, and chest tubes promote lung expansion

- **Ambulation:** improves ventilation, circulation, and secretion clearance.
- **Positioning:** upright positioning improves diaphragm movement and lung expansion.
- **Incentive spirometry:** encourages deep breathing and prevents atelectasis.
- **Chest tubes:** remove air or fluid so the lung can re-expand.

### Purpose of incentive spirometry

- Promotes sustained maximal inspiration.
- Helps prevent postoperative atelectasis.
- Improves alveolar inflation.

### Purpose of chest physiotherapy and postural drainage

- Loosens and mobilizes secretions.
- Uses percussion, vibration, coughing, and positioning to improve airway clearance.

### Rationale for pursed-lip breathing

- Prolongs exhalation.
- Helps prevent airway collapse.
- Improves carbon dioxide removal.
- Commonly taught to clients with COPD.

### General care of the client with a chest tube

- Assess respiratory status, breath sounds, oxygenation, and pain.
- Keep drainage system **below chest level**.
- Ensure tubing is not kinked or compressed.
- Check dressing and insertion site.
- Observe drainage amount, color, and trends.
- Encourage deep breathing and mobility as tolerated.

### If drainage suddenly increases

- Assess the patient first.
- Check for signs of bleeding, respiratory distress, or tube displacement.
- Measure and report the amount and characteristics of drainage promptly.

### Why clamping chest tubes is generally prohibited

- Clamping can trap air in the pleural space.
- This may cause a tension pneumothorax, which is life-threatening.
- Chest tubes are only clamped briefly and for specific provider-directed reasons.

### Hypoventilation vs hyperventilation vs hypoxia

- **Hypoventilation:** inadequate ventilation leading to CO2 retention.
- **Hyperventilation:** excessive ventilation causing low CO2.
- **Hypoxia:** inadequate oxygen available to tissues.

### Factors that contribute

#### Hypoventilation

- Sedatives or opioids
- Neuromuscular weakness
- Severe fatigue
- Obesity hypoventilation
- Airway obstruction

#### Hyperventilation

- Anxiety
- Pain
- Fever
- Hypoxemia
- Early sepsis

#### Hypoxia

- Airway obstruction
- Lung disease
- Anemia
- Poor cardiac output
- High altitude

### Complications of hyperventilation

- Dizziness
- Tingling
- Lightheadedness
- Respiratory alkalosis
- Muscle cramps

### Eating by mouth while receiving oxygen by mask

- If allowed and safe, remove the mask briefly for meals and use an alternative oxygen device if ordered, such as nasal cannula during meals.
- Reapply the mask promptly after eating.
- Monitor tolerance and oxygen saturation.

### Noninvasive positive pressure ventilation (CPAP/BiPAP)

- **CPAP:** continuous positive airway pressure; helps keep alveoli open.
- **BiPAP:** provides different inspiratory and expiratory pressures; helps ventilation more than CPAP.

### Indications for CPAP/BiPAP

- Sleep apnea
- COPD exacerbation
- Acute respiratory distress with increased work of breathing
- Pulmonary edema in selected patients
- Hypercapnic respiratory failure when the patient can still protect the airway

## Cardiac Function

### Flow of blood through the heart

- Vena cava -> right atrium -> tricuspid valve -> right ventricle -> pulmonic valve -> pulmonary artery -> lungs
- Lungs -> pulmonary veins -> left atrium -> mitral valve -> left ventricle -> aortic valve -> aorta -> body

### Cardiac conduction system

- SA node: normal pacemaker.
- AV node: delays impulse briefly.
- Bundle of His -> right and left bundle branches -> Purkinje fibers: spread impulse through ventricles.
- Coordinated conduction produces effective contraction and cardiac output.

### Cardiac output and its components

**Cardiac output = heart rate x stroke volume**

#### Preload

- Amount of ventricular stretch at end-diastole.
- Related to venous return.
- Too little preload lowers output; too much can cause congestion.

#### Afterload

- Resistance the ventricle must overcome to eject blood.
- Increased afterload, such as with hypertension, makes the heart work harder.

#### Contractility

- Strength of myocardial contraction.
- Reduced contractility lowers stroke volume and output.

#### Heart rate

- Rate that is too slow or too fast can decrease cardiac output.

### Relationship of cardiac output to oxygenation and perfusion

- Adequate cardiac output is needed to deliver oxygenated blood to tissues.
- Low output causes poor perfusion, fatigue, cool skin, decreased urine output, and altered mental status.

### Key techniques for assessing the cardiac system

- Inspect skin color, edema, and work of breathing.
- Palpate peripheral pulses and capillary refill.
- Auscultate heart sounds.
- Monitor blood pressure, heart rate, rhythm, oxygen saturation, and urine output.
- Assess for chest pain, activity tolerance, dizziness, and jugular venous distention.

### Factors that affect cardiovascular function and oxygenation

#### Cigarette smoking

- Causes vasoconstriction, endothelial damage, and reduced oxygen delivery.

#### High blood pressure

- Increases afterload and workload of the left ventricle.

#### Nutrition

- High saturated fat and sodium intake contribute to atherosclerosis and hypertension.
- Poor nutrition weakens cardiac and vascular health.

#### Activity

- Regular activity improves circulation and cardiac efficiency.
- Inactivity contributes to deconditioning and poor perfusion.

#### Diabetes

- Damages blood vessels and accelerates atherosclerosis.
- Increases risk for coronary artery disease and poor perfusion.

### How conduction disturbances affect cardiac output

- Dysrhythmias may reduce filling time or produce ineffective contractions.
- This lowers stroke volume and can decrease perfusion.

### Basic indicators of tachycardia and bradycardia

- **Tachycardia:** heart rate above 100 beats/min in adults.
- **Bradycardia:** heart rate below 60 beats/min in adults.
- Clinical significance depends on symptoms and perfusion.

### Cues associated with altered cardiac function

- Chest pain
- Palpitations
- Shortness of breath
- Fatigue
- Syncope or dizziness
- Hypotension
- Cool clammy skin
- Edema
- Decreased urine output

### Why ventricular tachycardia and ventricular fibrillation are life-threatening

- They prevent effective ventricular pumping.
- Cardiac output falls drastically or becomes absent.
- Ventricular fibrillation results in no organized cardiac output and requires immediate emergency response.

## Pain Management

### ATC vs PRN analgesic administration

- **ATC (around the clock):** scheduled dosing maintains consistent pain control and is useful for ongoing pain.
- **PRN:** given as needed; useful for breakthrough pain or intermittent pain.
- Many patients benefit from a combination of scheduled medication plus PRN breakthrough dosing.

### Signs that pain is well managed

- Patient reports acceptable pain level.
- Improved ability to move, cough, deep breathe, sleep, and participate in care.
- Stable vital signs related to comfort.

### Signs of excessive opioid sedation

- Difficult to arouse
- Slowed respirations
- Decreased oxygen saturation
- Slurred speech
- Confusion
- Pinpoint pupils may be present

### Barriers to effective pain management

- Fear of addiction or side effects
- Underdosing
- Poor pain assessment
- Communication barriers
- Cultural beliefs
- Delay in medication administration

### Complications of ineffective pain management

- Poor mobility
- Delayed healing
- Sleep disturbance
- Anxiety
- Increased stress response
- Reduced deep breathing and coughing, raising pulmonary complication risk

### Non-pharmacologic pain-relieving techniques

- Positioning
- Ice or heat when appropriate
- Relaxation breathing
- Guided imagery
- Music
- Distraction
- Massage
- Splinting an incision

## Fluids and Electrolytes

### Processes that regulate fluid volume and distribution

- Osmosis moves water across membranes.
- Diffusion moves solutes from high to low concentration.
- Filtration moves fluid due to pressure differences.
- Hormones such as ADH, aldosterone, and natriuretic peptides influence water and sodium balance.

### Intake and output basics

- Accurate I&O helps evaluate hydration, kidney function, and therapy response.
- Include all oral, enteral, and IV intake.
- Include urine, emesis, stool when measurable, drains, and other fluid losses.

### Common fluid and electrolyte disorders

- Fluid volume deficit
- Fluid volume excess
- Hyponatremia and hypernatremia
- Hypokalemia and hyperkalemia
- Hypocalcemia and hypercalcemia

### Fluid volume deficit

#### Risk factors

- Vomiting
- Diarrhea
- Fever
- Sweating
- Diuretics
- Poor intake
- Bleeding

#### Signs and symptoms

- Dry mucous membranes
- Poor skin turgor
- Thirst
- Tachycardia
- Hypotension or orthostatic hypotension
- Concentrated urine and low urine output
- Weight loss

### Fluid volume excess

#### Risk factors

- Heart failure
- Renal failure
- Excessive IV fluids
- Liver disease
- High sodium intake

#### Signs and symptoms

- Edema
- Weight gain
- Crackles
- Dyspnea
- Elevated blood pressure
- Bounding pulse
- Jugular venous distention

### How fluid and electrolyte imbalance can affect assessment findings

- **Blood pressure:** low with deficit, often high with overload.
- **Pulse:** rapid and weak with deficit; full or bounding with overload.
- **Cardiovascular status:** dysrhythmias can occur, especially with potassium imbalance.
- **Perfusion:** severe imbalance can reduce tissue perfusion and mental status.

## IV Therapy and Vascular Access

### Common uses of IV therapy

- Blood transfusion
- Parenteral nutrition
- Fluid and electrolyte replacement
- Medication administration

### Peripheral vs central line access

#### Peripheral IV

- Inserted into peripheral veins.
- Used for short-term therapy and many routine infusions.

#### Central line

- Tip terminates in a central vein, usually the superior vena cava.
- Used for long-term therapy, vesicants, parenteral nutrition, or need for central access.

### Types of central vascular access devices

#### Peripherally inserted central catheter (PICC)

- Inserted through a peripheral vein and advanced to a central vein.
- Good for medium- to long-term therapy.

#### Tunneled central venous catheter

- Surgically tunneled under the skin before entering the vein.
- Used for long-term therapy.

#### Implanted port

- Placed under the skin and accessed with a special needle.
- Useful for long-term intermittent therapy.

### Continuous vs intermittent bolus IV

- **Continuous infusion:** fluid runs steadily over time.
- **Intermittent bolus/IV piggyback:** medication or fluid runs at scheduled intervals.

### Priority assessments for clients with IVs

- Check site for redness, swelling, pain, leakage, or coolness.
- Verify solution, rate, and tubing connections.
- Monitor patency and dressing condition.
- Assess the patient for signs of fluid overload, infection, and therapy effectiveness.

### Complications of IV therapy

#### Inaccurate infusion rates

- Too fast can cause fluid overload and circulatory stress.
- Too slow may delay needed treatment.

#### Infiltration

- Non-vesicant fluid leaks into tissue.
- Site may be cool, swollen, pale, and uncomfortable.

#### Extravasation

- Vesicant solution leaks into tissue.
- Can cause severe tissue injury or necrosis.

#### Phlebitis

- Vein inflammation.
- Site may be warm, red, tender, and streaked.

#### Infection

- May be local or systemic.
- Risk increases with poor asepsis or prolonged device use.

#### Fluid overload

- Can occur when fluids infuse too rapidly or patient tolerance is poor.
- Watch for edema, crackles, dyspnea, and rising blood pressure.

#### Air embolism

- Air enters the venous system.
- Prevention: prime tubing, secure connections, clamp appropriately during line changes.

### Preventing IV complications

- Use aseptic technique.
- Assess site and patient frequently.
- Secure tubing and catheter.
- Change dressings and tubing per policy.
- Use infusion pumps when indicated.
- Stop the infusion promptly if complications are suspected.

## Quick Memorization Points

- Lower **Braden** score = higher pressure injury risk.
- **Stage 1** pressure injury = intact skin with nonblanchable redness.
- **Undermining/tunneling** are usually seen in **Stage 3 and Stage 4** pressure injuries.
- **Normal saline** is the usual non-cytotoxic wound cleanser.
- **Soap and water** handwashing is essential for **C. diff**.
- High **lactate** can signal poor perfusion and sepsis severity.
- **VT/VF** are life-threatening because they do not produce effective cardiac output.
- **Pursed-lip breathing** helps keep small airways open during exhalation.
- **Hypoventilation** raises CO2; **hyperventilation** lowers CO2; **hypoxia** means low tissue oxygen.

## Study Tips

- Compare look-alike terms side by side: acute vs chronic inflammation, localized vs systemic infection, infiltration vs extravasation, sterile vs medical asepsis.
- Memorize major emergency cues: sepsis, evisceration, chest tube problems, opioid oversedation, VT/VF.
- Study by asking: "What is it?", "Why does it happen?", "What do I assess?", and "What does the nurse do first?"
