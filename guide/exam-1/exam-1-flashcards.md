# Exam 1 Flashcards

These flashcards are grouped by the same sections as your Exam 1 outline and strengthened with nursing exam angles such as priority, safety, compare-and-contrast, and application cards.

## How to use this deck

- **Front** = the question side of the flashcard.
- **Back** = the answer side.
- **Why it matters** = a short exam or memory note.

## The Nursing Process

### Card 1

**Front:** What are the common five steps of the nursing process, and what extra step does the Open RN book separate out?

**Back:**
- Common five-step version: Assessment, Diagnosis, Planning, Implementation, Evaluation
- Open RN book version: Assessment, Diagnosis, Outcome Identification, Planning, Implementation, Evaluation
- In the five-step version, outcome identification is usually included inside planning.

**Why it matters:** Exams may use the five-step version, but this book teaches the ANA-style ADOPIE format.

### Card 2

**Front:** How do subjective and objective data differ?

**Back:**
- Subjective data are what the client says or feels.
- Objective data are what the nurse observes or measures.

**Why it matters:** This helps you sort findings correctly during assessment questions.

### Card 3

**Front:** How do primary and secondary sources differ?

**Back:**
- The primary source is the client when the client can answer reliably.
- Secondary sources include family, the chart, labs, and the health care team.

**Why it matters:** The source matters because some data are stronger or more direct than others.

### Card 4

**Front:** Why should nurses validate data?

**Back:**
- To confirm unusual, inconsistent, or incomplete data.
- To prevent acting on wrong or unclear information.

**Why it matters:** Exam questions often reward reassessment and validation before action when the client is stable.

### Card 5

**Front:** How is a nursing problem different from a medical diagnosis?

**Back:**
- A medical diagnosis names a disease or condition.
- A nursing problem focuses on the client response that nursing care can address.

**Why it matters:** This distinction is basic to care planning.

### Card 6

**Front:** Why is individualized client care important?

**Back:**
- Different clients have different goals, culture, strengths, risks, and support systems.
- A care plan should fit the actual client, not a generic picture.

**Why it matters:** Patient-centered care is a major nursing principle.

### Card 7

**Front:** How are high-, medium-, and low-priority problems usually organized?

**Back:**
- High priority: life-threatening, ABC, or major safety problems.
- Medium priority: important but not immediately life-threatening.
- Low priority: can wait longer if the client is stable.

**Why it matters:** Priority-setting is a major exam skill.

### Card 8

**Front:** What is a nursing outcome?

**Back:**
- A nursing outcome is the client-centered result expected after nursing care.

**Why it matters:** Outcomes tell you what success should look like.

### Card 9

**Front:** What makes an outcome SMART?

**Back:**
- Specific
- Measurable
- Achievable
- Relevant or Realistic
- Time-bound

**Why it matters:** SMART outcomes are easier to evaluate and defend on exams.

### Card 10

**Front:** How do planning and implementation differ?

**Back:**
- Planning decides priorities, outcomes, and interventions.
- Implementation carries out the interventions.

**Why it matters:** A question may describe an action and ask which nursing-process step it belongs to.

### Card 11

**Front:** How do nurse-initiated, provider-prescribed, and collaborative interventions differ?

**Back:**
- Nurse-initiated: the nurse can start independently.
- Provider-prescribed: requires an order.
- Collaborative: done with other health care team members.

**Why it matters:** This is a common “which intervention fits this role?” question.

### Card 12

**Front:** How do Maslow’s Hierarchy and the ABC method help prioritize care?

**Back:**
- ABC focuses on airway, breathing, and circulation in urgent situations.
- Maslow helps rank needs when the situation is more stable.
- Physiological needs come before safety, comfort, or teaching.

**Why it matters:** Use ABC first in emergencies, then Maslow for broader sorting.

### Card 13

**Front:** What happens during the evaluation step?

**Back:**
- The nurse compares actual client outcomes with expected outcomes.
- Outcomes may be met, partly met, or not met.

**Why it matters:** Evaluation closes the loop and tells you whether the plan worked.

### Card 14

**Front:** What should the nurse do if outcomes are not met?

**Back:**
- Reassess the client.
- Look for barriers or missing data.
- Revise the plan of care if needed.

**Why it matters:** If the outcome is missed, the answer is usually not “keep everything the same.”

### Card 15

**Front:** A client has low oxygen saturation, pain, and questions about discharge teaching. What should the nurse address first?

**Back:**
- Low oxygen saturation comes first because airway and breathing take priority over comfort and teaching.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic ABC priority question.

### Card 16

**Front:** The client says pain is 0 out of 10, but is grimacing, guarding, and refuses to move. What is the best nursing action?

**Back:**
- Validate the data by reassessing and exploring the difference between what the client says and what the nurse observes.

**Why it matters:** This is an exam-style example of why validation matters.


## Hygiene

### Card 1

**Front:** What special skin care considerations are important for older adults?

**Back:**
- Skin is thinner, drier, and more fragile.
- Use gentle handling, mild soap, and moisturizer.
- Avoid water that is too hot.

**Why it matters:** Older adult skin breaks down more easily.

### Card 2

**Front:** What hygiene considerations are important for clients with dementia?

**Back:**
- Use a calm voice and simple one-step directions.
- Keep a routine.
- Protect dignity and reduce overstimulation.

**Why it matters:** Behavior often improves when the nurse lowers confusion and stress.

### Card 3

**Front:** What hygiene considerations are important for obese clients?

**Back:**
- Inspect and dry skin folds carefully.
- Watch for rash, odor, moisture, and skin breakdown.
- Use enough staff or equipment to keep care safe.

**Why it matters:** Skin fold care is a common exam point.

### Card 4

**Front:** What are common risk factors for impaired skin integrity?

**Back:**
- Immobility
- Moisture or incontinence
- Poor nutrition
- Poor circulation
- Age
- Diabetes
- Friction and shearing

**Why it matters:** This is high-yield because many care decisions depend on skin risk.

### Card 5

**Front:** How should hearing aids be handled during hygiene care?

**Back:**
- Remove gently.
- Keep them dry unless the device instructions say otherwise.
- Clean correctly and store safely.

**Why it matters:** Clients lose access to communication when hearing aids are mishandled.

### Card 6

**Front:** How should dentures be handled during hygiene care?

**Back:**
- Handle over a towel or basin with water.
- Brush gently and rinse well.
- Check the mouth for redness or sores.

**Why it matters:** Dentures break easily and poor fit can harm nutrition and comfort.

### Card 7

**Front:** How should an artificial eye be cared for?

**Back:**
- Remove and clean gently if this is part of the nurse’s role.
- Protect the prosthesis from damage.
- Inspect the eye socket and surrounding tissue.

**Why it matters:** This is a careful-procedure question that also tests respect for dignity.

### Card 8

**Front:** What oral care changes help clients with altered oral mucosa?

**Back:**
- Use a soft brush or gentle swabs.
- Avoid harsh mouthwash if tissues are irritated.
- Keep lips moist and assess for sores or bleeding.

**Why it matters:** This is common in clients with dry mouth, oxygen, chemo, or mouth irritation.

### Card 9

**Front:** Why is oral care especially important for ventilated clients?

**Back:**
- It lowers the risk for ventilator-associated pneumonia.
- It helps manage secretions and protects oral tissue.

**Why it matters:** This is a patient-safety issue, not just a comfort issue.

### Card 10

**Front:** How should oral care be given to an unconscious client?

**Back:**
- Reduce aspiration risk with safe positioning if possible.
- Use suction as needed.
- Avoid pouring large amounts of fluid into the mouth.

**Why it matters:** The airway remains the main safety concern.

### Card 11

**Front:** What is appropriate routine ear care and client teaching?

**Back:**
- Clean the outer ear only.
- Do not put objects deep into the ear canal.
- Teach clients to report pain, drainage, or hearing changes.

**Why it matters:** This card helps separate safe ear care from unsafe habits.

### Card 12

**Front:** What are key safety points for cerumen impaction irrigation?

**Back:**
- Only irrigate when appropriate and with an intact eardrum.
- Use warm solution.
- Stop if there is pain, bleeding, or dizziness.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic “stop the procedure if” question.

### Card 13

**Front:** How do complete, partial, tub, shower, chlorhexidine (CHG), and bag baths differ?

**Back:**
- Complete bath: the whole body is bathed by the nurse.
- Partial bath: only selected body parts are bathed.
- Tub or shower: the client bathes in a tub or shower.
- CHG bath: uses chlorhexidine for infection prevention in some settings.
- Bag bath: uses prepackaged disposable cloths.

**Why it matters:** Bathing type questions test both terminology and care planning.

### Card 14

**Front:** How are eyelids cleaned safely?

**Back:**
- Clean from the inner canthus to the outer canthus.
- Use a clean part of the cloth for each stroke.

**Why it matters:** This is a small but common skill detail.

### Card 15

**Front:** Which clients are most at risk for fatigue during hygiene care?

**Back:**
- Older adults
- Weak or post-op clients
- Clients with heart or lung disease
- Clients with fever, pain, or low activity tolerance

**Why it matters:** The nurse may need to shorten care or let the client rest.

### Card 16

**Front:** Why does diabetic foot care require special precautions?

**Back:**
- Diabetes can reduce sensation and circulation.
- Small injuries may go unnoticed and heal slowly.

**Why it matters:** This explains why simple foot problems can become severe.

### Card 17

**Front:** What are key diabetic foot care instructions?

**Back:**
- Inspect feet daily.
- Wash and dry well, especially between toes.
- Moisturize, but not between toes.
- Never walk barefoot.
- Wear proper shoes and report sores quickly.

**Why it matters:** These are common patient-teaching points.

### Card 18

**Front:** A client refuses a bath. What is the nurse’s best first response?

**Back:**
- Assess why the client is refusing and support the client’s dignity and choice while still addressing essential hygiene needs.

**Why it matters:** Exam questions often reward assessment and respect before forceful action.


## Older Adult

### Card 1

**Front:** What are common myths or stereotypes about aging?

**Back:**
- That all older adults are confused, depressed, weak, asexual, or dependent.
- That aging always means disease.

**Why it matters:** Unsafe stereotypes can lead to poor nursing care.

### Card 2

**Front:** What are common normal physiological changes in older adults?

**Back:**
- Thinner skin
- Less thirst
- Reduced vision or hearing
- Lower muscle strength
- Slower kidney and liver function
- Reduced lung elasticity

**Why it matters:** Knowing normal aging helps separate normal change from disease.

### Card 3

**Front:** Why are older adults at increased risk for infection?

**Back:**
- Weaker immune response
- Less obvious fever response
- Chronic illness is more common
- Body barriers such as skin and mucous membranes are less protective

**Why it matters:** Infection may be harder to notice early in older adults.

### Card 4

**Front:** How do delirium, dementia, and depression differ?

**Back:**
- Delirium: sudden onset, fluctuating, poor attention, often reversible.
- Dementia: slow, progressive cognitive decline.
- Depression: low mood and loss of interest that may look like poor memory.

**Why it matters:** This is one of the highest-yield older adult comparisons.

### Card 5

**Front:** What nutritional challenges are common in aging?

**Back:**
- Lower appetite
- Dental problems
- Swallowing problems
- Money or access problems
- Medications that affect appetite or taste
- Loneliness or depression

**Why it matters:** Nutrition questions often connect body change with social change.

### Card 6

**Front:** What fall risk factors are common in older adults?

**Back:**
- Weakness or poor balance
- Vision problems
- Sedating medications
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Confusion
- Unsafe environment

**Why it matters:** Fall risk is a major older adult safety topic.

### Card 7

**Front:** What signs can suggest elder abuse?

**Back:**
- Unexplained bruises or injuries
- Fearfulness around caregivers
- Poor hygiene or malnutrition
- Delay in seeking care
- Sudden financial problems

**Why it matters:** These findings must be taken seriously and followed up.

### Card 8

**Front:** Why is elder abuse important to recognize?

**Back:**
- It can involve physical, emotional, sexual, financial abuse, or neglect.
- Nurses may be legally required to report suspected abuse.

**Why it matters:** This is both a safety and legal issue.

### Card 9

**Front:** What is polypharmacy and why is it dangerous?

**Back:**
- Polypharmacy means taking many medications, often more than needed.
- It raises the risk for confusion, falls, side effects, and drug interactions.

**Why it matters:** Polypharmacy is a major exam point in older adult care.

### Card 10

**Front:** How can loss, grief, and loneliness affect older adults?

**Back:**
- They can worsen mood, sleep, appetite, coping, and overall health.

**Why it matters:** Psychosocial health is part of nursing care, not an extra issue.

### Card 11

**Front:** What stress and coping challenges are common in aging?

**Back:**
- Chronic illness
- Caregiving stress
- Financial worries
- Less independence

**Why it matters:** Coping questions often ask you to assess support and not make assumptions.

### Card 12

**Front:** Why should nurses not ignore sexuality in older adults?

**Back:**
- Older adults still have sexual needs, questions, and concerns.
- Sexuality does not disappear with age.

**Why it matters:** Ignoring sexuality is a stereotype, not good nursing care.

### Card 13

**Front:** How can changing roles and relationships affect older adults?

**Back:**
- Retirement, widowhood, moving, caregiving, and dependence can change identity and relationships.

**Why it matters:** Role changes often affect mental health and coping.

### Card 14

**Front:** An older adult suddenly becomes confused and inattentive after being stable yesterday. What problem should the nurse suspect first?

**Back:**
- Delirium until proven otherwise, and the nurse should assess for acute causes such as infection, medication effects, or dehydration.

**Why it matters:** Research-based older adult care stresses that sudden confusion is not “normal aging.”

### Card 15

**Front:** How should the nurse communicate with an older adult who has hearing or vision loss?

**Back:**
- Face the client, speak clearly, reduce background noise, and make sure glasses or hearing aids are available.

**Why it matters:** This improves both safety and respect.


## Client Safety

### Card 1

**Front:** What environmental hazards commonly increase client injury risk?

**Back:**
- Clutter
- Wet floors
- Poor lighting
- Loose cords
- Wrong footwear
- Bed too high

**Why it matters:** Many safety questions begin with a room or home hazard.

### Card 2

**Front:** What are common fall prevention strategies?

**Back:**
- Assess risk
- Keep the bed low and locked
- Keep the call light in reach
- Use non-skid footwear
- Assist with toileting
- Round frequently

**Why it matters:** This is one of the most tested nursing safety topics.

### Card 3

**Front:** What makes ambulation safe?

**Back:**
- Assess the client first.
- Use proper footwear.
- Use a gait belt if needed.
- Stand on the weak side and watch for dizziness.

**Why it matters:** Safe ambulation questions combine preparation and technique.

### Card 4

**Front:** What is the correct response when a client starts to fall?

**Back:**
- Support the client and lower them safely.
- Protect the head.
- Do not try to hold the client upright by force.
- Assess after the fall.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic safety and skills question.

### Card 5

**Front:** How can nurses prevent electrical hazards?

**Back:**
- Do not use damaged cords.
- Keep equipment away from water.
- Dry hands before touching equipment.
- Avoid overloaded outlets.

**Why it matters:** This applies to both hospital and home settings.

### Card 6

**Front:** What is a Never Event?

**Back:**
- A serious, preventable safety event that should not happen if proper safety systems are used.

**Why it matters:** Never Events emphasize the importance of systems and safe practice.

### Card 7

**Front:** What are major Joint Commission National Patient Safety Goals?

**Back:**
- Correct client identification
- Better communication
- Medication safety
- Infection prevention
- Fall and harm prevention

**Why it matters:** These are broad safety priorities seen across settings.

### Card 8

**Front:** What does RACE stand for in fire safety?

**Back:**
- Rescue
- Activate the alarm
- Contain
- Extinguish or Evacuate

**Why it matters:** This is a standard memory card for fire response, and the book specifically says “activate the alarm.”

### Card 9

**Front:** What basic types of restraints may be used in health care?

**Back:**
- Physical restraints
- Chemical restraints
- Medical-surgical or behavioral restraints depending on the situation and policy

**Why it matters:** Knowing the type helps you think through the rules.

### Card 10

**Front:** What alternatives should be tried before restraints?

**Back:**
- Reorientation
- Toileting
- Pain control
- Family presence
- Sitter if available
- Bed alarms or low bed
- Safer environment changes

**Why it matters:** Restraints are a last resort.

### Card 11

**Front:** What is required before restraints are used?

**Back:**
- A proper provider order and ongoing reassessment according to policy.

**Why it matters:** The legal rules are a major exam point.

### Card 12

**Front:** Why are PRN restraint orders inappropriate?

**Back:**
- Restraint use must be based on a current assessment, not a blanket “as needed” order.

**Why it matters:** This is a direct legal and safety concept.

### Card 13

**Front:** How should restraints be secured safely?

**Back:**
- Use a quick-release knot.
- Secure to the bed frame, not the side rail.
- Leave enough room for circulation but not enough to slip out.

**Why it matters:** A restraint can become dangerous if secured incorrectly.

### Card 14

**Front:** What unexpected complications can restraints cause?

**Back:**
- Pressure injuries
- Pneumonia
- Contractures
- Nerve damage
- Circulatory impairment

**Why it matters:** This shows why restraints are not harmless.

### Card 15

**Front:** How can restraint-related complications be reduced?

**Back:**
- Reassess often.
- Remove restraints as soon as possible.
- Provide skin care, range of motion, hydration, nutrition, and toileting.

**Why it matters:** Prevention questions often ask what routine care is required.

### Card 16

**Front:** What should the nurse assess frequently when restraints are in use?

**Back:**
- Placement
- Skin condition
- Circulation
- Nerve function or sensation
- Basic needs and comfort

**Why it matters:** Frequent assessment is part of safe restraint use.

### Card 17

**Front:** A confused client keeps trying to climb out of bed. What should the nurse try before restraints?

**Back:**
- Meet needs such as pain or toileting, reorient the client, use a low bed or alarm, and increase observation if possible.

**Why it matters:** Exam questions favor least restrictive alternatives first.

### Card 18

**Front:** A small fire starts in a client room. What should the nurse think of first?

**Back:**
- RACE: Rescue anyone in danger, activate the alarm, contain the fire, then extinguish or evacuate as appropriate.

**Why it matters:** This card turns the acronym into action.


## Mobility

### Card 1

**Front:** What are the main principles of proper body mechanics?

**Back:**
- Keep a wide base of support.
- Bend knees, not the waist.
- Keep the load close.
- Avoid twisting.
- Use large muscle groups and ask for help when needed.

**Why it matters:** Body mechanics protect both the client and the nurse.

### Card 2

**Front:** What factors commonly affect mobility?

**Back:**
- Pain
- Weakness
- Age
- Injury or surgery
- Neurological problems
- Obesity
- Fear of falling
- Sedating medications

**Why it matters:** This is a common first-step mobility question.

### Card 3

**Front:** How can immobility affect blood pressure and heart rate?

**Back:**
- It can cause orthostatic hypotension and compensatory tachycardia.

**Why it matters:** The client may look fine in bed but become dizzy or unstable when standing.

### Card 4

**Front:** How can immobility affect bowel and bladder function?

**Back:**
- It can cause constipation and urinary stasis.

**Why it matters:** This is a high-yield “which system problem can happen with bed rest?” question.

### Card 5

**Front:** How can immobility affect the circulatory and respiratory systems?

**Back:**
- It raises the risk for deep vein thrombosis (DVT), atelectasis, and pneumonia.

**Why it matters:** Movement helps both blood flow and lung expansion.

### Card 6

**Front:** How can immobility affect muscles, skin, and mood?

**Back:**
- It can cause weakness, pressure injuries, boredom, and depression.

**Why it matters:** Immobility affects more than just movement itself.

### Card 7

**Front:** What does activity tolerance mean?

**Back:**
- It is how much activity the client can do safely without too much fatigue or instability.

**Why it matters:** The nurse should assess how the client responds, not just whether the activity was completed.

### Card 8

**Front:** What fall risk factors are especially important when a client is getting up?

**Back:**
- Weakness
- Dizziness
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Sedating medications
- Confusion
- Unsafe shoes or clutter

**Why it matters:** This helps you think before moving the client.

### Card 9

**Front:** How do Fowler’s, semi-Fowler’s, supine, prone, side-lying, and Sims’ positions differ?

**Back:**
- Fowler’s: upright
- Semi-Fowler’s: partly upright
- Supine: flat on back
- Prone: on abdomen
- Side-lying: on side
- Sims’: side-lying with upper leg flexed

**Why it matters:** Position terms are basic but heavily tested.

### Card 10

**Front:** What are key safety points in a bed-to-chair transfer?

**Back:**
- Lock the bed and chair.
- Place the chair close.
- Use a gait belt when appropriate.
- Let the client sit first if needed, then pivot safely.

**Why it matters:** Transfer questions focus on preparation and control.

### Card 11

**Front:** What are common positioning aids?

**Back:**
- Pillows
- Footboards
- Trochanter rolls
- Hand rolls
- Draw sheets
- Trapeze bars

**Why it matters:** Positioning aids prevent complications and support alignment.

### Card 12

**Front:** How does bed rest affect the respiratory system, and what nursing actions help?

**Back:**
- Bed rest reduces lung expansion and raises atelectasis and pneumonia risk.
- Turning, coughing, deep breathing, and incentive spirometry help.

**Why it matters:** This is a common cause-and-intervention question.

### Card 13

**Front:** How does bed rest affect the circulatory system, and what should the nurse remember before ambulation?

**Back:**
- Bed rest raises clot risk and can cause orthostatic hypotension.
- Change positions slowly and assess blood pressure before ambulation when indicated.

**Why it matters:** This is a safety-focused mobility card.

### Card 14

**Front:** How does bed rest affect the gastrointestinal and integumentary systems?

**Back:**
- Gastrointestinal: constipation is common.
- Integumentary: pressure injury risk rises.
- Fluids, fiber, movement, and turning help reduce these problems.

**Why it matters:** One body-system question often includes more than one answer area.

### Card 15

**Front:** How is a cane used correctly?

**Back:**
- The cane is usually held on the strong side.
- Move the cane and weak leg together, then move the strong leg.

**Why it matters:** This is a very common skills question.

### Card 16

**Front:** How is a walker used correctly?

**Back:**
- Move the walker first.
- Then move the weak leg.
- Then move the strong leg.

**Why it matters:** The safest answer usually follows this order.

### Card 17

**Front:** How should crutches be sized?

**Back:**
- The top should be below the axilla.
- Weight should go through the hands, not the armpits.
- Elbows should be slightly bent.

**Why it matters:** Wrong sizing can cause nerve injury.

### Card 18

**Front:** How do four-point, three-point, two-point, and swing-through crutch gaits differ?

**Back:**
- Four-point: very stable, often for weakness in both legs.
- Three-point: one leg is non-weight-bearing.
- Two-point: more natural pattern when both legs can bear some weight.
- Swing-through: both crutches move forward, then the body swings through.

**Why it matters:** Gait questions often test stability and weight-bearing rules together.


## Communication (Craven, Chapter 14)

### Card 1

**Front:** How do verbal and nonverbal communication differ?

**Back:**
- Verbal communication uses spoken or written words.
- Nonverbal communication includes facial expression, posture, touch, gestures, and tone of voice.

**Why it matters:** Nonverbal cues can support or contradict words.

### Card 2

**Front:** What are common therapeutic communication techniques?

**Back:**
- Active listening
- Silence
- Open-ended questions
- Clarifying
- Restating
- Reflecting feelings
- Summarizing
- Offering self

**Why it matters:** These techniques help clients feel heard and help nurses gather accurate data.

### Card 3

**Front:** What are common non-therapeutic communication examples?

**Back:**
- False reassurance
- Advice-giving
- Judging
- Changing the subject
- Asking “why” in a blaming way
- Minimizing feelings

**Why it matters:** These responses often shut the client down.

### Card 4

**Front:** What is process recording used for?

**Back:**
- It helps the nurse review an interaction, including what the client said, what the nurse said, and what could improve next time.

**Why it matters:** Process recording is a learning tool for communication skills.

### Card 5

**Front:** How is a nurse–patient relationship different from a social relationship?

**Back:**
- The nurse–patient relationship is professional, goal-directed, and focused on the client’s needs.
- A social relationship is personal and mutual.

**Why it matters:** Boundary questions often test this distinction.

### Card 6

**Front:** What does it mean for a nurse to be a patient advocate?

**Back:**
- The nurse protects client rights, speaks up about safety, supports informed choice, and helps the client’s preferences be heard.

**Why it matters:** Advocacy is a core nursing role.

### Card 7

**Front:** What are common signs of compassion fatigue?

**Back:**
- Emotional exhaustion
- Irritability
- Feeling numb or detached
- Less empathy
- Trouble recovering from work stress

**Why it matters:** Compassion fatigue affects both nurse well-being and care quality.

### Card 8

**Front:** What communication strategies help clients with special needs?

**Back:**
- Use interpreters for language barriers.
- Face clients with hearing loss.
- Use simple words and one idea at a time for cognitive problems.
- Allow extra time for a response.

**Why it matters:** Questions often ask for the safest or most respectful communication choice.

### Card 9

**Front:** A client says, “I don’t think I can do this anymore,” and begins crying. What is a therapeutic response?

**Back:**
- Acknowledge the feeling and invite the client to say more, such as “This sounds very hard for you. Tell me more about what you’re feeling.”

**Why it matters:** Therapeutic responses encourage expression instead of stopping it.

### Card 10

**Front:** How should the nurse communicate with a hearing-impaired client?

**Back:**
- Face the client.
- Reduce background noise.
- Speak clearly and make sure hearing aids are available if used.

**Why it matters:** Turning away or shouting is not good communication.

### Card 11

**Front:** Why is a professional interpreter usually safer than relying only on family for medical communication?

**Back:**
- Interpreters reduce errors, protect privacy, and improve accuracy for informed decisions.

**Why it matters:** This is a common safety and ethics question.

### Card 12

**Front:** What is one healthy response to signs of compassion fatigue?

**Back:**
- Recognize the signs early, seek support, rest, and use healthy coping rather than ignoring the problem.

**Why it matters:** The nurse’s well-being affects safe care.


## Documentation and Informatics (Craven, Chapter 10)

### Card 1

**Front:** How can nurses help maintain confidentiality in health records?

**Back:**
- Log off computers.
- Do not share passwords.
- Discuss client information only with those involved in care.
- Avoid public conversations about clients.

**Why it matters:** Confidentiality is both an ethical and legal duty.

### Card 2

**Front:** What are key legal documentation guidelines?

**Back:**
- Chart promptly.
- Be accurate, factual, objective, and complete.
- Avoid abbreviations in legal documentation when possible.
- Never chart before care is given.

**Why it matters:** This is a high-yield legal content area.

### Card 3

**Front:** How do paper and electronic charting differ?

**Back:**
- Paper charting is handwritten and less easy to share.
- Electronic charting is easier to access and read but still requires privacy and accuracy.

**Why it matters:** Both require legal and safe documentation.

### Card 4

**Front:** What is narrative charting?

**Back:**
- Narrative charting is chronological free-text documentation.

**Why it matters:** This is the simplest charting style to define.

### Card 5

**Front:** What does SOAP mean in charting?

**Back:**
- Subjective
- Objective
- Assessment
- Plan

**Why it matters:** SOAP is a classic problem-oriented charting format.

### Card 6

**Front:** What does PIE mean in charting?

**Back:**
- Problem
- Intervention
- Evaluation

**Why it matters:** PIE focuses on the problem and the nursing response.

### Card 7

**Front:** What does DAR or Focus charting mean?

**Back:**
- Data
- Action
- Response

**Why it matters:** DAR is often used in focus charting.

### Card 8

**Front:** What is charting by exception?

**Back:**
- It documents abnormal findings or important deviations instead of repeating every normal detail.

**Why it matters:** This style depends on clear standards for normal findings.

### Card 9

**Front:** When are incident reports completed, and who usually completes them?

**Back:**
- They are completed after unusual events such as falls, errors, or injuries.
- The person who witnessed or discovered the event usually completes the report according to policy.

**Why it matters:** Incident report questions test both timing and responsibility.

### Card 10

**Front:** What is the purpose of an incident report, and what should the nurse chart in the medical record instead?

**Back:**
- Incident reports support safety review and system improvement.
- In the medical record, chart the facts of what happened and the client’s response.
- Do not chart that an incident report was filed.

**Why it matters:** This is a common legal documentation trap question.

### Card 11

**Front:** What is the purpose of a hand-off report?

**Back:**
- To transfer essential client information safely from one caregiver to another.

**Why it matters:** Poor hand-off can directly harm safety.

### Card 12

**Front:** What should a good hand-off report include?

**Back:**
- Client identity
- Current condition
- Recent changes
- Treatments and lines
- Safety concerns
- What still needs follow-up

**Why it matters:** This is a practical communication-and-safety question.

### Card 13

**Front:** What are key safety points in verbal and telephone reporting?

**Back:**
- Be clear and concise.
- Include identifiers and urgent concerns.
- Read back important orders to confirm accuracy.

**Why it matters:** Read-back is a strong safety point on exams.

### Card 14

**Front:** What does SBAR stand for?

**Back:**
- Situation
- Background
- Assessment
- Recommendation

**Why it matters:** SBAR organizes communication in a standard way.

### Card 15

**Front:** Which documentation example is best: “Client seems fine” or “Client denies pain, respirations 18, skin warm and dry”?

**Back:**
- “Client denies pain, respirations 18, skin warm and dry” is better because it is specific, objective, and measurable.

**Why it matters:** The best charting answer is usually the most factual and concrete.
