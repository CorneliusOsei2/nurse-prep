# Exam 1 Guide

This guide follows the Exam 1 outline and turns it into study notes that are easier to read, easier to remember, and more useful for nursing exam questions.

## The Nursing Process

(This section is about how nurses think through care step by step.)

### Define and differentiate the five steps of the nursing process

- The nursing process is usually taught as **five steps (ADPIE)**:
- **Assessment:** collect data about the client.
- **Diagnosis:** identify nursing problems or human responses that nursing care can address.
- **Planning:** set priorities and decide interventions.
- **Implementation:** carry out the plan of care.
- **Evaluation:** compare the client’s actual response with the expected outcomes.

### Distinguish between types of assessment data

#### Subjective vs. Objective

- **Subjective data:** what the client says or feels, such as pain, nausea, fear, or dizziness.
- **Objective data:** what the nurse observes or measures, such as temperature, blood pressure, wound drainage, or swelling.

#### Primary vs. Secondary sources

- **Primary source:** the client, when the client can answer reliably.
- **Secondary sources:** family, caregivers, chart, laboratory results, and other health team members.

### Explain the rationale for validating data

- Validation means checking data that seem unusual, inconsistent, incomplete, or unclear.
- It helps prevent errors and wrong conclusions.
- The nurse should validate before acting on questionable information whenever the situation is not immediately life-threatening.

### Compare nursing problems vs. medical diagnoses

- A **medical diagnosis** identifies a disease or health problem, such as pneumonia or diabetes.
- A **nursing problem** focuses on the client’s response, such as impaired gas exchange, pain, or risk for falls.
- Nurses treat human responses and help manage the effects of the disease.

### Discuss the importance of individualized client care

- Clients do not all have the same goals, strengths, culture, support system, learning needs, or preferences.
- Good nursing care should match the client’s actual needs instead of using one plan for everyone.
- Individualized care improves safety, trust, and outcomes.

### Prioritize problems as high, medium, or low priority

- **High priority:** immediate threats to life, airway, breathing, circulation, and safety.
- **Medium priority:** important problems that are not immediately life-threatening but still need prompt attention.
- **Low priority:** problems that can wait longer, such as some education or long-term needs in a stable client.

### Define nursing outcomes

- Nursing outcomes are the client-centered results the nurse expects after care is given.
- They describe what the client should do, feel, or demonstrate.

### Identify components of well-written, client-centered outcomes using SMART criteria

- **Specific:** clearly states what should happen.
- **Measurable:** can be observed or measured.
- **Achievable:** realistic for the client.
- **Relevant/Realistic:** fits the client’s condition and priorities.
- **Time-bound:** includes a time frame.
- Good outcomes are client-centered, not nurse-centered.

### Distinguish between planning and implementation phases of nursing care

- **Planning:** deciding priorities, outcomes, and interventions.
- **Implementation:** actually doing the interventions and documenting them.
- Planning answers **what should be done**. Implementation answers **what was done**.

### Differentiate between nurse-initiated interventions, health care provider-prescribed interventions, and collaborative interventions

- **Nurse-initiated interventions:** actions the nurse can start independently, such as repositioning or teaching.
- **Health care provider-prescribed interventions:** actions that require an order, such as medications or treatments.
- **Collaborative interventions:** actions done with other team members, such as physical therapy, respiratory therapy, or dietitian care.

### Use Maslow’s Hierarchy and the ABC method to prioritize care

- **ABC method:** airway first, then breathing, then circulation.
- **Maslow:** physiological needs come before safety, love/belonging, esteem, and self-actualization.
- In emergencies, ABCs usually come first.
- In stable situations, Maslow can help sort non-emergency priorities.

### Describe the evaluation step and actions when outcomes are not met

- During evaluation, the nurse decides whether outcomes were met, partly met, or not met.
- If outcomes are not met, the nurse reassesses the client, looks for barriers, and changes the plan if needed.
- Nursing care should continue, change, or stop based on what the client actually needs.

## Hygiene

(This section is about skin care, daily care, and safe hygiene support.)

### Identify special hygiene/skin care considerations for older adults

- Older adults often have thinner, drier, more fragile skin.
- Hot water and strong soaps can dry the skin more.
- Gentle handling, mild soap, and moisturizer are important.
- They may fatigue more easily during hygiene care.

### Identify special hygiene/skin care considerations for clients with dementia

- Use a calm tone and simple one-step directions.
- Keep a routine and reduce distractions.
- Explain each step before touching the client.
- Protect dignity and avoid arguing if the client becomes upset.

### Identify special hygiene/skin care considerations for obese clients

- Inspect skin folds carefully.
- Dry folds well to reduce moisture, odor, rash, and infection risk.
- Use enough help and proper equipment for safe care.
- Watch for fatigue, pressure injuries, and difficulty reaching body areas.

### Discuss risk factors for impaired skin integrity

- Immobility
- Incontinence or excess moisture
- Poor nutrition and dehydration
- Older age
- Poor circulation and edema
- Obesity
- Diabetes
- Friction and shearing
- Sensory loss

### Describe care procedures for hearing aids

- Remove them gently.
- Keep them dry unless the product says otherwise.
- Clean according to instructions.
- Store them safely in a labeled container when not in use.
- Make sure they are returned to the correct client.

### Describe care procedures for dentures

- Handle dentures carefully over a towel or basin with water.
- Brush gently with denture cleaner or approved cleanser.
- Rinse well.
- Remove at night if appropriate.
- Assess the mouth and gums for redness, sores, or poor fit.

### Describe care procedures for artificial eyes

- Remove and reinsert gently if needed and if the nurse is expected to perform the task.
- Clean according to instructions.
- Protect the prosthesis from damage.
- Assess the socket and surrounding tissue for irritation or drainage.

### Review oral care techniques for clients with altered oral mucosa

- Use a soft toothbrush or gentle swabs.
- Avoid alcohol-based mouthwash if tissues are irritated.
- Keep lips moist.
- Check for sores, bleeding, dryness, white patches, and pain.

### Review oral care techniques for ventilated clients

- Provide regular oral care to reduce pneumonia risk.
- Suction secretions as needed.
- Use chlorhexidine (CHG) if it is part of policy or the care plan.
- Check lips, tongue, gums, and oral secretions often.

### Review oral care techniques for unconscious clients

- Position the client to reduce aspiration risk, often side-lying if possible.
- Use suction as needed.
- Do not pour large amounts of fluid into the mouth.
- Clean gently and frequently.

### Explain appropriate ear care: routine care, client teaching, and cerumen impaction irrigation

#### Routine care

- Clean only the outer ear.
- Do not place objects deep in the ear canal.

#### Client teaching

- Report pain, drainage, hearing changes, or dizziness.
- Teach correct care of hearing aids.

#### Cerumen impaction irrigation

- Only irrigate if appropriate and if the eardrum is intact.
- Use warm solution, not hot or cold.
- Stop if the client has pain, dizziness, nausea, or bleeding.

### Describe bathing techniques and types

- **Complete bath:** the nurse bathes the whole client.
- **Partial bath:** only selected body parts are bathed.
- **Tub bath:** the client bathes in a tub.
- **Shower:** the client stands or sits in a shower.
- **CHG bath:** uses chlorhexidine for infection prevention in some settings.
- **Bag bath:** a prepackaged set of warm disposable cloths used for bed bathing.

### Explain proper eyelid cleansing

- Clean from the inner canthus to the outer canthus.
- Use a clean area of the cloth for each stroke.
- Be gentle.

### Identify clients at risk for fatigue during hygiene care

- Older adults
- Clients with heart or lung disease
- Clients who are weak, post-op, anemic, or in pain
- Clients with fever or poor activity tolerance

### Outline diabetic foot care

#### Explain why special precautions are needed

- Diabetes can reduce sensation and blood flow.
- Small injuries may go unnoticed and heal slowly.

#### Identify the risks

- Ulcers
- Infection
- Burns from hot water
- Poor healing
- Amputation risk if injuries worsen

#### Review specific foot care instructions

- Inspect feet every day.
- Wash and dry carefully, especially between toes.
- Use moisturizer, but not between toes.
- Wear clean socks and well-fitting shoes.
- Never walk barefoot.
- Test water temperature first.
- Report redness, cuts, blisters, or nonhealing areas.

## Older Adult

(This section is about normal aging, risks, and respectful care of older adults.)

### Identify myths and stereotypes about aging

- Aging does not automatically mean confusion, depression, weakness, or dependence.
- Older adults are not all the same.
- Aging is not the same as disease.
- Older adults still have emotional, social, and sexual needs.

### Review normal physiological changes in older adults

- Skin becomes thinner and drier.
- Vision and hearing may decline.
- Thirst sensation often decreases.
- Lung elasticity decreases.
- Muscle mass and strength may decrease.
- Kidney and liver function may slow.
- Reaction time and balance may worsen.

#### Explain why older adults are at increased infection risk

- Immune response may be weaker.
- Skin and mucous membranes may be less protective.
- Chronic illness is more common.
- Fever response may be less obvious, so infection can be harder to notice early.

### Differentiate delirium, dementia, and depression

- **Delirium:** sudden onset, fluctuating, poor attention, often reversible, usually caused by illness, medication, or another acute problem.
- **Dementia:** slow progressive decline in memory and thinking.
- **Depression:** low mood, loss of interest, low energy, and may look like poor concentration or memory problems.

### Discuss nutritional challenges in aging

- Lower appetite
- Dental problems or dentures that do not fit well
- Trouble swallowing
- Less money or access to food
- Isolation or depression
- Many medications affecting appetite or taste
- Lower thirst, which increases dehydration risk

### Identify fall risk factors in the elderly

- Weakness or poor balance
- Vision problems
- Medications causing dizziness or sedation
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Urgency or frequent toileting
- Confusion or delirium
- Unsafe environment, clutter, or poor lighting

### Discuss signs and implications of elder abuse

- Unexplained bruises, burns, or injuries
- Poor hygiene, dehydration, or malnutrition
- Fearfulness around caregivers
- Delay in seeking care
- Sudden financial problems or missing belongings
- Elder abuse can be physical, emotional, sexual, financial, or neglect.
- Suspected abuse must be reported according to law and policy.

### Define polypharmacy and its consequences

- Polypharmacy means using many medications, often more than are truly needed.
- It raises the risk for drug interactions, side effects, falls, confusion, and poor adherence.

### Explore psychosocial aspects of aging

#### Loss, grief, loneliness

- Older adults may face retirement, death of loved ones, less independence, or moving from home.
- Loneliness can affect mood, sleep, nutrition, and health.

#### Stress and coping

- Chronic illness, caregiving, finances, and role changes can increase stress.
- Coping depends on support, health, personality, and resources.

#### Sexuality

- Older adults still have sexual needs and concerns.
- Nurses should not ignore sexuality because of age.

#### Changing roles and relationships

- Family roles may change.
- Older adults may become caregivers or may need more help from others.
- Loss of independence can affect identity and self-esteem.

## Client Safety

(This section is about preventing harm and responding correctly when safety risks appear.)

### Identify environmental hazards and interventions to reduce risks

- Clutter, wet floors, cords, poor lighting, wrong bed height, and poor footwear increase injury risk.
- Keep pathways clear.
- Keep frequently used items within reach.
- Use non-skid footwear.
- Keep the bed low when appropriate.
- Make sure assistive devices are nearby and working.

### List fall prevention strategies

- Assess fall risk regularly.
- Keep the call light in reach.
- Use non-skid footwear.
- Assist with toileting.
- Round often.
- Keep the bed low and locked.
- Use alarms if ordered or indicated.
- Remove clutter and improve lighting.

### Describe safe ambulation techniques

- Assess the client before ambulation.
- Use proper footwear.
- Use a gait belt when appropriate.
- Stand on the weak side.
- Allow the client to sit and dangle first if needed.
- Watch for dizziness or poor tolerance.

### Identify correct responses when a client starts to fall

- Do not try to hold the client upright by force.
- Widen your stance.
- Hold the gait belt if present.
- Support the client and lower them to the floor or against your leg safely.
- Protect the head.
- Assess the client after the fall.

### Prevent electrical hazards in clinical and home settings

- Do not use equipment with damaged cords.
- Keep electrical items away from water.
- Dry hands before handling equipment.
- Avoid overloaded outlets.
- Use grounded equipment.
- Teach home safety with cords, extension use, and appliance care.

### Define Never Events and their significance

- Never Events are serious, preventable safety events that should not happen if proper safety systems are in place.
- They are important because they reflect major safety failures and require reporting and system improvement.

### Identify the Joint Commission National Patient Safety Goals

- Use two identifiers to identify the client correctly.
- Improve communication among caregivers.
- Use medicines safely.
- Prevent infection.
- Identify safety risks such as falls.
- Improve alarm safety and other high-risk processes based on current goals and setting.

### Review fire safety procedures

#### What does R.A.C.E. stand for?

- **R:** Rescue
- **A:** Activate the alarm
- **C:** Contain
- **E:** Extinguish or Evacuate

### Understand the use of restraints

#### Types

- Physical restraints
- Chemical restraints
- Medical-surgical or behavioral restraints depending on the situation and policy

#### Alternatives

- Reorient the client.
- Meet pain, toileting, hunger, and comfort needs.
- Use a sitter if available.
- Move the client closer to the nurses’ station.
- Use bed alarms, low beds, or family support when appropriate.

#### Health care provider order requirements

- Restraints require a proper provider order.
- The order must match policy and the current client condition.
- Restraints must be time-limited and reassessed regularly.

#### Why PRN restraint orders are inappropriate

- Restraint use must be based on a current assessment, not a blanket “as needed” order.
- The nurse must reassess the need each time.

#### Proper securing methods

- Use quick-release knots.
- Secure to the bed frame, not side rails.
- Make sure the restraint is snug but not too tight.

#### Unexpected complications

- Pressure injuries
- Pneumonia
- Contractures
- Nerve damage
- Circulatory impairment

#### Preventing restraint-related complications

- Remove restraints as soon as possible.
- Reposition often.
- Provide range of motion, hydration, nutrition, and toileting.
- Check skin, circulation, sensation, breathing, and comfort.

#### Assessment frequency for restraints

- Follow policy and provider requirements.
- Assess placement, skin, circulation, nerve function, and basic needs frequently.

## Mobility

(This section is about moving safely, preventing complications, and using mobility aids correctly.)

### Review principles of proper body mechanics

- Keep a wide base of support.
- Bend at the knees, not the waist.
- Keep the load close to your body.
- Avoid twisting.
- Use large muscle groups.
- Ask for help or use equipment when needed.

### Identify factors affecting mobility

- Age
- Pain
- Weakness or fatigue
- Injury or surgery
- Neurological problems
- Fear of falling
- Obesity
- Sedating medications
- Chronic disease

### Recognize complications of immobility

#### Orthostatic hypotension

- Blood pressure falls when the client stands up.
- The client may feel dizzy or faint.

#### Tachycardia

- The heart rate may rise as the body tries to compensate.

#### Constipation

- Movement slows in the gastrointestinal tract.

#### Urinary stasis

- Urine sits in the bladder longer, increasing infection risk.

#### Deep vein thrombosis (DVT)

- Slow blood flow and poor movement increase clot risk.

#### Atelectasis/Pneumonia

- Lungs do not expand well, and secretions are harder to clear.

#### Impaired neuromuscular function

- Weakness and muscle wasting develop.

#### Pressure injuries

- Constant pressure reduces blood flow to skin and tissue.

#### Boredom and depression

- Long periods in bed affect mood and motivation.

### Discuss activity tolerance

- Activity tolerance means how much activity the client can do safely without too much fatigue or instability.
- The nurse watches heart rate, breathing, blood pressure, oxygen level, pain, and fatigue.

### Discuss fall risk factors

- Weakness
- Poor balance
- Sedating medication
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Confusion
- Urgency or incontinence
- Unsafe shoes or cluttered environment

### Differentiate between client positions

- **Fowler’s:** head of bed raised high.
- **Semi-Fowler’s:** head of bed raised moderately.
- **Supine:** flat on the back.
- **Prone:** flat on the abdomen.
- **Side-lying:** on the side.
- **Sims’:** side-lying with the upper leg flexed; often used for rectal procedures.

### Explain safe transfer and positioning

#### Bed to chair transfers

- Lock the bed and chair.
- Place the chair close to the bed.
- Use a gait belt when appropriate.
- Let the client sit first if needed before standing.
- Pivot safely and lower into the chair with control.

### Understand positioning aids

- Pillows
- Footboards
- Trochanter rolls
- Hand rolls
- Draw sheet
- Trapeze bar
- Boots or splints as ordered

### Interventions for clients on bed rest and their effect on body systems

#### Respiratory system

- Turn, cough, deep breathe, and use incentive spirometry if ordered.
- These actions help prevent atelectasis and pneumonia.

#### Circulatory system

- Change position slowly.
- Assess blood pressure before ambulation if orthostatic hypotension is a concern.
- Encourage leg exercises and movement as allowed.
- Use compression devices if ordered.

#### GI system

- Encourage fluids, fiber, and activity as allowed.
- Monitor bowel sounds and bowel pattern.

#### Integumentary system

- Reposition often.
- Use support surfaces and inspect skin regularly.

### Review mobility aids

#### Cane: proper use

- The cane is usually held on the strong side.
- Move the cane and weak leg together, then move the strong leg.

#### Walker: proper use

- Move the walker forward first.
- Then move the weak leg.
- Then move the strong leg.

#### Crutches: sizing

- Leave space below the axilla.
- Weight should be supported by the hands, not the armpits.
- Elbows should be slightly bent.

#### Gait types and weight-bearing implications

- **Four-point gait:** very stable, used when both legs can bear some weight.
- **Three-point gait:** used when one leg is non-weight-bearing.
- **Two-point gait:** used when both legs can bear partial weight and the client has more balance.
- **Swing-through gait:** both crutches move forward, then the legs swing through; often used with long-term crutch users.

## Communication (Craven, Chapter 14)

(This section is about how nurses build trust, gather accurate information, and support clients.)

### Differentiate between verbal vs. nonverbal communication

- **Verbal communication:** spoken or written words.
- **Nonverbal communication:** facial expression, posture, eye contact, gestures, touch, and tone of voice.
- Nonverbal cues can support or conflict with words.

### Identify therapeutic techniques

- Active listening
- Silence
- Open-ended questions
- Restating
- Reflecting
- Clarifying
- Focusing
- Summarizing
- Offering self
- Acknowledging feelings

### Review non-therapeutic communication examples

- Giving false reassurance
- Giving advice too quickly
- Changing the subject
- Judging or criticizing
- Asking “why” in a blaming way
- Minimizing the client’s feelings

### Review Table 14-4: process recording example

- Process recording is a written review of an interaction.
- It helps the nurse think about what the client said, what the nurse said, what worked well, and what could improve.
- It is a learning tool for communication skills.

### Contrast nurse-patient vs. social relationships

- **Nurse-patient relationship:** professional, goal-directed, time-limited, and focused on client needs.
- **Social relationship:** personal, mutual, and not focused on professional care goals.
- Nurses should keep professional boundaries.

### Explain the nurse’s role as patient advocate

- Protect the client’s rights and preferences.
- Speak up about safety concerns.
- Help the client understand options and make informed choices.
- Support the client if the client feels unheard or unsafe.

### Define and recognize signs of compassion fatigue

- Emotional exhaustion
- Irritability
- Less empathy
- Feeling detached or numb
- Trouble recovering emotionally from work stress

### Use effective communication strategies with clients with special needs

- Use interpreters for language barriers instead of relying on family alone.
- Face clients who have hearing impairment.
- Use glasses, hearing aids, and communication boards when needed.
- Use short, simple sentences for clients with cognitive changes.
- Give one idea at a time.
- Allow extra time for response.

## Documentation and Informatics (Craven, Chapter 10)

(This section is about safe charting, confidentiality, and clear reporting.)

### Discuss ways to maintain confidentiality in health records

- Log off computers when not in use.
- Do not share passwords.
- Discuss client information only with people involved in care.
- Avoid discussing clients in public areas.
- Protect printed and electronic records from unauthorized access.

### Review legal documentation guidelines (written and electronic)

- Chart promptly.
- Be accurate, factual, objective, and complete.
- Avoid abbreviations in legal documentation when possible.
- Record what was done and what the client response was.
- Never chart before care is given.
- Correct errors according to policy.
- Label late entries correctly.

### Compare charting types

#### Paper vs. Electronic

- **Paper charting:** handwritten, easier to misread, not as easy to access from different places.
- **Electronic charting:** faster to share, often safer for legibility, but still requires privacy and correct login practices.

#### Narrative

- Chronological free-text notes.

#### Problem-Oriented (SOAP, PIE, Focus/DAR)

- **SOAP:** Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan.
- **PIE:** Problem, Intervention, Evaluation.
- **DAR/Focus:** Data, Action, Response.

#### Charting by exception

- Only abnormal findings or deviations from the standard are charted.
- It saves time but requires understanding of normal standards.

### Identify guidelines for reporting unusual occurrences (incident reports)

#### Explain when they are completed

- Incident reports are completed after unusual events such as falls, errors, injuries, or equipment problems.

#### Identify who completes them

- The person who witnessed or discovered the event usually completes the report according to policy.

#### Discuss the purpose and appropriate documentation practices

- The purpose is safety review and system improvement, not punishment.
- In the chart, document the facts of what happened and the client’s response.
- Do not chart that an incident report was completed.

### Understand hand-off reports

#### Purpose and essential elements

- Handoff reports transfer essential information safely from one caregiver to another.
- Include client identity, diagnosis, current status, recent changes, treatments, safety concerns, and what still needs follow-up.

### Review verbal and telephone reporting

#### Use of SBAR

- SBAR helps organize urgent or important communication clearly.

#### Components of SBAR

- **S:** Situation
- **B:** Background
- **A:** Assessment
- **R:** Recommendation
- Read back telephone or verbal orders as required by policy to confirm accuracy.
