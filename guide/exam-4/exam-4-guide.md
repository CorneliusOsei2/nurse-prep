# Exam 4 Guide

This guide follows the Exam 4 outline and turns it into study notes that are correct, clear, and easier to learn.

## Diabetes

(This section is about how the body controls blood sugar and what happens when that control fails.)

### Explain normal regulation of blood glucose levels

- Insulin lowers blood glucose by helping glucose move from the blood into the cells.
- Glucagon raises blood glucose by telling the liver to release stored glucose.
- The liver stores glucose as glycogen and releases it when the body needs fuel.
- Cortisol and epinephrine can raise blood sugar, especially during stress or illness.
- In a healthy body, insulin and glucagon work together to keep blood glucose in a safe range.

### Explain the pathophysiology of diabetes

- Diabetes happens when the body does not make enough insulin, cannot use insulin well, or both.
- Because of this, glucose stays in the blood instead of moving into the cells.
- Cells do not get the fuel they need, even when there is a lot of glucose in the blood.
- High blood glucose over time damages blood vessels, nerves, kidneys, eyes, and the immune response.

### Differentiate between Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes

- **Type 1 diabetes:** autoimmune destruction of beta cells in the pancreas causes little or no insulin production.
- **Type 1 diabetes:** usually begins earlier in life, but it can happen at any age.
- **Type 1 diabetes:** requires insulin replacement and has a higher risk for diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).
- **Type 2 diabetes:** the body still makes insulin, but the cells resist it and the pancreas cannot keep up.
- **Type 2 diabetes:** is strongly linked to excess body weight, inactivity, family history, and age, though younger adults can also develop it.
- **Type 2 diabetes:** may be managed with lifestyle changes, oral medications, other injectable diabetes medicines, insulin, or a combination.
- **Type 2 diabetes:** has a higher risk for hyperglycemic hyperosmolar syndrome (HHS).

### Identify contributing factors/risk factors for diabetes

- Family history
- Overweight or obesity
- Physical inactivity
- Increasing age
- History of gestational diabetes
- Prediabetes
- Hypertension
- Dyslipidemia
- Certain ethnic backgrounds with higher risk
- Unhealthy diet and long-term poor metabolic control

### Explain interventions for management of diabetes

- The goal is to keep blood glucose as close to target as safely possible.
- Management usually includes monitoring, food planning, activity, weight control, medications, foot care, and follow-up care.

#### Glucose monitoring

- Check blood glucose as ordered and more often during illness or medication changes.
- Look for patterns, not just one number.
- Teach the client to know target ranges and when to call the provider.
- Keep a log if needed to connect glucose values with meals, exercise, and medications.

#### Weight management

- Modest weight loss can improve insulin sensitivity in many clients with Type 2 diabetes.
- Encourage healthy meal planning, portion control, and long-term habits instead of crash diets.
- Weight management can lower blood glucose and may lower blood pressure and lipid levels too.

#### Activity/Exercise

- Regular activity helps the body use insulin better.
- Exercise can lower blood glucose, improve circulation, and support weight control.
- Clients should check blood glucose as recommended and carry a fast sugar source if they are at risk for hypoglycemia.
- Encourage proper footwear and foot checks before and after activity.

### Identify priority assessments when caring for a client with diabetes

- Blood glucose values and trends
- Signs of hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia
- Food intake and ability to eat
- Hydration status and intake/output
- Weight changes
- Skin condition and wound healing
- Foot condition, sensation, and circulation
- Vision changes
- Medication use and adherence
- Presence of infection or illness
- Mental status changes

#### Identify normal fasting plasma glucose levels

- Normal fasting plasma glucose is **70 to 99 mg/dL**.
- A fasting value of **100 to 125 mg/dL** suggests prediabetes.
- A fasting value of **126 mg/dL or higher** on repeat testing supports diabetes diagnosis.

#### Identify recommended hemoglobin A1c levels

- For many nonpregnant adults with diabetes, a common goal is **less than 7%**.
- Targets can be individualized based on age, other illnesses, hypoglycemia risk, and overall condition.

#### Explain the significance of hemoglobin A1c results

- Hemoglobin A1c shows the average blood glucose over about the last **2 to 3 months**.
- A higher A1c means blood glucose has been running high over time.
- It helps show how well the full diabetes plan is working, not just what happened today.

### Acute complications of diabetes

#### Hypoglycemia

(Hypoglycemia means blood sugar is too low, usually below 70 mg/dL.)

##### Explain why this occurs

- Too much insulin or diabetes medicine
- Skipping or delaying meals
- Eating less than usual
- More exercise than usual without enough food
- Alcohol use, especially without food

##### Identify signs and symptoms of hypoglycemia

- Shaking
- Sweating
- Hunger
- Tachycardia
- Headache
- Irritability
- Dizziness
- Weakness
- Confusion
- Blurred vision
- Slurred speech
- Seizures or loss of consciousness if severe

##### Prioritize interventions for hypoglycemia

- Check the blood glucose if possible.
- If the client is awake and can swallow safely, give a fast-acting carbohydrate right away.
- Recheck the blood glucose after treatment.
- Repeat treatment if the blood glucose is still low.
- Follow with a snack or meal if the next meal is not soon.
- If the client is unconscious, cannot swallow, or is NPO, give glucagon or intravenous (IV) dextrose as ordered and protect the airway.

###### Explain the rationale for administering simple carbohydrates for hypoglycemia

- Simple carbohydrates are absorbed quickly.
- They raise blood glucose faster than foods with a lot of fat or protein.
- Fast treatment matters because the brain depends on glucose.

###### Explain the rule of 15

- Give **15 grams** of a fast-acting carbohydrate.
- Wait **15 minutes**.
- Recheck the blood glucose.
- If it is still low, repeat the treatment.
- When the glucose improves, give a snack or meal if needed to keep it from dropping again.

#### Hyperglycemia

(Hyperglycemia means blood sugar is too high.)

##### Explain why this occurs

- Too little insulin or missed diabetes medicine
- Illness or infection
- Stress
- Eating more carbohydrates than planned
- Steroid use
- Inactivity

##### Review medical management (medications) for hyperglycemia

- Give insulin or other ordered diabetes medications.
- Recheck blood glucose to see if treatment is working.
- Encourage fluids if allowed and if the client can drink.
- Treat the cause, such as infection or missed medication.
- Watch for worsening hyperglycemia, ketones, dehydration, or signs of DKA or HHS.

##### Explain nutrition education regarding carbohydrate counting and glycemic index

- **Carbohydrate counting** helps the client match food intake with medication and activity.
- Carbohydrates raise blood glucose the most, so portion awareness matters.
- The **glycemic index** describes how quickly a food raises blood glucose.
- Lower glycemic index foods often raise blood sugar more slowly.
- Clients still need balanced meals, not just a focus on one number or one food.

##### Discuss illness management and client education

- Illness usually raises blood glucose, even if the client is eating less.
- Clients should usually keep taking insulin and should not stop it without instructions.
- Check blood glucose more often during illness.
- Drink fluids if allowed.
- For clients at risk for DKA, check ketones as instructed.
- Call the provider for persistent vomiting, very high glucose, ketones, confusion, or trouble breathing.

###### Explain how stress affects blood sugar

- Stress hormones such as cortisol and epinephrine raise blood glucose.
- This is why illness, pain, and emotional stress can cause hyperglycemia.

##### Identify signs and symptoms of hyperglycemia

- Polyuria
- Polydipsia
- Polyphagia
- Warm dry skin
- Fatigue
- Blurred vision
- Headache
- Dehydration
- Nausea in more severe cases
- Confusion in more serious cases

###### Discuss the significance of ketonuria for a client with diabetes

- Ketonuria means ketones are present in the urine.
- It suggests the body is breaking down fat for energy because insulin is not working well enough.
- In a client with diabetes, especially Type 1, ketonuria is a warning sign for DKA.

#### Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)

(DKA is a life-threatening diabetes emergency with high blood sugar, ketones, and metabolic acidosis.)

##### Identify the type of diabetic client most at risk for developing DKA

- Clients with **Type 1 diabetes** are most at risk.
- It can also happen in some clients with Type 2 diabetes during major stress or severe illness.

##### Explain why this occurs

- Severe insulin lack means glucose cannot enter the cells well.
- The body starts breaking down fat for fuel.
- Fat breakdown produces ketones, which are acids.
- This leads to acidosis and severe dehydration.

##### Discuss the significance of ketonuria for a client with diabetes

- Ketonuria is a warning that dangerous ketone buildup may be happening.
- In a sick client with diabetes, ketonuria means the nurse should think about DKA and act quickly.

##### Ketoacidosis: Identify priority signs and symptoms

- Hyperglycemia
- Ketonuria or ketonemia
- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal pain
- Dehydration
- Tachycardia
- Hypotension
- Fruity breath odor
- Kussmaul respirations
- Weakness
- Confusion or decreased level of consciousness

##### Explain how DKA leads to metabolic acidosis

- DKA creates acidic ketone bodies in the blood.
- These acids lower the blood pH and lower bicarbonate.
- On an arterial blood gas (ABG), this shows **metabolic acidosis**.

##### Explain how DKA can lead to fluid volume deficit

- Very high glucose pulls water into the urine.
- This causes **osmotic diuresis**, which means the body loses large amounts of water and electrolytes.
- Vomiting can make the fluid loss worse.

##### Explain why fluid replacement is a priority intervention

- The client is often severely dehydrated.
- Fluids help improve blood pressure, kidney perfusion, circulation, and mental status.
- Fluids are started before or along with insulin because shock and dehydration are immediate dangers.

##### Identify the type of IV fluid used for fluid resuscitation

- Initial fluid resuscitation usually starts with an **isotonic crystalloid**, most often **0.9% sodium chloride (normal saline)**.
- Later fluids may change based on sodium level, glucose level, and the client's response.

##### Explain how ketoacidosis can contribute to hypokalemia

- Even if the first blood potassium looks normal or high, total body potassium is usually low in DKA.
- The body loses potassium in the urine during osmotic diuresis.
- When insulin treatment starts, potassium moves back into the cells.
- This can make the blood potassium drop quickly and cause **hypokalemia**.

##### Review the impact of abnormal potassium levels on the heart

- Low potassium can cause dangerous dysrhythmias, weak contractions, and electrocardiogram (ECG) changes.
- High potassium can also cause life-threatening dysrhythmias and cardiac arrest.
- Potassium problems are a major safety issue in DKA and HHS treatment.

##### Review signs and symptoms of abnormal potassium levels

- **Hypokalemia:** weakness, muscle cramps, constipation, irregular heart rhythm, flattened T waves
- **Hyperkalemia:** weakness, paresthesia, diarrhea, irregular heart rhythm, peaked T waves

##### Review ABG interpretation

- In DKA, the ABG usually shows **low pH** and **low bicarbonate (HCO3)**.
- This pattern means **metabolic acidosis**.
- The client may breathe fast and deep to try to lower carbon dioxide (CO2) as compensation.

##### Identify the type of insulin used in DKA

- **Regular insulin** is the insulin commonly used in DKA.
- It is usually given by IV infusion in the acute treatment phase.

#### Hyperglycemic hyperosmolar syndrome (HHS)

(HHS is a life-threatening diabetes emergency with very high blood sugar and severe dehydration, but little or no ketoacidosis.)

##### Identify the type of diabetic client most at risk for developing HHS

- Clients with **Type 2 diabetes** are most at risk.
- Older adults are especially at risk.

##### Explain why this occurs

- The client has enough insulin to prevent large ketone production, but not enough to control glucose.
- Blood glucose rises very high.
- Severe osmotic diuresis causes major water loss and dehydration.
- Infection, illness, some medications, or poor access to fluids can trigger HHS.

##### Explain how HHS differs from DKA

- HHS usually has **little or no ketone buildup**.
- HHS usually has **little or no significant acidosis**.
- HHS often has **much higher blood glucose** than DKA.
- HHS usually develops more slowly.
- HHS often causes stronger neurologic changes because dehydration and serum osmolality are severe.

##### Identify the type of client most at risk for developing HHS

- Older adults with Type 2 diabetes
- Clients who are dehydrated or have limited access to water
- Clients with infection, stroke, myocardial infarction, or other major illness

##### Identify signs and symptoms of HHS

- Extreme hyperglycemia
- Severe dehydration
- Dry mucous membranes
- Tachycardia
- Hypotension
- Weakness
- Confusion
- Seizures or coma in severe cases
- Little or no ketone odor or Kussmaul breathing

##### Identify collaborative treatment for HHS (see DKA)

- Rapid IV fluid replacement
- Regular insulin as ordered
- Frequent electrolyte monitoring and replacement
- Cardiac monitoring
- Treat the cause, such as infection
- Close monitoring of neurologic status, urine output, and vital signs

### Chronic complications of diabetes

#### Depressed immune response

- High glucose weakens the body's ability to fight infection.
- Wounds heal more slowly.
- Clients are at higher risk for skin infections, urinary tract infections, and foot ulcers.

#### Vascular effects

- Long-term hyperglycemia damages blood vessels.
- This causes both **macrovascular** and **microvascular** complications.

##### Macrovascular

###### Coronary artery disease (CAD)

- Diabetes speeds up atherosclerosis.
- This raises the risk for chest pain, myocardial infarction, and heart failure.

###### Stroke (cerebrovascular accident, CVA)

- Diabetes raises the risk for stroke because blood vessels are damaged over time.
- Uncontrolled blood pressure and lipids increase this risk more.

###### Peripheral arterial disease (PAD)

- Poor blood flow to the legs and feet increases the risk for pain, poor healing, ulcers, and amputation.

##### Microvascular

###### Retinopathy (eyes)

- Damaged small blood vessels in the retina can cause blurred vision and vision loss.

###### Nephropathy (kidneys)

- Kidney damage can lead to protein in the urine, declining kidney function, and chronic kidney disease.

###### Neuropathy (nerves)

- Nerve damage can cause numbness, burning, pain, weakness, and loss of protective sensation, especially in the feet.

### Identify priority safety actions when caring for a client with diabetic neuropathy

- Inspect the feet daily.
- Do not let the client walk barefoot.
- Check water temperature carefully before bathing.
- Avoid heating pads on numb areas.
- Use properly fitting shoes and clean socks.
- Protect the client from falls if balance or sensation is poor.
- Report new sores, redness, swelling, or drainage right away.

### Identify priority safety actions when caring for a client with diabetic retinopathy

- Keep pathways clear and uncluttered.
- Use good lighting and reduce glare.
- Put frequently used items in consistent places.
- Assist with ambulation if vision is poor.
- Encourage regular eye exams and good glucose control.
- Teach the client not to drive or do unsafe tasks if vision is significantly reduced.

### Differentiate between Somogyi effect and Dawn phenomena

- **Somogyi effect:** morning hyperglycemia caused by rebound hormones after overnight hypoglycemia
- **Dawn phenomenon:** morning hyperglycemia caused by early-morning hormone release without overnight hypoglycemia

### Identify priority actions

- Check blood glucose around **2 a.m. to 3 a.m.** if ordered or instructed.
- If the overnight glucose is low, think about **Somogyi effect**.
- If the overnight glucose is normal or high, think about **Dawn phenomenon**.
- Report patterns to the provider because insulin timing, evening snack, or medication dosing may need to change.

### Review appropriate foot care for the diabetic client

- Inspect feet every day.
- Wash feet with lukewarm water and dry well, especially between the toes.
- Do not soak feet for long periods.
- Use lotion on dry skin, but not between the toes.
- Cut nails carefully or get help if vision is poor or nails are thick.
- Wear clean socks and well-fitting shoes.
- Check the inside of shoes before putting them on.
- Do not walk barefoot.
- Report blisters, cuts, redness, swelling, or drainage right away.

## Sensory Alterations

(This section is about how changes in seeing, hearing, touching, smelling, and other senses affect safety and wellness.)

### Discuss the relationship of sensory function to a client's level of wellness

- Sensory function helps a person stay oriented, safe, independent, and connected with other people.
- Good sensory function supports mobility, communication, eating, sleep, and daily activities.
- When sensory function is altered, the client may have more risk for injury, isolation, anxiety, or confusion.

### Identify factors that contribute to sensory deprivation

- Vision or hearing loss
- Isolation
- Hospitalization in a quiet or unfamiliar room
- Limited activity or immobility
- Lack of meaningful social contact
- Neurologic problems that reduce sensory input

#### Identify signs and symptoms of sensory deprivation

- Boredom
- Apathy
- Difficulty concentrating
- Confusion
- Anxiety
- Restlessness
- Disorientation
- Hallucinations in more severe cases

#### Identify priority interventions

- Reorient the client often to person, place, and time.
- Provide clocks, calendars, glasses, hearing aids, and familiar objects.
- Encourage family visits and conversation if appropriate.
- Offer music, reading, television, or simple activities the client enjoys.
- Increase meaningful stimulation, not random noise or chaos.

### Identify factors that contribute to sensory overload

- Bright lights
- Loud noise
- Alarms
- Strong odors
- Pain
- Frequent interruptions
- Many staff or visitors talking at once
- Sleep deprivation
- Anxiety and unfamiliar surroundings

#### Identify signs of sensory overload

- Irritability
- Agitation
- Anxiety
- Restlessness
- Fatigue
- Headache
- Difficulty concentrating
- Trouble sleeping
- Wanting to withdraw or block out stimulation

#### Identify priority interventions

- Reduce noise, light, and unnecessary interruptions.
- Cluster care to allow rest.
- Speak calmly and one person at a time.
- Explain procedures before doing them.
- Limit visitors if overstimulation is a problem.
- Encourage rest and a predictable routine.

### Describe potential safety hazards in the home of a client with:

#### Impaired tactile sensation

- Burns from hot water or heating pads
- Cuts or injuries not felt right away
- Pressure injuries from shoes or prolonged pressure
- Frostbite or heat injury because the client cannot feel temperature well

#### Impaired olfactory sensation

- Not noticing smoke or fire
- Not noticing a gas leak
- Eating spoiled food because the smell warning is missing
- Missing dangerous chemical odors

#### Impaired vision

- Falls from clutter, cords, loose rugs, or poor lighting
- Trouble reading medication labels
- Trouble seeing stairs, curbs, or objects on the floor
- Trouble using appliances safely

### Identify basic safety measures for a client with impaired tactile sensation, olfactory sensation, and impaired vision

#### Impaired tactile sensation

- Check bath water with a thermometer or an area of skin with normal sensation.
- Avoid heating pads on numb areas.
- Inspect skin and feet every day.
- Wear protective shoes and gloves when needed.

#### Impaired olfactory sensation

- Install and test smoke and gas detectors.
- Check food dates and storage carefully.
- Ask for help checking for gas leaks or spoiled food if needed.
- Use good kitchen safety habits and ventilation.

#### Impaired vision

- Keep pathways clear.
- Use bright, even lighting and night-lights.
- Keep frequently used items in the same place.
- Mark important items with large print or high contrast.
- Use handrails and nonskid surfaces.
- Remove loose rugs and clutter.

## Endocrine

(This section covers major hormone problems from the thyroid, adrenal glands, pituitary area, and pancreas.)

### Review functions of hormones secreted by the pituitary, thyroid, parathyroid, adrenal glands, and pancreas

#### Pituitary gland

- **Thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH):** tells the thyroid to make thyroid hormones
- **Adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH):** tells the adrenal cortex to release cortisol
- **Growth hormone (GH):** supports growth and metabolism
- **Antidiuretic hormone (ADH):** helps the kidneys keep water in the body
- **Oxytocin:** helps with labor and milk letdown
- **Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) and luteinizing hormone (LH):** help control reproduction
- **Prolactin:** supports milk production

#### Thyroid gland

- **Triiodothyronine (T3) and thyroxine (T4):** increase metabolism, heat production, oxygen use, and growth
- **Calcitonin:** helps lower blood calcium

#### Parathyroid glands

- **Parathyroid hormone (PTH):** raises blood calcium and affects phosphate balance

#### Adrenal glands

- **Cortisol:** supports stress response, raises blood glucose, and affects metabolism
- **Aldosterone:** helps the body keep sodium and water and get rid of potassium
- **Adrenal androgens:** contribute to sexual development
- **Epinephrine and norepinephrine:** support the fight-or-flight response

#### Pancreas

- **Insulin:** lowers blood glucose
- **Glucagon:** raises blood glucose

### Identify common diagnostic thyroid tests

- Thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH)
- Free T4
- T3
- Thyroid antibodies when autoimmune disease is suspected
- Thyroid ultrasound
- Radioactive iodine uptake or scan in some cases

### Differentiate between hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism

- **Hypothyroidism:** too little thyroid hormone; the body slows down
- **Hyperthyroidism:** too much thyroid hormone; the body speeds up
- **Hypothyroidism:** fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, dry skin, constipation, bradycardia
- **Hyperthyroidism:** weight loss, heat intolerance, sweating, tremor, anxiety, tachycardia, diarrhea

### Hyperthyroidism: Graves disease

(Graves disease is an autoimmune form of hyperthyroidism.)

#### Explain the pathophysiology

- Antibodies stimulate the thyroid gland too much.
- The gland makes too much T3 and T4.
- Metabolism speeds up.
- The thyroid may enlarge and the eyes may be affected.

#### Discuss contributing factors

- Autoimmune disease
- Family history
- Female sex
- Smoking
- Stress may worsen symptoms in some clients

#### Explain the clinical manifestations

- Weight loss despite appetite
- Heat intolerance
- Sweating
- Tachycardia
- Palpitations
- Tremor
- Anxiety or irritability
- Diarrhea
- Warm moist skin
- Goiter
- Exophthalmos in Graves disease

#### Acute complication

##### Explain thyrotoxic crisis/thyroid storm

(Thyroid storm is a life-threatening sudden increase in thyroid hormone effect.)

- Thyroid storm is an emergency caused by extreme hyperthyroidism.
- It may be triggered by infection, trauma, surgery, childbirth, stroke, or stopping antithyroid medicine suddenly.
- Findings may include fever, severe tachycardia, hypertension early, agitation, delirium, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and possible heart failure.

##### Discuss priority care for thyrotoxic crisis

- Maintain airway, breathing, and circulation.
- Give oxygen as needed.
- Start IV fluids as ordered.
- Reduce fever with cooling measures.
- Monitor cardiac rhythm and vital signs closely.
- Give antithyroid medication, iodine solution, and beta blockers as ordered.
- Treat the cause, such as infection.

#### Discuss priority treatments

- Antithyroid medications
- Iodine preparations for short-term control
- Beta blockers for symptom control
- Radioactive iodine in some clients
- Thyroidectomy in selected cases
- Supportive care during thyroid storm

#### Explain the use of propylthiouracil (PTU), methimazole, and Lugol's solution

- **PTU** and **methimazole** reduce thyroid hormone production.
- **PTU** also reduces conversion of T4 to the more active T3.
- **Lugol's solution** is an iodine preparation used short term to reduce thyroid hormone release and lower thyroid gland vascularity.

##### Explain why this may be given preoperatively to a client with Graves disease

- These drugs help bring the client closer to a normal thyroid state before surgery.
- Iodine solutions may also reduce bleeding risk by making the gland less vascular.
- Preoperative control lowers the risk of thyroid storm during surgery.

##### Identify the expected therapeutic effect

- Lower heart rate
- Less tremor
- Less anxiety
- Lower heat intolerance and sweating
- Improved lab values toward normal

#### Discuss use of beta blockers for hyperthyroidism

- Beta blockers do not fix the thyroid problem itself.
- They control adrenergic symptoms such as tachycardia, palpitations, tremor, and anxiety.

##### Identify priority nursing implications for administering beta blockers

- Check heart rate and blood pressure first.
- Watch for bradycardia or hypotension.
- Use caution in clients with asthma or some heart conduction problems.
- Teach the client not to stop the drug suddenly unless told.

##### Identify the expected therapeutic effect

- Slower heart rate
- Less tremor
- Less palpitations
- Less anxiety related to overactivation of the sympathetic system

#### Exophthalmos

(Exophthalmos means the eyes bulge forward.)

##### Identify nursing actions

- Protect the eyes from drying and injury.
- Use artificial tears or eye lubricant as ordered.
- Raise the head of the bed.
- Encourage sunglasses outside if light bothers the client.
- Tape lids closed at night if the client cannot close the eyes fully.
- Report vision changes, pain, or severe redness.

#### Discuss use of super-saturated potassium iodide (SSKI)

- SSKI is another iodine preparation used short term.
- It decreases thyroid hormone release.
- It may be used before thyroid surgery or during severe hyperthyroid states.

#### Explain how hyperthyroidism can lead to hypothyroidism

- Treatment can reduce thyroid tissue or thyroid hormone production too much.
- Radioactive iodine, surgery, or strong antithyroid treatment can leave the client hypothyroid.

### Thyroidectomy: identify client care, possible adverse outcomes, and priority nursing interventions

- Keep the client in semi-Fowler position if ordered.
- Support the head and neck during movement.
- Monitor airway, breathing, and neck swelling closely.
- Check the dressing and behind the neck for bleeding.
- Monitor voice quality.
- Keep oxygen, suction, and tracheostomy supplies nearby if policy requires.
- Watch for hypocalcemia after surgery.

#### Explain why frequent assessment of the airway is necessary after this procedure

- Swelling or bleeding in the neck can compress the airway.
- Airway obstruction can happen quickly and become life threatening.

#### Explain why a client is at risk for hypocalcemia following a thyroidectomy

- The parathyroid glands can be injured, removed, or have reduced blood supply during surgery.
- Low parathyroid hormone causes the calcium level to drop.

#### Review what positive Chvostek and Trousseau signs indicate

- Positive **Chvostek sign** and **Trousseau sign** suggest **hypocalcemia**.
- They show increased nerve and muscle irritability, also called tetany.

#### Identify priority interventions for these signs

- Monitor the airway.
- Report the signs right away.
- Check calcium levels.
- Seizure precautions may be needed.
- Prepare to give calcium as ordered.

#### Explain why calcium gluconate may be considered a priority medication

- Calcium gluconate treats symptomatic hypocalcemia.
- It can reduce dangerous muscle spasms, tetany, and airway risk.

### Hypothyroidism

#### Explain the pathophysiology

- Hypothyroidism happens when the body does not have enough thyroid hormone.
- Metabolism slows down.
- Body systems work more slowly.

#### Identify common clinical manifestations

- Fatigue
- Weight gain
- Cold intolerance
- Dry skin
- Constipation
- Bradycardia
- Slow thinking
- Puffy face
- Depression
- Dry or thinning hair

#### Discuss contributing factors

- Hashimoto thyroiditis
- Thyroidectomy
- Radioactive iodine treatment
- Low iodine intake
- Pituitary problems
- Certain medications

#### Acute complication

##### Explain myxedema coma

(Myxedema coma is a life-threatening form of severe hypothyroidism.)

- Myxedema coma is severe untreated hypothyroidism with decompensation.
- Findings can include hypothermia, bradycardia, hypotension, hypoventilation, and altered mental status.
- This is a medical emergency.

##### Identify priority nursing actions for myxedema coma

- Maintain airway and support breathing.
- Monitor cardiac rhythm and blood pressure.
- Warm the client gently.
- Give IV thyroid hormone as ordered.
- Monitor neurologic status, glucose, and electrolytes.
- Treat the cause and provide ICU-level care if needed.

### Levothyroxine

#### Explain why levothyroxine is prescribed for hypothyroidism

- Levothyroxine replaces missing thyroid hormone.
- It helps return metabolism toward normal.

#### Nursing considerations

##### Identify priority actions when administering levothyroxine

- Give it at the same time each day.
- It is often taken in the morning on an empty stomach.
- Do not switch brands or stop it without guidance.
- Use caution and monitor closely in older adults or clients with heart disease.

##### Identify priority labs to check

- TSH
- Free T4

##### Identify assessments to make

- Heart rate and blood pressure
- Weight
- Energy level
- Bowel pattern
- Skin changes
- Temperature tolerance
- Signs of overreplacement such as palpitations, chest pain, or insomnia

##### Identify essential patient teaching

- Take the medication every day.
- It may take a few weeks to feel the full effect.
- Do not stop the medicine just because you feel better.
- Report chest pain, palpitations, or major changes in symptoms.
- Take calcium, iron, and antacids several hours apart from levothyroxine if instructed.

### Cushing syndrome

(Cushing syndrome means the body has too much cortisol for too long.)

#### Explain the pathophysiology

- Too much cortisol raises blood glucose, changes fat distribution, weakens protein tissue, and suppresses the immune response.
- This causes poor healing, muscle wasting, skin changes, and fluid problems.

#### Identify clinical manifestations

- Moon face
- Buffalo hump
- Central obesity
- Thin arms and legs
- Purple striae
- Easy bruising
- Hyperglycemia
- Hypertension
- Muscle weakness
- Delayed wound healing
- Frequent infection
- Mood changes
- Osteoporosis

#### Differentiate between exogenous and endogenous causes of Cushing syndrome

- **Exogenous:** caused by long-term corticosteroid use
- **Endogenous:** caused by the body making too much cortisol, such as from pituitary or adrenal tumors

#### Identify treatment and nursing interventions

- Treat the cause.
- If steroids caused the problem, taper them slowly as ordered.
- Monitor blood glucose, blood pressure, weight, and infection.
- Protect skin and prevent falls.
- Encourage rest and stress reduction.
- Support body image concerns.

#### Identify dietary management strategies

- High-protein diet to support healing
- Low-sodium diet to reduce fluid retention
- Adequate calcium and vitamin D
- Potassium-rich foods if potassium is low
- Control concentrated sugars if blood glucose is high

#### Discuss post-op care of a client undergoing an adrenalectomy

- Monitor vital signs closely.
- Watch for signs of bleeding and infection.
- Monitor intake/output and fluid balance.
- Watch for hypotension and signs of adrenal insufficiency.
- Give steroid replacement as ordered.
- Manage pain and encourage safe activity.

#### Explain why a client who has undergone an adrenalectomy is at risk for hypotension

- The body may suddenly have much less cortisol and aldosterone.
- Less aldosterone means the body loses sodium and water.
- This can lower blood pressure.

#### Review hormones produced by the adrenal glands

- Cortisol
- Aldosterone
- Adrenal androgens
- Epinephrine
- Norepinephrine

#### Explain why corticosteroids will be prescribed to a patient with Cushing syndrome after an adrenalectomy

- The removed gland can no longer make enough cortisol.
- The body needs steroid replacement until the remaining system can meet the body's needs, or lifelong if both glands are removed.

#### Nursing considerations

##### Identify priority actions when administering corticosteroids

- Give with food if appropriate.
- Do not stop suddenly.
- Watch for infection.
- Check blood glucose.
- Watch blood pressure and fluid status.

##### Identify priority labs to monitor

- Blood glucose
- Sodium
- Potassium
- Cortisol when ordered

##### Identify assessments to make

- Weight and edema
- Blood pressure
- Skin integrity
- Infection signs
- Mood changes
- Muscle weakness
- Wound healing

##### Review client education

- Take the drug exactly as ordered.
- Do not stop suddenly.
- Report infection, black stools, high blood sugar symptoms, or swelling.
- Follow diet and follow-up instructions.
- Carry medical alert information if long-term steroid use applies.

### Addison's disease

(Addison's disease is primary adrenal insufficiency. The body does not make enough cortisol and usually not enough aldosterone.)

#### Explain the pathophysiology

- The adrenal cortex is damaged and cannot make enough hormones.
- Low cortisol causes weak stress response and low blood glucose risk.
- Low aldosterone causes sodium loss, water loss, dehydration, and high potassium.

#### Identify contributing factors

- Autoimmune destruction
- Infection such as tuberculosis
- Adrenal injury or surgery
- Cancer involving the adrenal gland
- Certain medications

#### Identify clinical manifestations

- Fatigue
- Weakness
- Weight loss
- Poor appetite
- Hypotension
- Dizziness
- Abdominal pain
- Nausea and vomiting
- Hyperpigmentation
- Salt craving
- Hyponatremia
- Hyperkalemia
- Hypoglycemia

#### Discuss treatment and nursing interventions

- Give hormone replacement as ordered.
- Monitor vital signs, sodium, potassium, and glucose.
- Encourage fluid and sodium replacement as ordered.
- Watch for crisis during illness or stress.
- Teach lifelong treatment and follow-up.

#### Steroid replacement

##### Review client education

- Take steroid replacement exactly as ordered.
- Do not stop the medicine suddenly.
- Learn sick-day rules and when to increase steroids based on provider instructions.
- Carry a medical alert bracelet or card.
- Keep an emergency steroid plan if prescribed.

#### Fluid and sodium replacement

- Clients may need IV or oral fluids based on condition.
- Sodium replacement helps correct losses caused by low aldosterone.
- Watch blood pressure, daily weight, and signs of dehydration.

### Acute complication: Addisonian crisis

(Addisonian crisis is an emergency caused by severe sudden lack of adrenal hormones.)

#### Explain the pathophysiology

- The body suddenly cannot meet its cortisol and aldosterone needs.
- Shock, severe electrolyte imbalance, and hypoglycemia can happen quickly.

#### Identify contributing factors

- Infection
- Surgery
- Trauma
- Vomiting or diarrhea
- Severe stress
- Stopping steroid medicine suddenly
- Not taking prescribed replacement therapy

#### Identify clinical manifestations

- Severe hypotension
- Shock
- Weakness
- Confusion
- Abdominal pain
- Nausea and vomiting
- Dehydration
- Hyponatremia
- Hyperkalemia
- Hypoglycemia

#### Discuss treatment and nursing interventions

- Call for urgent help and treat it as an emergency.
- Start rapid IV fluids as ordered.
- Give IV corticosteroids as ordered.
- Monitor glucose, sodium, potassium, and cardiac rhythm.
- Treat the cause, such as infection.
- Watch neurologic status, urine output, and blood pressure closely.

#### Treat hypotension

- Give IV isotonic fluids as ordered.
- Monitor blood pressure and perfusion closely.

#### Treat hyperkalemia

- Cardiac monitoring is important because high potassium can cause lethal dysrhythmias.

##### Sodium polystyrene

- Sodium polystyrene helps remove potassium through the bowel.

##### Insulin

- Insulin moves potassium from the blood into the cells.
- Because insulin can lower blood glucose, the client may also need dextrose and close glucose monitoring.

#### Replace corticosteroids

- IV hydrocortisone or another ordered steroid is a key part of treatment.
- This replaces the missing stress hormone the body needs right away.

#### Treat hypoglycemia related to insulin

- Monitor blood glucose closely.
- Give dextrose as ordered if the blood glucose drops.
- Recheck glucose after treatment.

### Diabetes insipidus

(Diabetes insipidus, or DI, causes the body to lose too much water because antidiuretic hormone (ADH) is missing or not working.)

#### Explain the pathophysiology and relationship to ADH

- ADH normally tells the kidneys to keep water in the body.
- In DI, the body does not have enough ADH or the kidneys do not respond to it.
- The client makes very large amounts of dilute urine and loses free water.

#### Identify contributing factors

- Pituitary surgery
- Head injury
- Brain tumors
- Central nervous system disease
- Kidney resistance to ADH
- Certain medications

#### Identify clinical manifestations

- Polyuria
- Polydipsia
- Nocturia
- Very dilute urine
- Dry mucous membranes
- Dehydration
- Weakness
- Hypotension if severe
- Tachycardia if volume loss is significant

#### Identify the impact of DI on fluid and electrolyte balance

- DI causes large water losses.
- The client can develop **hypovolemia**.
- Sodium can rise, causing **hypernatremia**.

#### Explain how this disorder may impact a client's sleep

- Frequent urination and thirst often wake the client up at night.
- Sleep can become poor because of repeated trips to the bathroom and dehydration symptoms.

##### Review signs and symptoms of hypernatremia

- Intense thirst
- Dry mouth
- Restlessness
- Irritability
- Confusion
- Muscle twitching
- Seizures if severe

##### Identify a serious complication of hypernatremia

- Seizures, coma, or serious neurologic injury

##### Identify signs and symptoms of hypovolemia

- Hypotension
- Tachycardia
- Weak pulse
- Dry mucous membranes
- Poor skin turgor
- Dizziness
- Low central perfusion

#### Discuss treatment and nursing interventions

- Monitor intake/output very closely.
- Check daily weights.
- Monitor sodium and neurologic status.
- Replace fluids as ordered.
- Give ADH replacement if prescribed for central DI.
- Watch for dehydration and shock.

#### Desmopressin acetate: identify the action and expected therapeutic effect

- Desmopressin is a synthetic form of ADH.
- It helps the kidneys keep water in the body.
- Expected effects include less urine output, more concentrated urine, and better fluid balance.

#### Explain why the client is at risk for fluid volume excess with desmopressin

- If the client keeps too much water after treatment, fluid volume excess and hyponatremia can happen.
- This is why weight, urine output, sodium, and neurologic changes must be watched.

#### Identify a commonly used IV fluid for fluid and electrolyte replacement

- A **hypotonic fluid**, such as **0.45% sodium chloride**, may be used as ordered once the client's circulation is stable.
- If the client is in shock or very unstable, an isotonic fluid may be needed first.

### SIADH

(SIADH means the body is holding too much water because there is too much ADH.)

#### Explain the pathophysiology and relationship to ADH

- Too much ADH makes the kidneys keep water.
- The body holds extra water, which dilutes the sodium level.
- The urine becomes more concentrated and the blood becomes more diluted.

#### Identify contributing factors

- Pituitary or brain disorders
- Lung disease
- Certain cancers
- Some medications
- Head injury
- Central nervous system infection
- Postoperative pituitary problems

#### Identify clinical manifestations

- Low urine output
- Weight gain
- Headache
- Nausea
- Muscle cramps
- Confusion
- Lethargy
- Seizures if hyponatremia becomes severe

##### Review signs and symptoms of hyponatremia

- Nausea
- Headache
- Confusion
- Weakness
- Muscle cramps
- Decreased level of consciousness
- Seizures in severe cases

##### Identify a serious complication of hyponatremia

- Seizures, coma, or brain swelling

#### Identify the impact of SIADH on fluid and electrolyte balance

- SIADH causes **fluid volume excess** and **dilutional hyponatremia**.
- The client keeps water, but sodium becomes too low.

#### Discuss treatment and nursing interventions for SIADH

- Restrict fluids as ordered.
- Monitor intake/output and daily weights.
- Monitor sodium and neurologic status.
- Give hypertonic saline in severe cases if ordered.
- Give loop diuretics or demeclocycline when ordered.
- Use seizure precautions if sodium is very low.
- Treat the cause.

##### Explain why fluid restriction may be included in the treatment plan

- If the client drinks less, there is less extra water to dilute the sodium.
- This helps correct hyponatremia.

##### When SIADH is chronic, what are appropriate interventions to teach clients to help fluid restriction and thirst?

- Use small measured cups.
- Spread fluids through the day.
- Suck on ice chips if allowed.
- Chew sugar-free gum or suck on hard candy if allowed.
- Do frequent mouth care.
- Track fluid intake closely.

#### Explain why demeclocycline is used

- Demeclocycline reduces the kidney's response to ADH.
- This helps the body get rid of more water.

#### Identify the expected therapeutic effect

- More dilute urine output
- Less water retention
- Improved sodium level

#### Identify loop diuretics used in SIADH

- **Furosemide** is a common loop diuretic used.

#### Identify the expected therapeutic effect

- More water excretion
- Less fluid overload
- Better sodium concentration when used appropriately

#### Review risks associated with diuretics

- Dehydration
- Hypotension
- Hypokalemia
- Other electrolyte imbalances
- Kidney function changes

### Describe the transsphenoidal surgical approach and why it is used

(The transsphenoidal approach reaches the pituitary gland through the nose and sphenoid sinus.)

- This approach is often used to remove pituitary tumors.
- It avoids opening the skull and gives direct access to the pituitary area.

### Explain post-op care and client education

- Monitor neurologic status, intake/output, and sodium.
- Watch for clear nasal drainage that may mean cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) leak.
- Keep the head of the bed elevated as ordered.
- Tell the client to avoid coughing, nose blowing, bending forward, heavy lifting, and straining.
- If the client must sneeze, teach the client to sneeze with the mouth open.
- Use mouth care carefully.
- Teach the client to report severe headache, vision changes, excessive urine output, or confusion.

### Identify common complications and their associated signs and symptoms

- **CSF leak:** clear drainage from the nose, headache, infection risk
- **Meningitis:** fever, stiff neck, worsening headache, change in mental status
- **Diabetes insipidus:** very high urine output, thirst, hypernatremia
- **SIADH:** low urine output, confusion, hyponatremia
- **Visual changes:** blurred vision or new vision loss
- **Hormone imbalance:** weakness, fatigue, fluid or sodium changes

## Loss and Grief: End of Life care

(This section is about grieving, comfort-focused care, and respectful nursing care at the end of life.)

### Review characteristics of normal loss and grieving

- Grief is a normal response to loss.
- It can include sadness, anger, guilt, fear, sleep problems, poor appetite, crying, or trouble concentrating.
- Grief does not look the same in every person.
- A person may move back and forth between emotions instead of following one straight path.

### Review different models of grieving

- Grief models help nurses understand common patterns, but they are not exact rules.
- Not every person grieves in the same order or in the same way.

#### Kubler-Ross

- Denial
- Anger
- Bargaining
- Depression
- Acceptance
- These stages are not always linear and not everyone experiences all of them.

### Review major functional manifestations during grief

- **Physical:** fatigue, palpitations, muscle tension, appetite changes, sleep changes
- **Emotional:** sadness, anger, guilt, anxiety, loneliness
- **Cognitive:** poor concentration, disbelief, confusion, preoccupation with the loss
- **Social:** withdrawal, role changes, changed family interactions
- **Spiritual:** questioning beliefs, searching for meaning, renewed faith in some people

### Identify factors that affect grieving

- Relationship to the person or thing lost
- Age and developmental stage
- Culture and religion
- Type of loss
- Sudden versus expected death
- Previous losses
- Support system
- Mental health history
- Financial or social stress

### Differentiate between normal and abnormal grief

- **Normal grief:** intense but gradually becomes more manageable over time
- **Abnormal or complicated grief:** grief stays very intense, lasts a long time, or severely blocks daily function
- Warning signs include inability to function, persistent hopelessness, unsafe behavior, or thoughts of self-harm

### Differentiate between palliative care and hospice care

- **Palliative care:** focuses on comfort and quality of life at any stage of serious illness and can happen together with curative treatment
- **Hospice care:** focuses on comfort when the illness is no longer being treated for cure and life expectancy is limited

### Review physical signs of dying

- More sleeping and less responsiveness
- Less interest in food and fluids
- Trouble swallowing
- Decreased urine output
- Cool or mottled skin
- Irregular breathing or periods of apnea
- Changes in pulse and blood pressure
- Increased secretions or noisy breathing

### Identify appropriate methods for providing comfort in end-of-life care

#### Positioning

- Reposition gently for comfort.
- Use pillows to support the body and reduce pressure.
- Elevate the head of the bed if breathing is easier that way.

#### Oral care

- Provide frequent mouth care.
- Use moist swabs, lip moisturizer, and gentle cleaning.
- Oral care is often more important than pushing food or fluid in the final phase.

#### Nutrition

- Offer small amounts only if the client wants and can safely take them.
- Do not force food or fluids.
- Explain to family that loss of appetite is common near death.

#### Analgesics

- Give pain medicine as ordered and reassess comfort.
- Use both verbal and nonverbal signs of pain if the client cannot speak.
- Comfort is the priority.

### Review caring for the deceased

- Follow policy and legal steps.
- Provide privacy and allow family time if appropriate.
- Handle the body gently and respectfully.
- Follow cultural and religious practices when possible.
- Remove tubes and lines only if policy allows and there is no reason to keep them in place.
- Clean and position the body.
- Apply identification and prepare the body according to policy.

### Explore how your own feelings about death may influence your nursing care

- Self-awareness matters.
- Personal fear, sadness, or discomfort can change how a nurse communicates or avoids difficult moments.
- Nurses should reflect honestly, ask for support when needed, and keep the focus on the client's needs.

### Describe basic nursing interventions to provide appropriate care for a client with different cultural values

- Ask the client and family about preferences instead of guessing.
- Respect beliefs about touch, privacy, rituals, food, prayer, and who should be present.
- Use interpreters when needed.
- Support spiritual care or cultural practices when possible and safe.
- Avoid judging beliefs that differ from your own.

### Review therapeutic communication techniques

- Use presence and active listening.
- Use silence when the client or family needs time.
- Ask open-ended questions.
- Acknowledge feelings.
- Offer honest support without false reassurance.
- Do not rush the grieving person.
- Say simple truthful things such as, "I am here with you," or, "This is very hard."
