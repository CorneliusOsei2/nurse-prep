# Exam 1 Flashcards

These flashcards follow the Pharmacology Exam 1 guide and focus on simple wording, strong medication safety habits, and exam-style nursing judgment.

## How to use this deck

- **Front** = the question side.
- **Back** = the answer side.
- **Why it matters** = the short exam or bedside reminder.
- Each card has a **tag** so you know what skill it tests: (Recall), (Compare), (Safety), (Priority), (Application), (Patient Teaching), (Lab), (Hold Parameter), (Antidote), (Red Flag), or (Notify Provider).

## Pharmacologic Principles

### Card 1 (Application)

**Front:** A patient gets a new drug and feels a little dizzy. A different patient gets the same drug and breaks out in hives with facial swelling. Which reaction requires the fastest nursing action?

A. The dizziness, because it is a sign of drug toxicity requiring immediate intervention  
B. Both reactions require the same urgency since they are both adverse effects  
C. The hives with facial swelling, because it is an adverse effect requiring the nurse to stop the drug and get help  
D. Neither reaction is urgent because both are expected side effects of the medication  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**
- **Side effect:** A secondary effect that is expected and usually mild (like the dizziness).
- **Adverse effect:** An unwanted harmful effect (like a serious allergic reaction with hives and swelling).
- **Toxicity:** A dangerous level of the drug in the body — can damage organs.
- The allergic reaction (adverse effect) needs the fastest action — stop the drug and get help.

**Why it matters:** On the exam, you need to tell the difference between "expected and mild" and "harmful and urgent." If you see signs of allergy or toxicity, act fast.

### Card 2 (Compare)

**Front:** A nurse gives morphine (an opioid agonist) for pain. Later, the patient's breathing slows dangerously, and the nurse gives naloxone. Which statement best describes how an agonist and antagonist work?

A. An agonist activates a receptor to produce a response, while an antagonist blocks a receptor to stop a response  
B. An agonist blocks a receptor to prevent side effects, while an antagonist activates a receptor to reverse toxicity  
C. Both agonists and antagonists activate receptors, but antagonists produce a weaker response  
D. An agonist works only on pain receptors, while an antagonist works only on breathing receptors  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**
- **Agonist** = turns a receptor ON → creates a response (morphine activates opioid receptors → pain relief).
- **Antagonist** = blocks a receptor → stops a response (naloxone blocks opioid receptors → reverses the overdose).
- Think: agonist = "go," antagonist = "stop."

**Why it matters:** Many exam questions test whether you know a drug starts an effect or blocks one.

### Card 3 (Application)

**Front:** A patient needs pain relief fast. The provider orders IV morphine instead of oral morphine. Why does the IV form work the fastest?

A. IV drugs are formulated at a higher concentration than oral drugs  
B. IV drugs enter the bloodstream directly, skipping absorption and first-pass metabolism in the liver  
C. IV drugs are absorbed faster through the stomach lining than oral drugs  
D. IV drugs bind to proteins more quickly, which speeds up their effect  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**
- IV drugs go straight into the bloodstream — they skip absorption.
- Oral drugs must dissolve, pass through the stomach and intestines, and travel through the liver before reaching the blood (this is called "first-pass metabolism").
- That is why IV drugs have the fastest onset.

**Why it matters:** When the exam asks "which route works fastest," IV is almost always the answer. Sublingual is also fast because it skips the stomach and liver.

### Card 4 (Application)

**Front:** A patient who takes an oral heart medication starts having vomiting and diarrhea. The nurse notices the drug seems less effective. Which of the following best explains why?

A. The patient has developed tolerance to the medication and needs a higher dose  
B. The drug has reached toxic levels and is being rejected by the body  
C. The vomiting and diarrhea are adverse effects indicating the drug should be discontinued  
D. The vomiting and diarrhea are preventing the drug from staying in the GI tract long enough to be absorbed  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**
Things that can reduce drug absorption:
- Vomiting or diarrhea (the drug does not stay in the GI tract long enough)
- Food in the stomach (for some drugs)
- Changes in gastric pH
- Poor blood flow to tissues (for IM or SubQ injections)
- Crushing an extended-release tablet (this changes how the drug enters the body)

**Why it matters:** A correct drug order can still fail if something blocks absorption. Always think about what is happening in the patient's body.

### Card 5 (Compare)

**Front:** Why does nitroglycerin given under the tongue (sublingual) work faster than a pill swallowed whole?

A. Sublingual tablets contain a higher dose of the drug than oral tablets  
B. Sublingual drugs are absorbed faster because the stomach breaks them down more efficiently  
C. Sublingual drugs absorb through the tissues under the tongue directly into the blood, skipping the stomach and liver  
D. Sublingual drugs work faster because saliva activates the medication before it enters the body  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**
- Sublingual drugs absorb through the thin tissues under the tongue directly into the blood.
- They skip the stomach and the liver (no first-pass metabolism).
- Swallowed pills must dissolve, be absorbed in the gut, and pass through the liver first.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic compare question. Remember: sublingual = fast, because it skips the stomach and liver.

### Card 6 (Lab)

**Front:** A patient has low albumin (a protein in the blood). The nurse sees a highly protein-bound drug on the medication list. Why is this patient at higher risk for toxicity?

A. Low albumin means more of the drug is free and active in the blood, increasing the risk of a stronger effect and toxicity  
B. Low albumin causes the drug to be excreted too quickly by the kidneys, leading to a rebound toxic effect  
C. Low albumin prevents the drug from reaching its target receptor, so the provider increases the dose to a toxic level  
D. Low albumin causes the liver to metabolize the drug faster, producing toxic byproducts  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**
- Many drugs travel in the blood attached to albumin.
- Only the **free** (unattached) drug is active.
- Low albumin = more free drug floating around = stronger drug effect = higher chance of toxicity.

**Why it matters:** Older adults and very sick patients often have low albumin. Always think: "Could this drug be hitting harder than expected?"

### Card 7 (Application)

**Front:** A patient with liver failure is getting a medication that is processed (metabolized) by the liver. What should the nurse watch for?

A. The drug will be excreted unchanged by the kidneys, so no dose adjustment is needed  
B. The drug will be metabolized faster than normal, requiring higher doses to be effective  
C. The drug will bind to more albumin than usual, reducing its effect and requiring a higher dose  
D. The drug may build up to dangerous levels because the liver cannot break it down normally, increasing toxicity risk  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**
- The drug may be broken down more slowly → drug levels can build up.
- The risk of toxicity goes up.
- If the drug is a **prodrug** (a drug that needs the liver to activate it), it may not work well enough.
- The same dose can become unsafe when the liver is not working.

**Why it matters:** Watch for signs of drug buildup and toxicity in any patient with liver problems. The exam loves this scenario.

### Card 8 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** A patient on a statin (cholesterol drug) asks if it is okay to drink grapefruit juice every morning. What should you teach?

A. Grapefruit juice speeds up drug metabolism, making the statin less effective over time  
B. Grapefruit juice blocks liver enzymes that break down the drug, causing blood levels to rise and increasing toxicity risk  
C. Grapefruit juice interferes with drug absorption in the stomach, so the statin should be taken two hours apart  
D. Grapefruit juice has no significant effect on statins but should be avoided due to its high sugar content  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**
- Grapefruit blocks certain liver enzymes that break down some drugs.
- If the drug is not broken down normally, blood levels go up.
- Higher levels = more side effects and a risk of toxicity.
- Tell the patient: **avoid grapefruit with this medication** unless the provider says it is okay.

**Why it matters:** When you see grapefruit + medication on the exam, think "drug levels may go UP."

### Card 9 (Compare)

**Front:** A patient is taking Drug A. A new Drug B is added that inhibits the metabolism of Drug A. What effect should the nurse expect?

A. Drug A will be broken down faster, leading to lower blood levels and a weaker therapeutic effect  
B. Drug A will be excreted more quickly by the kidneys, requiring a higher dose to remain effective  
C. Drug A will be broken down more slowly, causing higher blood levels and an increased risk of toxicity  
D. Drug A will bind to fewer proteins, reducing its effect and requiring a dose increase  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**
- **Enhanced metabolism** → drug is broken down faster → lower levels → weaker effect.
- **Inhibited metabolism** → drug is broken down slower → higher levels → stronger effect → risk of toxicity.
- Exception: Prodrugs may behave the opposite way.

**Why it matters:** This helps you predict whether adding a new drug could make the current one stop working or become dangerous.

### Card 10 (Safety)

**Front:** Before scanning a patient's wristband and giving a medication, which of the following is included in the rights of medication administration?

A. Right patient, right drug, right dose, and right diagnosis  
B. Right patient, right drug, right dose, and right pharmacy  
C. Right patient, right drug, right cost, and right time  
D. Right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time, and right documentation  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**
The key medication rights:
- Right **patient** — check at least two identifiers
- Right **drug** — match the order
- Right **dose** — check the calculation
- Right **route** — oral, IV, IM, etc.
- Right **time** — on schedule
- Right **reason** — know why
- Right **documentation** — chart it
- Right **response** — did it work?
- Right **education** — teach the patient
- Right to **refuse** — the patient can say no

**Why it matters:** The rights of medication administration are the foundation of safe practice — and they show up on every exam.

### Card 11 (Priority)

**Front:** You are about to give a medication you have not given before. Which question is MOST important to ask yourself first?

A. "What assessment or lab should I check first, and is this drug safe for this patient right now?"  
B. "Has this drug been given to other patients on the unit today without any problems?"  
C. "Is this medication available in the automated dispensing machine on my floor?"  
D. "What is the cost of this drug compared to other options the provider could have ordered?"  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**
1. Why is this drug ordered for this patient?
2. What assessment or lab should I check FIRST? (like vital signs, potassium, etc.)
3. Is it safe for this patient RIGHT NOW?
4. What adverse effect am I watching for?
5. How will I know it worked?

**Why it matters:** This thinking process is what the exam is really testing. It separates a safe nurse from a task-doer.

### Card 33 (Application)

**Front:** A patient with kidney failure takes a drug that is mainly excreted by the kidneys. What does the nurse expect?

A. The drug will be metabolized faster by the liver to compensate for the kidney failure  
B. The drug will have a weaker effect because kidney failure reduces drug activation  
C. The drug will be absorbed more slowly from the GI tract, delaying its onset of action  
D. The drug may build up in the body because the kidneys cannot clear it normally, increasing the risk of toxicity  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**
- The kidneys cannot clear the drug as fast as normal → the drug stays in the body longer.
- Blood levels of the drug can build up → higher risk of toxicity.
- The provider may need to **lower the dose** or **increase the time between doses**.
- Monitor closely for signs of drug toxicity and check kidney function labs (like creatinine and BUN).

**Why it matters:** Just like liver failure changes how drugs are metabolized, kidney failure changes how drugs are removed. Always think: "Can this patient get rid of this drug safely?"

### Card 34 (Compare)

**Front:** A nurse gives a patient IV pain medication. When should the nurse plan to reassess the patient's pain level?

A. Within 5 minutes, because IV drugs always reach full effect immediately  
B. Within 15 to 30 minutes, because IV drugs have a fast onset compared to oral drugs  
C. Within 60 to 90 minutes, because all pain medications take at least one hour to work  
D. Only after the next scheduled dose, because reassessing too soon gives inaccurate results  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**
- **IV onset** is fast — often within minutes. The drug goes straight into the bloodstream.
- **Oral onset** is slower — usually 30-60 minutes. The drug must be absorbed from the gut and pass through the liver first.
- This changes when you reassess:
  - After IV medication → reassess in about **15-30 minutes**
  - After oral medication → reassess in about **30-60 minutes**
- If you reassess too early after an oral med, you might think it is not working when it just has not kicked in yet.

**Why it matters:** The exam may ask when to reassess after different routes. Matching your timing to the route keeps you from giving extra doses too soon.

### Card 35 (Safety)

**Front:** A patient on multiple drugs has a new drug added that is a strong CYP450 inhibitor. What should the nurse worry about?

A. The new drug will speed up metabolism of the other drugs, causing them to become less effective  
B. The new drug will block absorption of the other drugs in the GI tract, reducing their blood levels  
C. The new drug will slow the breakdown of the other drugs, raising their blood levels and increasing toxicity risk  
D. The new drug will increase kidney excretion of the other drugs, requiring higher doses to maintain effect  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**
- CYP450 enzymes in the liver break down many drugs.
- A **CYP450 inhibitor** slows down those enzymes → other drugs are broken down more slowly.
- This means blood levels of the other drugs can **rise** → increased risk of side effects and toxicity.
- The nurse should watch for signs of toxicity from the patient's existing medications.
- The provider may need to adjust doses of the other drugs.

**Why it matters:** Drug interactions through CYP450 are very common. When a new drug is added, always ask: "Could this change how the other drugs are processed?"

### Card 36 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** A patient asks why they cannot crush their extended-release pain tablet to make it easier to swallow. What is the nurse's best response?

A. "Crushing the tablet makes it taste very bitter and reduces patient satisfaction with the medication."  
B. "Crushing the tablet destroys the active ingredient so the drug will have no effect at all."  
C. "Crushing the tablet changes the color of the medication, making it harder to identify correctly."  
D. "Crushing the tablet releases the full dose at once instead of slowly over time, which could cause a dangerous overdose."  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**
- **Enteric-coated** tablets have a special coating that protects the stomach or protects the drug from stomach acid. Crushing removes that protection → can cause stomach irritation or the drug may not work.
- **Extended-release (ER/XR/SR)** tablets are designed to release the drug slowly over many hours. Crushing releases the full dose all at once → this can cause a dangerous overdose.
- Tell the patient: "Swallow these whole with water. Do not crush, chew, or break them."
- If the patient cannot swallow pills, ask the provider for a different form (like a liquid).

**Why it matters:** Crushing the wrong tablet is a medication error that can hurt the patient. The exam tests whether you know which forms must stay whole.

### Card 37 (Application)

**Front:** A patient in shock gets a SubQ injection. 30 minutes later there is no effect. What went wrong?

A. Poor blood flow to the tissues in shock prevents the SubQ drug from being absorbed into the bloodstream  
B. SubQ injections are always ineffective for emergency medications regardless of the patient's condition  
C. The drug was destroyed by enzymes in the subcutaneous tissue before it could be absorbed  
D. The patient developed an immune response to the SubQ injection site, blocking drug absorption  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**
- In shock, blood flow to the skin and tissues is very poor — the body redirects blood to the vital organs.
- SubQ (subcutaneous) injections depend on blood flow in the tissue to absorb the drug.
- With poor blood flow → the drug just sits in the tissue and is not absorbed.
- A better choice for a patient in shock is the **IV route** — it does not depend on tissue blood flow.

**Why it matters:** Route selection is not just about convenience — it is about whether the drug can actually reach the bloodstream. In emergencies, IV is the most reliable route.

### Card 38 (Lab)

**Front:** What lab helps assess kidney function for drug excretion decisions?

A. Serum albumin and total protein levels  
B. ALT and AST liver enzyme levels  
C. Complete blood count (CBC) and platelet count  
D. Serum creatinine and BUN (blood urea nitrogen)  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**
- **Serum creatinine** is the most commonly used lab. Normal is roughly 0.6-1.2 mg/dL (may vary by lab).
- **BUN (blood urea nitrogen)** is also used. Normal is roughly 7-20 mg/dL.
- **Creatinine clearance (CrCl)** or **GFR (glomerular filtration rate)** gives a more complete picture of how well the kidneys are filtering.
- If these values are abnormal, drug doses that depend on kidney excretion may need to be lowered.

**Why it matters:** Before giving a drug that the kidneys must clear, check kidney function. The exam expects you to connect the lab to the drug decision.

### Card 39 (Compare)

**Front:** What is the difference between a loading dose and a maintenance dose?

A. A loading dose is a smaller test dose given first to check for allergies, and a maintenance dose is the full regular dose  
B. A loading dose is a higher first dose to quickly reach a therapeutic blood level, and a maintenance dose is a smaller regular dose to keep the level steady  
C. A loading dose is given only by IV, and a maintenance dose is always given by mouth  
D. A loading dose and maintenance dose are the same amount, but given at different times of day  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**
- A **loading dose** is a higher first dose given to quickly reach a therapeutic level in the blood. It "loads" the body with enough drug to start working right away.
- A **maintenance dose** is a smaller, regular dose given after that to keep the drug level steady.
- Example: Digoxin may be started with a loading dose to quickly get the level into the therapeutic range, then continued with a lower daily maintenance dose.

**Why it matters:** If you only give the maintenance dose from the start, it can take a long time for the drug to build up to a working level. The exam may ask why a first dose is higher than the ongoing dose.

### Card 40 (Safety)

**Front:** A patient has a drug with a narrow therapeutic index. What makes this more dangerous than other drugs?

A. The drug must be given by IV only, which increases the risk of infection at the injection site  
B. The drug cannot be taken with any food or other medications, making dosing very difficult  
C. The difference between the dose that helps and the dose that harms is very small, so even minor changes can cause toxicity  
D. The drug has a very long half-life, meaning it takes weeks to reach a steady blood level  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**
- A **narrow therapeutic index (NTI)** means the difference between the dose that helps and the dose that harms is very small.
- Even a small change in dose or blood level can push the patient into toxicity.
- Examples: digoxin, warfarin, lithium, phenytoin, theophylline.
- These drugs need **frequent blood level monitoring** and careful dose adjustments.
- Watch extra closely if the patient has liver or kidney problems — drug levels can rise unexpectedly.

**Why it matters:** NTI drugs are high-risk. The exam tests whether you know to monitor levels more carefully and recognize early signs of toxicity.

### Card 41 (Application)

**Front:** Two drugs that are both highly protein-bound are given together. What could happen?

A. The drugs compete for binding sites on albumin, which can increase the free amount of one drug and raise the risk of toxicity  
B. The drugs bind to each other in the bloodstream, forming an inactive compound that is excreted by the kidneys  
C. The drugs both bind to albumin without affecting each other because there are enough binding sites for both  
D. The drugs cancel each other out, resulting in neither drug producing its intended therapeutic effect  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**
- Highly protein-bound drugs compete for the same spots on albumin in the blood.
- When two drugs compete, one drug may get "bumped off" → more of that drug is free (unbound) in the blood.
- More free drug = stronger effect = higher risk of side effects and toxicity.
- This is like two people fighting over one seat — someone ends up standing.

**Why it matters:** When you see two highly protein-bound drugs ordered together, think: "Could one drug increase the other's effect?" This is a common drug interaction question.

### Card 42 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** A patient asks "Why do I need blood tests for this medicine?" Which is the best explanation of therapeutic drug monitoring?

A. "The blood tests check if you are allergic to the medication so we can switch to a different one if needed."  
B. "The blood tests make sure your drug level is in the safe and effective range — not too low and not too high."  
C. "The blood tests are required by the pharmacy before they will refill your prescription each month."  
D. "The blood tests measure how fast your body absorbs the drug so we can change the time you take it."  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**
- Some medicines have a very small range between "enough to help" and "too much."
- Blood tests check that the drug level is in the **safe and effective zone** — not too low (will not work) and not too high (could be harmful).
- If the level is off, the provider can adjust the dose before problems happen.
- Simple way to explain: "The blood test helps us make sure your medicine is at just the right amount — like checking the temperature on an oven so your food cooks perfectly."

**Why it matters:** Patients who understand WHY they need lab work are more likely to keep their appointments. The exam may ask how you explain monitoring to a patient.
