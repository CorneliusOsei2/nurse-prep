## Exam #1

### Card 12 (Priority)

**Front:** A nurse gave the wrong dose of a medication. The patient seems fine right now. What should the nurse do FIRST?

A. Assess the patient for any adverse effects  
B. File an incident report with the charge nurse  
C. Call the pharmacy to verify the correct dose  
D. Administer an antidote as a precaution  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**

1. **Assess the patient first** — always.
2. Notify the provider.
3. Follow facility policy for reporting (incident report).
4. Keep monitoring the patient for any delayed effects.

**Why it matters:** The patient always comes before paperwork. On the exam, "assess first" is almost always correct after an error.

### Card 13 (Compare)

**Front:** Which statement correctly matches each analgesic class with its primary safety concern?

A. Acetaminophen causes GI bleeding; NSAIDs cause liver damage; Opioids cause kidney failure  
B. Acetaminophen causes liver damage; NSAIDs cause GI bleeding and kidney problems; Opioids cause respiratory depression  
C. Acetaminophen causes respiratory depression; NSAIDs cause liver toxicity; Opioids cause GI bleeding  
D. Acetaminophen causes kidney damage; NSAIDs cause sedation; Opioids cause liver damage  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**

- **Acetaminophen:** Treats pain and fever. ⚠️ Watch the **liver** — max dose matters. No anti-inflammatory effect.
- **NSAIDs:** Treat pain, fever, AND inflammation. ⚠️ Watch for **bleeding, stomach ulcers, and kidney problems**.
- **Opioids:** Treat moderate-to-severe pain. ⚠️ Watch **breathing (respiratory depression)**, sedation, constipation, and low blood pressure.

**Why it matters:** The exam loves to ask "which side effect matches which drug class." Know the key danger for each one.

### Card 14 (Antidote)

**Front:** What is the most important warning about naloxone (Narcan) when used to reverse opioid toxicity?

A. Naloxone can cause permanent liver damage if given more than once  
B. Naloxone should only be administered by the IV route to be effective  
C. Naloxone wears off faster than most opioids, so respiratory depression may return  
D. Naloxone eliminates all opioid effects permanently after a single dose  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**

Signs of opioid toxicity:
- Respiratory rate below 12 (or very slow, shallow breathing)
- Hard to wake up (excessive sedation)
- Pinpoint pupils
- Low blood pressure

Reversal: **Naloxone (Narcan)**
⚠️ Important warning: Naloxone wears off faster than most opioids. The patient can slide back into respiratory depression after it wears off. **Keep monitoring!**

**Why it matters:** Opioid safety is one of the most tested topics. Always remember: naloxone is short-acting — you may need to give more.

### Card 15 (Hold Parameter)

**Front:** A patient received IV morphine 30 minutes ago. You check on them and their respiratory rate is 10. What is your priority?

A. Document the respiratory rate and recheck in one hour  
B. Administer the next scheduled dose of morphine on time  
C. Stay with the patient, stimulate them, and prepare naloxone if breathing does not improve  
D. Call the pharmacy to change the morphine to an oral form  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**

1. **Stay with the patient** and stimulate them (call their name, rub their sternum).
2. If breathing does not improve, call for help and prepare naloxone.
3. Raise the head of the bed. Have oxygen ready.
4. Document everything and notify the provider.
- **Hold any further opioid doses** until the provider evaluates.

**Why it matters:** RR below 12 after an opioid = red flag. On the exam, respiratory depression is the #1 opioid concern.

### Card 16 (Red Flag)

**Front:** After giving IV morphine, which of the following findings needs immediate action?

A. The patient says "I feel a little itchy"  
B. Respiratory rate drops to 8  
C. The patient reports mild nausea  
D. The patient reports mild constipation  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**

- **(b) Respiratory rate of 8** needs immediate action.
- Mild itching and nausea are common side effects of opioids.
- A very low respiratory rate means the opioid is suppressing the brain's drive to breathe — this is life-threatening.

**Why it matters:** The exam tests whether you can separate "expected and mild" from "dangerous and urgent." Respiratory depression always wins as the priority.

### Card 17 (Application)

**Front:** Which of the following best describes what a nurse should reassess after giving an opioid pain medication?

A. Pain level, respiratory rate, sedation level, and blood pressure; reassess IV route in 15-30 minutes  
B. Temperature and blood pressure only; reassess in 2 hours  
C. Pain level only; reassess when the patient asks for the next dose  
D. Heart rhythm and urine output; reassess in 4 hours  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**

- **Pain level** (use the same scale — did it go down?)
- **Respiratory rate** (especially after opioids)
- **Sedation level** (are they too sleepy?)
- **Blood pressure** (if relevant)
- **Function** (can they cough, move, or rest now?)
- Reassess based on route: IV → about 15-30 min, oral → about 30-60 min.

**Why it matters:** Giving the drug is not the end. The nurse must follow up to check for relief AND for harm.

### Card 18 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** What is the most important safety teaching for a patient taking acetaminophen (Tylenol) along with over-the-counter medicines?

A. Acetaminophen should always be taken on an empty stomach for best absorption  
B. Many OTC cold and flu medicines already contain acetaminophen, making accidental overdose easy  
C. Acetaminophen can safely be taken in unlimited amounts if you drink plenty of water  
D. Acetaminophen is only dangerous when combined with prescription medications  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**

- Do not take more than **3-4 grams per day** (follow the label or provider instructions).
- Many OTC cold, flu, and pain medicines **already contain acetaminophen** — it is easy to take too much without knowing.
- Too much acetaminophen can cause **liver damage** — this can be life-threatening.
- **Avoid alcohol** while taking it (adds liver stress).
- Always read the label on every medicine for hidden acetaminophen.

**Why it matters:** Acetaminophen toxicity is a leading cause of liver failure. Teaching patients to check labels can save lives.

### Card 19 (Recall)

**Front:** Which insulin type has the fastest onset of action?

A. NPH insulin, with onset in 1-2 hours  
B. Regular insulin, with onset in 1-2 hours  
C. Rapid-acting insulin (lispro, aspart), with onset in about 15 minutes  
D. Long-acting insulin (glargine), with onset in about 15 minutes  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**

| Type | Example | Onset | Peak | Duration |
|------|---------|-------|------|----------|
| **Rapid-acting** | lispro, aspart | ~15 min | 1-2 hr | 3-5 hr |
| **Short-acting** | Regular | 30-60 min | 2-4 hr | 6-8 hr |
| **Intermediate** | NPH | 1-2 hr | 6-12 hr | 12-18 hr |
| **Long-acting** | glargine, detemir | 1-2 hr | minimal/none | ~24 hr |

**Why it matters:** The onset tells you when to watch for low blood sugar. The peak tells you when the risk is highest.

### Card 20 (Safety)

**Front:** A patient received rapid-acting insulin, but the meal tray has not arrived. Why is this a concern?

A. Rapid-acting insulin takes 2-4 hours to work, so the timing does not matter  
B. The insulin will be absorbed too slowly without food in the stomach  
C. The patient may develop hyperglycemia from the stress of not eating  
D. Rapid-acting insulin works in about 15 minutes and can cause hypoglycemia without food  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**

- Rapid-acting insulin starts working in about 15 minutes.
- If the patient does not eat soon after the dose, blood sugar can drop fast (hypoglycemia).
- **Always make sure food is on the way before giving rapid-acting insulin.**
- If the meal is delayed, hold the dose and notify the provider.

**Why it matters:** Timing errors with rapid insulin are one of the most common causes of hypoglycemia in hospitals.

### Card 21 (Recall)

**Front:** When mixing Regular insulin and NPH insulin in one syringe, which should be drawn up first?

A. NPH insulin first because it is the longer-acting insulin  
B. Either one can be drawn first as long as the total dose is correct  
C. NPH insulin first because it is cloudy and easier to see in the syringe  
D. Regular insulin first ("clear before cloudy") to prevent contaminating the Regular vial  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**

- **Clear** = Regular insulin (draw this up FIRST).
- **Cloudy** = NPH insulin (draw this up SECOND).
- This prevents contaminating the Regular insulin vial with NPH.

**Why it matters:** Drawing up in the wrong order can change the Regular insulin vial and affect future doses. This is a common exam question.

### Card 22 (Safety)

**Front:** Which of the following is an early sign of hypoglycemia?

A. Sweating, shakiness, and fast heartbeat  
B. Fruity-smelling breath and excessive thirst  
C. Flushed dry skin and elevated temperature  
D. Slow bounding pulse and facial swelling  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**

Early signs:
- Sweating, shakiness, hunger
- Fast heartbeat, anxiety
- Headache, weakness

Late or severe signs:
- Confusion, irritability
- Slurred speech, difficulty concentrating
- Seizures, loss of consciousness

**Why it matters:** Hypoglycemia can become a medical emergency if missed. If a patient on insulin acts confused or shaky, check the blood sugar right away.

### Card 23 (Application)

**Front:** A patient's blood sugar is 62 mg/dL and they are awake and can swallow. What is the correct first step using the Rule of 15?

A. Give 30 grams of fast-acting sugar and recheck in 30 minutes  
B. Give 15 grams of fast-acting sugar, wait 15 minutes, then recheck blood glucose  
C. Give a full meal with protein and wait 1 hour to recheck  
D. Administer IV dextrose immediately and recheck in 5 minutes  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**

The **Rule of 15**:
1. Give **15 grams** of fast-acting sugar (like 4 oz juice, glucose tablets, or regular soda).
2. Wait **15 minutes**.
3. Recheck the blood sugar.
4. If it is still below 70, repeat the steps.
5. Once stable, give a small snack with protein and carbs to prevent another drop.

**Why it matters:** This is the standard way to treat mild-to-moderate hypoglycemia. The exam expects you to know these numbers.

### Card 24 (Safety)

**Front:** Why is insulin considered a high-alert medication?

A. Insulin must be refrigerated at all times or it becomes toxic  
B. Insulin can only be administered by a provider, not by a nurse  
C. Even a small dose error can cause dangerous hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia  
D. Insulin requires a separate IV line and cannot be mixed with other fluids  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**

- Even a small dose mistake can cause dangerous hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia.
- The dose must match the **order**, the **blood glucose reading**, and the **patient's eating plan**.
- Many facilities require a **two-nurse check** before giving insulin.
- Always verify the type, dose, and expiration before giving.

**Why it matters:** High-alert drugs need extra caution every single time. The exam tests whether you treat insulin with the respect it deserves.

### Card 25 (Hold Parameter)

**Front:** A patient's heart rate is 54 bpm and metoprolol (a beta-blocker) is due. What should you do?

A. Give the metoprolol and recheck the heart rate in 30 minutes  
B. Give half the ordered dose to reduce the risk of bradycardia  
C. Give the metoprolol with a glass of orange juice to raise the heart rate  
D. Hold the metoprolol, notify the provider, and document your decision  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**

- **Hold the metoprolol** — do not give it.
- Beta-blockers lower heart rate. Giving it when HR is already low can cause dangerous bradycardia.
- A common hold parameter: **Hold if HR is below 60 bpm** (or per provider order).
- Notify the provider that you held the dose and why.
- Document the heart rate and your decision.

**Why it matters:** This is one of the most common hold-parameter questions on the exam. Always check HR before giving a beta-blocker.

### Card 26 (Hold Parameter)

**Front:** A patient's blood pressure is 88/52 mmHg and their scheduled antihypertensive is due. What should you do?

A. Give the medication and have the patient lie flat for 30 minutes  
B. Give half the dose and recheck blood pressure in one hour  
C. Give the medication with extra IV fluids to balance the blood pressure  
D. Hold the medication, assess the patient, and notify the provider  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**

- **Hold the antihypertensive** — giving it could drop the BP even further.
- Common hold parameter: **Hold if systolic BP is below 90-100 mmHg** (or per provider order).
- Assess the patient: Are they dizzy? Lightheaded? Check other vitals too.
- Notify the provider. Document your assessment and decision.

**Why it matters:** Giving a BP-lowering drug to someone whose blood pressure is already low can cause falls, fainting, or shock. The exam loves this scenario.

### Card 27 (Lab)

**Front:** A patient on furosemide (Lasix) has a potassium level of 3.1 mEq/L. Which symptoms should the nurse watch for?

A. Muscle weakness, leg cramps, and irregular heartbeat  
B. Facial flushing, fever, and high blood pressure  
C. Excessive thirst, frequent urination, and weight gain  
D. Joint pain, skin rash, and blurred vision  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**

- Normal potassium: 3.5-5.0 mEq/L → **3.1 is low (hypokalemia)**.
- Furosemide causes the kidneys to lose potassium.
- Watch for: muscle weakness, leg cramps, irregular heartbeat, fatigue.
- ⚠️ **Low potassium + digoxin = high risk for digoxin toxicity** — check if the patient also takes digoxin!
- Expect a potassium replacement order. Encourage potassium-rich foods (bananas, oranges, potatoes).

**Why it matters:** Loop diuretics and potassium go together on every exam. Always check K+ before and during treatment.

### Card 28 (Lab)

**Front:** Which combination of findings is most suggestive of digoxin toxicity?

A. Hypertension, tachycardia, and increased appetite  
B. Nausea, yellow-green visual halos, and bradycardia  
C. Weight gain, peripheral edema, and elevated potassium  
D. Fever, joint pain, and increased urine output  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**

Labs to monitor:
- **Digoxin level** — therapeutic range is 0.5-2.0 ng/mL
- **Potassium** — low K+ increases digoxin toxicity risk

Signs of digoxin toxicity:
- Nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite
- Visual changes (blurred vision, yellow-green halos)
- Bradycardia or irregular heart rhythm
- **Hold digoxin if HR is below 60** (common hold parameter)

**Why it matters:** Digoxin has a narrow therapeutic range. The exam often pairs digoxin questions with potassium questions.

### Card 29 (Antidote)

**Front:** Which statement correctly matches each anticoagulant with its reversal agent?

A. Heparin is reversed with vitamin K; warfarin is reversed with protamine sulfate  
B. Both heparin and warfarin are reversed with vitamin K  
C. Heparin is reversed with protamine sulfate; warfarin is reversed with vitamin K  
D. Both heparin and warfarin are reversed with protamine sulfate  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**

- **Heparin** antidote → **Protamine sulfate**
- **Warfarin** antidote → **Vitamin K (phytonadione)**; for severe bleeding, fresh frozen plasma may also be given.
- Heparin is monitored with **aPTT** (activated partial thromboplastin time).
- Warfarin is monitored with **PT/INR** (prothrombin time / international normalized ratio).

**Why it matters:** Knowing which antidote matches which blood thinner is a must-know for the exam. They will try to switch them — do not fall for it.

### Card 30 (Notify Provider)

**Front:** A patient on heparin develops new bruising, blood in the urine, and bleeding gums. What should you do FIRST?

A. Continue the heparin infusion and document the findings  
B. Assess the patient further, then notify the provider immediately  
C. Administer vitamin K to reverse the heparin effects  
D. Increase the IV fluid rate to dilute the heparin concentration  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**

1. **Stop and think: this is bleeding** — a serious complication of heparin.
2. Assess the patient further (check vitals, look for other bleeding signs).
3. **Notify the provider immediately.**
4. Expect orders for: aPTT lab draw, possible dose adjustment, and possibly protamine sulfate if bleeding is severe.
5. Hold the next heparin dose until you get new orders.
6. Document everything.

**Why it matters:** Bleeding is the #1 risk of blood thinners. Multiple signs of bleeding together = notify the provider right away.

### Card 31 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** Which of the following is correct teaching for a patient going home on warfarin (Coumadin)?

A. Avoid all green leafy vegetables completely while taking warfarin  
B. You no longer need regular blood tests once the initial dose is set  
C. Take aspirin freely for headaches since it does not interact with warfarin  
D. Keep vitamin K intake consistent and attend all INR blood test appointments  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**

- Take it at the **same time every day**.
- Go to **all INR blood test appointments** — the dose may need adjusting.
- Watch for signs of bleeding: unusual bruising, blood in urine or stool, bleeding gums, nosebleeds.
- **Keep vitamin K intake consistent** — do not suddenly eat a lot more or a lot less green leafy vegetables.
- Tell every healthcare provider and dentist that you take warfarin.
- Avoid aspirin and NSAIDs unless the provider says it is okay.

**Why it matters:** Warfarin requires ongoing monitoring and patient education. The exam loves warfarin teaching questions.

### Card 32 (Priority)

**Front:** You have four patients. Which one do you assess FIRST?

A. A patient on metformin with a blood sugar of 210  
B. A patient on morphine with a respiratory rate of 9  
C. A patient on warfarin with an INR of 2.5  
D. A patient on lisinopril with a dry cough  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**

- **(b) The patient on morphine with RR of 9** — this is respiratory depression, a life-threatening emergency.
- (a) Blood sugar of 210 is high but not immediately life-threatening.
- (c) INR of 2.5 is within the therapeutic range (2-3) — this is expected.
- (d) A dry cough is a common side effect of ACE inhibitors — report it, but it is not urgent.

**Why it matters:** Priority questions test: "Who is in the most danger right now?" Breathing problems almost always come first.

### Card 43 (Hold Parameter)

**Front:** Furosemide is due but potassium is 3.0 mEq/L. What should you do?

A. Hold the furosemide, notify the provider, and expect a potassium replacement order  
B. Give the furosemide with a potassium-rich snack like a banana  
C. Give half the furosemide dose to reduce potassium loss  
D. Give the furosemide and recheck potassium in 24 hours  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**

- Normal potassium is 3.5-5.0 mEq/L. A level of **3.0 is low (hypokalemia)**.
- Furosemide (Lasix) is a loop diuretic — it makes the kidneys lose even more potassium.
- Giving furosemide now could drop potassium further → dangerous heart rhythms.
- **Hold the furosemide.** Notify the provider.
- Expect an order for potassium replacement first.
- If the patient also takes digoxin, low potassium makes digoxin toxicity worse — flag this too.

**Why it matters:** Always check potassium before giving a loop diuretic. The exam pairs furosemide with potassium over and over.

### Card 44 (Hold Parameter)

**Front:** Blood glucose is 62 mg/dL and rapid-acting insulin is due. What should you do?

A. Give the insulin at a lower dose to prevent further blood sugar drop  
B. Give the insulin and then immediately offer a sugary drink  
C. Hold the insulin, treat the hypoglycemia first, and notify the provider  
D. Give the insulin as ordered since the provider calculated the correct dose  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**

- A blood sugar of 62 mg/dL is **below 70 — this is hypoglycemia**.
- Giving rapid-acting insulin now would drop the blood sugar even more → this is dangerous.
- **Hold the insulin.** Do NOT give it.
- Treat the hypoglycemia first using the Rule of 15 (15 g fast sugar → wait 15 min → recheck).
- Notify the provider about the low reading and the held dose.
- Document everything.

**Why it matters:** Never give insulin to a patient who is already hypoglycemic. The exam will test whether you check the glucose BEFORE giving insulin.

### Card 45 (Safety)

**Front:** Which combination of symptoms is most characteristic of digoxin toxicity?

A. Hypertension, tachycardia, and weight gain  
B. Fever, elevated white blood cell count, and cough  
C. Hyperkalemia, muscle cramps, and diarrhea  
D. Nausea, yellow-green visual halos, and bradycardia  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**

- **GI signs:** nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite (often the earliest clue)
- **Visual changes:** blurred vision, seeing yellow-green halos around lights
- **Heart changes:** bradycardia (HR below 60), irregular rhythms
- **Neuro signs:** confusion, fatigue, weakness
- Therapeutic digoxin level: **0.5-2.0 ng/mL** — anything above 2.0 is toxic
- ⚠️ **Low potassium makes digoxin toxicity worse** — always check K+ too

**Why it matters:** Digoxin toxicity can be life-threatening. On the exam, if you see nausea + visual changes + slow heart rate, think digoxin toxicity immediately.

### Card 46 (Application)

**Front:** A patient is on both heparin and warfarin at the same time. What is the reason for this overlap?

A. Warfarin takes 3-5 days to reach full effect, so heparin provides immediate protection  
B. Heparin and warfarin must be combined permanently to prevent any clot formation  
C. Warfarin works immediately but heparin is needed to prevent an allergic reaction to it  
D. Both drugs target the same clotting factor and must be given together for full effect  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**

- Heparin works **right away** (fast onset) — it prevents new clots immediately.
- Warfarin takes **3-5 days** to reach full effect — it is slow to start working.
- During those first few days, the patient needs heparin to stay protected while warfarin builds up.
- This overlap period is called **bridge therapy**.
- Once the INR is in the therapeutic range (usually 2-3), heparin is stopped and the patient continues on warfarin alone.

**Why it matters:** The exam loves to ask "why both at the same time?" The answer is always: warfarin is slow to start, so heparin covers the gap.

### Card 47 (Compare)

**Front:** How does ketorolac (Toradol) differ from ibuprofen in clinical use?

A. Ketorolac is an opioid while ibuprofen is an NSAID  
B. Ketorolac can be given IV/IM and is limited to 5 days due to high bleeding and kidney risk  
C. Ibuprofen is stronger than ketorolac and is limited to 3 days of use  
D. Ketorolac has no risk of GI bleeding because it bypasses the stomach  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**

- Both are **NSAIDs** — they treat pain and inflammation.
- **Ketorolac (Toradol):**
  - Can be given **IV or IM** (also comes in oral form)
  - Very strong — used for moderate-to-severe pain
  - **Limited to 5 days maximum** because of high risk for GI bleeding and kidney damage
- **Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin):**
  - Given **orally** (also available IV in some settings)
  - Used for mild-to-moderate pain
  - Can be used longer, but still watch for GI and kidney side effects

**Why it matters:** Ketorolac's 5-day limit is a favorite exam question. If you see a patient on ketorolac past 5 days, that is a concern.

### Card 48 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** What is the correct dietary teaching for a patient discharged on warfarin regarding vitamin K?

A. Avoid all foods containing vitamin K for the duration of treatment  
B. Eat as many green leafy vegetables as possible to boost healthy clotting  
C. Keep vitamin K intake consistent week to week and do not make sudden changes  
D. Take a daily vitamin K supplement to balance out the warfarin effects  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**

- Vitamin K works **against** warfarin — too much vitamin K can make warfarin less effective.
- Foods high in vitamin K include: dark green leafy vegetables (spinach, kale, broccoli, Brussels sprouts).
- **Do NOT tell the patient to avoid these foods completely.** Instead, teach them to keep their intake **consistent** — eat about the same amount each week.
- Sudden changes (eating a lot more or a lot less) can cause the INR to swing up or down.
- Simple way to say it: "You do not have to stop eating salads. Just do not eat a huge salad every day one week and then none the next week."

**Why it matters:** Many patients think they can never eat greens again. Your teaching should be clear: consistency is the key, not avoidance.

### Card 49 (Lab)

**Front:** A patient on warfarin has an INR of 3.8. What does this result indicate?

A. The INR is within the normal therapeutic range and no action is needed  
B. The blood is too thick and the warfarin dose should be increased  
C. The patient has a high risk of forming new blood clots  
D. The blood is too thin and the patient has an increased risk of bleeding  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**

- The therapeutic INR range for most patients on warfarin is **2.0-3.0**.
- An INR of 3.8 is **above the target** — the blood is too thin.
- This means the patient has a **higher risk of bleeding**.
- Assess for bleeding signs: bruising, blood in urine or stool, nosebleeds, bleeding gums.
- Notify the provider — the warfarin dose may need to be lowered or held.
- If bleeding is present, vitamin K may be ordered.

**Why it matters:** The exam expects you to know the normal INR range and act when it is too high. Too thin = bleeding risk. Too thick = clotting risk.

### Card 50 (Priority)

**Front:** Four patients need pain medication. Which one should you assess FIRST?

A. A patient with pain rated 8/10 and normal vital signs  
B. A patient with mild nausea after taking pain medication  
C. A patient with a respiratory rate of 10 after receiving an opioid  
D. A patient with mild itching after taking pain medication  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**

- **See the patient with RR 10 first.**
- A respiratory rate of 10 is low — this could be a sign of **opioid-induced respiratory depression**, which is life-threatening.
- The others are important but not immediately dangerous:
  - Pain 8/10 with normal vitals = uncomfortable but stable
  - Mild nausea = common opioid side effect
  - Itching = common opioid side effect
- **Breathing always comes first.**

**Why it matters:** Priority questions follow the ABCs — Airway, Breathing, Circulation. A low respiratory rate is a breathing problem, so it always gets attention first.

### Card 51 (Application)

**Front:** A patient on an opioid PCA has not pressed the button in 4 hours but is hard to wake. What should you do FIRST?

A. Assess respiratory rate and oxygen saturation immediately and stop the PCA pump  
B. Press the PCA button for the patient to help manage their pain  
C. Let the patient continue sleeping since they are not requesting medication  
D. Increase the PCA dose since the current setting is clearly not effective  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**

- This is a red flag for **oversedation or respiratory depression**.
- Even though the patient is not pressing the button, background infusion (if set) or accumulated doses may have built up.
- **Assess immediately:** check respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, and level of consciousness.
- If RR is low or the patient does not respond → call for help, prepare **naloxone (Narcan)**.
- **Stop the PCA pump** until the provider evaluates.
- Never assume the patient is just sleeping deeply — always check.

**Why it matters:** PCA safety means monitoring sedation levels, not just pain scores. The exam tests whether you recognize oversedation as a danger sign.

### Card 52 (Safety)

**Front:** Which set of signs indicates anaphylaxis during a first-dose antibiotic?

A. Mild rash on the arm, normal blood pressure, and stable breathing  
B. Facial swelling, difficulty breathing, hives, and a sudden drop in blood pressure  
C. Nausea, mild headache, and slight dizziness after the infusion  
D. Elevated temperature, chills, and muscle aches 2 hours later  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: B**

- Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction. It can start within **minutes** of the first dose.
- Signs to watch for:
  - Swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat
  - Difficulty breathing, wheezing, stridor
  - Hives or widespread rash
  - A sudden drop in blood pressure
  - Rapid, weak pulse
  - Feeling of doom or severe anxiety
- **Act fast:** Stop the infusion. Call for help. Prepare **epinephrine** (the first-line drug for anaphylaxis). Maintain the airway.

**Why it matters:** Always stay near the patient during the first few minutes of a new antibiotic. The exam tests whether you can spot anaphylaxis early and know that epinephrine is the priority drug.

### Card 53 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** What should you teach a patient about taking NSAIDs like ibuprofen?

A. NSAIDs should be taken on an empty stomach for faster absorption  
B. NSAIDs are completely safe for long-term daily use without monitoring  
C. Take NSAIDs with food or a full glass of water to protect the stomach lining  
D. NSAIDs should only be taken at bedtime to reduce daytime drowsiness  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: C**

- NSAIDs (like ibuprofen, naproxen) can irritate the stomach lining.
- Taking them with **food or a full glass of water** helps protect the stomach.
- Teach the patient: "Take this medicine with a meal or a snack — not on an empty stomach."
- Watch for warning signs of stomach problems: stomach pain, dark or tarry stools, nausea, vomiting blood.
- If stomach upset continues even with food, tell the provider — they may switch to a different medicine or add a stomach protector.

**Why it matters:** GI bleeding from NSAIDs is a real risk, especially in older adults. Simple teaching like "take with food" can prevent a serious complication.

### Card 54 (Compare)

**Front:** A nurse is comparing morphine and hydromorphone (Dilaudid). Which statement is correct about their relative strength?

A. Morphine and hydromorphone are equal in strength milligram for milligram  
B. Morphine is much stronger than hydromorphone per milligram  
C. Hydromorphone is weaker than morphine and requires larger doses for the same effect  
D. Hydromorphone is much stronger — 1 mg equals roughly 5-7 mg of morphine  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**

- **No.** Hydromorphone (Dilaudid) is much stronger than morphine per milligram.
- Roughly: **1 mg of hydromorphone ≈ 5-7 mg of morphine** (approximate equianalgesic dose).
- A small number for hydromorphone can give a big effect.
- This means a dose mix-up between the two can be very dangerous.
- Always double-check which opioid is ordered and the correct dose.

**Why it matters:** Confusing morphine and hydromorphone doses is a real-world medication error. The exam tests whether you understand that different opioids have different potencies.

### Card 55 (Application)

**Front:** A patient with chronic pain asks to increase their opioid dose. The provider orders multimodal pain management instead. What is the main benefit of this approach?

A. It uses multiple pain treatments together, allowing lower opioid doses with better overall control  
B. It replaces all medications with non-drug therapies like ice and relaxation only  
C. It increases the opioid dose gradually while adding a muscle relaxant for extra relief  
D. It eliminates the need for any follow-up pain assessments after discharge  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**

- **Multimodal pain management** means using more than one type of pain treatment together — each one works in a different way.
- Examples: an NSAID + a low-dose opioid + ice/heat + relaxation techniques.
- Benefits:
  - Lower opioid doses are needed → fewer opioid side effects
  - Better overall pain control (attacking pain from different angles)
  - Lower risk of opioid dependence and tolerance
- Simply increasing the opioid dose raises the risk of respiratory depression, constipation, and addiction.

**Why it matters:** The exam favors multimodal approaches. If a question asks about the best plan for chronic pain, combining treatments is usually the best answer.

### Card 56 (Safety)

**Front:** Which of the following correctly describes the Rule of 15 for treating hypoglycemia in a conscious patient?

A. Give 5 grams of sugar, wait 5 minutes, and recheck blood glucose  
B. Give 30 grams of sugar, wait 30 minutes, then provide a full meal  
C. Give 50 mL of IV dextrose, wait 10 minutes, then give glucagon IM  
D. Give 15 grams of fast-acting sugar, wait 15 minutes, and recheck blood glucose  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: D**

If the patient is **conscious and can swallow**:
1. **Give 15 grams** of fast-acting carbohydrate (4 oz juice, 4 glucose tablets, or 6 oz regular soda).
2. **Wait 15 minutes.** Do not recheck too soon.
3. **Recheck the blood glucose.**
4. If still below 70 mg/dL → **repeat** steps 1-3.
5. Once blood sugar is above 70, give a **small snack** with protein and carbs (like crackers and cheese) to keep it stable.

If the patient is **unconscious or cannot swallow**:
- Do NOT give anything by mouth — choking risk.
- Give **glucagon IM/SubQ** or **IV dextrose (D50)**.
- Turn the patient on their side after glucagon (in case of vomiting).

**Why it matters:** The exam loves this step-by-step protocol. Knowing what to do when the patient CAN'T swallow is what separates a strong answer from an average one.

### Card 57 (Lab)

**Front:** A patient on heparin has platelets dropping from 180,000 to 85,000 over 5 days. What is the most likely concern?

A. Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT), which requires stopping all heparin immediately  
B. A normal expected response to heparin therapy that requires no intervention  
C. Iron deficiency anemia caused by the heparin infusion  
D. An allergic skin reaction that is treated by reducing the heparin dose  

**Back:**
**Correct answer: A**

- Normal platelets: 150,000-400,000/μL.
- A significant drop in platelets while on heparin raises concern for **HIT — Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia**.
- HIT is a dangerous immune reaction where the body forms antibodies against heparin.
- Paradoxically, HIT increases the risk of **blood clots** (not just bleeding).
- **Stop heparin immediately** and notify the provider.
- The provider will order a HIT antibody test and switch to an alternative anticoagulant if needed.
- Do NOT give heparin flushes either — all heparin must stop.

**Why it matters:** A dropping platelet count on heparin is a red flag that the exam tests often. Remember: HIT = stop ALL heparin and think clots, not just bleeding.
