# Fundamentals Exam 1 Application Questions

These questions help you practice using Exam 1 content in realistic nursing situations. Focus on what is safest, what comes first, and what the nurse should assess, do, teach, report, or document.

## How to Use This File

- Try to answer each question before reading the answer.
- Watch for priority words such as **first**, **best**, **priority**, **safest**, and **needs follow-up**.
- For **SATA** questions, choose every correct answer.
- Read the explanation even if you got the question right. It helps you see why the other choices are weaker.

## The Nursing Process

### Question 1 (Priority)
**Question:** The nurse is caring for four clients. Which client should the nurse assess first?

A. A client with oxygen saturation 88% on room air who says, “I feel short of breath.”  
B. A client with incisional pain rated 7 out of 10 after walking.  
C. A client asking when discharge teaching will begin.  
D. A client who wants fresh water and a blanket.

**Correct answer:** A  
**Explanation:** The low oxygen saturation and shortness of breath point to a breathing problem. That makes this client the priority under ABCs, so the nurse should assess this client before addressing pain, teaching, or comfort needs. Comfort matters, but oxygenation comes first.

### Question 2 (Assessment / Validation)
**Question:** A client says, “My pain is 0 out of 10,” but the nurse sees grimacing, guarding, and refusal to turn in bed. What should the nurse do first?

A. Document that the client has no pain.  
B. Give the PRN opioid right away.  
C. Reassess and ask the client more about the discomfort.  
D. Ask a family member if the client usually avoids movement.

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** The client’s words and body language do not match here. When the picture is unclear and not immediately life-threatening, the nurse should reassess before documenting or giving a PRN opioid. A common trap is acting on one clue only instead of validating both verbal and nonverbal signs.

### Question 3 (Planning / SMART Outcome)
**Question:** Which nursing outcome is written best?

A. The nurse will teach the client how to walk safely.  
B. The client will improve mobility soon.  
C. The client will transfer from bed to chair with one-person assist by the end of the shift.  
D. The client will understand why walking is important.

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** This outcome is clear enough to measure because it says what the client will do, how much help is needed, and the time frame. That makes it the strongest choice for evaluating progress later in the shift. Choice A may sound useful, but it describes the nurse’s action, not the client’s outcome.

### Question 4 (Diagnosis)
**Question:** A client is admitted with pneumonia. Which statement is the best example of a nursing problem?

A. Pneumonia related to bacterial infection  
B. Ineffective airway clearance related to thick secretions  
C. Chest x-ray shows right lower lobe infiltrates  
D. Ceftriaxone 1 g IV every 24 hours

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** The important clue is that this option describes a client response the nurse can improve. Nursing diagnoses focus on human responses, not the medical disease itself. The pneumonia diagnosis, x-ray result, and medication order give context, but they are not the nursing problem.

### Question 5 (Evaluation)
**Question:** The care plan goal says, “Client will ambulate 30 feet with a walker by 1500.” At 1500, the client walks only 10 feet and becomes very tired. What should the nurse do next?

A. Mark the outcome as failed and continue the same plan tomorrow.  
B. Reassess the client, identify barriers, and revise the plan as needed.  
C. Tell the client to try harder at the next walk.  
D. Wait for the health care provider to write a new care plan.

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** The goal was not met, and the fatigue is the key clue. That means the nurse should reassess, look for barriers such as pain or weakness, and then adjust the plan. Telling the client to try harder skips the nursing process and misses the reason for the problem.

## Hygiene

### Question 6 (Delegation)
**Question:** Which task is most appropriate for the nurse to delegate to unlicensed assistive personnel (UAP)?

A. Teach a newly diagnosed client about diabetic foot care.  
B. Provide a routine partial bath to a stable postoperative client.  
C. Assess the oral mucosa of a client receiving oxygen.  
D. Perform the first bath for a confused client with dementia who is resisting care.

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** A routine partial bath for a stable postoperative client is predictable care. That makes it appropriate for UAP, while the nurse keeps tasks that need assessment, teaching, or judgment. The first bath for a confused client may look simple, but the safety risk makes it a nursing task.

### Question 7 (Safety / First Action)
**Question:** An unconscious client needs oral care. What should the nurse do first?

A. Place the client flat on the back.  
B. Position the client side-lying and have suction available.  
C. Pour mouthwash into the mouth to loosen secretions.  
D. Use a hard toothbrush to remove all debris quickly.

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Because the client is unconscious, airway protection is the first concern. Side-lying positioning with suction ready lowers the chance of aspiration and makes oral care safer. Lying the client flat or pouring liquid into the mouth would increase risk.

### Question 8 (Infection Prevention)
**Question:** A nursing student says, “Oral care for a ventilated client is mostly for comfort.” Which response by the nurse is best?

A. “Yes, it mainly helps dry lips.”  
B. “It is important because it lowers pneumonia risk and helps manage secretions.”  
C. “It matters only if the client can swallow.”  
D. “It is optional if suction is available.”

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** The key teaching point is that oral care does more than improve comfort. In a ventilated client, it helps manage secretions and lowers pneumonia risk, so it is a safety intervention as well as a comfort measure. Saying it is optional is the kind of trap that ignores infection prevention.

### Question 9 (Best Action)
**Question:** During morning hygiene care, a client with dementia becomes tense and pulls the washcloth away. What is the nurse’s best action?

A. Finish the bath quickly before the client becomes more upset.  
B. Use a calm voice, give one-step directions, and explain each step before touching the client.  
C. Tell the client the bath is required and cannot be refused.  
D. Ask a family member to hold the client still.

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Pulling away and becoming tense usually means the client is frightened or overstimulated. A calm voice, one-step directions, and explaining before touching are the safest way to reduce fear and gain cooperation. Rushing or forcing care often makes resistance worse.

### Question 10 (Patient Teaching, SATA)
**Question:** The nurse is teaching foot care to a client with diabetes. Which instructions should the nurse include? Select all that apply.

A. Inspect the feet every day.  
B. Dry carefully between the toes after washing.  
C. Test water temperature before washing the feet.  
D. Put lotion between the toes to prevent dryness.  
E. Wear clean socks and well-fitting shoes.

**Correct answer:** A, B, C, E  
**Explanation:** These choices all protect the feet from injury and infection, which is the main safety issue in diabetes. Daily inspection, careful drying, checking water temperature, and wearing shoes and socks all lower the chance of burns, skin breakdown, and unnoticed wounds. Lotion between the toes is the trap because extra moisture there can lead to skin breakdown or infection.

### Question 11 (Stop the Procedure)
**Question:** The nurse is irrigating a client’s ear for cerumen impaction. Which finding means the nurse should stop the procedure immediately?

A. A small amount of wax returns in the basin.  
B. The client says the solution feels warm.  
C. The client reports dizziness and ear pain.  
D. The irrigation solution runs back out of the ear.

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** Dizziness and ear pain are warning signs, not expected findings during ear irrigation. The safest response is to stop and reassess right away so the nurse does not cause further injury. A little wax returning or solution draining out can be normal, but acute symptoms are not.

## Older Adult

### Question 12 (Priority Assessment)
**Question:** An older adult who was alert yesterday is suddenly confused, restless, and unable to focus today. What is the nurse’s first action?

A. Assess for acute causes such as infection, medication effects, dehydration, or hypoxia.  
B. Reassure the family that this is a normal part of aging.  
C. Start memory games to check for dementia.  
D. Encourage the client to sleep and reassess tomorrow.

**Correct answer:** A  
**Explanation:** The sudden change is the biggest clue. In an older adult, new confusion and restlessness suggest delirium until proven otherwise, so the nurse should look for reversible causes like infection, dehydration, hypoxia, or medications. Waiting until tomorrow could delay treatment of a serious problem.

### Question 13 (Assessment)
**Question:** Which change in an older adult is most concerning for possible infection?

A. New confusion and weakness  
B. Gradual gray hair  
C. Dry skin on the lower legs  
D. Mild hearing loss over several years

**Correct answer:** A  
**Explanation:** New confusion and weakness matter because older adults often show infection in subtle ways. They may not have a strong fever response, so functional or mental status changes can be the early clue. The other options fit common aging changes, not an urgent shift.

### Question 14 (Safety / Reporting)
**Question:** Which situation should make the nurse suspect possible elder abuse and follow facility reporting policy?

A. An older adult asks for help opening a food tray.  
B. An older adult has bruises in different stages of healing and becomes quiet when the caregiver enters the room.  
C. An older adult wears glasses and uses a cane.  
D. An older adult says, “I go to bed early now.”

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Bruises in different stages of healing plus sudden quiet behavior around the caregiver are strong red flags. That pattern makes abuse a safety concern and requires reporting according to policy and law. The nurse does not need proof first; suspicion is enough to act.

### Question 15 (Risk Recognition)
**Question:** Which older adult is at highest risk for falls related to polypharmacy?

A. A client taking a daily multivitamin and calcium supplement  
B. A client taking a bedtime sedating antihistamine who now reports morning dizziness after starting a new blood pressure medication  
C. A client who uses reading glasses for small print  
D. A client who applies lotion to dry skin after bathing

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Morning dizziness after adding a blood pressure drug to a sedating antihistamine is the key clue. That combination can increase dizziness, confusion, and falls, especially in older adults. The other options do not carry the same medication-related risk.

## Client Safety

### Question 16 (Hazard Recognition)
**Question:** A client who is weak and urgently needs to use the bathroom calls for help. Which room finding should the nurse correct first?

A. The curtain is partly open.  
B. A wet floor is present near the bedside.  
C. A family photo is on the windowsill.  
D. An extra blanket is folded on the chair.

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** The wet floor is the immediate danger because this client is weak and needs to get up now. Fixing the fall hazard comes before privacy or comfort items. When one option can cause harm right away, that is usually the priority.

### Question 17 (Response to a Fall)
**Question:** While ambulating in the hallway, a client begins to fall. What should the nurse do?

A. Try to hold the client upright by pulling on both arms.  
B. Step back so the client can fall without pulling the nurse down.  
C. Support the client, lower the client to the floor, and protect the head.  
D. Grab the client under the arms and lift upward quickly.

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** Once the client starts to fall, the goal is to reduce injury. Supporting the client to the floor and protecting the head is the safest action. Pulling on the arms or trying to hold the client upright can injure both the client and the nurse.

### Question 18 (Least Restrictive Intervention)
**Question:** A confused client keeps trying to climb out of bed. What should the nurse do first?

A. Apply wrist restraints because the client might fall.  
B. Assess pain, toileting, hunger, and discomfort, then try reorientation and a low bed or alarm.  
C. Raise all four side rails and leave the room.  
D. Ask the health care provider for a PRN restraint order.

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Trying to climb out of bed often has a reason behind it. The nurse should first assess for pain, toileting, hunger, or confusion and try low-risk alternatives before using restraints. NCLEX often tests whether you choose the least restrictive action first.

### Question 19 (Restraint Safety)
**Question:** Which action by the nurse requires immediate correction when a restraint is applied?

A. Using a quick-release knot  
B. Securing the restraint to the bed frame  
C. Leaving room to insert two fingers under the restraint  
D. Securing the restraint to the side rail

**Correct answer:** D  
**Explanation:** The unsafe clue is that the restraint is tied to the side rail. Side rails move, so the restraint can tighten or pull in a way that injures the client. Tying it to the bed frame with a quick-release knot is the safer setup.

### Question 20 (Fire Safety)
**Question:** A small trash-can fire starts in a client’s room, and the client is still in bed. According to RACE, what should the nurse do first?

A. Rescue the client from immediate danger.  
B. Activate the fire alarm.  
C. Close the room door.  
D. Get the fire extinguisher.

**Correct answer:** A  
**Explanation:** The client is still in the room, so immediate danger is the key clue. In RACE, rescue comes first, before sounding the alarm or containing the fire. This is a classic sequence question, so remembering the order matters.

## Mobility

### Question 21 (First Ambulation)
**Question:** A client has been on bed rest for 2 days and is getting out of bed for the first time. What is the nurse’s best action?

A. Help the client stand as soon as the feet touch the floor.  
B. Ask the client to march in place right away.  
C. Help the client sit at the side of the bed, dangle, and assess for dizziness before standing.  
D. Tell the client to stand quickly so weakness does not get worse.

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** After two days of bed rest, the nurse needs to see how the client tolerates position changes. Dangling at the bedside gives time to assess for dizziness and orthostatic hypotension before standing. Standing quickly may seem efficient, but it skips an important safety check.

### Question 22 (Respiratory Complication Prevention)
**Question:** Which nursing action best helps prevent atelectasis and pneumonia in a client on bed rest?

A. Limiting fluids before sleep  
B. Encouraging turning, coughing, deep breathing, and use of incentive spirometry if ordered  
C. Keeping the client flat in bed as much as possible  
D. Restricting activity until lung sounds are clear

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Bed rest slows lung expansion and lets secretions collect. Turning, coughing, deep breathing, and incentive spirometry help reopen the lungs and clear secretions, so they are the best prevention steps. Keeping the client flat would work against that goal.

### Question 23 (Circulatory Complication Prevention)
**Question:** A client is on prolonged bed rest after surgery. Which intervention best helps reduce the risk for deep vein thrombosis (DVT)?

A. Encourage leg exercises and use compression devices as ordered.  
B. Place a pillow under both knees at all times.  
C. Limit fluid intake to reduce edema.  
D. Keep the client on strict bed rest until discharge.

**Correct answer:** A  
**Explanation:** Immobility slows venous return, which raises the risk for DVT. Leg exercises and compression devices help keep blood moving, so they are the safest preventive measures here. A pillow under both knees sounds comfortable, but it can reduce blood flow even more.

### Question 24 (Mobility Aid Use)
**Question:** A client has weakness in the left leg and uses a cane. Which instruction is correct?

A. Hold the cane in the left hand and move the strong leg first.  
B. Hold the cane in the right hand, move the cane and left leg together, then move the right leg.  
C. Hold the cane in the left hand and move the cane after both legs move.  
D. Hold the cane in either hand because hand choice does not matter.

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** The weak leg is the clue, so the cane should stay on the strong side for better balance. Moving the cane with the weak leg gives extra support when that side steps forward. Putting the cane on the weak side is a common test-day mistake.

### Question 25 (Walker Safety)
**Question:** The nurse teaches a client with a weak right leg to walk with a walker. Which sequence is correct?

A. Move the strong leg, then the walker, then the weak leg.  
B. Move the walker, then the weak leg, then the strong leg.  
C. Move the weak leg, then the strong leg, then the walker.  
D. Move both legs together, then slide the walker forward.

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** With a walker, stability comes first. Moving the walker first creates a safe base, then the weak leg steps in, and the strong leg follows. If the strong leg goes first, the weaker side is left without enough support.

## Communication

### Question 26 (Therapeutic Communication)
**Question:** A client says, “I don’t think I can do this anymore,” and starts to cry. Which response by the nurse is best?

A. “You need to stay strong.”  
B. “Do not worry. Everything will be fine.”  
C. “This sounds very hard for you. Tell me more about what you’re feeling.”  
D. “Why are you thinking like that?”

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** The client is crying and saying things feel unbearable, so the nurse needs to explore the feeling. This response names the emotion and invites the client to say more, which keeps communication open. False reassurance may sound kind, but it usually shuts the conversation down.

### Question 27 (Nontherapeutic Communication)
**Question:** Which nurse response is nontherapeutic?

A. “Tell me what worries you most right now.”  
B. “You should not feel upset about such a simple procedure.”  
C. “I will stay with you for a few minutes.”  
D. “Can you say more about what you mean?”

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** This statement judges the client and tells the client how to feel. Therapeutic communication makes room for the client’s reaction instead of minimizing it. On exams, answers that shame, judge, or dismiss feelings are usually the trap.

### Question 28 (Special Needs / Safety)
**Question:** A client with limited English proficiency needs teaching before a new procedure. What should the nurse do?

A. Ask the client’s teenage child to translate.  
B. Use a professional medical interpreter.  
C. Speak louder and use short words.  
D. Give the written handout only and return later.

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** The key issue is safe, accurate understanding before a new procedure. A professional medical interpreter protects communication and supports informed consent better than a family member or louder speech. Written papers alone are not enough when a language barrier is present.

### Question 29 (Advocacy)
**Question:** During discharge teaching, the health care provider speaks quickly and leaves. The client looks confused and says, “I still don’t understand my medicines.” What should the nurse do first?

A. Tell the client to read the papers again at home.  
B. Document that discharge teaching is complete.  
C. Contact the provider or continue teaching, use teach-back, and make sure the client understands before discharge.  
D. Ask a family member to explain the instructions later.

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** The client’s confusion is the clue that discharge teaching is not complete. The safest step is to keep teaching, use teach-back, and involve the provider if needed until the client truly understands the medicines. Sending the client home with papers only would be unsafe.

## Documentation and Informatics

### Question 30 (Documentation Judgment)
**Question:** Which charting entry is best?

A. “Client seems fine.”  
B. “Client stable. No problems.”  
C. “Client denies pain, respirations 18/min, skin warm and dry.”  
D. “Client doing much better today.”

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** Good charting uses facts that can be seen, measured, or reported clearly. This option gives specific assessment data instead of vague words like fine or better. That makes the record more useful and more legally sound.

### Question 31 (Incident Report / Charting)
**Question:** A client has an unwitnessed fall. Which statement best describes what the nurse should document in the medical record?

A. “Incident report completed after preventable fall.”  
B. “Found client on floor beside bed at 1015; 2-cm skin tear noted on right elbow; vital signs obtained; provider notified.”  
C. “Client fell because bed alarm was not loud enough.”  
D. “Risk management will review the event.”

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** The best charting after a fall sticks to the facts. This entry records what was found, the injury noted, the assessment steps, and provider notification without guessing or blaming. The medical record should describe the event, not mention an incident report.

### Question 32 (Handoff Report, SATA)
**Question:** The nurse is giving end-of-shift handoff report. Which items should be included? Select all that apply.

A. The client’s identity and main diagnosis  
B. Recent changes in condition or assessment findings  
C. Personal opinions such as “This client is difficult”  
D. Current treatments, lines, and safety concerns  
E. What still needs follow-up on the next shift

**Correct answer:** A, B, D, E  
**Explanation:** A safe handoff gives the next nurse the facts needed to care for the client right away. Identity, diagnosis, recent changes, current treatments, safety concerns, and follow-up needs all belong in report. Personal opinions are the trap because they do not help care and do not belong in professional communication.

### Question 33 (Telephone / Verbal Reporting)
**Question:** The nurse receives a telephone order from the health care provider for potassium replacement. What should the nurse do next?

A. Hang up and enter the order quickly before forgetting it.  
B. Read the order back to the provider to verify accuracy.  
C. Ask the nursing assistant to listen and confirm the order.  
D. Wait to document the order until after the medication is given.

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Telephone and verbal orders are easy to mishear. Reading the order back right away checks accuracy before anything is carried out. A quick write-down without verification can lead to the wrong drug or dose.

### Question 34 (Confidentiality / Informatics)
**Question:** Which action by the nurse violates confidentiality and safe electronic record practice?

A. Logging off the computer before leaving the workstation  
B. Discussing client care quietly with the oncoming nurse in report  
C. Sharing a login password with a coworker who forgot theirs  
D. Shredding printed notes with protected health information

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** The problem here is accountability. Each login must stay private so the chart clearly shows who entered what information. Sharing a password puts privacy and legal safety at risk, even if the coworker forgot theirs.

## Older Adult

### Question 35 (See First / Atypical Finding)
**Question:** The nurse is caring for four older adults. Which client should the nurse see first?

A. A client who was alert yesterday but is now suddenly restless, pulling at the gown, and unable to stay focused  
B. A client with mild forgetfulness who asks the same question again during lunch  
C. A client who says the hearing aid battery is weak  
D. A client who wants help finding reading glasses

**Correct answer:** A  
**Explanation:** This client has a sudden change in mental status, and that is the clue that raises priority. Restlessness and poor attention can signal delirium or another acute problem, which needs prompt assessment. Routine memory questions and sensory needs can wait.

### Question 36 (Atypical Infection / Functional Decline)
**Question:** An older adult has a temperature of 99°F (37.2°C), ate very little breakfast, and now needs much more help getting out of bed than yesterday. What is the nurse’s best interpretation?

A. These findings are expected age-related changes and can wait until tomorrow.  
B. Older adults often need a high fever before illness is suspected.  
C. New weakness, poor intake, and functional decline can be early signs of acute illness and need prompt assessment.  
D. The client is probably refusing activity because of mood only.

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** New weakness, poor intake, and needing more help than yesterday are not small changes in an older adult. They can be early signs of acute illness even without a high fever, so the nurse should take them seriously and assess promptly. A common trap is to dismiss these findings as normal aging.

## Client Safety

### Question 37 (Delegation / Safety)
**Question:** Which task is most appropriate for the nurse to delegate to UAP?

A. Ambulate a stable client to the bathroom with a gait belt after the nurse has already assessed the client’s tolerance  
B. Perform the first ambulation of a client who became dizzy when dangling at the bedside  
C. Teach a client how to use a walker on stairs  
D. Decide whether a confused client should be restrained

**Correct answer:** A  
**Explanation:** The nurse has already assessed this client, and the task is routine and predictable. That makes assisted ambulation with a gait belt appropriate for UAP. First-time ambulation after dizziness, teaching, and restraint decisions still require nursing judgment.

### Question 38 (Least Restrictive / First Action)
**Question:** An older adult with delirium keeps pulling at the oxygen tubing and says, “I need to get out of here.” What should the nurse do first?

A. Apply wrist restraints right away  
B. Ask another staff member to stay with the client, reorient the client, and check for pain, toileting, or other unmet needs  
C. Raise all four side rails and leave the room  
D. Request a PRN sedative order

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Pulling at tubing can happen when a client is scared, uncomfortable, hypoxic, or needs the toilet. The safest first step is to stay with the client, reorient, and look for unmet needs before moving to restraints or sedatives. Least restrictive care comes first.

### Question 39 (Restraint Reassessment)
**Question:** A client was placed in soft wrist restraints earlier for repeated attempts to remove a central line. The client is now calm and follows directions. What should the nurse do next?

A. Keep the restraints on until the next shift so the line stays protected  
B. Tighten the restraints to make sure the client cannot reach the tubing  
C. Remove the restraints as soon as it is safe and continue close observation with less restrictive measures  
D. Ask the provider for a PRN restraint order in case the behavior returns

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** The important clue is that the client is now calm and follows directions. Restraints should be removed as soon as it is safe, with close observation and safer alternatives in place. Keeping them on longer than needed turns a temporary measure into an unnecessary risk.

## Mobility

### Question 40 (Mobility Aid Judgment)
**Question:** A client has a weak left leg and uses a cane. Which statement by the client shows correct understanding about using the cane on stairs?

A. “I will go up the stairs with the cane and my weak leg first.”  
B. “I will go up with my strong leg first and go down with the cane and weak leg first.”  
C. “I will hold the cane on my weak side so it stays near the injured leg.”  
D. “I will use my walker on the stairs because it is more stable.”

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Stairs follow a simple rule: up with the strong leg, down with the cane and weak leg. That sequence gives the client the best balance and support when moving between steps. Holding the cane on the weak side is the tempting wrong answer.

### Question 41 (Crutch Safety)
**Question:** Which finding during crutch teaching requires immediate correction?

A. The top of each crutch is about 2 finger-widths below the axilla  
B. The client supports body weight through the hand grips  
C. The client leans body weight into the axillae while walking  
D. The elbows stay slightly flexed while holding the crutches

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** Leaning body weight into the axillae is the danger sign here. Crutches are meant to bear weight through the hands, because pressure in the axilla can damage nerves and tissue. The other findings show correct fit and technique.

### Question 42 (See First / Ambulation Safety)
**Question:** Before assisting a client who has been on bed rest to walk to the bathroom, which finding should the nurse address first?

A. The client says, “I feel lightheaded when I sit up.”  
B. The client asks for non-skid socks.  
C. The client asks where to place both hands on the walker.  
D. The client says the room feels a little cold.

**Correct answer:** A  
**Explanation:** Lightheadedness after bed rest points to possible orthostatic hypotension. That means the nurse should stop and assess before walking, because fall risk is now the priority. Non-skid socks are helpful, but they do not come before this warning sign.

## Communication

### Question 43 (Therapeutic Communication)
**Question:** A client waiting for biopsy results says, “I am scared the results will be bad.” Which response by the nurse is best?

A. “Do not think like that. I am sure everything will be fine.”  
B. “Everyone feels nervous. Try not to worry.”  
C. “Waiting can feel overwhelming. What worries you most right now?”  
D. “You should focus on staying positive.”

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** The key clue is fear, so the nurse should respond to the emotion first. This answer accepts the feeling and asks an open question, which helps the client share what is most concerning. Minimizing statements may sound comforting, but they often make clients feel unheard.

### Question 44 (Nontherapeutic Trap)
**Question:** Which nurse response is nontherapeutic?

A. “Tell me more about what happened before you stopped taking the medicine.”  
B. “If I were you, I would just take it and stop thinking about it.”  
C. “What concerns you most about taking it?”  
D. “Let us talk about what side effects you noticed.”

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** This response gives advice instead of helping the client think through the problem. Therapeutic communication supports the client’s own decision-making and keeps assessment going. Answers that begin with If I were you are often a test-day warning sign.

### Question 45 (Advocacy / Therapeutic Response)
**Question:** A client says, “I do not want this procedure until my daughter gets here. No one is listening to me.” What is the nurse’s best response?

A. “The provider already explained it, so you need to sign now.”  
B. “Why are you refusing something that will help you?”  
C. “You have the right to ask questions. I will pause this and help you speak with the provider before anything continues.”  
D. “Your daughter can read the paperwork later.”

**Correct answer:** C  
**Explanation:** The client is clearly saying to stop, and that matters. The nurse should protect the client’s right to pause, ask questions, and speak with the provider before anything continues. Pushing for a signature would ignore advocacy and informed consent.

## Documentation and Informatics

### Question 46 (Medication Error / Charting)
**Question:** After giving the wrong dose of a medication, which chart entry is best?

A. “Medication error made; incident report completed.”  
B. “Wrong dose of metoprolol given at 0900; heart rate and blood pressure assessed; provider notified; client monitored and new orders received.”  
C. “Another nurse distracted me, so the wrong dose was given.”  
D. “Risk management will investigate the event.”

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** After an error, the chart should show exactly what happened and how the client was assessed and managed. This entry includes the wrong dose, follow-up assessment, provider notification, and monitoring, which is what the next caregiver needs to know. Blame and incident report details do not belong in the medical record.

### Question 47 (Documentation Correction)
**Question:** The nurse performed wound care at 1000 but realizes at 1130 that it was not charted. What should the nurse do?

A. Enter the note as if it were written at 1000 so the record looks complete  
B. Make a late entry with the current time and document the care that was done at 1000 according to policy  
C. Skip the note because charting after the fact is not legal  
D. Ask a coworker to enter the note using their login

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** The key point is honesty in the record. A late entry with the current time follows legal documentation rules and still makes sure the wound care is charted. Backdating or using another person’s login creates a bigger problem.

### Question 48 (Incident Report / Follow-Up)
**Question:** During a transfer, a client starts to fall, but the nurse lowers the client into a chair and no injury is seen. What should the nurse do next?

A. Do nothing because the client never reached the floor  
B. Assess the client, document the facts and the client response, and follow facility policy for reporting the event  
C. Chart “near miss, no need to notify anyone”  
D. Document in the medical record that an incident report was filed

**Correct answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Even without visible injury, the event still needs follow-up. The nurse should assess the client, chart the facts and response, and follow facility policy for reporting. A common trap is assuming no floor contact means no action is needed.
