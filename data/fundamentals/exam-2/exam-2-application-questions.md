# Fundamentals Exam 2 Application Questions

These NCLEX-style practice questions focus on priorities, safety, and bedside clinical judgment for Fundamentals Exam 2. The wording is simple, but the nursing logic stays exam-ready.

## How to use this file

- Try to answer each question before looking at the correct answer.
- After each question, ask yourself why the other options are not the best choice.
- Focus on ABCs, safety, red flags, and what needs to be reported quickly.
- Redo missed questions until you can explain the idea in your own words.

## Skin Integrity, Inflammation, and Wound Healing

### Question 1 (Assessment)
**Question:** A postoperative client has a clean abdominal incision. On day 2, the edges are together, and the incision is red, warm, slightly swollen, and tender, with no pus. Which phase of wound healing is most likely?

A. Hemostasis  
B. Inflammatory phase  
C. Proliferative phase  
D. Maturation/remodeling phase

**Correct answer:** B. Inflammatory phase

**Explanation:** The big clue is day 2 with mild redness, warmth, swelling, and tenderness, while the edges stay together and there is no pus. That pattern fits the normal inflammatory phase of healing. On a test, do not confuse expected early inflammation with infection unless drainage becomes purulent or the symptoms keep getting worse.

### Question 2 (Priority)
**Question:** Which client is at highest risk for delayed wound healing?

A. A healthy 22-year-old with a small paper cut  
B. A 35-year-old with a sprained ankle and good nutrition  
C. A 78-year-old with diabetes, poor appetite, smoking history, and long-term prednisone use  
D. A 40-year-old with a simple clean incision and good circulation

**Correct answer:** C. A 78-year-old with diabetes, poor appetite, smoking history, and long-term prednisone use

**Explanation:** This choice stands out because the client has several healing problems at the same time: older age, diabetes, poor nutrition, smoking, and steroid use. Together, those clues mean slower tissue repair and higher infection risk. NCLEX questions like this usually reward the answer with the heaviest risk load, not the client with just one mild issue.

### Question 3 (First Action)
**Question:** A postoperative client coughs and the abdominal wound opens. Loops of bowel are visible. What is the nurse's best immediate action?

A. Gently place the organs back into the abdomen and cover with dry gauze  
B. Cover the organs with sterile saline-moistened dressings, stay with the client, and call for help  
C. Sit the client upright in high Fowler's position  
D. Ask the client to walk to bed to reduce tension on the incision

**Correct answer:** B. Cover the organs with sterile saline-moistened dressings, stay with the client, and call for help

**Explanation:** Seeing bowel means the wound has eviscerated, so the priority shifts to protecting the exposed organs right away. Sterile saline-moistened dressings help keep the tissue from drying out while you stay with the client and call for help. A common trap is wanting to push the organs back in, but that can cause more injury.

### Question 4 (Safety)
**Question:** A client twists an ankle 20 minutes ago. Which nursing action is most appropriate at this time?

A. Apply heat to increase blood flow  
B. Use rest, ice, compression, and elevation  
C. Massage the ankle deeply  
D. Keep the leg hanging down to check swelling

**Correct answer:** B. Use rest, ice, compression, and elevation

**Explanation:** The timing is the key clue here. In the first hours after a sprain, rest, ice, compression, and elevation help limit bleeding and swelling, so this is the safest response. Heat and deep massage may sound comforting, but too early they can make swelling worse.

## Pressure Injury and Wound Care

### Question 5 (Priority)
**Question:** An immobile older adult has incontinence, poor nutrition, and a low Braden score. Which nursing plan best helps prevent a pressure injury?

A. Massage red bony areas every shift  
B. Reposition at least every 2 hours, offload heels, keep skin clean and dry, and improve nutrition and hydration  
C. Keep the head of the bed above 45 degrees all day  
D. Use a donut cushion under the sacrum

**Correct answer:** B. Reposition at least every 2 hours, offload heels, keep skin clean and dry, and improve nutrition and hydration

**Explanation:** This client needs a full prevention plan, not one isolated action. Repositioning, heel offloading, moisture control, and better nutrition all target the main pressure injury risks in the stem. Test questions often tempt you with massage or donut cushions, but those can increase tissue damage instead of preventing it.

### Question 6 (Assessment)
**Question:** The nurse notes intact skin over the sacrum with nonblanchable redness. How should this be staged?

A. Stage 1 pressure injury  
B. Stage 2 pressure injury  
C. Stage 3 pressure injury  
D. Unstageable pressure injury

**Correct answer:** A. Stage 1 pressure injury

**Explanation:** Nonblanchable redness over intact skin is the defining clue. That means the skin is injured but not yet open, which makes this a Stage 1 pressure injury. If you saw a blister or partial-thickness skin loss, then Stage 2 would fit better.

### Question 7 (SATA)
**Question:** A nurse is cleansing a healing open wound. Which actions are appropriate? Select all that apply.

A. Use normal saline for routine cleansing.  
B. Clean from the center of the wound outward.  
C. Scrub healthy granulation tissue to remove pink tissue.  
D. Use enough pressure to remove loose debris without harming healthy tissue.  
E. Routinely use hydrogen peroxide to help the wound heal.

**Correct answer:** A, B, D

**Explanation:** The safe pattern is to protect healthy tissue while removing loose debris. Normal saline, cleaning from the center outward, and using gentle pressure all support healing. Scrubbing pink granulation tissue or routinely using hydrogen peroxide is a trap because both can damage new tissue.

### Question 8 (Application)
**Question:** A client has a deep wound with empty space and moderate drainage. Why does the nurse pack the wound as prescribed?

A. To let the surface close as fast as possible  
B. To help the wound heal from the inside outward and allow drainage to escape  
C. To keep the wound bed completely dry  
D. To increase pressure in the wound

**Correct answer:** B. To help the wound heal from the inside outward and allow drainage to escape

**Explanation:** A deep wound with empty space should heal from the bottom up. Packing supports that process and lets drainage escape instead of getting trapped under a surface that closes too soon. That is why letting the top seal over quickly is not the safe goal here.

## Infection Prevention and Control

### Question 9 (Isolation/PPE)
**Question:** A client has confirmed C. difficile diarrhea. Which nursing action is correct?

A. Use alcohol hand rub after care and wear only gloves  
B. Use contact precautions, wear gown and gloves, and wash hands with soap and water after care  
C. Use droplet precautions and a surgical mask  
D. Use an N95 respirator and a negative-pressure room

**Correct answer:** B. Use contact precautions, wear gown and gloves, and wash hands with soap and water after care

**Explanation:** C. difficile is the key clue because its spores call for contact precautions. Gown, gloves, and soap-and-water handwashing after care are the safest choices. Alcohol hand rub alone is the test-day trap, since it does not remove spores well.

### Question 10 (Isolation/PPE)
**Question:** Which isolation setup is best for a client with suspected pulmonary tuberculosis?

A. Private room with contact precautions  
B. Negative-pressure room and N95 respirator  
C. Surgical mask only when entering the room  
D. Protective isolation to protect the client from staff

**Correct answer:** B. Negative-pressure room and N95 respirator

**Explanation:** Suspected pulmonary tuberculosis means airborne spread. That points to a negative-pressure room and an N95 respirator for staff, which is the correct setup. A surgical mask alone does not give enough protection when entering the room.

### Question 11 (Priority)
**Question:** A client with suspected sepsis has orders for blood cultures and IV antibiotics. What should the nurse do first?

A. Start the antibiotic now and collect cultures later  
B. Collect the cultures first, then start the antibiotic promptly  
C. Wait for the white blood cell count to rise more  
D. Delay both until the client has a higher fever

**Correct answer:** B. Collect the cultures first, then start the antibiotic promptly

**Explanation:** The order of care matters here. Draw the blood cultures first so the organism can still be identified, then start the antibiotic without delay. The trap is waiting too long after cultures are collected, because sepsis treatment still needs to move quickly.

### Question 12 (Report-to-Provider)
**Question:** An older adult with pneumonia becomes newly confused. The respiratory rate is 30/min, blood pressure is 88/54 mm Hg, urine output is falling, and lactate is high. What is the nurse's priority interpretation?

A. Normal fatigue from fever  
B. Early sepsis with poor tissue perfusion  
C. Localized lung infection only  
D. Mild dehydration that can wait until morning

**Correct answer:** B. Early sepsis with poor tissue perfusion

**Explanation:** New confusion, fast breathing, low blood pressure, falling urine output, and a high lactate are not mild pneumonia findings. Together they show sepsis with poor tissue perfusion, which means the whole body is being affected. NCLEX likes this kind of red-flag cluster, so think early shock rather than just infection.

### Question 13 (Asepsis)
**Question:** Which task requires surgical asepsis (sterile technique)?

A. Helping a client brush teeth  
B. Inserting an indwelling urinary catheter  
C. Measuring blood pressure  
D. Assisting a client to a chair

**Correct answer:** B. Inserting an indwelling urinary catheter

**Explanation:** The clue is that the catheter enters a normally sterile body site. That makes sterile technique necessary to lower the chance of introducing bacteria. The other tasks involve routine clean care, not surgical asepsis.

## Respiratory Function

### Question 14 (Priority)
**Question:** A postoperative client has an oxygen saturation of 87% on room air, is anxious, and is breathing 28 times/min. What should the nurse do first?

A. Place the client in high Fowler's position, assess airway and breathing, and apply oxygen as ordered  
B. Give PRN pain medicine and recheck in 30 minutes  
C. Encourage the client to drink water  
D. Collect a sputum sample

**Correct answer:** A. Place the client in high Fowler's position, assess airway and breathing, and apply oxygen as ordered

**Explanation:** An oxygen saturation of 87%, anxiety, and rapid breathing tell you airway and breathing need attention first. Sitting the client up, assessing, and applying oxygen follow ABC priority and support gas exchange right away. Pain medicine or specimen collection can come later, once the client is more stable.

### Question 15 (Airway Safety)
**Question:** A client vomits and has a decreased level of consciousness. What is the nurse's priority action?

A. Give oral fluids to thin secretions  
B. Turn the client to the side and suction as needed  
C. Ask the client to use an incentive spirometer  
D. Place the client flat on the back

**Correct answer:** B. Turn the client to the side and suction as needed

**Explanation:** Vomiting plus decreased consciousness creates a high aspiration risk. Turning the client to the side and suctioning as needed protects the airway first, which is the safest move. Giving fluids or leaving the client flat would make the problem more dangerous.

### Question 16 (Technique)
**Question:** Which action shows correct technique for sterile airway suctioning?

A. Apply suction while inserting the catheter  
B. Insert the catheter without suction, then apply suction only while withdrawing for no more than 10 to 15 seconds  
C. Use clean gloves because the airway is already contaminated  
D. Suction continuously until all secretions are gone

**Correct answer:** B. Insert the catheter without suction, then apply suction only while withdrawing for no more than 10 to 15 seconds

**Explanation:** Safe suctioning starts with inserting the catheter without suction. Suction is applied only while withdrawing and only for a short time, because the airway can lose oxygen quickly and become irritated. A common mistake is suctioning on insertion or too long, which increases trauma and hypoxia.

### Question 17 (Compare/Contrast)
**Question:** Which oxygen device is best when the nurse must deliver a very exact oxygen concentration?

A. Nasal cannula  
B. Simple face mask  
C. Venturi mask  
D. Non-rebreather mask

**Correct answer:** C. Venturi mask

**Explanation:** The word exact is doing the work in this question. A Venturi mask is used when the nurse needs very precise oxygen control. A nonrebreather gives high oxygen, but it is not the best choice when exact concentration is the goal.

### Question 18 (Safety)
**Question:** The nurse sees continuous bubbling in the water-seal chamber of a chest tube drainage system. What is the best interpretation?

A. This always means the lung is fully expanded  
B. This usually suggests an air leak that should be assessed  
C. The chest tube should be clamped immediately  
D. The nurse should strip the tubing forcefully

**Correct answer:** B. This usually suggests an air leak that should be assessed

**Explanation:** Continuous bubbling in the water-seal chamber usually means air is leaking somewhere in the system. That clue tells the nurse to assess the client and the tubing rather than act blindly. Clamping right away is a trap because it can trap air and worsen the situation.

## Cardiac Function

### Question 19 (Priority)
**Question:** A client reports chest pressure with nausea and diaphoresis while walking in the hallway. What should the nurse do first?

A. Stop the activity, assess the client, and call for help while preparing for oxygen and cardiac evaluation  
B. Tell the client to finish the walk slowly  
C. Offer a snack because the symptoms may be hunger  
D. Place the client flat and leave to find the provider

**Correct answer:** A. Stop the activity, assess the client, and call for help while preparing for oxygen and cardiac evaluation

**Explanation:** Chest pressure with nausea and diaphoresis should make you think cardiac ischemia first. The safest move is to stop the activity, stay with the client, assess, and get help started. Test questions often include a minimizing option like hunger, but these symptoms are too serious to brush off.

### Question 20 (Compare/Contrast)
**Question:** Which finding is most consistent with left-sided heart failure?

A. Jugular venous distention and ankle edema only  
B. Crackles, orthopnea, and shortness of breath  
C. Warm flushed skin and bounding pulse  
D. Increased appetite and weight loss

**Correct answer:** B. Crackles, orthopnea, and shortness of breath

**Explanation:** Crackles, orthopnea, and shortness of breath point to fluid backing up into the lungs. That is the classic picture of left-sided heart failure. Jugular venous distention and dependent edema are tempting, but those fit right-sided failure better.

### Question 21 (Assessment)
**Question:** Which finding best suggests that a dysrhythmia is lowering cardiac output?

A. Warm skin and urine output of 60 mL/hr  
B. Heart rate of 58/min in a sleeping trained athlete  
C. Cool clammy skin and urine output of 15 mL/hr  
D. Strong peripheral pulses and normal capillary refill

**Correct answer:** C. Cool clammy skin and urine output of 15 mL/hr

**Explanation:** Cool clammy skin and urine output of 15 mL/hr are strong perfusion clues. They show the heart is not pumping enough blood to the skin and kidneys, so cardiac output is down. Warm skin and normal urine output would be much more reassuring.

### Question 22 (Emergency)
**Question:** A client is unresponsive, has no pulse, and the monitor shows ventricular fibrillation. Which action is the priority?

A. Listen to heart sounds for 2 full minutes  
B. Start CPR, call for help or activate the emergency response system, and defibrillate as soon as possible  
C. Give oral fluids  
D. Place the client in Trendelenburg position

**Correct answer:** B. Start CPR, call for help or activate the emergency response system, and defibrillate as soon as possible

**Explanation:** Once the client is unresponsive, pulseless, and in ventricular fibrillation, this is a cardiac arrest question. CPR and rapid defibrillation give the best chance of restoring circulation, so they come first. Do not lose time on low-value actions when there is no effective heartbeat.

## Pain Management

### Question 23 (Assessment Tool)
**Question:** A school-age child after surgery cannot rate pain with numbers. Which pain scale is the best choice?

A. Braden Scale  
B. Faces scale  
C. Passero Opioid-Induced Sedation Scale  
D. Glasgow Coma Scale

**Correct answer:** B. Faces scale

**Explanation:** The main clue is that the child cannot use a number scale. A faces scale lets the child communicate pain in a simple way, so it is the best fit here. The other tools measure different problems, which is a common NCLEX mismatch trap.

### Question 24 (Patient Teaching)
**Question:** Which statement by a postoperative client shows correct understanding of around-the-clock (ATC) and PRN pain medicine?

A. "I should wait until my pain is severe before asking for medicine."  
B. "Scheduled medicine can keep pain from building up, and PRN medicine can help with breakthrough pain."  
C. "PRN medicine is always better than scheduled medicine for ongoing pain."  
D. "If I sleep through a dose, pain control no longer matters."

**Correct answer:** B. "Scheduled medicine can keep pain from building up, and PRN medicine can help with breakthrough pain."

**Explanation:** Good pain teaching focuses on staying ahead of pain, not chasing it after it becomes severe. Scheduled medicine keeps a steady base level, and PRN medicine covers breakthrough pain when it appears. Waiting until pain is intense is a common mistake because pain is harder to control by then.

### Question 25 (Safety)
**Question:** A client received IV opioid medicine 20 minutes ago and is now very sleepy with a respiratory rate of 8/min. What is the nurse's priority action?

A. Give the next opioid dose later  
B. Hold the opioid, support airway and breathing, and notify the provider immediately  
C. Ask the client to rate the pain again in one hour  
D. Put the client flat and dim the lights

**Correct answer:** B. Hold the opioid, support airway and breathing, and notify the provider immediately

**Explanation:** A respiratory rate of 8/min after IV opioid medication is the red flag. That means breathing is being depressed, so the nurse must hold the opioid, support airway and breathing, and notify the provider right away. Reassessing later would delay care when the client is already unstable.

### Question 26 (Application)
**Question:** An abdominal surgery client says, "It hurts too much to cough." Which nursing action is best?

A. Teach the client to splint the incision and give pain medicine before coughing and deep breathing  
B. Tell the client not to cough until the incision fully heals  
C. Skip pain medicine to avoid dependence  
D. Keep the client flat in bed so the wound does not move

**Correct answer:** A. Teach the client to splint the incision and give pain medicine before coughing and deep breathing

**Explanation:** The key issue is not just pain; it is what the pain keeps the client from doing. Splinting the incision and giving pain medicine before coughing make deep breathing and airway clearance more effective. Telling the client to avoid coughing may feel protective, but it raises the risk for atelectasis and pneumonia.

## Fluids and Electrolytes

### Question 27 (Assessment)
**Question:** Which finding most strongly suggests fluid volume deficit?

A. Crackles, edema, and weight gain  
B. Dry mucous membranes, tachycardia, and dark concentrated urine  
C. Bounding pulse and jugular venous distention  
D. High blood pressure and moist skin

**Correct answer:** B. Dry mucous membranes, tachycardia, and dark concentrated urine

**Explanation:** Dry mucous membranes, tachycardia, and dark concentrated urine all point in the same direction: not enough circulating fluid. Those findings show dehydration and how the body tries to conserve water. Crackles, edema, and neck vein distention would push you toward fluid excess instead.

### Question 28 (Priority)
**Question:** A client has received a large amount of IV fluid. Now the client has crackles, shortness of breath, rising blood pressure, and neck vein distention. What should the nurse do first?

A. Slow or stop the infusion as appropriate, assess the client, and notify the provider  
B. Encourage the client to drink more fluids  
C. Raise the foot of the bed and keep the client flat  
D. Give foods high in potassium

**Correct answer:** A. Slow or stop the infusion as appropriate, assess the client, and notify the provider

**Explanation:** The stem gives a clear overload picture: crackles, shortness of breath, higher blood pressure, and neck vein distention after lots of IV fluid. The safest first move is to reduce or stop the fluid source, assess the client, and notify the provider. Keeping the client flat is a trap because it can make breathing even harder.

### Question 29 (SATA)
**Question:** Which findings are most consistent with hypocalcemia? Select all that apply.

A. Tingling around the mouth  
B. Carpopedal spasm with blood pressure cuff inflation  
C. Constipation  
D. Facial twitching when the facial nerve is tapped  
E. Decreased reflexes

**Correct answer:** A, B, D

**Explanation:** Low calcium makes nerves and muscles more excitable. That is why tingling, Trousseau's sign, and Chvostek's sign fit this question. Constipation and decreased reflexes can sound plausible, but they are more consistent with hypercalcemia.

### Question 30 (Evaluation)
**Question:** What is the most sensitive bedside indicator of fluid gain or loss over time?

A. Daily weight  
B. Skin color  
C. Appetite  
D. Temperature

**Correct answer:** A. Daily weight

**Explanation:** For fluid balance questions, daily weight is the best bedside trend. It picks up gain or loss more accurately than appetite, skin color, or temperature. A small weight change can reflect a meaningful fluid shift, which is why NCLEX favors this answer.

## Intravenous Therapy

### Question 31 (Access Selection)
**Question:** Which client is the best candidate for a central line such as a PICC rather than a short peripheral IV?

A. A client who needs one dose of IV antibiotic in the emergency department  
B. A client who needs total parenteral nutrition and irritating IV medication for several weeks  
C. A client who can take fluids by mouth  
D. A client with a stable, healed incision and no IV medication orders

**Correct answer:** B. A client who needs total parenteral nutrition and irritating IV medication for several weeks

**Explanation:** Several weeks of therapy, total parenteral nutrition, and irritating medication are the big clues here. Those needs make a central line the safer and more practical choice than a short peripheral IV. A brief, simple treatment is the kind of situation where peripheral access works better.

### Question 32 (Fluid Selection)
**Question:** A client has fluid volume deficit from vomiting and needs routine volume replacement. Which IV fluid type is generally the best initial choice?

A. Hypotonic fluid  
B. Hypertonic fluid  
C. Isotonic fluid  
D. No IV fluid should be used

**Correct answer:** C. Isotonic fluid

**Explanation:** Vomiting with routine volume loss calls for replacing fluid in the bloodstream first. Isotonic fluids do that without pulling large amounts of water into or out of cells, so they are the usual starting choice. Hypotonic or hypertonic fluids are more specific tools and not the best general answer here.

### Question 33 (Complication Recognition)
**Question:** A peripheral IV site is cool, pale, swollen, and uncomfortable. What is the nurse's best first action?

A. Continue the infusion and apply heat  
B. Stop the infusion because infiltration is likely  
C. Document the finding and reassess at the end of the shift  
D. Massage the site to improve flow

**Correct answer:** B. Stop the infusion because infiltration is likely

**Explanation:** A cool, pale, swollen IV site is a classic infiltration pattern. That means fluid is leaving the vein and entering the tissue, so the infusion should be stopped first to prevent more damage. Heat, massage, or waiting until later would let the problem get worse.

### Question 34 (Safety)
**Question:** A vesicant medication is running through a peripheral IV. The client reports burning at the site, and the tissue begins to swell. What complication should the nurse suspect?

A. Phlebitis  
B. Extravasation  
C. Air embolism  
D. Fluid overload

**Correct answer:** B. Extravasation

**Explanation:** The word vesicant matters here. When a tissue-damaging drug leaks out and the site burns and swells, think extravasation, not a minor IV irritation. This is the more serious complication because the tissue itself can be injured.

### Question 35 (Emergency)
**Question:** After a central line tubing disconnects, a client suddenly develops chest pain and shortness of breath. Which action is most appropriate while calling for urgent help?

A. Place the client on the left side with the head lower than the feet if tolerated  
B. Apply a warm compress to the insertion site  
C. Raise the head of bed to high Fowler's and observe  
D. Flush the line with saline

**Correct answer:** A. Place the client on the left side with the head lower than the feet if tolerated

**Explanation:** The sudden chest pain and shortness of breath right after a central line disconnect are the giveaway. That pattern fits air embolism, so left-side, head-down positioning if tolerated is the best immediate response while help is called. Warm compresses or flushing the line do nothing to fix the real danger.

## Additional Pressure Injury and Wound Care

### Question 36 (Complication Recognition)
**Question:** A postoperative leg incision was mildly red yesterday. Today the nurse finds increasing pain, warmth, firm induration, foul-smelling purulent drainage, and a temperature of 38.6°C (101.5°F). What is the nurse's best action?

A. Document expected inflammatory findings and reassess tomorrow  
B. Massage the area to improve circulation  
C. Assess the wound carefully and notify the provider because infection is likely  
D. Apply heat and encourage ambulation only

**Correct answer:** C. Assess the wound carefully and notify the provider because infection is likely

**Explanation:** Today's findings are much worse than simple postoperative redness. Increasing pain, firm induration, foul-smelling purulent drainage, and fever point to wound infection and need prompt assessment and reporting. The trap is calling these normal inflammation when the pattern has clearly shifted.

### Question 37 (Safety)
**Question:** An older adult has a dry, hard, black heel eschar with no drainage, redness, warmth, or odor. Which nursing action is best?

A. Soften the eschar with moist dressings so it can be removed  
B. Leave the eschar intact, offload the heel, and monitor for infection  
C. Massage the heel every shift to increase blood flow  
D. Scrub the heel with normal saline until pink tissue appears

**Correct answer:** B. Leave the eschar intact, offload the heel, and monitor for infection

**Explanation:** A dry, hard, intact heel eschar with no drainage or redness is usually protected, not opened. The best care is to offload the heel and watch closely for infection or other changes. Softening, scrubbing, or massaging can expose tissue and make the injury worse.

### Question 38 (Device-Related Risk)
**Question:** During skin assessment, the nurse sees nonblanchable redness behind a client's ears where oxygen tubing rests. What is the nurse's priority action?

A. Massage the red areas until the color returns  
B. Remove oxygen for several hours and leave the client on room air  
C. Reposition or pad the tubing, protect the skin, and reassess the area  
D. Ignore the finding because oxygen tubing does not cause pressure injuries

**Correct answer:** C. Reposition or pad the tubing, protect the skin, and reassess the area

**Explanation:** Nonblanchable redness under a device means pressure damage may already be starting. Repositioning or padding the tubing removes the cause while protecting the skin and keeping oxygen therapy safe. Massage is a trap here, and stopping needed oxygen without another plan is not safe.

## Additional Infection Prevention and Control

### Question 39 (Protective Isolation)
**Question:** A client receiving chemotherapy has severe neutropenia. Which action by the nurse requires correction?

A. Asking visitors to clean their hands before entering  
B. Bringing a fresh potted plant into the room  
C. Monitoring the client for fever  
D. Teaching the client to avoid sick visitors

**Correct answer:** B. Bringing a fresh potted plant into the room

**Explanation:** Severe neutropenia means the client needs protection from everyday sources of germs. A fresh potted plant can carry bacteria and fungi, so that action needs correction. Hand hygiene, fever monitoring, and avoiding sick visitors all support the same safety goal.

### Question 40 (Droplet Precautions)
**Question:** A client with influenza must leave the room for a chest x-ray. Which nursing action is correct?

A. Place a surgical mask on the client during transport  
B. Use a negative-pressure room during transport  
C. Tell the client that no mask is needed outside the room  
D. Use only contact precautions for the transport team

**Correct answer:** A. Place a surgical mask on the client during transport

**Explanation:** Because influenza spreads by droplets, the client should wear a surgical mask when leaving the room. That lowers the chance of spreading infection during transport. NCLEX often tests who wears the mask, so focus on the source control clue here.

## Additional Respiratory Function

### Question 41 (Oxygen Device Choice)
**Question:** A postoperative client suddenly becomes very short of breath, and the oxygen saturation falls to 82% while on 6 L/min by nasal cannula. Which oxygen device is best to deliver a high oxygen concentration quickly while the nurse gets more help?

A. Venturi mask  
B. Nonrebreather mask  
C. Simple face mask at 4 L/min  
D. Nasal cannula at 2 L/min

**Correct answer:** B. Nonrebreather mask

**Explanation:** This client is suddenly very short of breath with an oxygen saturation of 82% even on 6 L by nasal cannula. That means the nurse needs a device that can deliver a high oxygen concentration quickly, which is the nonrebreather. A Venturi mask is useful when precision matters more than speed and maximum support.

### Question 42 (Oxygen Safety)
**Question:** The provider writes an order for oxygen by simple face mask at 4 L/min. What should the nurse do?

A. Start the oxygen because any flow rate works with a simple mask  
B. Clarify the order because simple masks generally need at least about 5 L/min  
C. Change the order to room air without notifying anyone  
D. Place gauze inside the mask to absorb carbon dioxide

**Correct answer:** B. Clarify the order because simple masks generally need at least about 5 L/min

**Explanation:** The unsafe part of the order is the 4 L/min flow. A simple face mask generally needs at least about 5 L/min so carbon dioxide does not build up inside the mask. When an order looks unsafe, clarifying it is better than carrying it out as written.

### Question 43 (Aspiration Risk)
**Question:** A client one day after a stroke coughs repeatedly and has a wet, gurgly voice after taking a sip of water. What is the nurse's best action?

A. Encourage the client to take a few more sips and try again  
B. Keep the client NPO and request a swallowing evaluation  
C. Give pills in applesauce right away  
D. Lower the head of the bed so the client can rest

**Correct answer:** B. Keep the client NPO and request a swallowing evaluation

**Explanation:** Repeated coughing and a wet, gurgly voice after swallowing are classic aspiration clues. The safest step is to stop oral intake, keep the client NPO, and get a swallowing evaluation before giving anything else by mouth. Trying a few more sips is a common trap because it can send more fluid into the airway.

### Question 44 (Airway Priority)
**Question:** The nurse enters a room and finds a client receiving tube feeding lying flat, coughing, and making gurgling sounds. What should the nurse do first?

A. Increase the feeding rate to finish the bag sooner  
B. Turn off the feeding, raise the head of the bed, and suction as needed  
C. Flush the tube with water before assessing the client  
D. Leave the room to find the provider before doing anything else

**Correct answer:** B. Turn off the feeding, raise the head of the bed, and suction as needed

**Explanation:** Lying flat with tube feeding running and gurgling sounds raises immediate concern for aspiration. Turning off the feeding, raising the head of the bed, and suctioning as needed protects the airway first. Leaving the room before doing those steps would delay care at the worst time.

### Question 45 (Chest Tube Emergency)
**Question:** A client's chest tube becomes disconnected from the drainage system. What action is most appropriate?

A. Clamp the chest tube and leave it clamped until the provider arrives  
B. Place the end of the chest tube in sterile water to create a temporary water seal and call for help  
C. Milk the tubing forcefully toward the client  
D. Remove the chest tube completely

**Correct answer:** B. Place the end of the chest tube in sterile water to create a temporary water seal and call for help

**Explanation:** The priority is to keep air from being pulled into the pleural space. Placing the tube end in sterile water creates a temporary water seal until the system can be fixed and help arrives. Leaving the tube clamped is a trap because pressure can build and worsen the pneumothorax.

## Additional Cardiac Function

### Question 46 (Compare/Contrast)
**Question:** Which finding is most consistent with right-sided heart failure?

A. Crackles and orthopnea  
B. Jugular venous distention, hepatomegaly, and dependent edema  
C. Pink frothy sputum  
D. Shortness of breath that improves when sitting upright only

**Correct answer:** B. Jugular venous distention, hepatomegaly, and dependent edema

**Explanation:** Jugular venous distention, hepatomegaly, and dependent edema all show blood backing up on the venous side of the body. That pattern fits right-sided heart failure best. Crackles and orthopnea may tempt you, but those are more typical of left-sided failure.

## Additional Pain Management

### Question 47 (Reassessment)
**Question:** A nurse gives IV morphine for severe postoperative pain. When should the nurse reassess the client's pain and sedation first?

A. In about 15 to 30 minutes  
B. At the end of the shift  
C. In about 2 hours  
D. Only if the client asks for more medicine

**Correct answer:** A. In about 15 to 30 minutes

**Explanation:** IV morphine works quickly, so the first reassessment needs to happen soon. Checking in about 15 to 30 minutes lets the nurse judge both pain relief and early sedation or respiratory problems. Waiting hours or until the end of the shift would miss important changes.

### Question 48 (Delegation)
**Question:** Which task is appropriate for the RN to delegate to unlicensed assistive personnel (UAP) for a client who received an opioid 20 minutes ago?

A. Reassess the pain level and decide whether the medicine worked  
B. Assess the client for opioid oversedation  
C. Obtain vital signs and report if respirations are low or the client is hard to arouse  
D. Teach the client how to use the pain scale

**Correct answer:** C. Obtain vital signs and report if respirations are low or the client is hard to arouse

**Explanation:** This is a delegation question, so separate data collection from nursing judgment. UAP can obtain vital signs and report low respirations or trouble waking the client, but the RN must interpret those findings and decide what they mean. Teaching and reassessment stay with the nurse.

## Additional Fluids and Electrolytes

### Question 49 (Report-to-Provider)
**Question:** A client has a sodium level of 118 mEq/L. Which finding should the nurse report immediately?

A. Mild thirst  
B. New confusion and a seizure  
C. Dry skin on the arms  
D. Slight ankle swelling

**Correct answer:** B. New confusion and a seizure

**Explanation:** A sodium level of 118 mEq/L is already severe, and new confusion with seizure activity shows the brain is being affected. That makes this an emergency report, not a routine update. Mild thirst or dry skin may matter later, but they are not the immediate threat here.

### Question 50 (Emergency)
**Question:** A client with kidney failure has a potassium level of 6.4 mEq/L. Which finding is most urgent?

A. Peaked T waves and muscle weakness  
B. Dry mouth  
C. Constipation only  
D. Low urine specific gravity

**Correct answer:** A. Peaked T waves and muscle weakness

**Explanation:** With potassium at 6.4 mEq/L, the heart is the main concern. Peaked T waves show hyperkalemia is already affecting cardiac conduction, which makes this the most urgent finding. Muscle weakness fits too, but the ECG change is what raises the priority.

## Additional Intravenous Therapy

### Question 51 (Central Line Safety)
**Question:** The nurse inspects a central line dressing and finds that it is damp and peeling at the edges. What should the nurse do?

A. Reinforce the old dressing with tape and wait until the next routine change  
B. Change the dressing using sterile technique as soon as possible  
C. Remove the central line immediately without further assessment  
D. Leave it alone if the client has no fever

**Correct answer:** B. Change the dressing using sterile technique as soon as possible

**Explanation:** A central line dressing should stay clean, dry, and sealed. When it is damp and peeling, the infection barrier is no longer reliable, so it should be changed with sterile technique as soon as possible. Reinforcing the old dressing may look easier, but it is not the safest answer.

### Question 52 (Infection Prevention)
**Question:** Which action is most important each time the nurse accesses a central line to give medication?

A. Scrub the hub or needleless connector and let it dry before access  
B. Skip hand hygiene if clean gloves are worn  
C. Touch the cleaned connector with bare fingers to steady it  
D. Leave the line open to air for a few seconds before connecting the tubing

**Correct answer:** A. Scrub the hub or needleless connector and let it dry before access

**Explanation:** Each access point is a chance for germs to enter the bloodstream. Scrubbing the hub and letting it dry lowers that risk, so it is the most important routine step. Touching the cleaned connector or leaving the line open to air breaks sterile technique and creates an avoidable infection trap.
