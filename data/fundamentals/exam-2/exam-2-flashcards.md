# Exam 2 Flashcards

These are content-only flashcards grouped by the same sections as your outline and strengthened with research-informed nursing exam angles such as priority actions, safety traps, compare-and-contrast cues, and early recognition of complications.

## How to use this deck

- **Front** = the question side of the flashcard.
- **Back** = the answer side.
- **Why it matters** = a short memory or exam tip.

## Skin Integrity, Inflammation, and Wound Healing

### Card 1

**Front:** What are the main functions of the integumentary system?

**Back:**
- Protects the body from injury, germs, and fluid loss.
- Helps regulate body temperature.
- Provides sensation such as pain, touch, and temperature.
- Helps make vitamin D.
- Excretes a small amount of waste through sweat.

**Why it matters:** This is a foundation card because many skin, wound, and infection questions start with normal skin function.

### Card 2

**Front:** What factors commonly affect integumentary function and skin integrity?

**Back:**
- Age and fragile skin
- Poor nutrition or dehydration
- Poor circulation or low oxygen delivery
- Pressure, friction, and shearing
- Moisture or incontinence
- Immobility
- Chronic illness such as diabetes
- Medications such as corticosteroids

**Why it matters:** The exam often asks which patients are at higher risk for skin breakdown or delayed healing.

### Card 3

**Front:** What are the normal phases of wound healing?

**Back:**
- Hemostasis: bleeding stops and a clot forms.
- Inflammation: white blood cells clean the wound.
- Proliferation: granulation tissue and collagen fill the wound.
- Maturation or remodeling: scar strengthens over time.

**Why it matters:** Knowing the order of healing helps you judge whether a wound is progressing normally.

### Card 4

**Front:** How do nutrition and circulation or oxygen delivery affect wound healing?

**Back:**
- Protein, calories, vitamin C, zinc, and fluids are needed to build new tissue.
- Good circulation brings oxygen, nutrients, and immune cells to the wound.
- Poor perfusion or low oxygen slows collagen formation and repair.

**Why it matters:** Poor nutrition and poor blood flow are classic causes of delayed wound healing.

### Card 5

**Front:** How do impaired immune responses, obesity, age, and corticosteroids delay wound healing?

**Back:**
- Impaired immune response raises infection risk and weakens normal inflammation.
- Obesity reduces blood flow to fatty tissue and increases wound stress.
- Older age slows cell growth and skin repair.
- Corticosteroids suppress inflammation and collagen formation.

**Why it matters:** This is high-yield because these are common reasons healing is delayed even when wound care is done correctly.

### Card 6

**Front:** How do hemorrhage, infection, dehiscence, and evisceration delay wound healing?

**Back:**
- Hemorrhage causes poor perfusion and may separate tissue planes.
- Infection prolongs inflammation and damages healthy tissue.
- Dehiscence means the wound edges separate.
- Evisceration means organs protrude through the wound, making the situation an emergency.

**Why it matters:** These are classic wound-healing complications that show up in nursing priority questions.

### Card 7

**Front:** How can dehiscence and evisceration be prevented, and what should the nurse do if evisceration occurs?

**Back:**
- Prevent with splinting, good pain control, careful turning, and avoiding excessive strain on the incision.
- Support coughing with a pillow and reduce abdominal pressure when possible.
- If evisceration occurs, cover the organs with sterile saline-moistened dressings.
- Position the patient with knees bent if appropriate, stay with the patient, and notify the provider immediately.
- Do not try to push organs back inside.

**Why it matters:** The exam may test both prevention and the immediate emergency response.

### Card 8

**Front:** What happens during the inflammatory response?

**Back:**
- Blood vessels dilate.
- Capillary permeability increases.
- White blood cells move into the area.
- The area becomes red, warm, swollen, and painful.

**Why it matters:** These normal changes explain the local signs of inflammation and help you spot abnormal infection.

### Card 9

**Front:** How do localized and systemic inflammation differ?

**Back:**
- Localized inflammation stays in one area and causes redness, warmth, swelling, and pain there.
- Systemic inflammation affects the whole body and may cause fever, malaise, increased white blood cells, or fatigue.

**Why it matters:** The difference helps you tell whether the body is reacting in one spot or more broadly.

### Card 10

**Front:** How do acute and chronic inflammation differ?

**Back:**
- Acute inflammation starts quickly and is usually short-term.
- Chronic inflammation lasts a long time and may cause ongoing tissue damage and repair at the same time.

**Why it matters:** Questions often ask you to match the time course and body response to acute versus chronic inflammation.

### Card 11

**Front:** What does RICE stand for, and why is it used in inflammation and pain management?

**Back:**
- Rest
- Ice
- Compression
- Elevation
- It helps reduce swelling, pain, bleeding, and further injury after soft tissue trauma.

**Why it matters:** RICE is a simple but very common nursing care concept for minor injury and inflammation.

### Card 12

**Front:** When are heat and cold therapies contraindicated, and what medication classes commonly treat inflammation?

**Back:**
- Avoid heat with active bleeding, acute injury in the early phase, poor sensation, or poor circulation.
- Avoid cold with poor circulation, neuropathy, cold hypersensitivity, or skin that cannot be safely monitored.
- Corticosteroids strongly suppress inflammation but can delay healing and increase infection risk.
- Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) reduce pain and swelling but can cause stomach irritation, bleeding, or kidney problems.

**Why it matters:** The exam may test both safe therapy choices and the differences between corticosteroids and NSAIDs.


### Card 13 (Priority)

**Front:** A postoperative client coughs and the abdominal wound opens with organs visible. What is the nurse's priority response?

**Back:**
- Stay with the client and call for help immediately.
- Cover the organs with sterile saline-moistened dressings.
- Reduce strain on the wound and do not push the organs back inside.
- Prepare for emergency management.

**Why it matters:** Nursing exam questions heavily test immediate protection of exposed tissue and rapid escalation.

### Card 14 (Risk)

**Front:** Which client is at highest risk for delayed wound healing?

**Back:**
- The client with poor nutrition, diabetes, poor circulation, advanced age, and corticosteroid use has the highest risk.
- Combined risk factors matter more than a single mild risk factor.

**Why it matters:** Exam questions often hide the best answer inside a patient with several healing barriers at once.

### Card 15 (Compare)

**Front:** In an acute soft tissue injury, when is cold preferred and when might heat be used later?

**Back:**
- Cold is preferred early to reduce swelling, bleeding, and pain.
- Heat may be used later for stiffness or muscle tension if there is no active bleeding or acute inflammation.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic safety choice question on nursing exams.

## Pressure Injury and Wound Care

### Card 1

**Front:** How can wounds be described by type?

**Back:**
- Open versus closed
- Acute versus chronic
- Intentional versus unintentional
- Clean, clean-contaminated, contaminated, or dirty

**Why it matters:** This helps you organize wound questions before deciding on healing risk and care.

### Card 2

**Front:** How do pressure injuries develop, and what does blanching mean?

**Back:**
- Pressure injuries develop when prolonged pressure, friction, or shearing reduces blood flow to tissue.
- Blanching means the skin turns pale when pressed and then returns to its usual color.
- Nonblanchable redness suggests tissue damage and is a key sign of a Stage 1 pressure injury.

**Why it matters:** Blanching versus nonblanching redness is a classic exam distinction.

### Card 3

**Front:** How is the Braden Scale interpreted?

**Back:**
- It measures pressure injury risk.
- It looks at sensory perception, moisture, activity, mobility, nutrition, and friction or shear.
- Lower score means higher risk.

**Why it matters:** This is one of the most testable pressure injury tools in basic nursing exams.

### Card 4

**Front:** What nursing interventions help prevent pressure injuries?

**Back:**
- Reposition regularly.
- Offload heels and bony areas.
- Keep skin clean and dry.
- Reduce friction and shearing.
- Encourage mobility when possible.
- Use support surfaces and maintain good nutrition and hydration.

**Why it matters:** Prevention questions often ask you to choose the nursing action that best protects tissue perfusion.

### Card 5

**Front:** How do Stage 1 through Stage 4 pressure injuries differ?

**Back:**
- Stage 1: intact skin with nonblanchable redness.
- Stage 2: partial-thickness skin loss or blister.
- Stage 3: full-thickness skin loss into subcutaneous tissue.
- Stage 4: full-thickness loss with exposed muscle, tendon, or bone.

**Why it matters:** Knowing the stage is essential for wound description and planning care.

### Card 6

**Front:** What are unstageable pressure injuries and deep tissue pressure injuries, and where are undermining and tunneling most common?

**Back:**
- Unstageable means the base is covered by slough or eschar, so the true depth cannot be seen.
- Deep tissue pressure injury is a purple or maroon area of damaged tissue under intact or nonintact skin.
- Undermining and tunneling are most common in Stage 3 and Stage 4 injuries.

**Why it matters:** This helps you distinguish deeper injury patterns from the numbered stages.

### Card 7

**Front:** What are the general principles of basic wound cleansing?

**Back:**
- Use normal saline for routine cleansing unless otherwise ordered.
- Clean from least contaminated to most contaminated area.
- Be gentle so healthy tissue is not damaged.
- Do not scrub fragile granulation tissue.

**Why it matters:** Safe cleansing protects healthy tissue while reducing contamination.

### Card 8

**Front:** How do cytotoxic and non-cytotoxic wound cleansing agents differ?

**Back:**
- Non-cytotoxic agents, such as normal saline, do not damage healthy healing cells.
- Cytotoxic agents, such as hydrogen peroxide or povidone-iodine, can kill bacteria but also harm healing tissue.

**Why it matters:** Exam questions often ask which wound cleanser is safest for routine healing tissue.

### Card 9

**Front:** What is debridement, and how do the main methods differ?

**Back:**
- Debridement removes dead tissue so healing can progress.
- Mechanical debridement uses physical force, such as irrigation or wet-to-dry dressings.
- Autolytic debridement uses the body’s own enzymes under a moist dressing.
- Chemical or enzymatic debridement uses topical medication.
- Sharp or surgical debridement quickly cuts away dead tissue.

**Why it matters:** The exam may ask why debridement is needed and which method is fastest or gentlest.

### Card 10

**Front:** What do approximated, granulation tissue, eschar, and slough mean?

**Back:**
- Approximated: wound edges are closed and lined up well.
- Granulation tissue: healthy red or pink healing tissue.
- Eschar: dry, thick dead tissue, often black or brown.
- Slough: soft yellow, tan, or gray dead tissue.

**Why it matters:** These are common wound-description terms used in charting and exam questions.

### Card 11

**Front:** How are common types of wound drainage described?

**Back:**
- Exudate means fluid draining from a wound.
- Serous is clear or pale yellow and watery.
- Sanguineous is bloody.
- Serosanguineous is thin pink drainage.
- Purulent is thick pus-like drainage and often suggests infection.

**Why it matters:** Drainage type helps you decide whether a wound looks normal, bleeding, or infected.

### Card 12

**Front:** What do friction, shearing, maceration, induration, reactive hyperemia, and keloid mean?

**Back:**
- Friction is skin rubbing that scrapes the surface.
- Shearing happens when skin stays in place but deeper tissue slides.
- Maceration is soggy, over-moist skin.
- Induration is tissue hardening.
- Reactive hyperemia is temporary redness after pressure is relieved.
- Keloid is a thick raised scar that grows beyond the wound edges.

**Why it matters:** These terms help you describe skin damage and healing accurately.

### Card 13

**Front:** How is shearing prevented?

**Back:**
- Lift instead of dragging the patient.
- Use a draw sheet or lift device.
- Keep the head of the bed as low as safely possible when sliding is a problem.
- Reposition often.

**Why it matters:** Shearing causes deep tissue injury even when the skin surface looks less damaged.

### Card 14

**Front:** Why should patients be pre-medicated before complex dressing changes?

**Back:**
- Dressing changes can be painful.
- Better pain control improves tolerance and cooperation.
- Less pain can also reduce stress on the body during wound care.

**Why it matters:** This is a patient-safety and comfort question as much as a wound-care question.

### Card 15

**Front:** What are the main purposes of wound dressings, and why are some wounds packed?

**Back:**
- Dressings protect the wound from trauma and contamination.
- They help keep the wound moist enough to heal but not too wet.
- They help manage drainage and infection risk.
- Packing fills dead space, helps drainage escape, and supports healing from the inside out.

**Why it matters:** The exam often asks about the goal of dressing or packing rather than just the product name.

### Card 16

**Front:** When are foam, hydrogel, and hydrocolloid dressings commonly used?

**Back:**
- Foam: wounds with moderate drainage.
- Hydrogel: dry wounds that need moisture.
- Hydrocolloid: shallow wounds with light to moderate drainage.

**Why it matters:** Dressing selection usually depends on moisture needs and drainage amount.

### Card 17

**Front:** When are transparent film, gauze, and alginate dressings commonly used?

**Back:**
- Transparent film: shallow wounds with little or no drainage.
- Gauze: covering, cleansing, or packing wounds that need frequent changes.
- Alginates: wounds with moderate to heavy drainage.

**Why it matters:** This is another common compare-and-match dressing question.

### Card 18

**Front:** How does negative pressure wound therapy help wound healing?

**Back:**
- It applies controlled suction.
- It removes excess drainage and reduces edema.
- It helps draw wound edges together.
- It supports blood flow and granulation tissue growth.

**Why it matters:** Negative pressure wound therapy is high-yield because it combines drainage control and wound-bed support.


### Card 19 (Safety)

**Front:** A client has a dry, stable, dark heel eschar with no redness, drainage, or signs of infection. Should it be routinely debrided?

**Back:**
- Not usually.
- Stable, dry, uninfected heel eschar is often left intact unless the provider specifically directs otherwise or infection or instability develops.

**Why it matters:** Evidence-based wound guidance treats stable heel eschar differently from loose or infected necrotic tissue.

### Card 20 (Priority)

**Front:** An immobile client has nonblanchable redness over the sacrum. What are the priority nursing actions?

**Back:**
- Relieve pressure right away.
- Reposition the client and offload the area.
- Keep the skin clean and dry.
- Reassess the skin and address nutrition, moisture, friction, and shearing.

**Why it matters:** Stage 1 pressure injuries are treated by quickly restoring tissue protection and perfusion.

### Card 21 (Application)

**Front:** Which dressing choice best fits a dry wound versus a heavily draining wound?

**Back:**
- Dry wound: hydrogel helps add moisture.
- Heavily draining wound: alginate or foam is usually more appropriate because it absorbs drainage.

**Why it matters:** Exam questions often turn dressing selection into a moisture-balance problem.

### Card 22 (Assessment)

**Front:** Why must nurses also check under oxygen tubing, masks, braces, or other medical devices when looking for pressure injuries?

**Back:**
- Medical devices can create pressure injury just like a mattress or chair surface can.
- Skin should be checked anywhere pressure from a device touches the body.

**Why it matters:** Research-based pressure injury guidance emphasizes device-related pressure injuries, not just sacrum and heels.

## Infection Prevention and Control

### Card 1

**Front:** What normal defense mechanisms help prevent infection, and what factors reduce normal resistance?

**Back:**
- Normal defenses include intact skin, mucous membranes, tears, saliva, mucus, stomach acid, coughing, cilia, and normal flora.
- Resistance is reduced by poor nutrition, stress, fatigue, chronic illness, age, poor circulation, invasive devices, breaks in skin, and immunosuppressive drugs.

**Why it matters:** This card connects the body’s normal protection with common infection risk factors.

### Card 2

**Front:** What is the chain of infection, and how can nurses break it?

**Back:**
- Infectious agent
- Reservoir
- Portal of exit
- Mode of transmission
- Portal of entry
- Susceptible host
- Nurses break the chain with hand hygiene, cleaning equipment, PPE, isolation, wound care, and protecting body barriers.

**Why it matters:** The exam often asks which intervention breaks a specific link in the chain.

### Card 3

**Front:** How are white blood cells, lactate, and erythrocyte sedimentation rate used when assessing infection?

**Back:**
- Increased white blood cells, especially neutrophils, often suggest infection.
- High lactate suggests poor tissue perfusion and can support concern for severe sepsis.
- Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) is a nonspecific marker of inflammation.

**Why it matters:** These lab clues help you connect body response, perfusion, and infection severity.

### Card 4

**Front:** How are specimen cultures used in diagnosing infection?

**Back:**
- A culture identifies the organism causing infection.
- Sensitivity testing helps choose the right antimicrobial.
- Cultures should usually be obtained before antibiotics are started when possible.

**Why it matters:** This helps explain why cultures guide treatment instead of just confirming that infection exists.

### Card 5

**Front:** How do localized and systemic infection signs differ?

**Back:**
- Localized infection signs include redness, warmth, swelling, pain, tenderness, and purulent drainage in one area.
- Systemic infection signs include fever, chills, malaise, fatigue, increased white blood cells, and possible confusion or hypotension.

**Why it matters:** The exam often asks you to tell whether findings point to a local wound problem or a body-wide response.

### Card 6

**Front:** What specific nursing actions are important when caring for a client with Clostridioides difficile (C. diff)?

**Back:**
- Use contact precautions.
- Wear gown and gloves.
- Wash hands with soap and water rather than alcohol-based rub alone.
- Use dedicated equipment and appropriate environmental cleaning.

**Why it matters:** C. diff is a classic exam topic because it has a special hand hygiene rule.

### Card 7

**Front:** What are early signs and symptoms of sepsis?

**Back:**
- Fever or low temperature
- Tachycardia
- Tachypnea
- Altered mental status or confusion
- Hypotension
- Decreased urine output
- Elevated lactate

**Why it matters:** Early recognition of sepsis is high-priority nursing content.

### Card 8

**Front:** What are the phases of a febrile episode?

**Back:**
- Chill phase: temperature is rising, and the patient feels cold or shivers.
- Fever phase: temperature stays elevated and the skin feels warm.
- Flush or crisis phase: temperature falls and sweating increases.

**Why it matters:** These phases explain why the same patient can feel cold first and sweaty later.

### Card 9

**Front:** How do medical asepsis and surgical asepsis differ?

**Back:**
- Medical asepsis is clean technique and reduces the number of microorganisms.
- Surgical asepsis is sterile technique and keeps an area free of all microorganisms.

**Why it matters:** The exam often asks which nursing tasks require clean technique versus sterile technique.

### Card 10

**Front:** What are the main principles of surgical asepsis?

**Back:**
- Only sterile touches sterile.
- If a sterile item touches a nonsterile item, it is contaminated.
- Keep the sterile field above waist level and in sight.
- Moisture can contaminate a sterile field.
- If there is any doubt, treat the item as contaminated.

**Why it matters:** These principles guide many wound-care and procedure questions.

### Card 11

**Front:** How are standard, contact, droplet, and airborne precautions different?

**Back:**
- Standard precautions are used for all patients.
- Contact precautions are for organisms spread by touch or contaminated surfaces.
- Droplet precautions are for larger respiratory droplets.
- Airborne precautions are for very small particles that stay suspended in the air.

**Why it matters:** Isolation types are a very common nursing exam comparison.

### Card 12

**Front:** What is protective isolation, and how is it different from other isolation precautions?

**Back:**
- Protective isolation protects the patient from outside germs.
- It is used for immunocompromised patients.
- Standard, contact, droplet, and airborne precautions mainly protect others from the patient or from the spread of the organism.

**Why it matters:** This card helps separate patient-protection isolation from organism-containment isolation.

### Card 13

**Front:** Why are surgical, trauma, and older adult clients at higher risk for healthcare-associated infection?

**Back:**
- Surgical patients have incisions and invasive devices.
- Trauma patients may have open wounds and emergency procedures.
- Older adults often have weaker immune response, chronic illness, and slower healing.

**Why it matters:** The exam often asks which patient is more vulnerable and why.

### Card 14

**Front:** What nursing interventions help treat infection through airway clearance, fever management, and antimicrobial therapy?

**Back:**
- Encourage incentive spirometry, coughing, deep breathing, and hydration for airway clearance.
- Use antipyretics and cooling measures when appropriate for fever.
- Give antimicrobials on time and monitor response and side effects.

**Why it matters:** This combines the practical bedside actions nurses take while medical treatment is underway.


### Card 15 (Priority)

**Front:** An older adult becomes confused, tachycardic, tachypneic, hypotensive, and has a rising lactate level. What should the nurse suspect?

**Back:**
- Early sepsis or worsening systemic infection.
- The nurse should escalate care quickly, follow sepsis protocols, and closely monitor perfusion.

**Why it matters:** Early sepsis recognition is one of the most tested and most important nursing safety skills.

### Card 16 (Safety)

**Front:** When is soap and water preferred over alcohol-based hand rub?

**Back:**
- For Clostridioides difficile (C. diff)
- When hands are visibly soiled

**Why it matters:** This is a classic infection-control test point and a frequent NCLEX-style distractor.

### Card 17 (Isolation)

**Front:** Which isolation type matches tuberculosis, influenza, and C. diff?

**Back:**
- Tuberculosis: airborne precautions
- Influenza: droplet precautions
- C. diff: contact precautions

**Why it matters:** Isolation matching questions are common because they combine safety and practical nursing care.

### Card 18 (Priority)

**Front:** Which nursing action breaks the chain of infection most consistently across almost every setting?

**Back:**
- Correct hand hygiene.

**Why it matters:** Nursing exam prep consistently ranks hand hygiene as the most important basic infection-prevention step.

## Respiratory Function

### Card 1

**Front:** How do ventilation, perfusion, and gas exchange differ?

**Back:**
- Ventilation is movement of air in and out of the lungs.
- Perfusion is blood flow through the lungs and body tissues.
- Gas exchange is oxygen moving into blood and carbon dioxide moving out.

**Why it matters:** These three words are easy to confuse but are tested often.

### Card 2

**Front:** What nursing actions lower the risk of impaired oxygenation, and what are key respiratory assessment techniques?

**Back:**
- Lower risk by repositioning, ambulation, deep breathing, coughing, hydration, pain control, and incentive spirometry when needed.
- Assess respiratory rate, depth, rhythm, work of breathing, chest movement, lung sounds, oxygen saturation, cough, sputum, skin color, and mental status.

**Why it matters:** This card combines prevention with core respiratory assessment.

### Card 3

**Front:** What cues suggest altered oxygenation?

**Back:**
- Shortness of breath
- Rapid breathing
- Low oxygen saturation
- Restlessness or confusion
- Abnormal breath sounds
- Cyanosis in later or severe cases

**Why it matters:** These are the classic early bedside clues that oxygenation is worsening.

### Card 4

**Front:** What causes crackles, and what can they suggest clinically?

**Back:**
- Crackles are usually caused by fluid in the alveoli or by small airways popping open.
- They may suggest pulmonary edema, atelectasis, pneumonia, or heart failure.

**Why it matters:** The exam often asks you to connect a sound with a likely underlying problem.

### Card 5

**Front:** What causes rhonchi, and what can they suggest clinically?

**Back:**
- Rhonchi are usually caused by mucus in the larger airways.
- They often improve after coughing.
- They suggest retained secretions in the airways.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic sound-to-problem match question.

### Card 6

**Front:** What causes wheezing, and what can it suggest clinically?

**Back:**
- Wheezing is usually caused by narrowed or tightened airways.
- It often suggests bronchospasm or airway obstruction.

**Why it matters:** Wheezing is a high-yield clue for airway narrowing.

### Card 7

**Front:** What causes a pleural friction rub, and what does it suggest clinically?

**Back:**
- A pleural friction rub is caused by inflamed pleural surfaces rubbing together.
- It suggests pleural inflammation.

**Why it matters:** This helps distinguish pleural inflammation from fluid or mucus sounds.

### Card 8

**Front:** How does dehydration affect the respiratory system?

**Back:**
- Secretions become thicker and harder to clear.
- Mucus plugging becomes more likely.
- Risk of atelectasis increases.

**Why it matters:** Hydration matters because thick secretions are harder to cough out.

### Card 9

**Front:** Why do ambulation, positioning, incentive spirometry, and chest tubes help promote lung expansion?

**Back:**
- Ambulation helps move air and secretions.
- Upright positioning improves lung expansion.
- Incentive spirometry encourages deep breaths and helps prevent atelectasis.
- Chest tubes remove air or fluid so the lung can re-expand.

**Why it matters:** Many respiratory care questions test whether you understand the reason behind the intervention.

### Card 10

**Front:** What are chest physiotherapy and postural drainage used for, and why is pursed-lip breathing helpful?

**Back:**
- Chest physiotherapy and postural drainage help move mucus out of the lungs.
- Pursed-lip breathing slows exhalation and helps keep small airways open.
- Pursed-lip breathing is especially helpful in chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

**Why it matters:** This card covers common noninvasive ways to improve airway clearance and ventilation.

### Card 11

**Front:** What is the general nursing care for a client with a chest tube?

**Back:**
- Keep the drainage system below chest level.
- Assess respiratory status, pain, and oxygenation.
- Check tubing for kinks and dependent loops.
- Check the dressing and insertion site.
- Watch the amount and character of drainage and look for air leaks.

**Why it matters:** Chest tube care is a common priority topic because it mixes assessment and safety.

### Card 12

**Front:** Why is clamping a chest tube generally prohibited, and what should the nurse do if drainage suddenly increases?

**Back:**
- Clamping can trap air and cause tension pneumothorax.
- If drainage suddenly increases, assess the patient first, watch for bleeding or distress, and report it quickly.

**Why it matters:** The nurse must focus on patient condition first, not just the equipment.

### Card 13

**Front:** How do hypoventilation, hyperventilation, and hypoxia differ?

**Back:**
- Hypoventilation means not breathing enough, so carbon dioxide builds up.
- Hyperventilation means breathing too fast or too much, so carbon dioxide falls.
- Hypoxia means the tissues are not getting enough oxygen.

**Why it matters:** These are easy to mix up, so a compare card is high-yield.

### Card 14

**Front:** What factors commonly contribute to hypoventilation, hyperventilation, and hypoxia?

**Back:**
- Hypoventilation: sedatives, opioids, neuromuscular weakness, fatigue, obesity, airway obstruction.
- Hyperventilation: anxiety, pain, fever, early sepsis, low oxygen levels.
- Hypoxia: airway obstruction, lung disease, anemia, poor cardiac output, high altitude.

**Why it matters:** The exam may ask you to match the cause to the breathing problem.

### Card 15

**Front:** What complications can occur with hyperventilation, hypoventilation, and hypoxia?

**Back:**
- Hyperventilation can cause dizziness, tingling, and respiratory alkalosis.
- Hypoventilation can cause carbon dioxide retention, respiratory acidosis, and drowsiness.
- Hypoxia can cause organ damage, dysrhythmias, and even cardiac arrest if severe.

**Why it matters:** This card connects each problem to the danger it creates.

### Card 16

**Front:** What are early and late signs of hypoxia?

**Back:**
- Early signs: restlessness, anxiety, tachycardia, tachypnea, mild confusion.
- Late signs: cyanosis, severe confusion, decreased level of consciousness, and severe distress.

**Why it matters:** Early signs are subtle but important because late signs mean the patient is worsening.

### Card 17

**Front:** What are the priority interventions for respiratory distress, and how should the client be positioned?

**Back:**
- Assess airway, breathing, and circulation first.
- Position the client upright, usually high Fowler’s, if tolerated.
- Apply oxygen as ordered and stay with the patient.
- Call for help if distress is severe and prepare for higher-level support if needed.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic priority question: airway and breathing come before less urgent tasks.

### Card 18

**Front:** What do fatigue, dyspnea, orthopnea, and hemoptysis mean?

**Back:**
- Fatigue: unusual tiredness or weakness.
- Dyspnea: difficult or uncomfortable breathing.
- Orthopnea: shortness of breath when lying flat.
- Hemoptysis: coughing up blood.

**Why it matters:** These terms appear often in respiratory and cardiac question stems.

### Card 19

**Front:** How do deep breathing and intentional coughing help with airway clearance?

**Back:**
- Deep breathing opens alveoli and helps prevent atelectasis.
- Intentional coughing moves secretions up and out of the airway.

**Why it matters:** The goal is to improve ventilation and clear mucus, especially after surgery or illness.

### Card 20

**Front:** What is the priority care for a client who vomits and has a decreased level of consciousness?

**Back:**
- Turn the client to the side if possible.
- Protect the airway.
- Suction as needed.
- Watch closely for aspiration and respiratory distress.

**Why it matters:** This is an airway-priority question.

### Card 21

**Front:** What is the correct technique for sterile airway suctioning?

**Back:**
- Use sterile technique.
- Hyperoxygenate as ordered before and between passes.
- Insert the catheter without suction.
- Apply suction only while withdrawing the catheter.
- Limit each pass to about 10 to 15 seconds.

**Why it matters:** Safe suctioning clears the airway without causing avoidable hypoxia or trauma.

### Card 22

**Front:** Why is sterile technique essential during airway suctioning, and why is continuous suction harmful?

**Back:**
- Sterile technique helps keep new germs out of the airway.
- Continuous suction can injure the airway and remove too much oxygen.

**Why it matters:** This explains the safety logic behind suctioning technique.

### Card 23

**Front:** What care is needed for a client receiving oxygen by nasal cannula, and when is humidification used?

**Back:**
- Make sure the cannula fits correctly.
- Check skin around the ears and nose.
- Monitor comfort and oxygen saturation.
- Use humidification when oxygen dries the nose or throat, especially with higher flow or longer use.

**Why it matters:** This is basic bedside oxygen care that often appears on exams.

### Card 24

**Front:** What are common risks of oxygen therapy, and how can the nurse reduce them?

**Back:**
- Risks include fire, dry mucous membranes, skin breakdown, oxygen toxicity, and carbon dioxide retention in selected patients.
- Prevent problems with no smoking, safe equipment use, skin checks, humidification when needed, and the lowest safe oxygen level.

**Why it matters:** Oxygen is a medication, so it must be given safely and monitored.

### Card 25

**Front:** How do the simple face mask, Venturi mask, and non-rebreather mask differ, and how does a client eat when receiving oxygen by mask?

**Back:**
- Simple face mask gives a moderate amount of oxygen.
- Venturi mask gives a very exact oxygen concentration.
- Non-rebreather gives a high concentration of oxygen.
- For eating, the mask may be briefly removed or changed to nasal cannula if ordered and tolerated, then reapplied promptly.

**Why it matters:** The exam often asks which oxygen device best fits the need.

### Card 26

**Front:** What are noninvasive positive pressure ventilation, continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP), and bilevel positive airway pressure (BiPAP) used for?

**Back:**
- They use a mask to support breathing without intubation.
- CPAP gives one continuous pressure to keep alveoli open.
- BiPAP gives different inspiratory and expiratory pressures to support ventilation more strongly.
- They are used for sleep apnea, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease exacerbation, pulmonary edema in selected patients, and respiratory distress when the airway can still be protected.

**Why it matters:** This is a common treatment-selection topic in respiratory care.


### Card 27 (Priority)

**Front:** A postoperative client has an oxygen saturation of 87%, is anxious, and says the incision hurts. What should the nurse do first?

**Back:**
- Address oxygenation first.
- Reposition the client, assess breathing, and apply or adjust oxygen as ordered before focusing on less urgent problems.

**Why it matters:** Exam-style nursing questions strongly favor airway and breathing priorities over comfort measures.

### Card 28 (Application)

**Front:** Which oxygen device is usually best when a precise oxygen concentration is needed? Which is best for severe hypoxia?

**Back:**
- Venturi mask: best when exact oxygen concentration is needed.
- Non-rebreather mask: best for severe hypoxia when high oxygen concentration is needed quickly.

**Why it matters:** Device selection is a common compare-and-contrast exam angle.

### Card 29 (Safety)

**Front:** Continuous bubbling appears in the water-seal chamber of a chest tube system. What does this usually suggest?

**Back:**
- An air leak.
- The nurse should assess the system from the patient toward the drainage unit to help identify the source.

**Why it matters:** Chest tube troubleshooting questions often test whether you can recognize an air leak quickly.

### Card 30 (Red Flag)

**Front:** Which hypoxia signs are late and more dangerous than early restlessness or anxiety?

**Back:**
- Cyanosis
- Marked confusion or decreased level of consciousness
- Severe respiratory distress

**Why it matters:** Late signs mean the patient is getting worse, not better.

### Card 31 (Safety)

**Front:** If suctioning causes desaturation, distress, or bradycardia, what should the nurse do?

**Back:**
- Stop suctioning.
- Oxygenate the client as ordered.
- Reassess before attempting another pass.

**Why it matters:** Safe suctioning means knowing when to stop, not just how to start.

## Cardiac Function

### Card 1

**Front:** What is the normal flow of blood through the heart?

**Back:**
- Vena cava → right atrium → tricuspid valve → right ventricle → pulmonic valve → pulmonary artery → lungs
- Lungs → pulmonary veins → left atrium → mitral valve → left ventricle → aortic valve → aorta → body

**Why it matters:** Students are often asked to trace blood flow or identify where oxygenation happens.

### Card 2

**Front:** What is the normal cardiac conduction pathway?

**Back:**
- Sinoatrial (SA) node starts the impulse.
- Atrioventricular (AV) node delays it briefly.
- Bundle of His, bundle branches, and Purkinje fibers spread it through the ventricles.

**Why it matters:** This is the electrical pathway that makes coordinated pumping possible.

### Card 3

**Front:** How do preload, afterload, contractility, and heart rate affect cardiac output and perfusion?

**Back:**
- Preload is ventricular filling or stretch before contraction.
- Afterload is the resistance the heart must pump against.
- Contractility is the strength of the squeeze.
- Heart rate that is too fast or too slow can reduce effective cardiac output.

**Why it matters:** The exam often asks which factor would decrease cardiac output or tissue perfusion.

### Card 4

**Front:** How do smoking, high blood pressure, poor nutrition, inactivity, and diabetes affect cardiovascular function?

**Back:**
- Smoking reduces oxygen delivery and damages blood vessels.
- High blood pressure increases afterload and heart workload.
- Poor nutrition contributes to artery narrowing and high blood pressure.
- Inactivity weakens cardiovascular efficiency.
- Diabetes damages blood vessels and raises coronary disease risk.

**Why it matters:** These are major modifiable cardiovascular risk factors.

### Card 5

**Front:** How do conduction disturbances affect cardiac output, and what are the basic indicators of tachycardia and bradycardia?

**Back:**
- Abnormal conduction can make the heart beat too fast, too slow, or ineffectively, lowering cardiac output.
- Tachycardia in adults is heart rate above 100 beats per minute.
- Bradycardia in adults is heart rate below 60 beats per minute.

**Why it matters:** Questions may ask how rhythm change affects perfusion, not just the number itself.

### Card 6

**Front:** What cues suggest altered cardiac function?

**Back:**
- Chest pain
- Shortness of breath
- Fatigue
- Dizziness or syncope
- Hypotension
- Cool clammy skin
- Edema
- Decreased urine output

**Why it matters:** These are common perfusion and cardiac-output clues.

### Card 7

**Front:** Why are ventricular tachycardia and ventricular fibrillation life-threatening, and what are the immediate life-saving actions?

**Back:**
- They stop the ventricles from pumping blood effectively.
- Cardiac output drops sharply or becomes absent.
- Call for help, start cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR), and use an automated external defibrillator (AED) or defibrillator as soon as possible.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic emergency question where defibrillation and CPR are central.

### Card 8

**Front:** How does left heart failure affect oxygenation and circulation, and what can peripheral edema suggest?

**Back:**
- Left heart failure causes blood to back up into the lungs, leading to crackles and shortness of breath.
- Forward flow to the body decreases, so perfusion can fall.
- Peripheral edema suggests fluid backup and poor cardiac function, especially in heart failure.

**Why it matters:** This card links fluid backup with both lung and body findings.

### Card 9

**Front:** How are myocardial ischemia, chest pain, and myocardial infarction related?

**Back:**
- Myocardial ischemia means heart muscle is not getting enough oxygen-rich blood.
- Ischemia often causes chest pain or pressure.
- If the blood flow stays blocked too long, myocardial infarction occurs and heart tissue dies.

**Why it matters:** This is central to understanding acute coronary syndrome questions.


### Card 10 (Priority)

**Front:** A client reports chest pressure with diaphoresis and nausea. What is the nurse's priority concern?

**Back:**
- Suspected myocardial ischemia or myocardial infarction.
- The nurse should rapidly assess, escalate care, and support oxygenation and cardiac evaluation.

**Why it matters:** Nursing exams commonly present myocardial infarction through symptom clusters, not the diagnosis name first.

### Card 11 (Compare)

**Front:** How do left-sided and right-sided heart failure findings usually differ?

**Back:**
- Left-sided heart failure: lung findings such as crackles, dyspnea, orthopnea, and poor oxygenation.
- Right-sided heart failure: body-fluid backup such as peripheral edema, jugular venous distention, and weight gain.

**Why it matters:** This compare card makes heart failure patterns easier to remember under test pressure.

### Card 12 (Priority)

**Front:** A client is unresponsive and the monitor shows ventricular fibrillation. What actions come first?

**Back:**
- Call for help or activate the emergency response system.
- Start high-quality cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR).
- Defibrillate as soon as possible.

**Why it matters:** This is a true must-know emergency sequence for nursing exams and real care.

## Pain Management

### Card 1

**Front:** How do acute, chronic, referred, radiating, superficial, and deep visceral pain differ?

**Back:**
- Acute pain starts suddenly and is usually short-term.
- Chronic or persistent pain is ongoing and usually lasts longer than 6 months.
- Referred pain is felt in a different area from the source.
- Radiating pain spreads from one area to another.
- Superficial pain comes from skin or tissue near the surface.
- Deep visceral pain comes from internal organs and is often hard to locate.

**Why it matters:** Pain-type questions often test whether you can recognize the pattern described.

### Card 2

**Front:** How do nociceptive and neuropathic pain differ?

**Back:**
- Nociceptive pain comes from tissue injury or inflammation.
- Neuropathic pain comes from nerve damage or abnormal nerve signaling.

**Why it matters:** This distinction is important because different pain types may respond to different treatments.

### Card 3

**Front:** What are common physiological and behavioral manifestations of pain?

**Back:**
- Physiological: increased heart rate, increased blood pressure, faster breathing, sweating, muscle tension.
- Behavioral: grimacing, guarding, crying, restlessness, withdrawal.

**Why it matters:** The nurse assesses both what the body does and how the person acts.

### Card 4

**Front:** How do the numerical pain scale, faces scale, and Passero Opioid-Induced Sedation Scale differ?

**Back:**
- Numerical scale is useful when the patient can rate pain with a number.
- Faces scale helps children or patients who cannot use numbers well.
- Passero scale measures sedation level and helps assess opioid safety.

**Why it matters:** The exam may ask which scale is most appropriate for a particular patient.

### Card 5

**Front:** What are the three main categories of analgesic drug therapy?

**Back:**
- Nonopioids
- Opioids
- Adjuvant or co-analgesic drugs

**Why it matters:** This basic medication classification is common pain-management content.

### Card 6

**Front:** What strategies improve pain management, and how do around-the-clock (ATC) and as-needed (PRN) analgesics differ?

**Back:**
- Assess pain often and reassess after treatment.
- Teach the patient to report pain early.
- Around-the-clock (ATC) dosing gives scheduled relief for ongoing pain.
- As-needed (PRN) dosing is used for intermittent or breakthrough pain.

**Why it matters:** This card connects assessment, teaching, and medication timing.

### Card 7

**Front:** What signs suggest pain is well managed, and what signs suggest opioid oversedation?

**Back:**
- Well managed: patient says pain is acceptable and can move, cough, breathe deeply, sleep, and participate in care better.
- Oversedation: hard to arouse, slowed breathing, low oxygen saturation, confusion, or very sleepy appearance.

**Why it matters:** The goal is comfort without dangerous respiratory depression.

### Card 8

**Front:** What barriers and complications are associated with poor pain management, and what nonpharmacologic methods can help?

**Back:**
- Barriers include fear of addiction, poor assessment, communication problems, cultural beliefs, and delayed dosing.
- Poor pain control can reduce mobility, sleep, healing, and deep breathing.
- Helpful nonpharmacologic methods include repositioning, relaxation breathing, distraction, music, guided imagery, massage, and splinting an incision.

**Why it matters:** Pain management questions often combine barriers, outcomes, and alternative strategies.


### Card 9 (Safety)

**Front:** A client received an opioid and is now very sleepy with a respiratory rate of 8. What is the priority nursing action?

**Back:**
- Hold the opioid.
- Support airway and breathing.
- Notify the provider and be prepared for interventions such as naloxone if ordered or indicated.

**Why it matters:** Pain control never comes before safe breathing.

### Card 10 (Application)

**Front:** Which pain scale is usually the best fit for a child or a patient who cannot easily use numbers?

**Back:**
- The faces scale is often the best choice.

**Why it matters:** Nursing exams often ask you to match the patient to the best assessment tool.

### Card 11 (Strategy)

**Front:** Why is scheduled around-the-clock (ATC) analgesia sometimes better than waiting until pain becomes severe?

**Back:**
- It prevents pain from building up.
- It can improve movement, coughing, sleep, and participation in care.
- Severe pain is often harder to control once it is established.

**Why it matters:** This is a common rationale question in postoperative and ongoing pain care.

## Fluids and Electrolytes

### Card 1

**Front:** How do osmosis, diffusion, filtration, and hormones help regulate fluid balance, and why is accurate intake and output important?

**Back:**
- Osmosis moves water across membranes.
- Diffusion moves solutes from higher to lower concentration.
- Filtration moves fluid because of pressure differences.
- Antidiuretic hormone (ADH), thirst, and the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) help regulate water and sodium.
- Accurate intake and output helps evaluate hydration, kidney function, and treatment response.

**Why it matters:** This card covers the basic physiology behind fluid management.

### Card 2

**Front:** What causes fluid volume deficit, and what signs and symptoms suggest it?

**Back:**
- Causes include vomiting, diarrhea, fever, sweating, bleeding, diuretics, and poor intake.
- Signs include dry mucous membranes, thirst, tachycardia, low blood pressure, low urine output, concentrated urine, and weight loss.

**Why it matters:** Fluid deficit is a classic nursing assessment problem.

### Card 3

**Front:** What causes fluid volume excess, and what signs and symptoms suggest it?

**Back:**
- Causes include heart failure, kidney failure, liver disease, excessive intravenous fluids, and high sodium intake.
- Signs include edema, weight gain, crackles, shortness of breath, elevated blood pressure, bounding pulse, and neck vein distention.

**Why it matters:** This helps you recognize fluid overload quickly.

### Card 4

**Front:** How can fluid and electrolyte imbalance change assessment findings?

**Back:**
- Blood pressure, pulse, and cardiac rhythm may change.
- Neck veins, mucous membranes, and edema may change.
- Level of consciousness, respiratory pattern, oxygen saturation, reflexes, bowel sounds, and urine characteristics may change.
- Daily weight is a sensitive sign of fluid gain or loss.

**Why it matters:** The exam often expects you to connect fluid shifts with whole-body assessment findings.

### Card 5

**Front:** What are common findings of hyponatremia and hypernatremia?

**Back:**
- Hyponatremia may cause headache, confusion, nausea, and seizures.
- Hypernatremia may cause thirst, restlessness, weakness, and neurologic changes.

**Why it matters:** Sodium problems especially affect neurological status.

### Card 6

**Front:** What are common findings of hypokalemia and hyperkalemia?

**Back:**
- Hypokalemia may cause weakness, constipation, and dysrhythmias.
- Hyperkalemia may cause weakness and dangerous dysrhythmias.

**Why it matters:** Potassium changes are high-yield because they strongly affect cardiac rhythm.

### Card 7

**Front:** What are common findings of hypocalcemia and hypercalcemia, and what do Chvostek’s and Trousseau’s signs suggest?

**Back:**
- Hypocalcemia may cause tingling, muscle cramps, tetany, and seizures.
- Hypercalcemia may cause weakness, constipation, confusion, and decreased reflexes.
- Chvostek’s sign is facial twitching when the facial nerve is tapped.
- Trousseau’s sign is hand or carpal spasm when a blood pressure cuff is inflated.
- Both signs suggest hypocalcemia.

**Why it matters:** Calcium imbalance is often tested with these named signs.

### Card 8

**Front:** What nursing interventions help correct altered fluid and electrolyte balance?

**Back:**
- Replace missing fluids or electrolytes carefully.
- Monitor labs, heart rhythm, intake and output, weight, and symptoms.
- Restrict fluids when the body cannot safely handle extra fluid.
- Teach food sources such as sodium in processed foods, potassium in bananas and potatoes, and calcium in dairy and fortified foods.

**Why it matters:** This card focuses on practical nursing action and patient teaching.

### Card 9

**Front:** How does the nurse evaluate whether interventions for fluid and electrolyte imbalance are working?

**Back:**
- Reassess vital signs and mental status.
- Recheck daily weight and intake and output.
- Review follow-up laboratory values.
- Look for improvement in symptoms such as edema, weakness, confusion, or poor urine output.

**Why it matters:** Evaluation questions ask how you know the care plan is helping.


### Card 10 (High-Yield)

**Front:** What is one of the most sensitive bedside indicators of fluid gain or loss?

**Back:**
- Daily weight.

**Why it matters:** Nursing exams often reward the answer that trends fluid status most accurately over time.

### Card 11 (Red Flag)

**Front:** A client has muscle weakness and tall peaked T waves on the electrocardiogram (ECG). Which electrolyte imbalance is most concerning?

**Back:**
- Hyperkalemia.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic life-threatening electrolyte pattern linked to dangerous dysrhythmias.

### Card 12 (Priority)

**Front:** A client receives a large amount of intravenous fluid and now has crackles, rising blood pressure, and neck vein distention. What problem should the nurse suspect?

**Back:**
- Fluid volume excess or fluid overload.
- The nurse should slow or stop fluids as appropriate, assess the client, and escalate care based on the situation and orders.

**Why it matters:** This is a common exam scenario linking IV therapy to pulmonary and circulatory overload.

### Card 13 (Red Flag)

**Front:** A client has a positive Trousseau's sign. What imbalance does this suggest and why is it important?

**Back:**
- It suggests hypocalcemia.
- Hypocalcemia can increase neuromuscular irritability and raise the risk for tetany or seizures.

**Why it matters:** Named signs are often tested because they connect a physical finding to an electrolyte problem.

## Intravenous Therapy

### Card 1

**Front:** How do parenteral and enteral routes differ, and how do isotonic, hypertonic, and hypotonic fluids differ?

**Back:**
- Parenteral means given outside the digestive tract, such as intravenous therapy.
- Enteral means given through the digestive tract, such as by mouth or feeding tube.
- Isotonic fluids stay mainly in the vascular space.
- Hypertonic fluids pull water into the bloodstream from the cells.
- Hypotonic fluids move water from the bloodstream into the cells.

**Why it matters:** These are core IV-therapy definitions.

### Card 2

**Front:** What are common indications for intravenous therapy, and how do peripheral and central lines differ?

**Back:**
- Intravenous therapy is used for blood, nutrition, fluid and electrolyte replacement, and medication administration.
- A peripheral line is used for shorter-term therapy in smaller veins.
- A central line ends in a large central vein and is used for longer-term therapy, certain irritating drugs, or special access needs.

**Why it matters:** This card covers why intravenous access is needed and which type is used.

### Card 3

**Front:** How do a peripherally inserted central catheter (PICC), tunneled central line, implanted port, and continuous versus intermittent intravenous therapy differ?

**Back:**
- A PICC enters through a peripheral vein and ends centrally.
- A tunneled central line travels under the skin before entering the vein and is used for long-term therapy.
- An implanted port sits under the skin and is accessed with a special needle.
- Continuous infusion runs steadily over time.
- Intermittent bolus or piggyback runs at specific intervals.

**Why it matters:** This card helps compare common long-term access devices and infusion patterns.

### Card 4

**Front:** What are the priority assessments when caring for a client with an intravenous line?

**Back:**
- Check for redness, swelling, pain, leakage, warmth, or coolness at the site.
- Verify the solution, tubing, rate, and pump settings.
- Make sure the dressing is clean, dry, and secure.
- Monitor the patient for infection, fluid overload, and whether the therapy is helping.

**Why it matters:** This is the bedside assessment card for routine IV care.

### Card 5

**Front:** What problems can occur if an intravenous infusion runs too fast or too slow, and how can IV therapy contribute to fluid overload or air embolism?

**Back:**
- Too fast can cause fluid overload and strain on the heart and circulation.
- Too slow can delay needed treatment.
- IV therapy can contribute to fluid overload if the patient receives more fluid than the heart or kidneys can handle.
- Air embolism can occur if air enters the tubing or bloodstream through poor line management.

**Why it matters:** Rate errors are common safety questions in IV therapy.

### Card 6

**Front:** How do infiltration, phlebitis, extravasation, and infection differ?

**Back:**
- Infiltration: regular IV fluid leaks into surrounding tissue.
- Phlebitis: the vein becomes inflamed, warm, red, and tender.
- Extravasation: a medicine or fluid that can damage tissue leaks into the tissue.
- Infection: microorganisms enter the site or bloodstream.

**Why it matters:** These complications are often tested as a compare-and-contrast set.

### Card 7

**Front:** What nursing actions help prevent complications of intravenous therapy?

**Back:**
- Use aseptic technique.
- Prime tubing and secure connections.
- Assess the site and patient frequently.
- Use infusion pumps when indicated.
- Change dressings and tubing according to policy.
- Stop the infusion promptly if a complication is suspected.

**Why it matters:** Prevention is the main nursing focus in safe IV therapy.

### Card 8 (Priority)

**Front:** An intravenous site is cool, pale, swollen, and uncomfortable. What is the most likely problem and what should the nurse do first?

**Back:**
- Infiltration is most likely.
- Stop the infusion and address the intravenous site according to policy.

**Why it matters:** This is one of the most common IV complication scenarios on nursing exams.

### Card 9 (Safety)

**Front:** How is extravasation more serious than simple infiltration?

**Back:**
- Extravasation involves a fluid or medication that can damage tissue.
- It can cause severe tissue injury and requires rapid action.

**Why it matters:** The exam often tests whether you know which IV complication is most dangerous to tissue.

### Card 10 (Emergency)

**Front:** If an air embolism is suspected from an intravenous line, what position is classically used while calling for urgent help?

**Back:**
- Place the client on the left side with the head lower than the feet while calling for urgent help. This is the classic teaching response.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic nursing safety response tied to rare but dangerous IV complications.

### Card 11 (Application)

**Front:** Which fluid type is most commonly used for routine volume replacement in many hypovolemic situations without a major sodium shift?

**Back:**
- An isotonic fluid.

**Why it matters:** Nursing exams often expect you to match the general fluid type to the clinical goal.
