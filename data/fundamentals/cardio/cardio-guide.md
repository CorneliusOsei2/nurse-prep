# Cardio Guide

This guide is written in a friendly, plain-English style, but it still keeps the important nursing facts correct.

## Review cardiovascular physiology

(This section explains how the heart moves blood and helps deliver oxygen to the body.)

### Explain the flow of blood through the heart

(This is the path blood follows through the heart, lungs, and body.)

- Body -> vena cava -> right atrium
- Right atrium -> tricuspid valve -> right ventricle
- Right ventricle -> pulmonic valve -> pulmonary artery -> lungs
- Lungs -> pulmonary veins -> left atrium
- Left atrium -> mitral valve -> left ventricle
- Left ventricle -> aortic valve -> aorta -> body

### Review the cardiac conduction system

(This is the heart's electrical system. It tells the heart when to beat.)

- **SA node:** starts the heartbeat. It is the heart's natural pacemaker.
- **AV node:** slows the signal for a moment so the ventricles can fill.
- **Bundle of His:** carries the signal from the AV node into the ventricles.
- **Right and left bundle branches:** carry the signal down each side of the ventricles.
- **Purkinje fibers:** spread the signal through the ventricles so they squeeze together.
- Good conduction helps the heart pump in a strong, organized way.

### Explain the relationship of cardiac output (preload, afterload, contractility, and heart rate) to oxygenation and perfusion

(Cardiac output is how much blood the heart pumps. Perfusion means blood flow reaching tissues.)

**Cardiac output = heart rate x stroke volume**

#### Preload

(Preload means how full the ventricle is before it squeezes.)

- It depends on how much blood returns to the heart.
- Too little preload means there is less blood to pump out.
- Too much preload can cause fluid backup and extra stretching of the heart.

#### Afterload

(Afterload means how hard the heart has to push to get blood out.)

- It is the resistance the ventricle pumps against.
- High blood pressure raises afterload.
- Higher afterload makes the heart work harder.

#### Contractility

(Contractility means how strongly the heart squeezes.)

- Strong squeezing improves stroke volume.
- Weak squeezing lowers stroke volume and cardiac output.
- Heart muscle damage can lower contractility.

#### Heart rate

(Heart rate matters, but faster is not always better.)

- If the heart rate is too slow, cardiac output may fall.
- If the heart rate is too fast, the heart may not fill well between beats.
- Either extreme can reduce blood flow to tissues.

#### Why this matters for oxygenation and perfusion

(The body needs enough blood flow to carry oxygen to the brain, kidneys, skin, and other tissues.)

- Good cardiac output helps move oxygen-rich blood through the body.
- Low cardiac output can lead to fatigue, cool skin, confusion, low urine output, and poor tissue perfusion.

## Identify key techniques for assessing the cardiovascular system

(These are the main nursing checks used to see how well the heart and circulation are working.)

- Check **blood pressure, heart rate, heart rhythm, oxygen saturation, and temperature**.
- Observe **skin color, skin temperature, and skin moisture**.
- Look for **cyanosis, pallor, edema, and jugular venous distention (swollen neck veins)**.
- Feel **peripheral pulses** and compare sides.
- Check **capillary refill**.
- Listen to **heart sounds** and note any extra sounds or murmurs.
- Ask about **chest pain, palpitations, shortness of breath, fatigue, dizziness, and activity tolerance**.
- Monitor **urine output and mental status**, because poor perfusion often shows up there.

## Discuss cardiac factors that affect cardiac function

(These are common things that help or hurt cardiovascular health.)

### Explain how cigarette smoking affects cardiovascular function

- Nicotine narrows blood vessels.
- Smoking damages blood vessel walls.
- Carbon monoxide reduces oxygen delivery.
- Smoking raises the risk for blocked arteries, heart attack, stroke, and poor circulation.

### Explain how high blood pressure affects cardiovascular function

- High blood pressure raises afterload.
- The left ventricle has to work harder to pump blood out.
- Over time, this can weaken the heart and damage blood vessels.

### Explain how nutrition affects cardiovascular function

- Too much saturated fat, trans fat, and sodium can worsen blocked arteries and high blood pressure.
- Poor nutrition can weaken the heart and body tissues.
- Good nutrition supports blood vessel health, heart function, and fluid balance.

### Explain how activity affects cardiovascular function

- Regular activity helps circulation and strengthens the heart.
- Inactivity increases the risk for venous stasis, blood clots, and poor overall conditioning.
- Movement also helps lower swelling in the legs by helping venous return.

### Explain how diabetes affects cardiovascular function

- Diabetes damages blood vessels over time.
- It raises the risk for coronary artery disease, stroke, and poor peripheral circulation.
- High blood sugar also slows healing and worsens tissue perfusion.

## Identify manifestations of impaired cardiovascular function

(This section is about the common signs that the heart or circulation may not be working well.)

### Vital signs

(Vital sign changes can be early clues that the cardiovascular system is stressed.)

- Tachycardia or bradycardia
- High blood pressure or low blood pressure
- Irregular pulse
- Low oxygen saturation if circulation or oxygen delivery is poor
- Increased respiratory rate when the body is trying to compensate

### Heart muscle damage

(Heart muscle damage means the heart muscle is not getting enough oxygen or has already been injured.)

- Chest pain or pressure
- Shortness of breath
- Fatigue
- Nausea or sweating
- Weak pumping and lower cardiac output
- Dysrhythmias may occur

### Valve dysfunction

(Heart valves should open and close well. If they do not, blood flow becomes less efficient.)

- Murmur
- Fatigue
- Shortness of breath
- Edema
- Dizziness or syncope in more serious cases
- Signs of fluid backup if the valve problem is severe

### Conduction problems

(Conduction problems are electrical problems in the heart.)

- Palpitations
- Irregular pulse
- Dizziness
- Syncope
- Fatigue
- Shortness of breath
- Chest discomfort in some patients

### Blood flow alterations

(These are problems with circulation through vessels and tissues.)

#### Arteries

(Arteries carry blood away from the heart.)

- Weak or absent pulses
- Cool skin
- Pale skin
- Pain from poor blood flow
- Numbness or tingling
- Delayed healing if tissue is not getting enough oxygen

#### Capillary

(Capillaries are tiny vessels where oxygen and nutrients move into tissues.)

- Delayed capillary refill
- Swelling when fluid leaks into tissues
- Poor exchange of oxygen and nutrients
- Skin changes or slow healing

#### Impaired tissue perfusion

(Impaired tissue perfusion means the tissues are not getting enough blood flow.)

- Cool clammy skin
- Confusion or restlessness
- Decreased urine output
- Fatigue or weakness
- Delayed wound healing
- Chest pain or leg pain depending on which tissue is affected

## Explain how disturbances in conduction affect cardiac output and cardiac function

(If the electrical signal is not normal, the pumping action may not be normal either.)

- The heart may beat too fast, too slow, or irregularly.
- The ventricles may not fill well before they contract.
- The squeeze may become less effective.
- Cardiac output can fall.
- Less blood reaches the brain, kidneys, skin, and other tissues.

### Identify basic indicators of tachycardia and bradycardia

- **Tachycardia:** heart rate above 100 beats/min in adults.
- **Bradycardia:** heart rate below 60 beats/min in adults.
- The most important question is whether the patient still has good perfusion.
- Concerning signs include dizziness, weakness, hypotension, chest pain, shortness of breath, or syncope.

### Explain why ventricular tachycardia and ventricular fibrillation are defined as life-threatening dysrhythmias

(These rhythms are dangerous because the ventricles stop pumping blood effectively.)

- **Ventricular tachycardia (VT):** the ventricles beat so fast that filling and output can drop sharply.
- **Ventricular fibrillation (VF):** the ventricles quiver instead of pumping.
- With VF, there is no effective cardiac output.
- Without fast treatment, perfusion stops and death can occur.

## Explain methods for preventing venous stasis

(Venous stasis means blood is pooling in the veins instead of moving back to the heart well.)

- Encourage early ambulation.
- Reposition often.
- Teach leg exercises and ankle pumps.
- Avoid long periods of sitting or standing still.
- Avoid crossing the legs for long periods.
- Use sequential compression devices (SCDs) or compression stockings if ordered.
- Maintain enough hydration when not contraindicated.
- Give anticoagulants as ordered for patients at risk.

## Explain how the following factors may contribute to edema development

(Edema means swelling from extra fluid in the tissues.)

### Increased capillary permeability

(This means the capillaries become leakier than normal.)

- Common with inflammation, burns, allergic reactions, and sepsis.
- Fluid and proteins leak out into the tissue.
- This causes swelling.

### Increased hydrostatic pressure

(This means pressure inside the blood vessels is pushing fluid out.)

- Seen in heart failure and venous obstruction.
- Higher pressure pushes fluid from the bloodstream into the tissues.
- This often causes dependent edema, especially in the legs.

### Decreased oncotic pressure

(Oncotic pressure is the pull created by proteins, especially albumin, that helps keep fluid in the blood vessels.)

- Low albumin means less fluid is pulled back into the bloodstream.
- Fluid stays in the tissues instead.
- Common causes include liver disease, kidney disease, and severe malnutrition.

## Explain the risks associated with edema

(Edema is not just swelling. It can create real problems.)

- Skin can stretch and break down.
- Movement can become harder and more painful.
- Wounds may heal more slowly.
- Infection risk can rise if skin breaks down.
- Severe edema can reduce oxygen and nutrient movement into tissues.
- Pulmonary edema can make breathing difficult and is an emergency.

## Identify interventions to help reduce edema

(The best intervention depends on the cause, but these are common nursing steps.)

- Elevate the swollen area when appropriate.
- Encourage ambulation and range-of-motion exercises.
- Reposition often.
- Use compression devices or stockings if ordered and appropriate.
- Monitor daily weights, intake and output, and lung sounds.
- Protect the skin and keep it clean and dry.
- Limit sodium if ordered.
- Give diuretics as ordered.
- Treat the cause, such as heart failure, venous problems, or low protein states.

## Quick Memorization Points

(Use these short facts for fast review.)

- **SA node** starts the heartbeat.
- **AV node** delays the signal for a moment.
- **Preload** = how full the ventricle is before it squeezes.
- **Afterload** = how hard the heart has to push to get blood out.
- **Contractility** = how strongly the heart squeezes.
- **Tachycardia** is above 100 beats/min in adults.
- **Bradycardia** is below 60 beats/min in adults.
- **VT and VF** are emergencies because effective cardiac output can stop.
- **Venous stasis** raises the risk for clot formation.
- **Edema** can come from leaky capillaries, too much vessel pressure, or too little oncotic pull.

## Study Tips

(Use these to study actively instead of just rereading.)

- For every topic, ask: **What is it? Why does it happen? What do I assess? What does the nurse do first?**
- Compare look-alike terms side by side: **preload vs afterload**, **tachycardia vs bradycardia**, **arterial flow problems vs venous stasis**.
- Memorize the emergency ideas first: **poor perfusion, VT/VF, pulmonary edema, and signs of worsening circulation**.
