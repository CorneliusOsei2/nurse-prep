# Cardio Flashcards

These flashcards follow the cardio guide and use a simple, exam-friendly style.

## How to use this deck

- **Front** = the question side.
- **Back** = the answer side.
- **Why it matters** = a short reason this card matters for tests or clinical thinking.

## Review cardiovascular physiology

### Card 1

**Front:** What is the flow of blood through the heart?

**Back:**
- Body -> vena cava -> right atrium
- Right atrium -> tricuspid valve -> right ventricle
- Right ventricle -> pulmonic valve -> pulmonary artery -> lungs
- Lungs -> pulmonary veins -> left atrium
- Left atrium -> mitral valve -> left ventricle
- Left ventricle -> aortic valve -> aorta -> body

**Why it matters:** This is the basic path you need for heart and oxygenation questions.

### Card 2

**Front:** What is the normal order of the cardiac conduction system?

**Back:**
- SA node starts the beat.
- AV node slows the signal briefly.
- Bundle of His carries the signal into the ventricles.
- Right and left bundle branches carry it down.
- Purkinje fibers spread it through the ventricles.

**Why it matters:** If you know the pathway, dysrhythmias make more sense.

### Card 3

**Front:** What do preload, afterload, contractility, and heart rate mean?

**Back:**
- **Preload:** how full the ventricle is before it squeezes.
- **Afterload:** how hard the heart has to push to get blood out.
- **Contractility:** how strongly the heart squeezes.
- **Heart rate:** how fast the heart beats.

**Why it matters:** These four ideas explain why cardiac output goes up or down.

### Card 4

**Front:** Why does cardiac output matter for oxygenation and perfusion?

**Back:**
- Cardiac output is how much blood the heart pumps.
- Good cardiac output helps carry oxygen to tissues.
- Low cardiac output can cause cool skin, fatigue, confusion, and low urine output.

**Why it matters:** Many nursing questions are really asking whether tissue perfusion is good enough.

## Assessing cardiovascular function

### Card 1

**Front:** What are the key techniques for assessing the cardiovascular system?

**Back:**
- Check blood pressure, heart rate, rhythm, oxygen saturation, and temperature.
- Look at skin color, temperature, and moisture.
- Check for edema and swollen neck veins.
- Feel pulses and capillary refill.
- Listen to heart sounds.
- Ask about chest pain, palpitations, shortness of breath, dizziness, and fatigue.
- Monitor urine output and mental status.

**Why it matters:** Good assessment helps you catch poor perfusion early.

### Card 2

**Front:** How do smoking, high blood pressure, nutrition, activity, and diabetes affect cardiovascular function?

**Back:**
- Smoking narrows and damages blood vessels.
- High blood pressure raises afterload and makes the heart work harder.
- Poor nutrition can worsen blocked arteries and high blood pressure.
- Activity supports circulation; inactivity raises venous stasis risk.
- Diabetes damages blood vessels and worsens tissue perfusion.

**Why it matters:** These are major modifiable cardiovascular risk factors.

## Manifestations of impaired cardiovascular function

### Card 1

**Front:** What vital sign changes can suggest impaired cardiovascular function?

**Back:**
- Tachycardia or bradycardia
- Hypertension or hypotension
- Irregular pulse
- Low oxygen saturation
- Increased respiratory rate when the body is compensating

**Why it matters:** Vital signs often change before the full problem is obvious.

### Card 2

**Front:** What findings can suggest heart muscle damage?

**Back:**
- Chest pain or pressure
- Shortness of breath
- Fatigue
- Nausea or sweating
- Weak pumping and lower cardiac output
- Dysrhythmias may happen

**Why it matters:** These can point to myocardial ischemia or infarction.

### Card 3

**Front:** What findings can suggest valve dysfunction?

**Back:**
- Murmur
- Fatigue
- Shortness of breath
- Edema
- Dizziness or syncope in more serious cases
- Signs of fluid backup if the problem is severe

**Why it matters:** Valve problems reduce efficient forward blood flow.

### Card 4

**Front:** What findings can suggest conduction problems?

**Back:**
- Palpitations
- Irregular pulse
- Dizziness
- Syncope
- Fatigue
- Shortness of breath
- Chest discomfort in some cases

**Why it matters:** Electrical problems can quickly affect cardiac output.

### Card 5

**Front:** What findings suggest blood flow alteration in arteries, capillaries, or tissue perfusion?

**Back:**
- **Arteries:** weak pulses, cool or pale skin, pain, numbness, delayed healing.
- **Capillaries:** delayed capillary refill, swelling, poor exchange of oxygen and nutrients.
- **Poor tissue perfusion:** confusion, low urine output, cool clammy skin, fatigue, delayed healing.

**Why it matters:** These findings help you connect circulation problems with what you see at the bedside.

## Conduction problems and dysrhythmias

### Card 1

**Front:** How do disturbances in conduction affect cardiac output?

**Back:**
- The heart may beat too fast, too slow, or irregularly.
- Ventricles may not fill well.
- Contractions may become less effective.
- Cardiac output can drop.
- Less blood reaches the body's tissues.

**Why it matters:** This is the link between rhythm problems and patient symptoms.

### Card 2

**Front:** What are the basic indicators of tachycardia and bradycardia?

**Back:**
- **Tachycardia:** above 100 beats/min in adults.
- **Bradycardia:** below 60 beats/min in adults.
- What matters most is whether the patient still has good perfusion.
- Worrying signs include dizziness, weakness, hypotension, chest pain, shortness of breath, or syncope.

**Why it matters:** A number matters, but symptoms and perfusion matter more.

### Card 3 (Priority)

**Front:** Why are ventricular tachycardia (VT) and ventricular fibrillation (VF) life-threatening?

**Back:**
- VT can make the ventricles pump so fast that output drops sharply.
- VF means the ventricles are quivering instead of pumping.
- VF causes no effective cardiac output.
- These rhythms can quickly lead to loss of perfusion and death.

**Why it matters:** VT and VF are major emergency rhythms.

## Venous stasis and edema

### Card 1

**Front:** What nursing actions help prevent venous stasis?

**Back:**
- Early ambulation
- Frequent repositioning
- Leg exercises and ankle pumps
- Avoid sitting or standing still for long periods
- Avoid crossing the legs for long periods
- Use SCDs or compression stockings if ordered
- Maintain hydration when appropriate
- Give anticoagulants as ordered for at-risk patients

**Why it matters:** Venous stasis raises the risk for clot formation.

### Card 2 (Compare)

**Front:** How do increased capillary permeability, increased hydrostatic pressure, and decreased oncotic pressure cause edema?

**Back:**
- **Increased capillary permeability:** capillaries get leaky, so fluid and proteins move into tissue.
- **Increased hydrostatic pressure:** pressure inside the vessel pushes fluid out into tissue.
- **Decreased oncotic pressure:** low protein in the blood means fluid is not pulled back into the vessel well.

**Why it matters:** These are the three big ways edema develops.

### Card 3

**Front:** What are the risks associated with edema?

**Back:**
- Skin breakdown
- Poor mobility and discomfort
- Slower wound healing
- Higher infection risk if skin opens
- Poor oxygen and nutrient movement into tissues
- Breathing problems if pulmonary edema develops

**Why it matters:** Edema can be a serious patient-safety problem, not just a comfort problem.

### Card 4

**Front:** What interventions help reduce edema?

**Back:**
- Elevate the swollen area when appropriate.
- Encourage ambulation and range-of-motion exercises.
- Reposition often.
- Use compression if ordered and appropriate.
- Monitor daily weights, intake and output, and lung sounds.
- Protect the skin.
- Limit sodium if ordered.
- Give diuretics as ordered.
- Treat the underlying cause.

**Why it matters:** Good edema care is about both symptom relief and cause control.

### Card 5 (Priority)

**Front:** A client with worsening edema suddenly develops crackles and shortness of breath. What should the nurse think first?

**Back:**
- Think about possible **pulmonary edema** or worsening fluid overload.
- Assess breathing, oxygenation, and lung sounds right away.
- Position the client upright if tolerated.
- Notify the provider and follow urgent orders.

**Why it matters:** Edema becomes an emergency when it starts affecting breathing.

### Card 6 (Application)

**Front:** An immobile client keeps the legs dependent all day and rarely walks. What complication risk should the nurse think about, and what helps prevent it?

**Back:**
- Think about **venous stasis** and possible clot formation.
- Prevention includes ambulation, leg exercises, repositioning, hydration if allowed, and compression devices if ordered.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic nursing prevention question.
