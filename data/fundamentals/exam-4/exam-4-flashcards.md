# Exam 4 Flashcards

These flashcards are grouped by the same sections as your Exam 4 outline and strengthened with nursing exam angles such as compare-and-contrast, priority, safety, and application questions.

## How to use this deck

- **Front** = the question side of the flashcard.
- **Back** = the answer side.
- **Why it matters** = a short memory or exam tip.

## Diabetes

### Card 1

**Front:** How is blood glucose normally regulated?

**Back:**
- Insulin lowers blood glucose.
- Glucagon raises blood glucose.
- The liver stores and releases glucose as needed.
- Stress hormones like cortisol and epinephrine can raise blood sugar.

**Why it matters:** This is the base concept under almost every diabetes question.

### Card 2

**Front:** What is the basic pathophysiology of diabetes?

**Back:**
- The body does not make enough insulin, cannot use insulin well, or both.
- Glucose stays in the blood instead of moving into the cells.
- Over time, high blood glucose damages blood vessels, nerves, kidneys, eyes, and immune function.

**Why it matters:** If you know this, the complications make more sense.

### Card 3

**Front:** How do Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes differ?

**Back:**
- Type 1: autoimmune destruction of beta cells, little or no insulin, higher DKA risk.
- Type 2: insulin resistance with relative insulin deficiency, stronger link to obesity and inactivity, higher HHS risk.

**Why it matters:** Nursing exams love this compare card.

### Card 4

**Front:** What fasting plasma glucose values are normal, prediabetes, and diabetes?

**Back:**
- Normal: 70 to 99 mg/dL
- Prediabetes: 100 to 125 mg/dL
- Diabetes: 126 mg/dL or higher on repeat testing

**Why it matters:** This is a classic threshold question.

### Card 5

**Front:** What does hemoglobin A1c show, and what is a common goal for many adults with diabetes?

**Back:**
- A1c shows average blood glucose over about 2 to 3 months.
- A common goal for many nonpregnant adults is less than 7%.

**Why it matters:** A1c tells you long-term control, not just today's glucose.

### Card 6

**Front:** What are priority nursing assessments for a client with diabetes?

**Back:**
- Blood glucose trends
- Food intake
- Signs of hypo- or hyperglycemia
- Hydration status
- Skin and wound healing
- Foot condition and sensation
- Vision changes
- Infection signs
- Mental status

**Why it matters:** This is the nursing-priority frame for diabetes care.

### Card 7

**Front:** What management steps are important in diabetes care besides medication?

**Back:**
- Glucose monitoring
- Weight management
- Activity and exercise
- Meal planning
- Foot care
- Follow-up care and teaching

**Why it matters:** Diabetes care is never just about insulin.

### Card 8

**Front:** What commonly causes hypoglycemia in a client with diabetes?

**Back:**
- Too much insulin or diabetes medicine
- Skipping meals
- Eating less than usual
- More exercise than usual without enough food
- Alcohol use without enough food

**Why it matters:** Causes help you prevent repeats.

### Card 9

**Front:** What are common signs and symptoms of hypoglycemia?

**Back:**
- Shaking
- Sweating
- Hunger
- Tachycardia
- Headache
- Irritability
- Weakness
- Confusion
- Blurred vision
- Seizures or loss of consciousness if severe

**Why it matters:** Hypoglycemia can become an emergency fast.

### Card 10 (Priority)

**Front:** A client is awake, shaky, sweaty, and has a glucose of 58 mg/dL. What should the nurse do first?

**Back:**
- Give a fast-acting carbohydrate if the client can swallow safely.
- Recheck glucose in 15 minutes.

**Why it matters:** Treat low sugar fast when the client is conscious and safe to swallow.

### Card 11

**Front:** Why are simple carbohydrates used for hypoglycemia?

**Back:**
- They absorb quickly.
- They raise blood glucose faster than fatty or high-protein foods.

**Why it matters:** The exam may ask why juice works faster than a sandwich.

### Card 12

**Front:** What is the rule of 15 for hypoglycemia?

**Back:**
- Give 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate.
- Wait 15 minutes.
- Recheck the glucose.
- Repeat if still low.

**Why it matters:** This is a very testable nursing action.

### Card 13

**Front:** What commonly causes hyperglycemia?

**Back:**
- Too little insulin
- Missed medication
- Illness or infection
- Stress
- Excess carbohydrates
- Steroid use

**Why it matters:** The cause often guides treatment.

### Card 14

**Front:** What are common signs and symptoms of hyperglycemia?

**Back:**
- Polyuria
- Polydipsia
- Polyphagia
- Warm dry skin
- Fatigue
- Blurred vision
- Dehydration
- Confusion in severe cases

**Why it matters:** These findings should make you think high glucose right away.

### Card 15

**Front:** What is the basic difference between carbohydrate counting and glycemic index teaching?

**Back:**
- Carbohydrate counting focuses on how much carbohydrate the client eats.
- Glycemic index focuses on how quickly a food raises blood glucose.

**Why it matters:** Both matter, but they are not the same thing.

### Card 16

**Front:** How does illness or stress affect blood sugar?

**Back:**
- Stress hormones like cortisol and epinephrine raise blood glucose.
- Clients often need closer monitoring during illness.

**Why it matters:** Sick-day questions are common.

### Card 17

**Front:** Why is ketonuria important in a client with diabetes?

**Back:**
- It shows the body is breaking down fat for energy.
- It is a warning sign of insulin deficiency and possible DKA.

**Why it matters:** Ketonuria is not something to ignore in diabetes.

### Card 18

**Front:** How do DKA and HHS differ?

**Back:**
- DKA: more common in Type 1, ketones present, metabolic acidosis present.
- HHS: more common in Type 2, very severe hyperglycemia and dehydration, little or no ketoacidosis.

**Why it matters:** This is one of the biggest compare-and-contrast cards in diabetes.

### Card 19

**Front:** Which client is most at risk for DKA?

**Back:**
- A client with Type 1 diabetes.

**Why it matters:** High-yield exam fact.

### Card 20

**Front:** Which client is most at risk for HHS?

**Back:**
- An older adult with Type 2 diabetes.

**Why it matters:** Also a high-yield exam fact.

### Card 21

**Front:** What are priority signs and symptoms of DKA?

**Back:**
- Hyperglycemia
- Ketonuria or ketonemia
- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal pain
- Dehydration
- Fruity breath
- Kussmaul respirations
- Confusion

**Why it matters:** DKA has a classic pattern.

### Card 22

**Front:** What are priority signs and symptoms of HHS?

**Back:**
- Very high blood glucose
- Severe dehydration
- Dry mucous membranes
- Tachycardia
- Hypotension
- Major neurologic changes such as confusion, seizure, or coma
- Little or no ketone signs

**Why it matters:** HHS often looks more neurologic and dehydration-heavy.

### Card 23

**Front:** Why does DKA cause metabolic acidosis?

**Back:**
- Fat breakdown produces acidic ketones.
- Ketones lower blood pH and bicarbonate.

**Why it matters:** This links diabetes to ABG interpretation.

### Card 24

**Front:** Why does DKA cause fluid volume deficit?

**Back:**
- High glucose causes osmotic diuresis.
- The client loses large amounts of water and electrolytes in the urine.

**Why it matters:** This explains why dehydration is so severe.

### Card 25

**Front:** Why is fluid replacement a priority in DKA and HHS?

**Back:**
- The client is often severely dehydrated.
- Fluids help improve blood pressure, perfusion, kidney function, and mental status.

**Why it matters:** The answer is not "insulin first" when the client is volume depleted.

### Card 26

**Front:** What IV fluid is commonly used first for DKA fluid resuscitation?

**Back:**
- 0.9% sodium chloride (normal saline), an isotonic fluid.

**Why it matters:** This is a very common exam question.

### Card 27

**Front:** What type of insulin is commonly used in DKA?

**Back:**
- Regular insulin, often by IV infusion.

**Why it matters:** Not every insulin can be given this way.

### Card 28

**Front:** Why is potassium such an important issue in DKA treatment?

**Back:**
- Total body potassium is usually low because of urine losses.
- Insulin pushes potassium into the cells.
- Potassium can drop quickly during treatment and cause dangerous dysrhythmias.

**Why it matters:** This is a life-safety point.

### Card 29

**Front:** What are common heart-related concerns with low and high potassium?

**Back:**
- Low potassium can cause dysrhythmias and flattened T waves.
- High potassium can cause dysrhythmias and peaked T waves.

**Why it matters:** Potassium problems can become fatal quickly.

### Card 30

**Front:** What are the main chronic diabetes complications?

**Back:**
- Depressed immune response
- Macrovascular disease: CAD, stroke, PAD
- Microvascular disease: retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy

**Why it matters:** Exams often group complications this way.

### Card 31

**Front:** What are priority safety actions for diabetic neuropathy?

**Back:**
- Inspect feet daily.
- Do not walk barefoot.
- Avoid burns on numb areas.
- Wear proper shoes.
- Use fall precautions if needed.

**Why it matters:** The main issue is lost protective sensation.

### Card 32

**Front:** What are priority safety actions for diabetic retinopathy?

**Back:**
- Keep pathways clear.
- Use good lighting.
- Keep needed items in consistent places.
- Assist with ambulation if vision is poor.

**Why it matters:** This is a safety-first question, not just an eye question.

### Card 33

**Front:** What is the difference between Somogyi effect and dawn phenomenon?

**Back:**
- Somogyi effect: rebound morning hyperglycemia after overnight hypoglycemia.
- Dawn phenomenon: morning hyperglycemia from early-morning hormones without overnight hypoglycemia.

**Why it matters:** Students mix these up a lot.

### Card 34 (Priority)

**Front:** A client has repeated morning hyperglycemia. What finding helps you think Somogyi effect instead of dawn phenomenon?

**Back:**
- A low blood glucose around 2 a.m. to 3 a.m.

**Why it matters:** Overnight lows point toward rebound hyperglycemia.

### Card 35

**Front:** What are key diabetic foot care teaching points?

**Back:**
- Inspect feet daily.
- Wash and dry carefully.
- Do not walk barefoot.
- Wear well-fitting shoes.
- Report cuts, blisters, redness, or drainage quickly.

**Why it matters:** This teaching helps prevent ulcers and amputation.

## Sensory Alterations

### Card 36

**Front:** Why does sensory function matter to a client's wellness?

**Back:**
- It supports safety, orientation, communication, independence, and daily functioning.

**Why it matters:** This is the big-picture answer for the whole section.

### Card 37

**Front:** What commonly causes sensory deprivation?

**Back:**
- Vision or hearing loss
- Isolation
- Quiet or monotonous surroundings
- Limited activity
- Lack of meaningful stimulation

**Why it matters:** The cause guides the intervention.

### Card 38

**Front:** What are common signs of sensory deprivation?

**Back:**
- Boredom
- Apathy
- Confusion
- Anxiety
- Restlessness
- Hallucinations in severe cases

**Why it matters:** Sensory deprivation can look like mental status change.

### Card 39

**Front:** What are priority nursing interventions for sensory deprivation?

**Back:**
- Reorient often.
- Provide clocks, calendars, glasses, and hearing aids.
- Encourage meaningful stimulation and family interaction.

**Why it matters:** The goal is helpful input, not random noise.

### Card 40

**Front:** What commonly causes sensory overload?

**Back:**
- Loud noise
- Bright lights
- Alarms
- Pain
- Frequent interruptions
- Too many people talking at once
- Sleep loss

**Why it matters:** This is common in hospital settings.

### Card 41

**Front:** What are common signs of sensory overload?

**Back:**
- Irritability
- Agitation
- Anxiety
- Fatigue
- Headache
- Difficulty concentrating
- Trouble sleeping

**Why it matters:** These are stress-overload clues.

### Card 42

**Front:** What are priority nursing interventions for sensory overload?

**Back:**
- Reduce noise and light.
- Cluster care.
- Limit unnecessary interruptions.
- Speak calmly and one person at a time.
- Promote rest.

**Why it matters:** The answer is usually to reduce stimulation, not add more.

### Card 43

**Front:** What home hazards matter most with impaired tactile sensation?

**Back:**
- Burns
- Cuts
- Pressure injuries
- Heat or cold injury not noticed right away

**Why it matters:** The client may not feel damage happening.

### Card 44

**Front:** What safety teaching helps a client with impaired tactile sensation?

**Back:**
- Check water temperature carefully.
- Avoid heating pads on numb areas.
- Inspect skin and feet every day.
- Wear protective footwear.

**Why it matters:** Loss of sensation means loss of early warning.

### Card 45

**Front:** What risks and safety measures matter for impaired smell?

**Back:**
- Risks: missing smoke, gas leaks, or spoiled food.
- Safety measures: smoke and gas detectors, careful food checks, good kitchen safety.

**Why it matters:** Smell changes can become home-safety problems.

### Card 46

**Front:** What home safety measures help a client with impaired vision?

**Back:**
- Keep pathways clear.
- Use good lighting.
- Keep needed items in the same place.
- Use large print or high contrast.
- Remove loose rugs and clutter.

**Why it matters:** Most answers focus on fall prevention and organization.

## Endocrine

### Card 47

**Front:** What key hormones are secreted by the pituitary, thyroid, parathyroid, adrenal glands, and pancreas?

**Back:**
- Pituitary: TSH, ACTH, GH, ADH, oxytocin, FSH, LH, prolactin
- Thyroid: T3, T4, calcitonin
- Parathyroid: PTH
- Adrenal: cortisol, aldosterone, androgens, epinephrine, norepinephrine
- Pancreas: insulin, glucagon

**Why it matters:** This is the hormone map for the whole endocrine section.

### Card 48

**Front:** What are common diagnostic thyroid tests?

**Back:**
- TSH
- Free T4
- T3
- Thyroid antibodies
- Thyroid ultrasound
- Radioactive iodine uptake or scan in some cases

**Why it matters:** Learn the basic tests before disease questions.

### Card 49

**Front:** How do hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism differ?

**Back:**
- Hypothyroidism slows the body down: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, bradycardia.
- Hyperthyroidism speeds the body up: weight loss, heat intolerance, tremor, tachycardia, diarrhea, anxiety.

**Why it matters:** This is another major compare card.

### Card 50

**Front:** What is Graves disease, and what are common manifestations?

**Back:**
- Graves disease is an autoimmune form of hyperthyroidism.
- Common findings: weight loss, heat intolerance, sweating, tremor, tachycardia, goiter, anxiety, and exophthalmos.

**Why it matters:** Graves disease is the classic hyperthyroid disorder.

### Card 51

**Front:** What findings suggest thyroid storm?

**Back:**
- High fever
- Severe tachycardia
- Agitation or delirium
- Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
- Possible heart failure or collapse

**Why it matters:** This is a life-threatening emergency.

### Card 52 (Priority)

**Front:** What is priority care for thyroid storm?

**Back:**
- Support airway, breathing, and circulation.
- Give oxygen and IV fluids as ordered.
- Reduce fever.
- Monitor cardiac rhythm.
- Give antithyroid drugs, iodine, and beta blockers as ordered.
- Treat the trigger.

**Why it matters:** Think emergency stabilization plus cause treatment.

### Card 53

**Front:** What do PTU, methimazole, and Lugol's solution do?

**Back:**
- PTU and methimazole reduce thyroid hormone production.
- PTU also reduces T4-to-T3 conversion.
- Lugol's solution reduces thyroid hormone release and gland vascularity short term.

**Why it matters:** This is a favorite medication comparison.

### Card 54

**Front:** Why are iodine preparations used before thyroid surgery in Graves disease?

**Back:**
- They help lower hormone release and reduce gland vascularity.
- This helps lower bleeding risk and lowers the risk of thyroid storm.

**Why it matters:** Preop purpose questions are common.

### Card 55

**Front:** Why are beta blockers used in hyperthyroidism?

**Back:**
- They control symptoms like tachycardia, palpitations, tremor, and anxiety.
- They do not fix the thyroid problem itself.

**Why it matters:** They treat the fast, overactive body response.

### Card 56

**Front:** What nursing care helps a client with exophthalmos?

**Back:**
- Protect the eyes from drying and injury.
- Use lubricating drops as ordered.
- Raise the head of the bed.
- Tape lids closed at night if needed.
- Report vision changes.

**Why it matters:** Eye care is a major Graves disease nursing issue.

### Card 57

**Front:** How can hyperthyroidism treatment lead to hypothyroidism?

**Back:**
- Radioactive iodine, surgery, or strong antithyroid treatment can reduce hormone production too much.

**Why it matters:** Treatment can swing the client to the opposite problem.

### Card 58

**Front:** What are priority complications to watch for after thyroidectomy?

**Back:**
- Airway obstruction from swelling or bleeding
- Hemorrhage
- Hypocalcemia from parathyroid injury
- Voice changes or laryngeal nerve injury

**Why it matters:** Post-thyroidectomy questions are high-yield safety questions.

### Card 59

**Front:** Why must the airway be assessed often after thyroidectomy?

**Back:**
- Neck swelling or bleeding can compress the airway quickly.

**Why it matters:** Airway comes first.

### Card 60

**Front:** Why is hypocalcemia a risk after thyroidectomy?

**Back:**
- The parathyroid glands can be injured, removed, or lose blood supply during surgery.

**Why it matters:** Low calcium can cause tetany and airway problems.

### Card 61 (Priority)

**Front:** A client after thyroidectomy has tingling around the mouth and a positive Trousseau sign. What does this mean, and what should the nurse do?

**Back:**
- It suggests hypocalcemia.
- Monitor the airway, report it, check calcium, and prepare to give calcium as ordered.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic postop priority scenario.

### Card 62

**Front:** Why may calcium gluconate be a priority medication after thyroidectomy?

**Back:**
- It treats symptomatic hypocalcemia and can reduce tetany and airway risk.

**Why it matters:** Know the reason, not just the drug name.

### Card 63

**Front:** What are common manifestations of hypothyroidism?

**Back:**
- Fatigue
- Weight gain
- Cold intolerance
- Dry skin
- Constipation
- Bradycardia
- Depression
- Slow thinking

**Why it matters:** Think "body slowed down."

### Card 64

**Front:** What is myxedema coma?

**Back:**
- A life-threatening severe hypothyroid state with hypothermia, bradycardia, hypotension, hypoventilation, and altered mental status.

**Why it matters:** This is the major emergency complication of hypothyroidism.

### Card 65 (Priority)

**Front:** What are priority nursing actions for myxedema coma?

**Back:**
- Support airway and breathing.
- Monitor cardiac rhythm and blood pressure.
- Warm gently.
- Give IV thyroid hormone as ordered.
- Monitor neurologic status, glucose, and electrolytes.

**Why it matters:** Think emergency support plus replacement therapy.

### Card 66

**Front:** What are key levothyroxine teaching points?

**Back:**
- Take it every day.
- Usually take it in the morning on an empty stomach.
- Do not stop it suddenly.
- Separate it from calcium, iron, and antacids if instructed.
- Report chest pain or palpitations.

**Why it matters:** Medication teaching is very testable.

### Card 67

**Front:** What labs and assessments matter most with levothyroxine therapy?

**Back:**
- Labs: TSH and free T4
- Assessments: heart rate, blood pressure, weight, bowel pattern, energy level, and signs of overreplacement

**Why it matters:** Safe monitoring matters as much as giving the drug.

### Card 68

**Front:** What causes Cushing syndrome, and what are common manifestations?

**Back:**
- Cause: too much cortisol, often from steroids or tumors.
- Findings: moon face, buffalo hump, central obesity, purple striae, easy bruising, hyperglycemia, hypertension, muscle weakness, poor healing, infection risk.

**Why it matters:** Cushing has a very recognizable pattern.

### Card 69

**Front:** How do exogenous and endogenous Cushing syndrome differ?

**Back:**
- Exogenous: caused by long-term corticosteroid use.
- Endogenous: caused by the body making too much cortisol, such as from pituitary or adrenal tumors.

**Why it matters:** The cause affects the treatment.

### Card 70

**Front:** What nursing care and diet teaching are important in Cushing syndrome?

**Back:**
- Monitor glucose, blood pressure, weight, skin, and infection.
- Protect from falls and skin breakdown.
- Teach high protein, low sodium, adequate calcium and vitamin D, and control of excess sugars.

**Why it matters:** The syndrome affects many body systems.

### Card 71

**Front:** Why is a client at risk for hypotension after adrenalectomy?

**Back:**
- Loss of cortisol and aldosterone can lead to sodium and water loss and lower blood pressure.

**Why it matters:** Postop adrenal questions often focus on hemodynamic risk.

### Card 72

**Front:** What hormones do the adrenal glands produce?

**Back:**
- Cortisol
- Aldosterone
- Adrenal androgens
- Epinephrine
- Norepinephrine

**Why it matters:** This supports Addison, Cushing, and crisis questions.

### Card 73

**Front:** Why might a client need corticosteroids after adrenalectomy?

**Back:**
- The body may not be able to make enough cortisol after the gland is removed, so replacement is needed.

**Why it matters:** Removal creates a hormone-replacement issue.

### Card 74

**Front:** What is Addison's disease, and what are common manifestations?

**Back:**
- Addison's disease is primary adrenal insufficiency.
- Findings: fatigue, weight loss, hypotension, weakness, hyperpigmentation, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia, and salt craving.

**Why it matters:** Think "not enough adrenal hormones."

### Card 75

**Front:** What teaching is important for a client with Addison's disease?

**Back:**
- Take steroid replacement exactly as ordered.
- Do not stop suddenly.
- Learn sick-day rules.
- Wear medical alert identification.
- Know signs of crisis.

**Why it matters:** Lifelong teaching prevents emergencies.

### Card 76

**Front:** What can trigger Addisonian crisis, and what findings suggest it?

**Back:**
- Triggers: infection, surgery, stress, vomiting, diarrhea, missed steroids.
- Findings: severe hypotension, shock, weakness, confusion, dehydration, hyperkalemia, hyponatremia, hypoglycemia.

**Why it matters:** This is the main emergency complication of Addison's disease.

### Card 77 (Priority)

**Front:** What is priority treatment for Addisonian crisis?

**Back:**
- Rapid IV fluids
- IV corticosteroids
- Close monitoring of sodium, potassium, glucose, blood pressure, and cardiac rhythm
- Treat the cause

**Why it matters:** Think shock treatment plus hormone replacement.

### Card 78

**Front:** How can hyperkalemia be treated in Addisonian crisis?

**Back:**
- Sodium polystyrene can help remove potassium.
- Insulin shifts potassium into cells.
- Dextrose may be needed with insulin to prevent hypoglycemia.

**Why it matters:** This combines endocrine and electrolyte management.

### Card 79

**Front:** What is diabetes insipidus, and how is it related to ADH?

**Back:**
- Diabetes insipidus happens when ADH is lacking or not working.
- The kidneys do not keep enough water, so the client makes large amounts of dilute urine.

**Why it matters:** DI is a water-loss problem, not a blood-sugar problem.

### Card 80

**Front:** What are common manifestations of diabetes insipidus?

**Back:**
- Polyuria
- Polydipsia
- Nocturia
- Very dilute urine
- Dehydration
- Hypernatremia
- Hypovolemia if severe

**Why it matters:** Think "too much water out."

### Card 81

**Front:** What does desmopressin do, and what risk must the nurse watch for?

**Back:**
- Desmopressin is synthetic ADH.
- It lowers urine output and helps retain water.
- Watch for fluid volume excess and hyponatremia.

**Why it matters:** The treatment can swing the client too far the other way.

### Card 82

**Front:** What IV fluid may be used for DI fluid replacement once the client is stable?

**Back:**
- A hypotonic fluid such as 0.45% sodium chloride may be used as ordered once stable.

**Why it matters:** This is a common fluid question.

### Card 83

**Front:** What is SIADH, and what happens to fluid and sodium balance?

**Back:**
- SIADH means too much ADH.
- The body holds water.
- Sodium becomes diluted, causing hyponatremia.
- The urine becomes more concentrated.

**Why it matters:** Think "too much water in."

### Card 84

**Front:** What are common signs of hyponatremia, and what is a serious complication?

**Back:**
- Nausea, headache, confusion, weakness, muscle cramps, decreased consciousness
- Serious complication: seizure or coma

**Why it matters:** Severe hyponatremia is a neurologic emergency.

### Card 85

**Front:** What are common treatments for SIADH?

**Back:**
- Fluid restriction
- Sodium monitoring
- Daily weights and strict intake/output
- Hypertonic saline in severe cases
- Loop diuretics or demeclocycline as ordered
- Treat the cause

**Why it matters:** Treatment focuses on less water and safer sodium.

### Card 86

**Front:** What teaching can help a client with chronic SIADH follow fluid restriction?

**Back:**
- Use small measured cups.
- Spread fluids through the day.
- Use ice chips, sugar-free gum, or hard candy if allowed.
- Do frequent mouth care.

**Why it matters:** Chronic SIADH teaching is practical and exam-friendly.

### Card 87

**Front:** Why are demeclocycline and furosemide used in SIADH?

**Back:**
- Demeclocycline reduces the kidney's response to ADH.
- Furosemide helps excrete more water.
- Both can help improve sodium concentration when used appropriately.

**Why it matters:** Know the purpose, not just the drug names.

### Card 88

**Front:** What is the transsphenoidal surgical approach, and why is it used?

**Back:**
- It reaches the pituitary gland through the nose and sphenoid sinus.
- It is used to remove pituitary tumors.

**Why it matters:** This is a key term in pituitary surgery questions.

### Card 89 (Priority)

**Front:** What postop teaching and complication checks are important after transsphenoidal surgery?

**Back:**
- Avoid coughing, nose blowing, bending, and straining.
- If sneezing happens, keep the mouth open.
- Watch for clear nasal drainage, DI, SIADH, meningitis, and vision changes.

**Why it matters:** This is mostly a complication-prevention question.

## Loss and Grief: End of Life care

### Card 90

**Front:** What is normal grief, and how does it differ from abnormal or complicated grief?

**Back:**
- Normal grief is painful but gradually becomes more manageable.
- Abnormal or complicated grief stays intense, lasts a long time, or severely blocks daily function.

**Why it matters:** The exam may ask you to recognize when grief needs more help.

### Card 91

**Front:** What are the Kubler-Ross stages of grief, and what important limit should you remember?

**Back:**
- Denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance
- Important limit: grief is not always linear and not everyone has all stages.

**Why it matters:** The stages help, but they are not strict rules.

### Card 92

**Front:** What factors affect how a person grieves?

**Back:**
- Relationship to the loss
- Age and development
- Culture and religion
- Sudden versus expected death
- Support system
- Previous losses
- Mental health history

**Why it matters:** Grief is individual.

### Card 93

**Front:** How do palliative care and hospice care differ?

**Back:**
- Palliative care can happen at any stage and can be given with curative treatment.
- Hospice focuses on comfort when cure is no longer the goal and life expectancy is limited.

**Why it matters:** Students often confuse these two.

### Card 94

**Front:** What are common physical signs that death may be near?

**Back:**
- More sleep and less responsiveness
- Less interest in food and fluids
- Trouble swallowing
- Decreased urine output
- Cool or mottled skin
- Irregular breathing or apnea
- Increased secretions

**Why it matters:** Family education often depends on these findings.

### Card 95

**Front:** What comfort measures are most important in end-of-life nursing care?

**Back:**
- Gentle positioning
- Frequent oral care
- Small amounts of nutrition or fluid only if wanted and safe
- Analgesics and symptom relief

**Why it matters:** Comfort, not force, is the goal.

### Card 96

**Front:** What are basic points in caring for the body after death?

**Back:**
- Follow policy and legal steps.
- Provide privacy.
- Handle the body gently and respectfully.
- Follow cultural and religious preferences when possible.
- Apply identification and prepare the body per policy.

**Why it matters:** Dignity continues after death.

### Card 97

**Front:** Why does a nurse need to reflect on personal feelings about death?

**Back:**
- Personal fear or discomfort can change communication and care.
- Self-awareness helps the nurse stay supportive and focused on the client's needs.

**Why it matters:** Good end-of-life care includes self-awareness.

### Card 98

**Front:** What nursing actions support clients and families with different cultural values at end of life?

**Back:**
- Ask about preferences instead of assuming.
- Respect rituals, privacy, touch, prayer, and family roles.
- Use interpreters when needed.
- Support spiritual or cultural practices when safe.

**Why it matters:** Cultural humility is part of safe, respectful care.

### Card 99

**Front:** What therapeutic communication techniques are most helpful in grief and end-of-life care?

**Back:**
- Presence
- Active listening
