# Exam 4 Application Questions

These practice questions are written to help you think through common Fundamentals Exam 4 situations in a calm, NCLEX-style way. Focus on safety, priority actions, red flags, teaching, and what the nurse should do first.

## How to use this file

- Try to answer each question before looking at the answer.
- Ask yourself **why** the best answer is safest or most important.
- For priority questions, think about airway, breathing, circulation, safety, and urgent complications.
- For SATA questions, choose **all** correct options.
- If you miss a question, read the explanation and look for the pattern behind it.

## Diabetes

### Question 1 (Assessment)
**Question:** A 19-year-old client with Type 1 diabetes comes to the emergency department with abdominal pain, vomiting, deep rapid breathing, and fruity-smelling breath. Which complication should the nurse suspect first?

A. Hypoglycemia  
B. Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)  
C. Hyperglycemic hyperosmolar syndrome (HHS)  
D. Dawn phenomenon

**Correct answer:** B. Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)

**Explanation:** Fruity breath, vomiting, and deep rapid breathing point to acid buildup from ketones, which is classic DKA in a young client with Type 1 diabetes. That makes DKA the best answer here. HHS is usually seen in older adults with Type 2 diabetes and little or no ketosis.

### Question 2 (First Action)
**Question:** A client with diabetes is awake, shaky, sweaty, and has a blood glucose of 58 mg/dL. What should the nurse do first?

A. Give 4 oz of fruit juice  
B. Administer regular insulin  
C. Call dietary for a full meal tray  
D. Recheck the glucose in 1 hour

**Correct answer:** A. Give 4 oz of fruit juice

**Explanation:** Shaky, sweaty, awake, and 58 mg/dL means the client needs sugar now. Because the client can swallow, 4 oz of juice is the safest first step. Insulin is the trap answer because it would drive the glucose even lower.

### Question 3 (Priority)
**Question:** A post-op client with diabetes is NPO, becomes confused, and has a blood glucose of 49 mg/dL. Which action is best?

A. Give peanut butter crackers  
B. Give IV dextrose as ordered  
C. Encourage the client to walk  
D. Hold all diabetes medicines until morning

**Correct answer:** B. Give IV dextrose as ordered

**Explanation:** The biggest clue is confusion with a glucose of 49 in a client who is NPO. That means the nurse should treat severe hypoglycemia quickly without using the mouth, so IV dextrose is the best action. Crackers may seem simple, but they are not safe when swallowing may be impaired.

### Question 4 (Complication Recognition)
**Question:** Which client is most likely experiencing hyperglycemic hyperosmolar syndrome (HHS)?

A. A 22-year-old with Type 1 diabetes, glucose 320 mg/dL, positive ketones, and deep rapid respirations  
B. A 68-year-old with Type 2 diabetes, glucose 780 mg/dL, severe dehydration, confusion, and minimal ketones  
C. A 45-year-old who skipped lunch, glucose 54 mg/dL, and tremors  
D. A 30-year-old with fasting glucose 128 mg/dL on repeat testing

**Correct answer:** B. A 68-year-old with Type 2 diabetes, glucose 780 mg/dL, severe dehydration, confusion, and minimal ketones

**Explanation:** Older age, Type 2 diabetes, very high glucose, dehydration, and confusion all point toward HHS. That pattern makes this client the best match. The option with ketones and deep rapid breathing fits DKA instead.

### Question 5 (Patient Teaching)
**Question:** A client with diabetes has influenza and says, "I am not eating much, so I will skip my insulin today." Which nurse response is best?

A. "That is fine if you drink juice instead."  
B. "You should usually keep taking insulin, check your glucose more often, and drink fluids if allowed."  
C. "Only take insulin if your glucose is above 300 mg/dL."  
D. "Stop insulin until your ketones are negative."

**Correct answer:** B. "You should usually keep taking insulin, check your glucose more often, and drink fluids if allowed."

**Explanation:** Illness often raises blood glucose even when the client is eating less. That is why sick-day teaching usually includes continuing insulin, checking glucose more often, and drinking fluids if allowed. Skipping insulin is a common test-day mistake because it can lead to DKA.

### Question 6 (Assessment)
**Question:** A client has repeated morning hyperglycemia. At 2:30 a.m., the blood glucose is 48 mg/dL. Which pattern does this finding suggest?

A. Dawn phenomenon  
B. Somogyi effect  
C. HHS  
D. Good long-term control

**Correct answer:** B. Somogyi effect

**Explanation:** A low glucose during the night followed by a high reading in the morning shows rebound hyperglycemia. That makes Somogyi effect the best answer. Dawn phenomenon can also cause a high morning glucose, but it does not come after overnight hypoglycemia.

### Question 7 (SATA)
**Question:** The nurse teaches foot care to a client with diabetic neuropathy. Which statements show correct understanding? Select all that apply.

A. "I will inspect my feet every day."  
B. "I can soak my feet in hot water to improve circulation."  
C. "I will dry well between my toes after washing."  
D. "I will wear shoes even inside the house."  
E. "I should put lotion between my toes if the skin is dry."

**Correct answer:** A, C, D

**Explanation:** Daily inspection, careful drying between the toes, and wearing shoes protect feet that may have less feeling. Those choices lower the risk of burns, skin breakdown, and small injuries. Hot soaking and lotion between the toes are tempting, but they can damage the skin.

### Question 8 (Safety)
**Question:** A client with diabetic retinopathy is preparing to go home. Which home change is best to reduce injury risk?

A. Move furniture often so the client stays alert  
B. Keep walkways clear and use bright, even lighting  
C. Turn lights off at night to promote sleep  
D. Store frequently used items in different places

**Correct answer:** B. Keep walkways clear and use bright, even lighting

**Explanation:** The key clue here is poor vision, so the home should be safe and easy to predict. Clear paths and bright, even lighting reduce falls and help the client move with more confidence. Moving furniture or storing items in new places would make the space harder to use safely.

## Sensory Alterations

### Question 9 (Assessment)
**Question:** An older adult has been hospitalized for 6 days in a quiet room with little family contact. The client is now disoriented and says, "I keep seeing shadows moving." Which problem is most likely?

A. Sensory overload  
B. Sensory deprivation  
C. Acute pain  
D. Hypoglycemia

**Correct answer:** B. Sensory deprivation

**Explanation:** Several quiet days with little contact can leave an older adult without enough meaningful stimulation. Confusion and seeing shadows fit sensory deprivation, so that is the best choice. Sensory overload is the opposite problem and happens when there is too much input.

### Question 10 (First Action)
**Question:** A client in a busy intensive care unit says the lights, alarms, and frequent interruptions are "too much" and cannot sleep. What should the nurse do first?

A. Turn on the TV for distraction  
B. Reduce noise and light and cluster care  
C. Ask several staff members to explain the plan at once  
D. Encourage more visitors for support

**Correct answer:** B. Reduce noise and light and cluster care

**Explanation:** The client is telling you the environment feels overwhelming, and the lack of sleep supports sensory overload. The safest first move is to lower noise, light, and interruptions so the brain can rest. More visitors, more talking, or TV would only add to the problem.

### Question 11 (Safety)
**Question:** A client has impaired tactile sensation in both feet. Which teaching is most important?

A. "Use a heating pad on your feet before bed."  
B. "Check bath water temperature with a thermometer or an area of skin with normal feeling."  
C. "Go barefoot at home so you can notice skin changes faster."  
D. "Wear tight socks to keep your feet warm."

**Correct answer:** B. "Check bath water temperature with a thermometer or an area of skin with normal feeling."

**Explanation:** When feeling is reduced, the client may not notice heat until a burn has already happened. Checking bath water with a thermometer or an area of skin with normal sensation is the safest teaching. Heating pads and barefoot walking are common trap answers because they raise injury risk.

### Question 12 (Safety)
**Question:** A client with impaired smell asks how to stay safe at home. Which response is best?

A. "Use scented candles to check whether you can still smell."  
B. "Install and test smoke and gas detectors and check food dates carefully."  
C. "Keep the kitchen windows closed so smells stay inside."  
D. "Taste food to see if it is spoiled."

**Correct answer:** B. "Install and test smoke and gas detectors and check food dates carefully."

**Explanation:** Loss of smell makes it harder to notice smoke, gas, or spoiled food. That is why detectors and careful checking of food dates are the safest advice. Tasting food to test it sounds practical, but it can be dangerous.

### Question 13 (Safety)
**Question:** Which home setup is safest for a client with low vision?

A. Loose rugs removed, night-lights added, and items kept in the same place  
B. Dim lighting used to reduce glare and objects moved away from the walls  
C. More decorative furniture placed near walkways  
D. Cleaning supplies stored in unlabeled containers to reduce clutter

**Correct answer:** A. Loose rugs removed, night-lights added, and items kept in the same place

**Explanation:** Low vision calls for a steady setup, good lighting, and fewer tripping hazards. Removing loose rugs, adding night-lights, and keeping items in the same place does all of that. Clutter or frequent changes in the room are common wrong-answer traps.

### Question 14 (SATA)
**Question:** The nurse plans care for a client with sensory deprivation. Which interventions are appropriate? Select all that apply.

A. Reorient the client often to person, place, and time  
B. Provide clocks, calendars, glasses, or hearing aids  
C. Limit all conversation so the client can rest  
D. Encourage familiar music or family visits if the client wants them  
E. Increase random noise to "stimulate the brain"

**Correct answer:** A, B, D

**Explanation:** This client needs meaningful, organized stimulation, not silence or random noise. Reorientation, clocks, calendars, glasses, hearing aids, and familiar music or family visits help the client stay connected and less confused. Isolation does not fix sensory deprivation.

## Endocrine

### Question 15 (Assessment)
**Question:** Which assessment finding is most consistent with Graves disease?

A. Weight gain, bradycardia, and dry skin  
B. Heat intolerance, tachycardia, tremor, and bulging eyes  
C. Cold intolerance, constipation, and puffy face  
D. Hypotension, bronze skin, and salt craving

**Correct answer:** B. Heat intolerance, tachycardia, tremor, and bulging eyes

**Explanation:** Heat intolerance, fast heart rate, tremor, and bulging eyes are classic signs of too much thyroid hormone. That pattern fits Graves disease best. The other choices describe hypothyroidism or adrenal disease instead.

### Question 16 (Priority)
**Question:** A client with hyperthyroidism suddenly has a temperature of 103.4°F (39.7°C), heart rate of 148/min, agitation, and vomiting. Which action is the nurse's priority?

A. Place warm blankets on the client and reduce fluids  
B. Treat this as thyroid storm: support airway, start cooling measures, and notify the provider rapidly  
C. Give levothyroxine as ordered PRN  
D. Encourage the client to walk to reduce anxiety

**Correct answer:** B. Treat this as thyroid storm: support airway, start cooling measures, and notify the provider rapidly

**Explanation:** A sudden high fever, severe tachycardia, agitation, and vomiting in a client with hyperthyroidism is a red-flag picture for thyroid storm. That makes airway support, cooling, and rapid notification the priority. Warm blankets or walking would waste time and make the situation less safe.

### Question 17 (First Action)
**Question:** Six hours after thyroidectomy, a client reports throat tightness. The nurse notes frequent swallowing and neck swelling. What should the nurse do first?

A. Offer ice chips and reassess in 30 minutes  
B. Support the airway and notify the provider or rapid response immediately  
C. Lower the head of the bed  
D. Ask the client to speak so voice can be checked

**Correct answer:** B. Support the airway and notify the provider or rapid response immediately

**Explanation:** Frequent swallowing with throat tightness and neck swelling after thyroidectomy suggests bleeding that may press on the airway. Because airway comes first, the nurse should support breathing and get rapid help right away. Waiting to reassess or offering ice chips could delay emergency care.

### Question 18 (Complication Recognition)
**Question:** After thyroidectomy, which finding suggests hypocalcemia?

A. Warm flushed skin and diarrhea  
B. Tingling around the mouth and a positive Trousseau sign  
C. Polyuria and intense thirst  
D. Bounding pulse and hypertension

**Correct answer:** B. Tingling around the mouth and a positive Trousseau sign

**Explanation:** Tingling around the mouth and a positive Trousseau sign are classic clues that calcium is low. After thyroid surgery, that can happen if the parathyroid glands were affected, so hypocalcemia is the best answer. This matters because the client can progress to tetany.

### Question 19 (Priority)
**Question:** A client with longstanding hypothyroidism is very sleepy, has a temperature of 95°F (35°C), heart rate of 42/min, blood pressure of 86/50 mm Hg, and respirations of 8/min. Which problem should the nurse suspect?

A. Addisonian crisis  
B. Thyroid storm  
C. Myxedema coma  
D. SIADH

**Correct answer:** C. Myxedema coma

**Explanation:** Very low temperature, slow pulse, low blood pressure, slow respirations, and decreased alertness all point to severe hypothyroidism. That cluster fits myxedema coma, which is an emergency. Thyroid storm would look much more fast and hot, not slow and cold.

### Question 20 (Patient Teaching)
**Question:** Which statement by a client taking levothyroxine shows correct understanding?

A. "I will take it with breakfast and my calcium tablet."  
B. "I will stop taking it when I start to feel better."  
C. "I will take it every morning on an empty stomach and report chest pain or palpitations."  
D. "I should switch brands often if one is expensive."

**Correct answer:** C. "I will take it every morning on an empty stomach and report chest pain or palpitations."

**Explanation:** Levothyroxine absorbs best on an empty stomach, so taking it first thing in the morning is the safe teaching point. Reporting chest pain or palpitations also matters because the dose may be too strong. Calcium with breakfast is a common trap because it can lower absorption.

### Question 21 (Assessment)
**Question:** Which postoperative finding should the nurse report immediately in a client who had an adrenalectomy for Cushing syndrome?

A. Blood pressure 82/50 mm Hg  
B. Blood glucose 162 mg/dL  
C. Mild incisional pain rated 3/10  
D. Bruising that was present before surgery

**Correct answer:** A. Blood pressure 82/50 mm Hg

**Explanation:** After adrenalectomy, very low blood pressure is the finding you cannot ignore. It may mean adrenal insufficiency and poor perfusion, so it needs immediate reporting. Mild pain or a moderate glucose reading is not as urgent.

### Question 22 (Patient Teaching)
**Question:** Which statement by a client with Addison disease shows a need for more teaching?

A. "I should wear medical alert identification."  
B. "I will never stop my steroid medicine suddenly."  
C. "If I am sick or under major stress, I may need more steroid as instructed."  
D. "Once I feel well, I can skip my steroid on busy days."

**Correct answer:** D. "Once I feel well, I can skip my steroid on busy days."

**Explanation:** The problem in that statement is skipping steroid doses when feeling well. Clients with Addison disease usually need lifelong replacement, and missed doses can lead to Addisonian crisis. Test questions often reward teaching about medical alert ID and extra steroid during stress.

### Question 23 (Priority)
**Question:** A client with Addison disease has vomiting, severe weakness, blood pressure 78/46 mm Hg, glucose 60 mg/dL, and potassium 6.0 mEq/L. Which action is best?

A. Encourage oral fluids and rest  
B. Treat as Addisonian crisis with rapid IV isotonic fluids and IV hydrocortisone as ordered  
C. Restrict fluids and give a loop diuretic  
D. Place the client flat and wait for repeat labs

**Correct answer:** B. Treat as Addisonian crisis with rapid IV isotonic fluids and IV hydrocortisone as ordered

**Explanation:** Vomiting, severe weakness, hypotension, low glucose, and high potassium show a client in Addisonian crisis. The safest response is rapid isotonic IV fluids and IV hydrocortisone as ordered. Oral fluids or waiting for more data is too weak for this emergency.

### Question 24 (Assessment)
**Question:** A client after pituitary surgery has urine output of 1100 mL over 4 hours, intense thirst, dry mucous membranes, and very dilute urine. Which disorder should the nurse suspect?

A. SIADH  
B. Diabetes insipidus  
C. Hypothyroidism  
D. Cushing syndrome

**Correct answer:** B. Diabetes insipidus

**Explanation:** Very large amounts of dilute urine plus intense thirst and dry mucous membranes point to major water loss. That pattern fits diabetes insipidus, not SIADH. The danger is falling blood volume from losing too much free water.

### Question 25 (SATA)
**Question:** Which nursing actions are appropriate for a client with SIADH? Select all that apply.

A. Restrict fluids as ordered  
B. Monitor daily weights  
C. Encourage large amounts of water  
D. Use seizure precautions if sodium is very low  
E. Monitor neurologic status for confusion

**Correct answer:** A, B, D, E

**Explanation:** With SIADH, the body holds on to water and sodium gets diluted. That is why fluid restriction, daily weights, neurologic checks, and seizure precautions are appropriate. Encouraging extra water would make the problem worse, not better.

### Question 26 (Report to Provider)
**Question:** Which finding after transsphenoidal pituitary surgery should the nurse report immediately?

A. Mild sore throat when swallowing  
B. Small amount of dried blood on the mustache dressing  
C. Clear watery drainage from the nose  
D. Need for mouth care every few hours

**Correct answer:** C. Clear watery drainage from the nose

**Explanation:** Clear watery drainage from the nose after transsphenoidal surgery is the key red flag here. It may be cerebrospinal fluid, so the nurse should report it right away because infection risk is serious. Mild sore throat and a little old blood are more expected findings.

## Loss and Grief: End of Life Care

### Question 27 (Assessment)
**Question:** Which client shows complicated grief and may need additional referral or urgent follow-up?

A. A spouse who is crying and sleeping poorly 2 weeks after a death  
B. An adult child who says grief comes in waves and accepts family support  
C. A partner who, months after the death, cannot function, feels hopeless every day, and talks about wanting to die  
D. A parent who becomes quiet during funeral planning and asks for time alone

**Correct answer:** C. A partner who, months after the death, cannot function, feels hopeless every day, and talks about wanting to die

**Explanation:** Months later, this person still cannot function, feels hopeless every day, and talks about wanting to die. Those are danger signs of complicated grief and possible suicide risk, so extra follow-up is needed. Early crying, poor sleep, or wanting some time alone can still fit normal grief.

### Question 28 (Referral)
**Question:** Which client is the best candidate for palliative care rather than hospice?

A. A client with terminal illness who has stopped all disease-directed treatment and is expected to live only a few weeks  
B. A client with metastatic cancer receiving chemotherapy who wants better pain and symptom control  
C. A healthy older adult who wants to review advance directives  
D. A client who died 1 hour ago and needs postmortem care

**Correct answer:** B. A client with metastatic cancer receiving chemotherapy who wants better pain and symptom control

**Explanation:** The key clue is that the client still wants chemotherapy but also needs better symptom control. Palliative care fits that situation because it can be given along with disease-directed treatment. Hospice is usually chosen when comfort is the main goal and life expectancy is limited.

### Question 29 (Therapeutic Communication)
**Question:** A family member says, "I don't know how we can get through this." Which nurse response is best?

A. "Everything happens for a reason."  
B. "You need to stay strong for the patient."  
C. "This is very hard. Tell me what worries you most right now."  
D. "At least your loved one lived a long life."

**Correct answer:** C. "This is very hard. Tell me what worries you most right now."

**Explanation:** The best response starts by naming the emotion and opening the door for more sharing. That is why the therapeutic answer uses empathy and an open-ended question. Clichés or telling someone to stay strong often shut the conversation down.

### Question 30 (Patient and Family Teaching)
**Question:** A family asks about changes they may see in the last hours of life. Which nurse response is best?

A. "If the patient stops eating, we must start force-feeding to prevent suffering."  
B. "Cool skin, less urine, more sleep, and irregular breathing can happen as the body slows down."  
C. "Noisy breathing always means the patient is in severe pain."  
D. "These changes mean the patient is improving and saving energy."

**Correct answer:** B. "Cool skin, less urine, more sleep, and irregular breathing can happen as the body slows down."

**Explanation:** Cool skin, less urine, more sleep, and irregular breathing are common signs that the body is slowing down. Giving that simple explanation helps the family know what to expect and keeps the focus on comfort. Force-feeding or saying noisy breathing always means pain would be misleading.

### Question 31 (Comfort Care)
**Question:** A dying client has a very dry mouth and a weak swallow. Which nursing action is best for comfort?

A. Offer large glasses of water every hour  
B. Insert a feeding tube right away  
C. Provide frequent oral care with moist swabs and lip moisturizer  
D. Encourage the family to insist that the client eat

**Correct answer:** C. Provide frequent oral care with moist swabs and lip moisturizer

**Explanation:** The key problem is dry mouth, but the weak swallow means large drinks are not safe. Frequent oral care with moist swabs and lip moisturizer gives comfort without raising aspiration risk. Feeding tubes or pressure to eat miss the comfort goal.

### Question 32 (Cultural Sensitivity)
**Question:** The nurse is caring for a dying client from a culture unfamiliar to the staff. What is the best nursing action?

A. Follow the unit's usual routine because it is safest  
B. Ask the client and family about preferred rituals, touch, privacy, and who should be present  
C. Avoid discussing spiritual practices because it may offend the family  
D. Wait for the chaplain to ask all cultural questions

**Correct answer:** B. Ask the client and family about preferred rituals, touch, privacy, and who should be present

**Explanation:** When the staff is unfamiliar with a client's culture, the safest step is to ask instead of assume. Talking with the client and family about rituals, touch, privacy, and who should be present keeps care respectful and individual. Waiting for someone else to ask can delay good care.

### Question 33 (Postmortem Care)
**Question:** After death is confirmed, what action is most appropriate?

A. Remove all tubes immediately, even if policy does not allow it  
B. Provide privacy, support the family, and prepare the body according to policy and any cultural or religious wishes  
C. Leave the body as it is until housekeeping arrives  
D. Ask the family to clean and position the body

**Correct answer:** B. Provide privacy, support the family, and prepare the body according to policy and any cultural or religious wishes

**Explanation:** After death, the nurse's job is to protect dignity, support the family, and follow policy. That is why providing privacy and preparing the body according to policy and any cultural or religious wishes is best. Removing tubes right away is a common trap because rules and circumstances vary.

## Diabetes

### Question 34 (Medication Safety)
**Question:** A client with Type 1 diabetes is scheduled to receive rapid-acting insulin with breakfast. The breakfast tray has not arrived yet. What should the nurse do?

A. Give the insulin now so the glucose does not rise  
B. Hold the insulin until the meal is present and the client is ready to eat  
C. Replace the rapid-acting insulin with long-acting insulin  
D. Give half the dose now and half after the meal

**Correct answer:** B. Hold the insulin until the meal is present and the client is ready to eat

**Explanation:** Rapid-acting insulin works quickly, so timing is the key clue. If the meal is not there yet, the safest plan is to wait until food is present and the client is ready to eat. Giving it early can cause hypoglycemia if the tray is delayed.

### Question 35 (Patient Teaching)
**Question:** A client who self-injects insulin says, "I always use the exact same spot in my abdomen because it hurts less." Which nurse response is best?

A. "That is the best way to keep insulin absorption predictable."  
B. "You should move around within the same general area instead of using the exact same spot every time."  
C. "You should switch to your thigh for every injection from now on."  
D. "It is only a problem if you reuse needles."

**Correct answer:** B. "You should move around within the same general area instead of using the exact same spot every time."

**Explanation:** Using the exact same spot over and over can cause tissue changes that make absorption less reliable. The better coaching is to rotate within the same general area. Switching to a totally different body area every time can also change absorption too much.

### Question 36 (Priority)
**Question:** A client arrives with suspected diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). The client is hypotensive, very dehydrated, and has Kussmaul respirations. Which collaborative action should the nurse expect first?

A. Start IV regular insulin immediately  
B. Begin isotonic IV fluids  
C. Restrict fluids to avoid overload  
D. Give oral potassium supplements

**Correct answer:** B. Begin isotonic IV fluids

**Explanation:** The client is hypotensive and very dehydrated, so circulation is the first problem to support. In DKA, isotonic IV fluids usually come before insulin because they help restore perfusion. Fluid restriction would move care in the wrong direction.

### Question 37 (Safety)
**Question:** A client with DKA is about to start an insulin infusion. The latest potassium level is 3.1 mEq/L. Which action is best?

A. Start the insulin infusion because glucose control is the only priority  
B. Hold insulin and replace potassium as ordered before starting the infusion  
C. Stop all IV fluids until the potassium rises  
D. Encourage the client to eat a banana and recheck later

**Correct answer:** B. Hold insulin and replace potassium as ordered before starting the infusion

**Explanation:** A potassium of 3.1 mEq/L is a major safety clue before starting insulin. Insulin pushes potassium into cells, so the level could fall even lower and trigger dangerous dysrhythmias. Holding insulin and replacing potassium first is the safest choice.

## Sensory Alterations

### Question 38 (Communication)
**Question:** The nurse is teaching a client with hearing loss about a new medication. Which action is best?

A. Face the client, reduce background noise, and speak clearly at a normal pace  
B. Stand behind the client and speak loudly  
C. Cover the mouth while speaking so the client focuses on the words  
D. Give the teaching quickly so the client does not get tired

**Correct answer:** A. Face the client, reduce background noise, and speak clearly at a normal pace

**Explanation:** Good teaching starts with a setup the client can follow. Facing the client, lowering background noise, and speaking clearly at a normal pace makes it easier to hear and read lips. Shouting, covering your mouth, or rushing usually makes communication worse.

### Question 39 (SATA)
**Question:** The nurse admits a client with low vision to a new hospital room. Which nursing actions promote safety and orientation? Select all that apply.

A. Introduce yourself each time you enter the room

D. Rearrange the furniture often so the client learns the room layout faster

**Explanation:** Safety grows when the room is explained clearly and kept consistent. Introducing yourself, pointing out the call light and bathroom, and leaving needed items in the same place helps the client stay oriented. Rearranging furniture or discouraging help would increase risk.

### Question 40 (Home Safety)
**Question:** Which home safety teaching is most important for a client with significant hearing loss who lives alone?

B. "Install alert devices such as smoke alarms that use flashing lights or vibration."

## Endocrine

### Question 41 (Airway Emergency)
**Question:** Twelve hours after thyroidectomy, a client becomes restless and develops high-pitched inspiratory stridor. What should the nurse do first?

A. Offer sips of water to soothe the throat  
B. Support the airway, apply oxygen, and notify the provider or rapid response immediately  
C. Ask the client to cough forcefully to clear secretions  
D. Reassess again after giving pain medicine

**Correct answer:** B. Support the airway, apply oxygen, and notify the provider or rapid response immediately

**Explanation:** High-pitched stridor after thyroidectomy means the airway may be closing. That makes oxygen, airway support, and rapid help the priority. Water, coughing, or waiting are dangerous delays in an ABC problem.

### Question 42 (Complication Recognition)
**Question:** Twenty-four hours after thyroidectomy, a client reports tingling around the mouth and hand cramping. Which action is best?

A. Document this as expected postoperative discomfort  
B. Encourage coughing and deep breathing  
C. Notify the provider, check calcium, and prepare to give calcium gluconate as ordered  
D. Give the next dose of levothyroxine early

**Correct answer:** C. Notify the provider, check calcium, and prepare to give calcium gluconate as ordered

**Explanation:** Tingling around the mouth and hand cramping point to symptomatic hypocalcemia after thyroidectomy. The safest next step is to report it, check calcium, and get ready to treat before the client moves toward tetany or seizures. It is more serious than routine postop discomfort.

### Question 43 (Priority)
**Question:** An older adult with severe hypothyroidism is admitted with pneumonia. The client is very hard to arouse, temperature is 94.8°F (34.9°C), heart rate is 40/min, blood pressure is 84/48 mm Hg, and respirations are 7/min. What is the nurse's priority?

A. Maintain airway and breathing support and notify the provider rapidly  
B. Encourage the client to ambulate to warm up  
C. Place the client on a cooling blanket  
D. Give a sedative so the client can rest

**Correct answer:** A. Maintain airway and breathing support and notify the provider rapidly

**Explanation:** This client is cold, slow, hypotensive, and barely arousable, which is a classic myxedema coma picture. Because respirations are only 7/min, airway and breathing support must come first. Comfort measures or activity would ignore the most urgent threat.

## Loss and Grief: End of Life Care

### Question 44 (Family Support)
**Question:** A family member says, "He has stopped eating. Are we starving him?" Which nurse response is best?

A. "You need to keep feeding him so he does not suffer."  
B. "Loss of appetite is common near the end of life. We can focus on comfort, mouth care, and small sips only if he wants them and can swallow safely."  
C. "This means death will happen in the next hour."  
D. "We should place a feeding tube right away."

**Correct answer:** B. "Loss of appetite is common near the end of life. We can focus on comfort, mouth care, and small sips only if he wants them and can swallow safely."

### Question 45 (Therapeutic Communication)
**Question:** In the last hours of life, a client's breathing becomes noisy and irregular. The family looks frightened. What should the nurse say first?

A. "This always means the client is choking."  
B. "Let me explain what may be happening and what we can do to keep your loved one comfortable."  
C. "There is nothing more to do now."  
D. "You should leave the room so the client can rest."

**Correct answer:** B. "Let me explain what may be happening and what we can do to keep your loved one comfortable."

**Explanation:** The family needs calm guidance before anything else. This response explains that the nurse will talk through what is happening and keep the client comfortable, which lowers fear and builds trust. Saying the client is choking or that nothing can be done would make the moment harder.
