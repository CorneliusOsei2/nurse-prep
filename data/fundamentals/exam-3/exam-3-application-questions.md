# Fundamentals Exam 3 Application Questions

These practice questions are made to help you apply Exam 3 ideas in realistic, NCLEX-style situations. Focus on safety, priority actions, abnormal findings, and the reason behind the best answer.

## How to use this file

- Try each question before reading the answer.
- Look for key clues like airway, breathing, urine output, pain, confusion, and sudden change.
- If a question asks for the **first** or **best** action, think about safety and the most urgent need.
- Read the explanation even when you get the question right.

## Acid-Base Imbalance

### Question 1 (ABG Interpretation)
**Question:** A client with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease becomes drowsy after receiving several doses of opioid pain medication. The arterial blood gas results are pH 7.30, PaCO2 55 mm Hg, and HCO3 25 mEq/L. Which acid-base imbalance does the nurse identify?

A. Respiratory acidosis  
B. Respiratory alkalosis  
C. Metabolic acidosis  
D. Metabolic alkalosis

**Correct answer:** A. Respiratory acidosis

**Explanation:** The key clue is low pH with a high PaCO2 and a normal HCO3. That pattern means the problem starts in the lungs, not the kidneys. After several opioid doses, hypoventilation is the likely cause, so respiratory acidosis is the best answer.

### Question 2 (First Action)
**Question:** A client is hyperventilating during a panic episode. The arterial blood gas results are pH 7.49, PaCO2 30 mm Hg, and HCO3 24 mEq/L. What is the nurse's best first action?

A. Ask the client to breathe into a paper bag  
B. Stay with the client and coach slow breathing  
C. Start a sodium bicarbonate infusion  
D. Encourage the client to walk in the hallway

**Correct answer:** B. Stay with the client and coach slow breathing

**Explanation:** The client is blowing off too much CO2, which fits respiratory alkalosis from panic hyperventilation. First, the nurse should stay present and coach slow breathing to bring the pattern back under control safely. Breathing into a paper bag can sound familiar, but it is not the safest first move if anxiety is not the only issue.

### Question 3 (Clinical Recognition)
**Question:** A client with diabetic ketoacidosis has deep, rapid respirations. What should the nurse understand about this finding?

A. It means the client is developing metabolic alkalosis  
B. It is a normal sleep-related breathing pattern  
C. It is the body's attempt to compensate for metabolic acidosis  
D. It shows that the client no longer needs insulin

**Correct answer:** C. It is the body's attempt to compensate for metabolic acidosis

**Explanation:** Deep, rapid respirations in diabetic ketoacidosis are a compensation sign. The body is trying to lower CO2 to offset the metabolic acidosis. That is why this finding points to compensation, not recovery or a reason to stop insulin.

### Question 4 (SATA)
**Question:** A client has had severe watery diarrhea for 2 days. Which findings support metabolic acidosis with fluid volume loss? **Select all that apply.**

A. Dry mucous membranes with urine output of 20 mL/hr  
B. Deep, rapid respirations  
C. Serum potassium of 3.0 mEq/L  
D. Arterial blood gas with pH 7.50 and HCO3 31 mEq/L

**Correct answer:** A, B, C

**Explanation:** Two big clues here are severe diarrhea and signs of dehydration. Diarrhea can cause loss of bicarbonate, water, and potassium, so metabolic acidosis with fluid volume loss makes sense. Choices A, B, and C fit that picture, while the ABG in D shows alkalosis instead.

## Client Education

### Question 5 (Priority)
**Question:** The nurse plans to teach a client with a new ileostomy how to change the pouch. The client reports pain of 8/10 and keeps closing the eyes between questions. What should the nurse do first?

A. Give the written handout and continue teaching  
B. Ask a family member to complete the teaching  
C. Treat the pain and return when the client is more ready to learn  
D. Teach quickly before the next medication round

**Correct answer:** C. Treat the pain and return when the client is more ready to learn

**Explanation:** Pain rated 8/10 and drifting off between questions show the client is not ready to learn. Good teaching starts when the client can focus and take in the information. Treating the pain first is the safest choice, while rushed teaching or just handing over papers is unlikely to stick.

### Question 6 (Evaluation)
**Question:** Which action best evaluates learning in the psychomotor domain for self-injection of insulin?

A. The client says, “I understand why I need insulin.”  
B. The client lists two symptoms of hypoglycemia.  
C. The client says, “I feel nervous about giving myself a shot.”  
D. The client draws up the insulin and gives the injection correctly.

**Correct answer:** D. The client draws up the insulin and gives the injection correctly.

**Explanation:** The psychomotor domain is about performing a skill, not just talking about it. A correct return demonstration shows the client can actually prepare and give the insulin safely. The other options measure knowledge or feelings, not hands-on ability.

### Question 7 (Teaching and Communication)
**Question:** A client with limited English needs discharge teaching about a new blood thinner. Which nursing action best supports safe learning and adherence?

A. Ask the adult daughter to interpret all instructions  
B. Speak louder and repeat the same words  
C. Use a professional interpreter and ask the client to teach back the key points  
D. Give the client a long printed packet to read at home

**Correct answer:** C. Use a professional interpreter and ask the client to teach back the key points

**Explanation:** The safety clue is limited English with important discharge teaching about a high-risk medication. A professional interpreter helps the client receive accurate information, and teach-back shows whether the key points were truly understood. Relying on family or a long packet can leave dangerous gaps.

## Urinary Elimination

### Question 8 (Priority)
**Question:** The nurse receives morning report. Which client should the nurse assess first?

A. A client who voided 600 mL once during the night  
B. A client with an indwelling catheter and urine output of 25 mL in the last hour  
C. A client with stress incontinence who leaked urine when coughing  
D. A client who needs help collecting a clean-catch specimen

**Correct answer:** B. A client with an indwelling catheter and urine output of 25 mL in the last hour

**Explanation:** Urine output of 25 mL in an hour is the red flag in this report. That low output can mean poor kidney perfusion, dehydration, or a catheter problem, so it needs early assessment. The other clients have needs, but they are not as urgent.

### Question 9 (Teaching)
**Question:** A client says, “I leak urine when I laugh or sneeze.” Which teaching is best?

A. Try to delay voiding as long as possible  
B. Perform pelvic floor (Kegel) exercises regularly  
C. Limit all fluids after 10 a.m.  
D. Use an indwelling catheter during the day

**Correct answer:** B. Perform pelvic floor (Kegel) exercises regularly

**Explanation:** Leaking with laughing or sneezing points to stress incontinence. Kegel exercises strengthen the pelvic floor and address the cause, which makes this the best teaching. Long delays in voiding, severe fluid restriction, or daytime catheter use can create more problems.

### Question 10 (Assessment)
**Question:** A postoperative client voids 75 mL but still reports suprapubic pressure and the feeling of “not emptying.” What is the best next action?

A. Encourage another glass of water and reassess tomorrow  
B. Use a bladder scanner to check the post-void residual volume  
C. Document the finding as expected after surgery  
D. Insert an indwelling catheter without further assessment

**Correct answer:** B. Use a bladder scanner to check the post-void residual volume

**Explanation:** The important clue is that the client voided only a small amount and still feels pressure. That raises concern for urinary retention, so a bladder scan is the best next step. NCLEX often rewards the least invasive assessment before moving to catheterization.

### Question 11 (Specimen Collection)
**Question:** The nurse needs a urine culture from a client with an indwelling catheter. Which action is correct?

A. Disconnect the catheter from the drainage tubing and collect urine  
B. Take urine from the drainage bag because it has the largest amount  
C. Clean the sampling port and obtain the specimen from the port using sterile technique  
D. Clamp the tubing for 12 hours, then pour urine into a clean cup

**Correct answer:** C. Clean the sampling port and obtain the specimen from the port using sterile technique

**Explanation:** For a urine culture, the goal is a clean specimen without breaking the closed system. The sampling port is the correct place to collect it after cleaning and using sterile technique. Taking urine from the bag is a common trap because it increases contamination.

### Question 12 (Recognition)
**Question:** An older adult is brought to the clinic for new confusion, weakness, and poor appetite. Temperature is 37.2°C (99°F). Which statement best explains why the nurse suspects a urinary tract infection?

A. Older adults must have a high fever for a urinary tract infection  
B. New confusion can be an atypical sign of urinary tract infection in older adults  
C. Urinary tract infections always cause severe flank pain first  
D. Mild weakness is a normal part of aging

**Correct answer:** B. New confusion can be an atypical sign of urinary tract infection in older adults

**Explanation:** In older adults, urinary tract infections do not always look dramatic. New confusion, weakness, and poor appetite can be the main clues even without a high fever. That makes option B the best explanation of the nurse's concern.

### Question 13 (SATA)
**Question:** A client has continuous bladder irrigation after prostate surgery. Which findings require immediate nursing action? **Select all that apply.**

A. The client reports suprapubic pain and bladder pressure  
B. The drainage bag suddenly stops filling  
C. The urine is light pink  
D. The tubing has a visible kink

**Correct answer:** A, B, D

**Explanation:** Continuous bladder irrigation must flow freely, so pain, pressure, no drainage, and a kink all matter right away. Those findings suggest obstruction and possible clot retention. Light pink urine can be expected early, which is why C is not the urgent choice.

## Sleep

### Question 14 (Safety and Teaching)
**Question:** An older adult asks for a sleeping pill every night in the hospital. Which response by the nurse is best?

A. “That is the safest way to help older adults sleep.”  
B. “Sleep medicines can increase falls and confusion, so we should try non-drug steps first.”  
C. “You should avoid all movement in the evening.”  
D. “Taking alcohol with the pill will make it work better.”

**Correct answer:** B. “Sleep medicines can increase falls and confusion, so we should try non-drug steps first.”

**Explanation:** The key clue is that the client is an older adult asking for a sleep medicine every night. In this age group, sedatives can increase confusion and falls, so the safer plan is to try non-drug measures first. NCLEX often favors the option that lowers harm before adding medication.

### Question 15 (Assessment)
**Question:** Which client finding should the nurse follow up first as possible sleep apnea?

A. Wakes after drinking coffee at dinner  
B. Has vivid dreams during REM sleep  
C. Snores loudly, has pauses in breathing at night, and is sleepy during the day  
D. Moves the legs sometimes before falling asleep

**Explanation:** Loud snoring, pauses in breathing, and daytime sleepiness form the classic sleep apnea pattern. This matters because the problem affects breathing and oxygenation during sleep. The other choices describe common sleep issues, but they do not fit apnea as well.

## Bowel Elimination

### Question 16 (Safety and Priority)
**Question:** A client who has been taking antibiotics has had 4 watery stools in 24 hours. What is the nurse's best first action?

A. Give an antidiarrheal medication right away  
B. Place the client on contact precautions and notify the provider  
C. Encourage the client to drink milk with meals  
D. Document the stools as an expected side effect only

**Correct answer:** B. Place the client on contact precautions and notify the provider

**Explanation:** Recent antibiotic use plus watery stools should make the nurse think about an infectious diarrhea problem. The first priority is preventing spread and getting the provider involved, so contact precautions come first. Giving an antidiarrheal right away can be a trap because it ignores the infection risk.

### Question 17 (Best Action)
**Question:** During a large-volume cleansing enema, the client reports abdominal cramping. What should the nurse do?

A. Increase the flow rate so the enema finishes quickly  
B. Stop or slow the flow, ask the client to breathe slowly, and restart gently when cramping improves  
C. Remove the tube immediately and discard the solution  
D. Turn the client supine

**Correct answer:** B. Stop or slow the flow, ask the client to breathe slowly, and restart gently when cramping improves

**Explanation:** Cramping during an enema usually means the solution is infusing too quickly. Slowing or stopping the flow gives the bowel time to relax and makes the procedure safer. Pushing the fluid faster would worsen the discomfort instead of fixing it.

### Question 18 (Teaching)
**Question:** Which statement by a client with a new ileostomy shows correct understanding?

A. “My stool will usually be formed, so dehydration is not a big concern.”  
B. “I should expect liquid output and watch closely for signs of fluid loss.”  
C. “A pale, dry stoma is normal during healing.”  
D. “I only need to empty the pouch once a day.”

**Correct answer:** B. “I should expect liquid output and watch closely for signs of fluid loss.”

**Explanation:** With an ileostomy, output is often liquid, so dehydration is a real risk. The correct teaching is to watch the amount and look for signs of fluid loss. Formed stool and a pale, dry stoma would not be expected findings.

### Question 19 (Assessment)
**Question:** Which ostomy assessment finding should the nurse report immediately?

A. Stoma is pink and moist  
B. Small amount of gas in the pouch  
C. Stoma is dark purple and dry  
D. Output is liquid on the first postoperative day after an ileostomy

**Correct answer:** C. Stoma is dark purple and dry

**Explanation:** A healthy stoma should be pink or red and moist. Dark purple color with dryness suggests poor blood flow, which can mean ischemia. That makes this the finding to report right away, not the expected gas or liquid output.

## Nutrition

### Question 20 (Best Nutrition Choice)
**Question:** A client had a stroke and has severe dysphagia. The gastrointestinal tract works normally. Which nutrition plan is best if the client cannot safely eat enough by mouth?

A. Total parenteral nutrition through a central line  
B. Enteral tube feeding  
C. A regular diet with thin liquids  
D. NPO with only IV fluids

**Correct answer:** B. Enteral tube feeding

**Explanation:** The key clue is that the gastrointestinal tract still works. When the gut works, enteral feeding is usually safer and more appropriate than total parenteral nutrition. NCLEX often tests this simple rule because it guides many nutrition questions.

### Question 21 (Safety)
**Question:** A small-bore feeding tube was just inserted. The provider has already written the feeding order. What should the nurse do before starting the first feeding?

A. Flush the tube with water and start the feeding  
B. Confirm tube position according to policy, including required x-ray confirmation before first use  
C. Place the client flat for comfort  
D. Ask the client if the tube feels like it is in the stomach

**Correct answer:** B. Confirm tube position according to policy, including required x-ray confirmation before first use

**Explanation:** Before the first feeding, tube placement must be confirmed the right way. That protects the client from feeding into the wrong location and aspirating. How the tube feels or a ready order does not replace required placement verification.

### Question 22 (SATA)
**Question:** The nurse is caring for a client receiving continuous enteral feeding. Which actions help prevent aspiration? **Select all that apply.**

A. Keep the head of the bed 30 to 45 degrees  
B. Verify tube placement per policy  
C. Start the feeding at the full goal rate right away  
D. Monitor for coughing, breathing changes, or poor tolerance

**Correct answer:** A, B, D

**Explanation:** Aspiration prevention depends on position, placement, and monitoring. Keeping the head of the bed up, verifying tube placement, and watching for coughing or breathing changes all support safe feeding. Starting at the full goal rate right away is the trap because feedings are usually advanced more carefully.

### Question 23 (Priority)
**Question:** The nurse receives an order to start total parenteral nutrition through a newly placed central catheter. The placement has not yet been confirmed. What is the nurse's best action?

A. Start the infusion at half rate until placement is confirmed  
B. Start the infusion because the provider already ordered it  
C. Hold the total parenteral nutrition and confirm line placement before infusing  
D. Infuse the total parenteral nutrition through a peripheral IV instead

**Correct answer:** C. Hold the total parenteral nutrition and confirm line placement before infusing

**Explanation:** The danger here is using a new central line before its position is confirmed. Total parenteral nutrition is highly concentrated, so infusing it through a misplaced line can cause serious harm. The safest choice is to hold it until placement is verified, even if the order is already written.

## Self-Concept

### Question 24 (Recognition)
**Question:** A client says, “I want to care for my father at home, but I also need to keep working full-time. I feel pulled in two directions.” Which role problem is this?

A. Role ambiguity  
B. Role conflict  
C. Role strain  
D. Disturbed body image

**Correct answer:** B. Role conflict

**Explanation:** The client feels torn between being a worker and a caregiver. That means two roles are competing at the same time, which is role conflict. Role strain would be pressure within one role, so it is not the best match here.

### Question 25 (Communication)
**Question:** A client says, “Since my surgery, I feel ugly and useless.” Which nurse response best supports positive self-concept?

A. “You should not think like that.”  
B. “Your family still loves you, so do not worry.”  
C. “This change has been hard. Tell me what worries you most about your body now.”  
D. “Try not to look at the incision for a few weeks.”

**Correct answer:** C. “This change has been hard. Tell me what worries you most about your body now.”

**Explanation:** The client is sharing a painful feeling about body image and self-worth. A therapeutic response first acknowledges that feeling and invites the client to say more. Quick reassurance or advice may sound kind, but it often shuts the conversation down.

## Stress and Coping

### Question 26 (Recognition)
**Question:** After the provider leaves the room, a client who just received bad news begins shouting at the nurse. Which defense mechanism is the client most likely using?

A. Sublimation  
B. Displacement  
C. Compensation  
D. Repression

**Correct answer:** B. Displacement

**Explanation:** The anger starts after bad news from the provider, but it gets aimed at the nurse. That move toward a safer target is the key clue for displacement. The question is testing where the feeling starts and where it gets shifted.

### Question 27 (Therapeutic Communication)
**Question:** A client awaiting biopsy results says, “I cannot handle this anymore.” What is the nurse's best response?

A. “Everything will be okay.”  
B. “You need to stay positive.”  
C. “Tell me more about what feels hardest right now.”  
D. “Let’s not think about it until the results come back.”

**Correct answer:** C. “Tell me more about what feels hardest right now.”

**Explanation:** The client is overwhelmed and needs space to talk, not a promise that everything will be fine. An open-ended response helps the nurse assess what feels hardest and offers support without false reassurance. That is why option C is the best therapeutic answer.

### Question 28 (Priority)
**Question:** The nurse is caring for a client with ineffective coping who has become withdrawn, is not sleeping, and says, “No one would miss me anyway.” What should the nurse do first?

A. Teach deep-breathing exercises  
B. Ask directly about thoughts of self-harm and ensure safety  
C. Tell the client to call family for support  
D. Leave the client alone to rest

**Correct answer:** B. Ask directly about thoughts of self-harm and ensure safety

**Explanation:** The words “No one would miss me anyway” turn this into a safety question. When suicide risk is possible, the nurse should ask directly about self-harm and protect the client first. Relaxation teaching or family support can come later, but not before the danger is assessed.

## Perioperative Nursing

### Question 31 (Preoperative Safety)
**Question:** During the preoperative interview, which statement should the nurse report immediately to the anesthesia provider?

A. “I brought my hearing aids.”  
B. “My father had severe muscle rigidity and a very high fever after surgery.”  
C. “I feel nervous about the operation.”  
D. “I wear glasses when I read.”

**Correct answer:** B. “My father had severe muscle rigidity and a very high fever after surgery.”

**Explanation:** A family history of severe rigidity and very high fever after surgery is a major anesthesia red flag. Those clues point to malignant hyperthermia, which can be life-threatening. That is why the anesthesia provider needs to hear this immediately.

### Question 32 (RN Role)
**Question:** The nurse brings a client to preoperative holding. The consent form is signed, but the client says, “I do not really understand what the surgeon plans to do.” What should the nurse do?

A. Witness the signature and continue the preparation  
B. Explain the surgery in detail for the surgeon  
C. Stop the process and notify the surgeon or provider that the client has questions  
D. Ask the family to explain the procedure

**Correct answer:** C. Stop the process and notify the surgeon or provider that the client has questions

**Explanation:** A signed consent is not enough if the client says there is no real understanding. Informed consent requires that the provider explain the procedure and answer questions. The nurse should stop the process and notify the surgeon or provider instead of trying to fill in the explanation.

### Question 33 (Intraoperative Safety)
**Question:** In the operating room, the circulating nurse notices the surgical site has not been marked yet. What is the best action?

A. Continue because the consent form is already signed  
B. Ask the scrub technician to mark the site  
C. Stop the process and have the correct provider verify and mark the site before surgery continues  
D. Wait until after the first incision to complete the time-out

**Correct answer:** C. Stop the process and have the correct provider verify and mark the site before surgery continues

**Explanation:** An unmarked surgical site is a wrong-site surgery risk. The safest action is to pause the process until the correct provider verifies and marks the site. Consent alone does not replace the full safety check.

### Question 34 (Immediate Postoperative Priority)
**Question:** A client arrives in the post-anesthesia care unit after abdominal surgery. What should the nurse do first when the client is restless and the oxygen saturation is 89%?

A. Give opioid medication for pain  
B. Raise the head of the bed, support the airway, and apply oxygen as needed  
C. Reinforce the dressing  
D. Offer ice chips for dry mouth

**Correct answer:** B. Raise the head of the bed, support the airway, and apply oxygen as needed

**Explanation:** Restlessness with an oxygen saturation of 89% points to an airway and breathing problem first. Following ABCs, the nurse should improve positioning, support the airway, and give oxygen as needed before handling comfort issues. Pain medicine and dressing care matter, but not before oxygenation.

### Question 35 (Postoperative Judgment)
**Question:** Two hours after surgery, a client has blood pressure 86/54 mm Hg, heart rate 124/min, cool clammy skin, urine output 20 mL/hr, and rapidly increasing sanguineous drainage on the dressing. Which complication should the nurse suspect first?

A. Paralytic ileus  
B. Hemorrhage  
C. Urinary tract infection  
D. Atelectasis

**Correct answer:** B. Hemorrhage

**Explanation:** Low blood pressure, fast heart rate, cool clammy skin, low urine output, and more bloody drainage all point in the same direction. Together, these are classic signs of postoperative hemorrhage and shock. That makes bleeding the first complication to suspect, not problems like ileus or infection.

### Question 36 (Priority)
**Question:** Fifteen minutes after a blood transfusion begins for postoperative anemia, the client reports chills and low back pain. What should the nurse do first?

A. Slow the transfusion and observe  
B. Stop the transfusion and keep the IV line open with normal saline  
C. Give acetaminophen and continue the transfusion  
D. Document the finding as an expected response

**Correct answer:** B. Stop the transfusion and keep the IV line open with normal saline

**Explanation:** Chills and low back pain soon after blood starts infusing are strong reaction clues. The safest first step is to stop the transfusion right away and keep the IV line open with normal saline. Slowing the blood and waiting would delay treatment of a possible hemolytic reaction.

## Additional Acid-Base Imbalance Practice

### Question 37 (ABG Compensation)
**Question:** A client with severe diarrhea has arterial blood gas results of pH 7.32, PaCO2 32 mm Hg, and HCO3 17 mEq/L. Which acid-base problem does the nurse identify?

A. Metabolic acidosis with respiratory compensation  
B. Respiratory acidosis with metabolic compensation  
C. Metabolic alkalosis with respiratory compensation  
D. Respiratory alkalosis without compensation

**Correct answer:** A. Metabolic acidosis with respiratory compensation

**Explanation:** The pH is low, so the client is in acidosis. Bicarbonate is also low, which makes the main problem metabolic, and the low PaCO2 shows the lungs are trying to compensate. Severe diarrhea fits this pattern because it can cause bicarbonate loss.

### Question 38 (ABG Interpretation)
**Question:** A client has had continuous nasogastric suction for 2 days after bowel surgery. The arterial blood gas results are pH 7.48, PaCO2 46 mm Hg, and HCO3 30 mEq/L. Which interpretation is best?

A. Respiratory alkalosis  
B. Metabolic alkalosis with partial respiratory compensation  
C. Metabolic acidosis with full compensation  
D. Respiratory acidosis without compensation

**Correct answer:** B. Metabolic alkalosis with partial respiratory compensation

**Explanation:** The pH shows alkalosis, and the high bicarbonate points to a metabolic cause. The slightly high PaCO2 suggests the lungs are trying to compensate, but not fully. Continuous nasogastric suction fits because losing stomach acid can lead to metabolic alkalosis.

## Additional Urinary Elimination Practice

### Question 39 (CAUTI Prevention Priority)
**Question:** The nurse is reviewing four clients with indwelling urinary catheters. Which client should the nurse advocate to have the catheter removed first to lower catheter-associated urinary tract infection risk?

A. A client in septic shock who needs hourly urine output  
B. A client 1 day after laparoscopic hysterectomy who is walking, has stable vital signs, and can use the bathroom  
C. A client with a severe sacral wound and an order to keep urine away from the wound  
D. A client receiving comfort care who has severe distress during bed changes

**Correct answer:** B. A client 1 day after laparoscopic hysterectomy who is walking, has stable vital signs, and can use the bathroom

**Explanation:** To lower CAUTI risk, the nurse should look for the client who no longer truly needs the catheter. This client is walking, stable, and able to use the bathroom, so removal is appropriate. The other clients still have stronger reasons to keep catheter drainage in place.

### Question 40 (Urinary Retention Judgment)
**Question:** A client had spinal anesthesia 6 hours ago. The client voided 50 mL twice, reports suprapubic pressure, and has a bladder scan of 620 mL. What is the nurse's best action?

A. Encourage more oral fluids and reassess in 8 hours  
B. Document the finding as expected after surgery  
C. Prepare to empty the bladder per order, usually with intermittent catheterization  
D. Insert an indwelling catheter and leave it in place for several days

**Correct answer:** C. Prepare to empty the bladder per order, usually with intermittent catheterization

**Explanation:** A bladder scan of 620 mL with suprapubic pressure is a clear retention picture. The bladder needs to be emptied promptly to prevent overdistention, and intermittent catheterization is usually the best short-term approach if ordered. Leaving a long-term indwelling catheter would be more than this situation calls for.

### Question 41 (Catheter Maintenance Safety)
**Question:** Which action by a newly hired nurse caring for a client with an indwelling urinary catheter requires correction?

A. Securing the catheter to the client's thigh  
B. Keeping the drainage bag below bladder level during transport  
C. Separating the catheter from the drainage tubing to make sure urine flows freely  
D. Performing hand hygiene before and after catheter care

**Correct answer:** C. Separating the catheter from the drainage tubing to make sure urine flows freely

**Explanation:** The closed drainage system should stay closed. Disconnecting the catheter from the tubing raises infection risk and requires correction. The other actions support safe catheter care and help prevent CAUTI.

## Additional Bowel and Ostomy Practice

### Question 42 (Ileostomy Red Flag)
**Question:** A client with a new ileostomy has had about 1800 mL of liquid output in the last 24 hours. Which finding is most important for the nurse to follow up immediately?

A. Pink moist stoma  
B. Dry mouth, dizziness when standing, and urine output of 20 mL/hr  
C. Small amount of gas in the pouch  
D. Concern about odor after eating eggs

**Correct answer:** B. Dry mouth, dizziness when standing, and urine output of 20 mL/hr

**Explanation:** An ileostomy output of 1800 mL in 24 hours can drain fluid quickly. Dry mouth, dizziness on standing, and urine output of 20 mL/hr show that dehydration is already affecting blood flow and the kidneys. That is the urgent problem, not normal stoma color or odor concerns.

### Question 43 (Bowel Obstruction Warning)
**Question:** A client with an ileostomy says, “Nothing has come out for 6 hours.” The client also has cramping, nausea, and a swollen stoma. What should the nurse do first?

A. Tell the client this can wait until the next day  
B. Give a stimulant laxative  
C. Notify the provider promptly because bowel obstruction is possible  
D. Encourage the client to eat a high-fiber snack

**Correct answer:** C. Notify the provider promptly because bowel obstruction is possible

**Explanation:** No output plus cramping, nausea, and a swollen stoma is a bowel obstruction warning pattern. The safest first move is to notify the provider promptly so the problem can be addressed early. Waiting, adding fiber, or giving a laxative could make things worse.

## Additional Nutrition Practice

### Question 44 (Aspiration Recognition)
**Question:** A client receiving continuous tube feeding after a stroke suddenly starts coughing, has wet breath sounds, and oxygen saturation drops from 96% to 90%. What is the nurse's first action?

A. Increase the feeding rate to finish sooner  
B. Stop the feeding and raise the head of the bed  
C. Give an antidiarrheal medication

**Correct answer:** B. Stop the feeding and raise the head of the bed

**Explanation:** Coughing, wet breath sounds, and a falling oxygen saturation during tube feeding are strong aspiration clues. The nurse should stop the feeding and raise the head of the bed to protect the airway first. Routine tube care can wait until breathing is safer.

### Question 45 (Aspiration Prevention)
**Question:** A client receiving enteral feeding must lie flat for a bedside procedure for about 30 minutes. What is the nurse's best action to reduce aspiration risk?

A. Continue the feeding because the rate is low  
B. Add extra water to thin the formula  
C. Pause the feeding until the client can safely be upright again  
D. Turn the client fully onto the stomach during the feeding

**Correct answer:** C. Pause the feeding until the client can safely be upright again

**Explanation:** Enteral feeding is safest when the client can stay appropriately positioned. If the client must lie flat, pausing the feeding lowers aspiration risk right away. A slow rate does not make flat positioning safe.

## Additional Sleep Practice

### Question 46 (Older Adult Safety)
**Question:** An 82-year-old client receives a PRN sleeping pill at bedtime and says, “I usually get up twice at night to urinate.” Which nursing action is most important for safety tonight?

A. Encourage the client to walk alone to keep the legs strong  
B. Offer a large glass of water with the sleeping pill  
C. Assist with toileting before sleep and remind the client to call before getting up  
D. Close the door tightly and avoid checking on the client

**Correct answer:** C. Assist with toileting before sleep and remind the client to call before getting up

**Explanation:** An older adult who takes a sleeping pill and gets up at night has a clear fall risk. Helping with toileting before sleep and reminding the client to call before standing addresses that risk directly. Independence is valuable, but safety comes first tonight.

## Additional Stress and Coping Practice

### Question 47 (Suicide Risk Recognition)
**Question:** Which client statement requires the most immediate follow-up for suicide risk?

A. “I feel overwhelmed and tired.”  
B. “I wish I could sleep and not think about any of this.”  
C. “I saved my pain pills, and I plan to take them tonight after my family leaves.”  
D. “I do not want visitors right now.”

**Correct answer:** C. “I saved my pain pills, and I plan to take them tonight after my family leaves.”

**Explanation:** The most dangerous clue is the detailed plan. The client has a method, a time, and access to the pills, which signals very high suicide risk. That is much more urgent than general hopelessness or wanting to be alone.

### Question 48 (Suicide Safety Priority)
**Question:** A hospitalized client tells the nurse, “Yes, I am thinking about killing myself right now.” What should the nurse do first?

A. Leave the room to find the provider  
B. Stay with the client and get immediate help while making the environment safer  
C. Ask the client to promise not to self-harm  
D. Change the subject to reduce anxiety

**Correct answer:** B. Stay with the client and get immediate help while making the environment safer

**Explanation:** Once a client says there are current suicidal thoughts, this becomes an immediate safety problem. The nurse should stay with the client, get help, and reduce access to harm right away. A no-harm promise is not reliable enough to protect the client.

## Additional Perioperative Practice

### Question 50 (Complication Recognition)
**Question:** During surgery, the client's carbon dioxide level keeps rising, and the surgical team notes muscle rigidity and a fast heart rate. Which complication should the nurse suspect?

A. Malignant hyperthermia  
B. Atelectasis  
C. Urinary retention  
D. Paralytic ileus

**Correct answer:** A. Malignant hyperthermia

**Explanation:** Rising carbon dioxide, muscle rigidity, and tachycardia during surgery point to malignant hyperthermia. The pattern matters because this is a fast-moving anesthesia emergency, not a routine postoperative issue. Early recognition is what makes option A the safest answer.

### Question 51 (Postoperative Emergency)
**Question:** On the second postoperative day, a client coughs and says something “popped.” The nurse sees loops of bowel protruding through the abdominal incision. What is the nurse's first action?

A. Reinsert the organs into the abdomen  
B. Cover the area with sterile saline-moistened dressings and call for help  
C. Give the client food and water for comfort  
D. Ask the client to walk to bed to reduce pressure

**Correct answer:** B. Cover the area with sterile saline-moistened dressings and call for help

**Explanation:** Loops of bowel outside the incision mean evisceration. The priority is to protect the exposed organs with sterile saline-moistened dressings and call for immediate help. Trying to push them back in is a common trap and can cause more harm.
