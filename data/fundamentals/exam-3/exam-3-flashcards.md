# Exam 3 Flashcards

These flashcards are grouped by the same sections as your Exam 3 outline and strengthened with nursing exam angles such as compare-and-contrast, priority, safety, and application questions.

## How to use this deck

- **Front** = the question side of the flashcard.
- **Back** = the answer side.
- **Why it matters** = a short memory or exam tip.

## Acid-Base imbalance

### Card 1

**Front:** How do respiratory acidosis, respiratory alkalosis, metabolic acidosis, and metabolic alkalosis differ?

**Back:**
- Respiratory acidosis: low pH, high carbon dioxide (CO2)
- Respiratory alkalosis: high pH, low carbon dioxide (CO2)
- Metabolic acidosis: low pH, low bicarbonate (HCO3)
- Metabolic alkalosis: high pH, high bicarbonate (HCO3)

**Why it matters:** This is the main compare card for acid-base questions.

### Card 2

**Front:** What are the normal values for pH, PaCO2, and bicarbonate on an arterial blood gas (ABG)?

**Back:**
- pH: 7.35 to 7.45
- PaCO2: 35 to 45 mm Hg
- HCO3: 22 to 26 mEq/L

**Why it matters:** You cannot interpret ABGs correctly without the normal ranges.

### Card 3

**Front:** What is a simple step-by-step way to interpret ABGs?

**Back:**
- Check the pH first.
- Check the carbon dioxide (CO2).
- Check the bicarbonate (HCO3).
- Decide if the problem is respiratory or metabolic.
- Then look for compensation.

**Why it matters:** This gives you a safe order for most ABG questions.

### Card 4

**Front:** How does respiratory rate affect acid-base balance?

**Back:**
- Faster breathing blows off more carbon dioxide and can cause alkalosis.
- Slower or shallow breathing keeps carbon dioxide in and can cause acidosis.

**Why it matters:** Breathing changes can affect pH quickly.

### Card 5

**Front:** What are Kussmaul respirations, and what imbalance are they linked to?

**Back:**
- Kussmaul respirations are deep, rapid breathing.
- They are most often linked to metabolic acidosis.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic exam term, especially with diabetic ketoacidosis.

### Card 6

**Front:** How can diarrhea cause metabolic acidosis and fluid volume deficit?

**Back:**
- Diarrhea causes loss of bicarbonate in stool, which can lead to metabolic acidosis.
- It also causes loss of water and electrolytes, which can lead to dehydration.

**Why it matters:** This card links GI losses to both acid-base and fluid status.

### Card 7

**Front:** How can vomiting cause metabolic alkalosis and fluid volume deficit?

**Back:**
- Vomiting causes loss of stomach acid, which can lead to metabolic alkalosis.
- It also causes loss of fluids, chloride, and potassium.

**Why it matters:** Vomiting and diarrhea affect the body in different acid-base ways.

### Card 8

**Front:** What are priority nursing interventions for acid-base imbalance?

**Back:**
- Assess airway, breathing, and circulation.
- Monitor ABGs, respirations, mental status, and electrolytes.
- Treat the cause.
- Support oxygenation and hydration as ordered.

**Why it matters:** Nursing exams usually reward cause treatment plus close monitoring.

### Card 9 (ABG)

**Front:** The ABG shows pH 7.30, PaCO2 50, HCO3 24. What is the imbalance?

**Back:**
- Respiratory acidosis.

**Why it matters:** Low pH plus high carbon dioxide points to the lungs.

### Card 10 (ABG)

**Front:** The ABG shows pH 7.30, PaCO2 38, HCO3 18. What is the imbalance?

**Back:**
- Metabolic acidosis.

**Why it matters:** Low pH plus low bicarbonate points to a metabolic problem.

### Card 11 (ABG)

**Front:** The ABG shows pH 7.50, PaCO2 30, HCO3 24. What is the imbalance?

**Back:**
- Respiratory alkalosis.

**Why it matters:** High pH plus low carbon dioxide usually means hyperventilation.

### Card 12 (ABG)

**Front:** The ABG shows pH 7.50, PaCO2 40, HCO3 30. What is the imbalance?

**Back:**
- Metabolic alkalosis.

**Why it matters:** High pH plus high bicarbonate points to a metabolic cause.

## Client Education

### Card 1

**Front:** What are the three domains of learning?

**Back:**
- Psychomotor
- Cognitive
- Affective

**Why it matters:** These domains help you match the teaching method to the learning goal.

### Card 2

**Front:** What is psychomotor learning?

**Back:**
- Learning a physical skill, such as using an inhaler or changing a dressing.

**Why it matters:** Psychomotor learning is tested through performance, not just talking.

### Card 3

**Front:** What is cognitive learning?

**Back:**
- Learning facts, ideas, or understanding, such as why a medication is taken.

**Why it matters:** This is the domain used for knowledge and understanding.

### Card 4

**Front:** What is affective learning?

**Back:**
- Learning related to feelings, values, beliefs, or attitudes.

**Why it matters:** Behavior change often depends on affective learning.

### Card 5

**Front:** Why should the nurse assess current knowledge, language or culture, topic priority, and a realistic teaching approach before teaching?

**Back:**
- To know where to start
- To adapt teaching to the client
- To teach the most important topics first
- To avoid giving too much information at once

**Why it matters:** Good teaching is planned, not random.

### Card 6

**Front:** Why is adherence a better goal than compliance?

**Back:**
- Adherence means the client understands the plan and takes part in it.
- Compliance sounds passive and one-sided.

**Why it matters:** Modern nursing teaching focuses on partnership, not orders only.

### Card 7

**Front:** What factors promote learning?

**Back:**
- Readiness
- Motivation
- Comfort
- Good timing
- Simple language
- A quiet setting
- Repetition and practice

**Why it matters:** Learning works best when the client is ready and able to focus.

### Card 8

**Front:** What makes a learning outcome SMART?

**Back:**
- Specific
- Measurable
- Achievable
- Relevant or Realistic
- Time-bound

**Why it matters:** SMART outcomes are easier to teach and evaluate.

### Card 9

**Front:** What are good methods for evaluating learning?

**Back:**
- Teach-back
- Return demonstration
- Follow-up questions
- Observation of actual behavior

**Why it matters:** Evaluation shows whether teaching worked, not just whether it was given.

### Card 10

**Front:** What barriers can make learning harder?

**Back:**
- Pain
- Fatigue
- Anxiety
- Low literacy
- Language barriers
- Hearing or vision problems
- Cognitive changes

**Why it matters:** If these barriers are not addressed, teaching may fail.

### Card 11

**Front:** What teaching methods work well for adults and older adults?

**Back:**
- Tie teaching to a current need.
- Use short sessions and simple words.
- Give one idea at a time.
- Use large print and allow extra time if needed.
- Use demonstration for skills.

**Why it matters:** Adult teaching works best when it is practical and respectful.

## Urinary Elimination

### Card 1

**Front:** What factors commonly affect urinary elimination?

**Back:**
- Age
- Fluid intake
- Medications
- Pain
- Surgery and anesthesia
- Mobility
- Infection
- Obstruction
- Neurologic problems

**Why it matters:** These factors explain many urinary changes seen in nursing care.

### Card 2

**Front:** What is the minimum normal hourly urine output for an adult, and why does output below this matter?

**Back:**
- At least 30 mL/hour, or about 0.5 mL/kg/hour
- Lower output may suggest dehydration, poor perfusion, retention, or kidney problems

**Why it matters:** This is a must-know bedside safety number.

### Card 3

**Front:** How do oliguria, polyuria, dysuria, and hematuria differ?

**Back:**
- Oliguria: too little urine
- Polyuria: too much urine
- Dysuria: painful urination
- Hematuria: blood in the urine

**Why it matters:** These basic terms appear often in exam questions.

### Card 4

**Front:** What are common signs and symptoms of urinary retention?

**Back:**
- Small frequent voids
- Feeling unable to empty
- Bladder distention
- Suprapubic pressure
- Weak stream
- Overflow dribbling

**Why it matters:** Retention can be hidden behind frequent small voids.

### Card 5

**Front:** What is post-void residual, and why is a bladder scanner useful?

**Back:**
- Post-void residual is the urine left in the bladder after voiding.
- A bladder scanner checks this amount without inserting a catheter.

**Why it matters:** This helps identify retention while lowering infection risk.

### Card 6

**Front:** How do stress, urge, mixed, overflow, and functional incontinence differ?

**Back:**
- Stress: leakage with coughing or laughing
- Urge: leakage after a sudden urge
- Mixed: stress plus urge
- Overflow: leaking from an overfull bladder
- Functional: can’t reach the toilet in time

**Why it matters:** Incontinence treatment depends on the type.

### Card 7

**Front:** Why are Kegel exercises useful in urinary care?

**Back:**
- They strengthen pelvic floor muscles.
- They are especially helpful for stress incontinence.

**Why it matters:** This is a common nursing teaching point.

### Card 8

**Front:** What is the normal urine specific gravity range?

**Back:**
- About 1.005 to 1.030

**Why it matters:** Specific gravity helps you think about hydration status.

### Card 9

**Front:** What can white blood cells, red blood cells, ketones, glucose, and protein in the urine suggest?

**Back:**
- White blood cells: infection or inflammation
- Red blood cells: bleeding, stones, trauma, or infection
- Ketones: fat breakdown, starvation, or diabetic ketoacidosis
- Glucose: high blood sugar
- Protein: kidney damage or kidney stress

**Why it matters:** Urinalysis often gives the nurse early clues.

### Card 10

**Front:** What are the key steps in a 24-hour urine collection?

**Back:**
- Discard the first urine.
- Collect all urine after that for 24 hours.
- Keep it stored as ordered.
- Collect the final urine at the end time.

**Why it matters:** Missing one void can ruin the test.

### Card 11

**Front:** How do random, clean-catch, and catheter urine specimens differ?

**Back:**
- Random: collected any time
- Clean-catch: midstream after cleaning
- Catheter specimen: taken from the sampling port using sterile technique

**Why it matters:** The correct specimen type supports correct results.

### Card 12

**Front:** What is the correct idea behind a clean-catch urine specimen?

**Back:**
- Clean the peri area first.
- Start voiding.
- Catch the midstream urine in the sterile cup.

**Why it matters:** This lowers contamination.

### Card 13

**Front:** How should a urine specimen be collected from an indwelling catheter?

**Back:**
- Never collect it from the drainage bag.
- Use the sampling port.
- Clean the port and use sterile technique.

**Why it matters:** The bag urine is old and contaminated.

### Card 14

**Front:** Why is a urine culture ordered when a urinary tract infection (UTI) is suspected?

**Back:**
- To identify the specific organism
- To help choose the correct antibiotic

**Why it matters:** Culture and sensitivity guide treatment.

### Card 15

**Front:** How do fluid intake, hygiene, regular voiding, and cranberry products help prevent urinary tract infections (UTIs)?

**Back:**
- Fluids help flush bacteria.
- Hygiene lowers bacterial spread.
- Regular voiding keeps urine from sitting too long.
- Cranberry may help some patients, but it does not replace antibiotics.

**Why it matters:** UTI prevention questions are common.

### Card 16

**Front:** What are the priority assessments for a client with an indwelling catheter?

**Back:**
- Urine amount, color, clarity, and odor
- Catheter patency
- Kinks or dependent loops
- Bag position below bladder
- Pain, fever, or infection signs
- Ongoing need for the catheter

**Why it matters:** Catheter care is mostly about assessment and infection prevention.

### Card 17

**Front:** What are common indications and risks of urinary catheterization?

**Back:**
- Indications: retention, selected surgery, accurate output in selected critically ill clients, severe wounds, comfort care
- Risks: infection, trauma, discomfort, reduced mobility

**Why it matters:** Nurses should know both when a catheter is needed and why it should be removed early.

### Card 18

**Front:** What nursing actions help prevent catheter-associated urinary tract infection (CAUTI)?

**Back:**
- Avoid unnecessary catheters.
- Insert with sterile technique.
- Keep a closed system.
- Keep the bag below the bladder.
- Prevent kinks and dependent loops.
- Remove the catheter as soon as possible.

**Why it matters:** CAUTI prevention is a major safety topic.

### Card 19

**Front:** How do upper and lower urinary tract infection symptoms differ?

**Back:**
- Lower UTI: dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic discomfort
- Upper UTI: fever, chills, flank pain, nausea, vomiting

**Why it matters:** Upper UTI is usually more serious.

### Card 20

**Front:** What are atypical urinary tract infection signs in an older adult?

**Back:**
- New confusion
- Weakness
- Poor appetite
- Functional decline
- Change from usual behavior

**Why it matters:** Older adults may not have the usual urinary symptoms or fever.

### Card 21

**Front:** What is the purpose of continuous bladder irrigation (CBI)?

**Back:**
- To keep urine flowing
- To prevent clots from blocking the catheter
- It is often used after bladder or prostate surgery

**Why it matters:** CBI questions focus on patency and bleeding assessment.

### Card 22

**Front:** What are the priority assessments with continuous bladder irrigation (CBI)?

**Back:**
- Inflow and outflow
- Urine color
- Clots
- Pain or spasms
- Bladder distention
- Catheter patency

**Why it matters:** You must know whether the bladder is draining safely.

### Card 23 (Priority)

**Front:** A client with continuous bladder irrigation has suprapubic pain and little drainage. What should the nurse think about first?

**Back:**
- Possible blockage from a kink or clot
- The nurse should assess the client and system right away

**Why it matters:** Poor drainage can quickly lead to bladder distention and pain.

## Sleep

### Card 1

**Front:** What are the stages of a normal sleep cycle?

**Back:**
- Stage 1 non-rapid eye movement (NREM): light sleep
- Stage 2 NREM: true sleep
- Stage 3 NREM: deep sleep
- Rapid eye movement (REM): dreaming sleep

**Why it matters:** The exam may ask you to compare restful sleep with dreaming sleep.

### Card 2

**Front:** How do REM and NREM sleep differ?

**Back:**
- NREM is quieter, more restorative sleep.
- REM is the dreaming stage with more brain activity and muscle relaxation.

**Why it matters:** REM and NREM have different body effects.

### Card 3

**Front:** What are common characteristics of insomnia, sleep apnea, narcolepsy, and restless legs syndrome?

**Back:**
- Insomnia: trouble sleeping
- Sleep apnea: breathing pauses during sleep
- Narcolepsy: sudden daytime sleep attacks
- Restless legs: urge to move the legs, worse at night

**Why it matters:** These are the most common sleep-disorder comparisons.

### Card 4

**Front:** What factors and behaviors commonly interfere with sleep?

**Back:**
- Pain
- Stress
- Caffeine
- Nicotine
- Alcohol
- Screens before bed
- Noise and light
- Frequent nighttime voiding

**Why it matters:** Many sleep problems can be improved without medication.

### Card 5

**Front:** What nursing interventions help promote normal sleep in adults?

**Back:**
- Reduce noise and light.
- Control pain.
- Encourage a regular sleep routine.
- Limit late caffeine.
- Encourage bedtime toileting.
- Cluster care if possible.

**Why it matters:** These are common non-drug nursing actions.

### Card 6

**Front:** How are herbal products, hypnotics, and sedatives used for sleep?

**Back:**
- They may help some clients sleep.
- They should be used carefully and usually after non-drug methods are considered.

**Why it matters:** Sleep aids are not harmless.

### Card 7

**Front:** What are key safety issues with sleep aids?

**Back:**
- Falls
- Confusion
- Daytime drowsiness
- Oversedation
- Dependence
- Dangerous interaction with alcohol or other sedatives

**Why it matters:** Older adults are especially at risk.

## Bowel Elimination

### Card 1

**Front:** How do aging, diet, fluid intake, mobility, pain, and surgery affect bowel elimination?

**Back:**
- Aging, low fiber, low fluids, immobility, pain, and surgery can all slow bowel movement and raise constipation risk.

**Why it matters:** These are common causes behind bowel problems.

### Card 2

**Front:** What are common signs of constipation, and what often causes it?

**Back:**
- Hard dry stool, straining, bloating, infrequent bowel movements
- Causes include low fiber, low fluids, immobility, opioids, surgery, and ignoring the urge to go

**Why it matters:** Constipation is one of the most common bowel issues on exams.

### Card 3

**Front:** What interventions help prevent or treat constipation?

**Back:**
- Increase fluids if allowed.
- Increase fiber gradually.
- Encourage activity.
- Offer regular time for toileting.
- Use stool softeners or laxatives as ordered.

**Why it matters:** Prevention is often the best answer in constipation questions.

### Card 4

**Front:** What foods are high in fiber and bulk-forming?

**Back:**
- Fruits
- Vegetables
- Beans
- Whole grains
- Bran
- Oats

**Why it matters:** Food teaching is often part of bowel care.

### Card 5

**Front:** How do stimulant, bulk-forming, hyperosmotic, and emollient laxatives differ?

**Back:**
- Stimulant: makes the bowel move more strongly
- Bulk-forming: adds bulk and water to stool
- Hyperosmotic: pulls water into the bowel
- Emollient: softens stool

**Why it matters:** Laxative class questions are common.

### Card 6

**Front:** How can diarrhea lead to fluid volume deficit?

**Back:**
- Diarrhea causes rapid loss of water and electrolytes.
- This can lead to dehydration and low blood pressure.

**Why it matters:** Diarrhea is not just a bowel problem; it is a fluid problem too.

### Card 7

**Front:** How can diarrhea cause metabolic acidosis and potassium imbalance?

**Back:**
- It causes loss of bicarbonate, which can lead to metabolic acidosis.
- It also causes potassium loss, which can lead to hypokalemia.

**Why it matters:** This links bowel elimination to acid-base and electrolyte content.

### Card 8

**Front:** What isolation precautions are used for suspected or confirmed Clostridioides difficile (C. difficile)?

**Back:**
- Contact precautions
- Gown and gloves
- Soap and water hand hygiene

**Why it matters:** C. difficile is a classic safety and isolation topic.

### Card 9

**Front:** What is important when collecting a stool specimen or stool culture?

**Back:**
- Use a clean container.
- Do not mix the stool with urine or toilet water.
- Label it correctly.
- Send it promptly.

**Why it matters:** Good collection technique supports accurate lab results.

### Card 10

**Front:** What is the purpose of an enema?

**Back:**
- To relieve constipation
- To empty the bowel before a procedure
- To give medication in some cases

**Why it matters:** The exam may ask why the enema was ordered.

### Card 11

**Front:** What is the general idea of giving a large-volume rectal enema, and what should the nurse do if cramping occurs?

**Back:**
- Position the client on the left side, use warm solution, lubricate the tip, and let the fluid run in slowly.
- If cramping occurs, slow or stop the flow and restart more slowly after the cramp improves.

**Why it matters:** Cramping is a common safety point in enema questions.

### Card 12

**Front:** What is a gastrointestinal (GI) ostomy?

**Back:**
- A surgically created opening of the intestine through the abdominal wall so stool can leave the body.

**Why it matters:** This is the basic definition behind all ostomy care questions.

### Card 13

**Front:** How do ileostomy and colostomy differ?

**Back:**
- Ileostomy: from the small intestine and usually more liquid output
- Colostomy: from the large intestine and usually more formed output

**Why it matters:** Stool consistency helps you tell the difference.

### Card 14

**Front:** Why are clients with a new ileostomy at higher risk for dehydration?

**Back:**
- Ileostomy output is more liquid, so the client loses more water and electrolytes.

**Why it matters:** This is a major patient teaching point.

### Card 15

**Front:** What does pouching an ostomy mean, and what are the priority assessments?

**Back:**
- Pouching means applying and sealing an ostomy bag over the stoma.
- Assess stool consistency, stoma color and moisture, and the skin around the stoma.

**Why it matters:** Pouching and assessment are basic ostomy care skills.

### Card 16

**Front:** What nutritional teaching is important for a client with a new ostomy?

**Back:**
- Introduce foods slowly.
- Chew well.
- Watch for foods that cause gas, odor, or blockage.
- Drink enough fluids, especially with an ileostomy.

**Why it matters:** Ostomy nutrition teaching helps prevent early problems.

### Card 17

**Front:** What are key nursing actions for insertion, securement, and basic care of a nasogastric (NG) tube?

**Back:**
- Position the client upright.
- Insert as ordered.
- Confirm placement before first use.
- Secure the tube.
- Provide oral and nasal care.
- Monitor drainage and function.

**Why it matters:** NG care questions are common in bowel and surgical content.

### Card 18

**Front:** What signs suggest an NG tube is not functioning properly?

**Back:**
- Nausea
- Vomiting
- Abdominal distention
- Discomfort
- Little or no drainage when expected
- Respiratory distress if the tube is misplaced

**Why it matters:** Tube problems can quickly become safety problems.

### Card 19

**Front:** What nursing actions help maintain NG tube patency and function?

**Back:**
- Check placement per policy.
- Keep tubing free from kinks.
- Check suction settings.
- Irrigate or flush if ordered.
- Keep the drainage system below the stomach.

**Why it matters:** Patency questions often focus on basic troubleshooting.

### Card 20

**Front:** How can nasogastric suction contribute to metabolic alkalosis?

**Back:**
- NG suction removes stomach acid.
- Losing stomach acid makes the body more alkaline.

**Why it matters:** This is a very common surgery and GI connection question.

## Nutrition

### Card 1

**Front:** Which clients are at risk for nutritional problems?

**Back:**
- Older adults
- Clients with dysphagia
- Clients with chronic illness or wounds
- Clients with poor appetite, nausea, or vomiting
- Clients who are NPO for a long time
- Clients with limited food access

**Why it matters:** Risk recognition comes before nutrition treatment.

### Card 2

**Front:** How does malnourishment affect health outcomes?

**Back:**
- Delays healing
- Increases infection risk
- Causes weakness
- Raises pressure injury risk
- Leads to slower recovery

**Why it matters:** Malnutrition affects almost every part of recovery.

### Card 3

**Front:** What are common therapeutic diets?

**Back:**
- Clear liquid
- Full liquid
- Soft
- Low sodium
- Cardiac
- Consistent carbohydrate
- High protein
- Renal
- NPO

**Why it matters:** Diet selection is a common nursing teaching topic.

### Card 4

**Front:** How does inadequate protein contribute to edema and poor wound healing?

**Back:**
- Low protein lowers albumin, which can lead to edema.
- Protein is also needed for tissue repair and healing.

**Why it matters:** This connects nutrition to skin, fluid balance, and wound care.

### Card 5

**Front:** How should nutrition be approached with dysphagia, NPO status, nausea, or vomiting?

**Back:**
- Dysphagia: use texture changes and aspiration precautions
- NPO: no food or fluids by mouth; support nutrition another way if needed
- Nausea or vomiting: small bland meals when allowed, antiemetics, and hydration

**Why it matters:** This is a practical bedside decision card.

### Card 6

**Front:** How do enteral and parenteral nutrition differ?

**Back:**
- Enteral uses the gastrointestinal tract.
- Parenteral goes into the bloodstream through a vein.

**Why it matters:** This is one of the most basic nutrition comparisons.

### Card 7

**Front:** When is enteral nutrition used, and when is parenteral nutrition used?

**Back:**
- Enteral: when the gut works but the client cannot eat enough safely
- Parenteral: when the gut cannot be used or cannot absorb enough

**Why it matters:** The safest route is usually the gut if the gut works.

### Card 8

**Front:** What are the main benefits of enteral feedings?

**Back:**
- Keep the gut working
- More natural
- Lower infection risk than total parenteral nutrition in many cases
- Usually less expensive

**Why it matters:** Enteral feeding is preferred when possible.

### Card 9

**Front:** What are common complications of enteral feedings?

**Back:**
- Aspiration
- Diarrhea
- Nausea or vomiting
- Tube displacement
- Tube clogging
- Poor tolerance

**Why it matters:** Nurses monitor for these during routine tube feeding care.

### Card 10

**Front:** What are key safety steps before and when starting a feeding through an indwelling tube?

**Back:**
- Confirm placement before first use by x-ray.
- Check placement per policy after that.
- Raise the head of the bed.
- Start slowly with the ordered formula and rate.
- Flush as ordered.

**Why it matters:** Starting a tube feeding safely is heavily tested.

### Card 11

**Front:** Why are x-ray confirmation, gastric residual checks, and gastrointestinal aspirate pH checks important in enteral feeding?

**Back:**
- X-ray confirms placement before first use.
- Residual checks help assess tolerance and delayed emptying.
- pH checks can help support placement checks per policy.

**Why it matters:** These checks help prevent aspiration and poor feeding tolerance.

### Card 12

**Front:** What nursing actions help prevent aspiration with enteral feeding?

**Back:**
- Keep the head of the bed 30 to 45 degrees.
- Check placement per policy.
- Start slowly.
- Watch for poor tolerance.
- Give oral care.

**Why it matters:** Aspiration prevention is the biggest enteral feeding safety issue.

### Card 13

**Front:** Why are enteral feedings started at slow rates, and what helps prevent or treat diarrhea from enteral feeds?

**Back:**
- Slow rates help the gastrointestinal tract adjust.
- Use a pump as ordered, start slowly, avoid contamination, assess medications, and watch hydration and formula tolerance.

**Why it matters:** Diarrhea with tube feedings is common and often preventable.

### Card 14

**Front:** What are the main benefits of total parenteral nutrition (TPN)?

**Back:**
- It provides complete nutrition when the gastrointestinal tract cannot be used.
- It can be adjusted to meet the client’s needs closely.

**Why it matters:** TPN is used when enteral feeding is not possible or not enough.

### Card 15

**Front:** What are common complications of total parenteral nutrition (TPN)?

**Back:**
- Infection
- Hyperglycemia
- Fluid overload
- Electrolyte imbalance
- Central line problems

**Why it matters:** TPN is effective, but it requires very close monitoring.

### Card 16

**Front:** How are complications of total parenteral nutrition (TPN) prevented, and why must central catheter placement be confirmed before starting it?

**Back:**
- Use sterile central line care, infusion pumps, glucose checks, lab checks, and close monitoring.
- Central line placement must be confirmed because TPN is concentrated and can seriously damage tissue if it is not in the right vein.

**Why it matters:** This is a major TPN safety card.

## Self-Concept

### Card 1

**Front:** What factors affect self-concept?

**Back:**
- Body image
- Health and illness
- Culture
- Family feedback
- Social roles
- Stress and loss

- Role ambiguity: unclear expectations

- Encourage strengths.

- It can affect confidence, communication, bias, and boundaries.
- Self-awareness helps the nurse provide safer and more respectful care.

**Why it matters:** Nurses need to reflect on themselves, not just the patient.

## Stress and Coping

### Card 1

**Front:** How does the body naturally respond to stress?

**Back:**
- The body tries to maintain homeostasis.
- The sympathetic nervous system activates fight-or-flight.
- Hormones such as adrenaline and cortisol rise.
- The parasympathetic system helps recovery later.

**Why it matters:** Stress questions often connect body changes to symptoms.

### Card 2

**Front:** What are normal coping strategies?

**Back:**
- Problem solving
- Talking with others
- Exercise
- Deep breathing
- Prayer or spiritual support
- Rest and healthy routines

**Why it matters:** Good coping is an important nursing strength to support.

### Card 3

**Front:** What are common defense mechanisms?

**Back:**
- Denial
- Projection
- Displacement
- Regression
- Rationalization
- Repression
- Sublimation
- Compensation

**Why it matters:** Exams often ask you to match behavior to the defense mechanism.

### Card 4

**Front:** What factors can affect how well a client copes?

**Back:**
- Health
- Fatigue
- Support system
- Finances
- Beliefs
- Past experiences
- Developmental stage

**Why it matters:** Coping ability is not the same for every person.

### Card 5

**Front:** What nursing interventions help clients with ineffective coping?

**Back:**
- Use therapeutic communication.

### Card 6

- Chronic illness

## Perioperative Nursing

### Card 1

**Front:** What factors can affect how a client responds to surgery?

**Back:**
- Age
- Nutrition
- Obesity
- Smoking
- Chronic disease
- Infection
- Medications
- Allergies
- Alcohol or drug use
- Fluid and electrolyte problems

**Why it matters:** These factors help the nurse recognize surgical risk.

### Card 2

**Front:** What are key RN responsibilities in the preoperative phase?

**Back:**
- Assessment
- Safety checks
- Teaching
- Consent support
- Preparation for surgery

**Why it matters:** Preop care is mostly about preparation and prevention.

### Card 3

**Front:** What are priority preoperative assessments?

**Back:**
- Allergies
- Procedure and site
- Vital signs
- NPO status
- Medication history
- Past anesthesia reactions
- Baseline respiratory and cardiac status

**Why it matters:** These checks prevent avoidable surgical harm.

### Card 4

**Front:** Why are latex allergy, iodine allergy, and past anesthesia reactions especially important before surgery?

**Back:**
- Latex and iodine exposures can trigger serious reactions.
- Past anesthesia reactions can warn of future problems, including malignant hyperthermia.

**Why it matters:** These are classic preop safety questions.

### Card 5

**Front:** Why are aspirin, anticoagulants, and some herbal supplements important before surgery?

**Back:**
- They can increase bleeding risk or interact with anesthesia.
- Common herbs of concern include garlic, ginkgo, and ginseng.

**Why it matters:** Medication review is a major preop nursing role.

### Card 6

**Front:** What preoperative teaching is important for NPO status, medications, and postoperative expectations?

**Back:**
- Explain NPO instructions clearly.
- Clarify which medicines to take or hold.
- Teach pain control, coughing, deep breathing, and early ambulation before surgery.

**Why it matters:** Clients learn better before anesthesia than after it.

### Card 7

**Front:** What is the nurse’s role with informed consent?

**Back:**
- Make sure the consent is signed as required.
- Witness the signature if policy says so.
- Report if the client is confused or still has unanswered questions.

**Why it matters:** The provider explains the procedure, but the nurse protects safety and understanding.

### Card 8

**Front:** How do diagnostic, palliative, ablative, constructive, cosmetic, and reconstructive surgeries differ?

**Back:**
- Diagnostic: finds the cause
- Palliative: relieves symptoms
- Ablative: removes diseased tissue
- Constructive: restores function
- Cosmetic: improves appearance
- Reconstructive: repairs or rebuilds a body part

**Why it matters:** Surgery type questions test basic definitions.

### Card 9

**Front:** What are key RN responsibilities in the intraoperative phase?

**Back:**
- Maintain safety
- Maintain asepsis
- Position the client correctly
- Monitor and document
- Communicate with the surgical team

**Why it matters:** Intraop nursing is highly focused on protection and coordination.

### Card 10

**Front:** What is the Joint Commission Universal Protocol before surgery?

**Back:**
- Correct patient
- Correct procedure
- Correct site
- Site marking when needed
- Time-out before starting

**Why it matters:** This is one of the most tested perioperative safety systems.

### Card 11

**Front:** What nursing actions help prevent injury during surgery?

**Back:**
- Safe positioning
- Padding pressure points
- Preventing hypothermia
- Maintaining airway support
- Using surgical asepsis correctly

**Why it matters:** Surgical injury prevention is a core intraoperative role.

### Card 12

**Front:** What are the basic principles of surgical aseptic technique?

**Back:**
- Sterile touches sterile.
- If in doubt, it is contaminated.
- Keep the sterile field above waist level and in sight.
- Moisture contaminates.

**Why it matters:** These rules are used in many skills and surgery questions.

### Card 13

**Front:** What are the nurse’s main priorities in the immediate postoperative phase?

**Back:**
- Receive a clear handoff.
- Assess airway, breathing, and circulation first.
- Monitor pain, level of consciousness, wound, drains, and urine output.

**Why it matters:** Postop care begins with ABCs, not routine paperwork.

### Card 14

**Front:** Why does clear postoperative communication improve safety?

**Back:**
- Good handoff helps prevent missed complications, delays, and treatment errors.

**Why it matters:** Communication is a safety intervention, not just a formality.

### Card 15

**Front:** After anesthesia, what comes first in nursing priority?

**Back:**
- Airway first, then breathing, then circulation.

**Why it matters:** This is the safest answer to many postop priority questions.

### Card 16

**Front:** What are common postoperative respiratory complications?

**Back:**
- Airway obstruction
- Aspiration
- Atelectasis
- Pneumonia
- Hypoxemia
- Oversedation

**Why it matters:** Respiratory problems are some of the earliest and most dangerous postop complications.

### Card 17

**Front:** What are early signs of hypoxia in a postoperative client, and why is the risk high after surgery?

**Back:**
- Restlessness, anxiety, tachycardia, tachypnea, and lower oxygen saturation
- Risk is higher because of anesthesia, pain, shallow breathing, immobility, and opioids

**Why it matters:** Early signs are subtle, but catching them early prevents decline.

### Card 18

**Front:** What breath sounds are common with atelectasis or pneumonia, and what helps prevent these problems?

**Back:**
- Crackles and sometimes decreased breath sounds
- Prevention includes coughing, deep breathing, incentive spirometry, hydration, early ambulation, and suctioning when needed

**Why it matters:** This combines assessment with prevention.

### Card 19

**Front:** What positioning helps reduce aspiration or airway obstruction after surgery?

**Back:**
- Side-lying or head elevated as appropriate

**Why it matters:** Positioning is often the first airway-protection step.

### Card 20

**Front:** How do pulse oximetry, oxygen therapy, and sedation monitoring help prevent postoperative hypoxemia?

**Back:**
- Oximetry tracks oxygen levels.
- Oxygen therapy supports oxygenation.
- Sedation monitoring helps detect breathing suppression early.

**Why it matters:** Hypoxemia prevention is a major postop nursing job.

### Card 21

**Front:** What are common postoperative circulatory complications, and what signs suggest bleeding or hemorrhage?

**Back:**
- Complications include bleeding, hypovolemia, shock, and thrombus formation.
- Bleeding signs include tachycardia, low blood pressure, pale cool skin, swelling, rising drainage, restlessness, and low urine output.

**Why it matters:** Bleeding can become life-threatening quickly.

### Card 22

**Front:** What should the nurse do first if postoperative bleeding or hemorrhage is suspected?

**Back:**
- Assess the client immediately.
- Support airway, breathing, and circulation.
- Check the wound and drains.
- Notify the provider or rapid response as needed.

**Why it matters:** The client is assessed first, not just the dressing.

### Card 23

**Front:** What are priority actions when a client is receiving blood products, and what signs suggest a hemolytic transfusion reaction?

**Back:**
- Verify the blood carefully, monitor closely, and stop the transfusion if a reaction is suspected.
- Hemolytic reaction signs include fever, chills, low back pain, shortness of breath, hypotension, and dark urine.

**Why it matters:** Blood transfusion reactions are emergency-level safety events.

### Card 24

**Front:** How do early ambulation, sequential compression devices, leg exercises, hydration, and anticoagulants help prevent postoperative thrombus formation?

**Back:**
- They improve circulation, reduce venous stasis, and lower clot risk.

**Why it matters:** DVT prevention is a standard postoperative priority.

### Card 25

**Front:** What fluid and electrolyte assessments matter after surgery, and how do intravenous fluids relate to the surgical client?

**Back:**
- Monitor vital signs, intake and output, urine output, weight, edema, lung sounds, labs, and mental status.
- Intravenous fluids replace losses and support blood pressure, but too much can cause overload.

**Why it matters:** Surgical clients can become unstable from both deficit and excess.

### Card 26

**Front:** What is malignant hyperthermia, and why is an elevated temperature after surgery important?

**Back:**
- Malignant hyperthermia is a dangerous reaction to certain anesthetics that causes very high temperature, muscle rigidity, and severe instability.
- An elevated postop temperature may also suggest atelectasis, infection, or dehydration.

**Why it matters:** Not all fevers mean the same thing after surgery.

### Card 27

**Front:** When a postop client has an elevated temperature, what additional assessments are important?

**Back:**
- Check the lungs, wound, urine, drainage, hydration status, and timing after surgery.

**Why it matters:** The nurse must look for the cause, not just note the fever.

### Card 28

**Front:** Why is neurologic assessment important after surgery, and what should the nurse assess?

**Back:**
- It helps detect oversedation, hypoxia, stroke-like changes, or delayed recovery from anesthesia.
- Assess level of consciousness, orientation, and responsiveness.

**Why it matters:** Mental status changes may be an early warning sign.

### Card 29

**Front:** What are the priority postoperative assessments?

**Back:**
- Airway
- Breathing
- Oxygen saturation
- Circulation
- Pain
- Level of consciousness
- Wound and drains
- Urine output
- Temperature

**Why it matters:** This is the core postop assessment list.

### Card 30

**Front:** What are the priority postoperative nursing interventions?

**Back:**
- Maintain airway and safe positioning.
- Monitor closely.
- Control pain safely.
- Encourage deep breathing, coughing, and ambulation.
- Check wound, drains, and urine output.

**Why it matters:** These actions prevent the most common postop complications.

### Card 31

**Front:** What wound problems are more likely after surgery in clients with obesity, and how can the nurse help prevent dehiscence or evisceration?

**Back:**
- Obesity raises wound tension, lowers blood flow to fatty tissue, and raises infection risk.
- Splinting the incision during coughing or movement helps prevent dehiscence and evisceration.

**Why it matters:** This is a high-yield wound-healing and postop safety concept.

### Card 32

**Front:** What are signs of an infected surgical wound?

**Back:**
- Redness
- Warmth
- Swelling
- Pain
- Purulent drainage
- Bad odor
- Fever

**Why it matters:** Infected wound questions often focus on recognizing the findings early.

### Card 33

**Front:** What gastrointestinal assessments matter after surgery, and what is paralytic ileus?

**Back:**
- Assess bowel sounds, distention, nausea, vomiting, passing gas, and diet tolerance.
- Paralytic ileus means the bowel is not moving normally after surgery or anesthesia.

**Why it matters:** GI recovery is a routine part of postop care.

### Card 34

**Front:** How is diet usually advanced after being NPO, and why does ambulation help bowel recovery?

**Back:**
- Diet usually starts with clear liquids and advances as tolerated.
- Ambulation helps stimulate peristalsis and move gas through the bowel.

**Why it matters:** This is a common postop GI teaching card.

### Card 35

**Front:** How does anesthesia affect urinary elimination after surgery, and what should the nurse assess?

**Back:**
- Anesthesia can reduce bladder sensation and bladder emptying, causing retention.
- Assess time of last void, bladder distention, discomfort, urine output, and catheter function if present.

**Why it matters:** Urinary retention is a common postop problem.

### Card 36

**Front:** What signs and symptoms can suggest postoperative pain, and what are key principles of analgesic administration?

**Back:**
- Pain may show as client report, grimacing, guarding, restlessness, and increased heart rate or blood pressure.
- Assess before and after giving analgesics, give them safely and on time, and watch for oversedation.

**Why it matters:** Pain control supports both comfort and recovery.

### Card 37

**Front:** Why does poor pain control increase the risk for complications of immobility after surgery?

**Back:**
- Pain can reduce coughing, deep breathing, movement, and sleep.
- This raises the risk for atelectasis, pneumonia, clots, constipation, and slower healing.

**Why it matters:** Pain management is not only about comfort; it also protects function and recovery.
