## Acid-Base imbalance

(This section is about how the body keeps the blood from becoming too acidic or too alkaline.)

### Differentiate between respiratory acidosis, respiratory alkalosis, metabolic acidosis, and metabolic alkalosis

- **Respiratory acidosis:** the blood becomes more acidic because the lungs are not removing enough carbon dioxide (CO2). The pH goes down and the PaCO2 goes up.
- **Respiratory alkalosis:** the blood becomes too alkaline because the lungs are blowing off too much carbon dioxide (CO2). The pH goes up and the PaCO2 goes down.
- **Metabolic acidosis:** the blood becomes more acidic because there is too much acid or too little bicarbonate (HCO3). The pH goes down and the bicarbonate goes down.
- **Metabolic alkalosis:** the blood becomes too alkaline because there is too much bicarbonate or too much acid loss. The pH goes up and the bicarbonate goes up.
- **Respiratory problems** are mainly about **carbon dioxide**.
- **Metabolic problems** are mainly about **bicarbonate**.

### Accurately interpret arterial blood gases (ABGs)

(ABGs tell you about pH, carbon dioxide, bicarbonate, and oxygenation.)

- Normal **pH:** 7.35 to 7.45
- Normal **PaCO2:** 35 to 45 mm Hg
- Normal **HCO3:** 22 to 26 mEq/L

Use this order:

1. Check the **pH**. Decide if the blood is acidic or alkaline.
2. Check the **PaCO2**. This tells you if the respiratory system is causing the problem.
3. Check the **HCO3**. This tells you if the metabolic system is causing the problem.
4. Decide if the body is **uncompensated, partly compensated, or fully compensated**.

Simple memory:

- **pH down + CO2 up = respiratory acidosis**
- **pH up + CO2 down = respiratory alkalosis**
- **pH down + HCO3 down = metabolic acidosis**
- **pH up + HCO3 up = metabolic alkalosis**

### Explain how respiratory rate impacts acid-base balance

- **Fast breathing** blows off more carbon dioxide (CO2), so the blood becomes more alkaline.
- **Slow or shallow breathing** keeps carbon dioxide (CO2) in the body, so the blood becomes more acidic.
- This is why breathing changes can quickly affect acid-base balance.

### Describe Kussmaul respirations

(This is a very deep, fast breathing pattern the body uses when the blood is too acidic.)

- Kussmaul respirations are **deep, rapid breathing**.
- The body uses this pattern to blow off carbon dioxide (CO2) and try to raise the pH.

### Identify the acid-base imbalance associated with Kussmaul respirations

- Kussmaul respirations are most often seen with **metabolic acidosis**.
- A common example is **diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)**.

### Explain how diarrhea can cause metabolic acidosis and impact fluid status

- Diarrhea causes the body to lose **bicarbonate** in the stool.
- Losing bicarbonate can lead to **metabolic acidosis**.
- Diarrhea also causes loss of **water and electrolytes**, so it can cause **fluid volume deficit**.
- Potassium can also be lost, so the client may develop **hypokalemia**.

### Explain how vomiting can cause metabolic alkalosis and impact fluid status

- Vomiting causes loss of **stomach acid**.
- Losing acid can lead to **metabolic alkalosis**.
- Vomiting also causes loss of **fluid, chloride, and potassium**.
- This can lead to **dehydration**, low blood pressure, weakness, and poor perfusion.

### Identify nursing interventions for acid-base imbalance

- Assess **airway, breathing, and circulation (ABCs)** first.
- Monitor respiratory rate, depth, effort, and oxygenation.
- Monitor arterial blood gases (ABGs), electrolytes, and mental status.
- Treat the **cause** of the problem.
- Give oxygen or ventilatory support if needed.
- Replace fluids and electrolytes as ordered.
- Monitor heart rhythm, especially if potassium is abnormal.
- Watch urine output and intake/output closely.

## Client Education

(This section is about how nurses teach patients in a way they can understand and use.)

### Differentiate between the three different domains of learning

#### Psychomotor

- Learning a **physical skill**
- Example: using an inhaler, giving insulin, changing a dressing

#### Cognitive

- Learning **facts, ideas, or understanding**
- Example: knowing what a medication does or when to call the provider

#### Affective learning

- Learning related to **feelings, values, beliefs, or attitudes**
- Example: accepting a diagnosis or deciding to stop smoking

### Explain why the following factors should be assessed before teaching

#### Knowledge

- The nurse needs to know what the client already knows.
- This prevents teaching too little or too much.

#### Cultural and language needs

- Teaching must fit the client’s language, beliefs, and health practices.
- Use an interpreter when needed.

#### Prioritization of topics

- Teach the most important and safety-related topics first.
- Clients may not be ready to learn everything at one time.

#### Realistic approach

- Teaching should match the client’s energy, pain level, time, and ability.
- Small, clear teaching is often better than too much teaching at once.

### Discuss how promoting client understanding and participation supports adherence rather than compliance

- **Compliance** sounds like the client is just following orders.
- **Adherence** means the client understands the plan and takes part in it.
- When clients understand the reason for care and help make the plan, they are more likely to follow it.
- This supports respect, partnership, and long-term success.

### Identify factors that promote learning

- Readiness to learn
- Motivation
- Comfort and pain control
- A quiet environment
- Trust in the nurse
- Good timing
- Simple language
- Practice and repetition
- Family or support person involvement when appropriate

### Review the development of appropriate and SMART learning outcomes

- **Specific:** clear and focused
- **Measurable:** can be seen or checked
- **Achievable:** realistic for the client
- **Relevant/Realistic:** meaningful for the client’s needs
- **Time-bound:** includes when it should happen

Example:

- “By the end of teaching, the client will correctly show how to use the incentive spirometer.”

### Review methods for evaluating learning

- Ask the client to explain the information back in their own words (**teach-back**).
- Ask the client to perform the skill (**return demonstration**).
- Ask clear follow-up questions.
- Observe if the client actually uses the teaching correctly.
- Reassess later if needed.

### Identify barriers that may negatively affect learning

- Pain
- Fatigue
- Anxiety
- Low literacy
- Language barrier
- Hearing or vision problems
- Cognitive changes
- Cultural mismatch
- Lack of motivation
- Too much information at once

### Review methods of teaching that are appropriate to adults and the older adult

- Connect teaching to the client’s current problem or goal.
- Respect the client’s past experience.
- Use short, clear teaching sessions.
- Give one main idea at a time.
- Use simple written instructions.
- Use large print for older adults when needed.
- Reduce background noise.
- Allow extra time for questions and responses.
- Use demonstration and return demonstration when teaching skills.

## Urinary Elimination

(This section is about normal urination, urinary problems, urine tests, and urinary catheter care.)

### Identify factors that affect urinary elimination (Box 34-1)

- Age
- Fluid intake
- Medications such as diuretics
- Surgery and anesthesia
- Pain
- Mobility
- Infection
- Obstruction such as enlarged prostate
- Neurologic problems
- Emotional stress
- Privacy and usual habits

### Identify the ‘minimum’ normal hourly urinary output

- The usual minimum adult urine output is **at least 30 mL/hour**.
- Another common way to say it is **0.5 mL/kg/hour** in adults.

### Explain the significance of output less than this

- Output below this can suggest **dehydration, poor kidney perfusion, urinary retention, or worsening kidney function**.
- It should be assessed and reported, especially if it is new or ongoing.

### Differentiate between oliguria, polyuria, dysuria, and hematuria

- **Oliguria:** too little urine output
- **Polyuria:** too much urine output
- **Dysuria:** painful or burning urination
- **Hematuria:** blood in the urine

### Describe signs and symptoms of urinary retention

- Feeling the need to void but being unable to empty well
- Small frequent voids
- Suprapubic discomfort or pressure
- Bladder distention
- Overflow dribbling
- Weak urine stream
- Feeling that the bladder is not empty

### Explain post void residual and use of a bladder scanner

- **Post void residual (PVR)** is the amount of urine left in the bladder after the client urinates.
- It helps show whether the bladder is emptying well.
- A **bladder scanner** is a noninvasive tool that uses sound waves to estimate how much urine is still in the bladder.
- A high post-void residual suggests **urinary retention**.

### Differentiate between the different types of incontinence

#### Stress incontinence

- Urine leaks with coughing, laughing, sneezing, or lifting.
- It is often linked to weak pelvic floor muscles.

#### Urge incontinence

- Urine leaks because the client has a strong sudden urge to void.
- It is often called **overactive bladder**.

#### Mixed incontinence

- A combination of stress and urge incontinence

#### Overflow incontinence

- The bladder stays too full and urine leaks out in small amounts.
- It is common with urinary retention or enlarged prostate.

#### Functional incontinence

- The urinary system may work normally, but the client cannot get to the toilet in time.
- Causes include poor mobility, confusion, or difficulty with clothing.

### Explain the purpose for pelvic floor training (Kegel exercises) as it relates to urination

(Kegel exercises tighten and strengthen the muscles that support the bladder.)

- Kegel exercises strengthen the pelvic floor muscles.
- Stronger pelvic floor muscles help support the bladder and urethra.
- This is especially helpful for **stress incontinence**.

### Assessment of urine

#### Urinalysis (Table 34-3)

##### Identify normal specific gravity

- Normal specific gravity is usually about **1.005 to 1.030**.

##### Explain the significance of the presence of WBCs, RBCs, ketones, glucose, and protein

- **White blood cells (WBCs):** may suggest infection or inflammation
- **Red blood cells (RBCs):** may suggest bleeding, stones, trauma, or infection
- **Ketones:** may suggest fat breakdown, starvation, or diabetic ketoacidosis
- **Glucose:** may suggest high blood sugar, often with diabetes
- **Protein:** may suggest kidney damage or kidney stress

### Explain the procedure for a 24-hour timed urine specimen collection

- Start the collection at the ordered time.
- Have the client empty the bladder at the start time and **discard that first urine**.
- After that, collect **all urine for the next 24 hours**.
- Keep the urine stored as directed, often on ice or refrigerated.
- At the end of 24 hours, collect the final urine and send it to the lab.
- Label the start and stop times clearly.

### Differentiate between random, clean-catch, and sterile specimen from a catheter

- **Random specimen:** collected at any time
- **Clean-catch specimen:** collected midstream after cleaning the periurethral area
- **Sterile specimen from a catheter:** collected from the catheter sampling port using sterile technique

### Explain the procedure for obtaining a clean-voided urine specimen

- Wash hands.
- Clean the peri area correctly.
- Begin urinating into the toilet.
- Catch the **midstream** urine in the sterile cup.
- Do not touch the inside of the cup or lid.
- Cap and send the specimen promptly.

### Review the procedure for obtaining a urine specimen from an indwelling catheter

- Do **not** collect urine from the drainage bag.
- Use the sampling port.
- Clean the port as directed by policy.
- Use sterile technique to withdraw the sample.
- Place it into a sterile container.
- Label and send promptly.

### Explain why a urine culture is obtained when a urinary tract infection (UTI) is suspected

- A urine culture helps identify the **specific bacteria** causing the infection.
- It also helps show which antibiotic is most likely to work (**sensitivity testing**).

### Describe rationale for the following interventions in preventing UTIs

#### Promoting fluid intake

- More fluids help flush bacteria out of the urinary tract.

#### Hygiene

- Good perineal hygiene lowers the number of bacteria near the urethra.
- Wiping front to back helps reduce spread of rectal bacteria.

#### Measures to promote voiding

- Regular emptying of the bladder helps prevent urine from sitting too long.
- Voiding after sexual activity may help lower bacterial entry.

#### Cranberry supplementation

- Cranberry may help reduce bacterial attachment in some patients.
- It is not a replacement for antibiotics when infection is present.

### Identify priority assessments for a client with an indwelling catheter

- Amount of urine
- Color, clarity, and odor
- Catheter patency
- Kinks or dependent loops
- Bag position below the bladder
- Securement of the catheter
- Pain, fever, or signs of infection
- Ongoing need for the catheter

### Review indications for urinary catheterization

- Acute urinary retention
- Need for accurate urine output in selected critically ill patients
- Some perioperative situations
- Severe wounds when urine must be kept away from the area
- Comfort care at end of life in selected cases

### Identify risks associated with urinary catheterization

- Catheter-associated urinary tract infection (CAUTI)
- Urethral trauma
- Bladder spasms
- Reduced mobility
- Discomfort

### Explain interventions to prevent catheter-associated infection

- Avoid unnecessary catheter use.
- Insert using sterile technique.
- Perform hand hygiene.
- Maintain a closed drainage system.
- Keep the drainage bag below bladder level.
- Prevent kinks and dependent loops.
- Provide routine catheter and perineal care per policy.
- Remove the catheter as soon as it is no longer needed.

### Differentiate between upper and lower urinary tract infection signs and symptoms

#### Lower urinary tract infection

- Dysuria
- Frequency
- Urgency
- Suprapubic discomfort
- Cloudy or bloody urine

#### Upper urinary tract infection

- Fever
- Chills
- Flank or back pain
- Nausea and vomiting
- More severe illness

### Review atypical signs and symptoms of urinary tract infection in the elderly client

- New confusion
- Weakness
- Poor appetite
- Functional decline
- Change from usual behavior
- Fever may be absent or mild

### Explain the purpose of closed continuous bladder irrigation

- Closed continuous bladder irrigation (CBI) is used to keep urine flowing and help prevent clot blockage.
- It is often used after bladder or prostate surgery.

### Identify priority assessments when caring for a client with a closed continuous bladder irrigation

- Inflow and outflow amounts
- Urine color
- Presence of clots
- Catheter patency
- Bladder spasms
- Suprapubic distention or pain

### Identify priority actions if the continuous bladder irrigation is not draining appropriately

- Assess the client first.
- Check for kinks, clamps, or dependent loops.
- Look for clots.
- Make sure the irrigation solution is flowing as ordered.
- Follow agency policy and notify the provider promptly if drainage is blocked or the client has pain or distention.

## Sleep

(This section is about normal sleep, common sleep problems, and nursing actions to improve rest.)

### Identify the stages of a normal sleep cycle

- **Stage 1 non-rapid eye movement (NREM):** very light sleep
- **Stage 2 NREM:** true sleep begins
- **Stage 3 NREM:** deep restorative sleep
- **Rapid eye movement (REM):** dreaming sleep, active brain, relaxed muscles
- A full sleep cycle is usually about **80 to 100 minutes**.
- Most adults go through **4 to 6 cycles** each night.

### Discuss characteristics of common sleep disorders

- **Insomnia:** trouble falling asleep, staying asleep, or waking too early
- **Sleep apnea:** repeated pauses in breathing, often with snoring and daytime sleepiness
- **Narcolepsy:** sudden sleep attacks during the day
- **Restless legs syndrome:** uncomfortable urge to move the legs, often worse at rest or at night

### Identify factors and behaviors that interfere with sleep

- Pain
- Anxiety or stress
- Caffeine
- Nicotine
- Alcohol
- Screen use before bed
- Noise or light
- Night shift work
- Frequent urination at night
- Illness or medications

### Discuss nursing interventions to promote normal sleep cycles for adults

- Keep a regular sleep schedule.
- Reduce noise and light.
- Cluster nursing care when possible.
- Control pain.
- Encourage a bedtime routine.
- Limit late caffeine.
- Avoid heavy meals right before sleep.
- Encourage daytime activity when appropriate.
- Encourage the client to void before bedtime.

### Discuss the general use of herbal therapy, hypnotics, and sedatives

- Non-drug sleep measures should be tried first when possible.
- Some clients use herbal products such as melatonin or valerian.
- Hypnotics and sedatives may be used short-term for sleep in some cases.
- All sleep aids should be used carefully because they can cause side effects and interactions.

### Identify safety issues with the use of sleep aides

- Falls
- Confusion
- Daytime drowsiness
- Oversedation
- Dependence
- Dangerous interaction with alcohol or other sedating drugs
- Extra risk in older adults

## Bowel Elimination

(This section is about normal bowel function, constipation, diarrhea, enemas, ostomies, and nasogastric tube care.)

### Identify how these factors may impact normal bowel elimination

#### Aging

- Peristalsis slows with age.
- Constipation becomes more common.

#### Diet/nutrition

- Low fiber intake can slow stool movement.
- Poor intake can reduce stool bulk.

#### Fluid intake

- Low fluid intake makes stool harder and drier.

#### Altered mobility

- Less movement slows bowel motility.

#### Pain

- Pain may make a client avoid using the toilet or straining.

#### Surgery

- Surgery, anesthesia, and opioids can slow bowel activity.

### Differentiate between common bowel elimination problems

#### Constipation

- Hard, dry, or infrequent stools
- Straining
- Bloating
- Feeling of incomplete emptying

##### Identify factors that contribute to constipation

- Low fiber intake
- Low fluid intake
- Immobility
- Opioids
- Ignoring the urge to defecate
- Depression
- Abdominal surgery

##### Identify interventions to prevent or treat constipation

- Increase fluids if allowed.
- Increase fiber gradually.
- Encourage activity.
- Offer regular time for toileting.
- Use stool softeners or laxatives as ordered.
- Treat the cause when possible.

### Identify high fiber, bulk-forming foods

- Fruits
- Vegetables
- Beans
- Whole grains
- Bran
- Oats

### Differentiate between stimulant, bulk-forming, hyperosmotic, and emollient laxatives

- **Stimulant laxatives:** make the bowel move more strongly (example: senna, bisacodyl)
- **Bulk-forming laxatives:** add bulk and water to the stool (example: psyllium)
- **Hyperosmotic laxatives:** pull water into the bowel (example: polyethylene glycol, lactulose)
- **Emollient laxatives/stool softeners:** soften the stool (example: docusate)

#### Diarrhea

- Frequent loose or watery stools

##### Explain how diarrhea can lead to fluid volume deficit

- Diarrhea causes loss of water and electrolytes.
- This can quickly lead to dehydration.

### Review the signs and symptoms of fluid volume deficit from exam 2

- Dry mucous membranes
- Thirst
- Tachycardia
- Low blood pressure
- Low urine output
- Weight loss
- Dizziness

##### Explain how diarrhea can contribute to metabolic acidosis and potassium imbalance

- Diarrhea can cause loss of bicarbonate, leading to **metabolic acidosis**.
- It can also cause potassium loss, leading to **hypokalemia**.

### Review ABG interpretation from exam 2

- Check **pH** first.
- Then check **PaCO2**.
- Then check **HCO3**.
- Then decide whether compensation is happening.

##### Determine appropriate isolation precautions for clients suspected or confirmed to have Clostridioides difficile (C. difficile)

- Use **contact precautions**.
- Wear gown and gloves.
- Wash hands with **soap and water**.
- Use appropriate room and equipment cleaning.

### Review the process of obtaining a stool specimen

- Use a clean specimen container.
- Avoid mixing stool with urine or toilet water.
- Label the specimen correctly.
- Send it to the lab promptly.

#### Stool culture

- A stool culture is collected to identify the organism causing infection.

### Enemas

(An enema puts fluid into the rectum to help empty the bowel or give medicine.)

#### Explain the purpose of enemas

- Relieve constipation
- Empty the bowel before procedures
- Give medication

### Describe the general procedure for administering a rectal enema

- Position the client in left Sims position.
- Use warm solution.
- Lubricate the tip.
- Insert gently.
- Raise the solution to the correct height.
- Allow the fluid to run in slowly.
- Encourage the client to hold the enema as tolerated.

### Explain why cramping can occur during a large volume rectal enema

- Cramping can happen if the fluid goes in too fast.
- Cramping can also happen if the solution is too cold.
- The bowel stretches and spasms.

### Explain what should be done if cramping occurs during administration of a rectal enema

- Stop or slow the flow.
- Encourage slow deep breathing.
- Restart more slowly when the cramping improves.

### Explain what a GI ostomy is

- A gastrointestinal (GI) ostomy is a surgically created opening of the intestine through the abdominal wall so stool can leave the body.

### Differentiate between ileostomy and colostomy (location and stool consistency)

- **Ileostomy:** comes from the ileum in the small intestine and usually has more liquid output
- **Colostomy:** comes from the colon and usually has more formed stool, especially if it is lower in the colon

### Discuss why clients with a new ileostomy are at risk for dehydration

- Ileostomy output is more liquid.
- The body can lose a lot of water and electrolytes through the stool.

### Explain what ‘pouching’ an ostomy means

- Pouching means placing and sealing an ostomy bag over the stoma to collect stool.

### Identify priority assessments during ostomy care

#### Stool consistency

- Helps show how the ostomy is functioning
- Helps show hydration status

#### Stoma appearance

- A healthy stoma is usually moist and pink to red.
- Pale, dark, dry, or black changes are concerning.

#### Peri-stomal skin

- Skin around the stoma should stay clean, dry, and intact.
- Redness and breakdown suggest leakage or irritation.

### Identify nutritional considerations for clients with new ostomies

- Introduce foods slowly.
- Encourage fluids.
- Chew food well.
- Watch for foods that cause gas, odor, or blockage.
- Clients with ileostomies often need extra fluids and may need more sodium depending on output.

### Describe nursing actions during insertion, securement, and basic care of a nasogastric tube

(A nasogastric, or NG, tube goes through the nose into the stomach.)

- Explain the procedure.
- Position the client upright.
- Insert the tube gently as ordered.
- Confirm placement per policy and by x-ray before first use.
- Secure the tube well.
- Connect suction or feeding as ordered.
- Provide oral and nasal care.
- Monitor drainage and tube function.

### Identify signs and symptoms that a client may demonstrate if the NG tube is not functioning appropriately

- Nausea
- Vomiting
- Abdominal distention
- Abdominal discomfort
- No drainage when drainage is expected
- Respiratory distress or coughing if placement is wrong

### Identify interventions that are appropriate for ensuring patency and function of an NG tube

- Check tube placement per policy.
- Keep tubing free from kinks.
- Check the suction setting.
- Irrigate or flush if ordered.
- Keep the drainage canister below the stomach.
- Make sure the tube is secured.

### Explain how nasogastric suction may contribute to metabolic alkalosis

- Nasogastric suction removes stomach acid.
- Losing acid can make the body more alkaline.
- This can lead to **metabolic alkalosis**.

## Nutrition

(This section is about nutrition risk, therapeutic diets, and enteral or parenteral feeding.)

### Identify clients at risk for nutritional problems

- Older adults
- Clients with swallowing problems
- Clients who are NPO for long periods
- Clients with nausea or vomiting
- Clients with chronic illness
- Clients with wounds
- Clients with poor appetite
- Clients with poverty or limited food access

### Discuss the relationship of malnourishment with a client’s risk for poor health outcomes

- Malnourishment delays healing.
- It increases infection risk.
- It causes weakness and fatigue.
- It can increase pressure injury risk.
- It can lead to longer recovery and more complications.

### Review common therapeutic diets

- Regular diet
- Clear liquid diet
- Full liquid diet
- Soft diet
- Low sodium diet
- Cardiac diet
- Consistent carbohydrate or diabetic diet
- High protein diet
- Renal diet
- Nothing by mouth (**NPO**)

### Explain how inadequate protein contributes to edema and poor wound healing

- Low protein can lower albumin.
- Low albumin makes fluid leak into tissues, causing **edema**.
- Protein is also needed to build and repair tissue.
- Without enough protein, wound healing is slower.

### Discuss nutrition therapy in relation to the client with dysphagia, NPO, nausea, and vomiting

- **Dysphagia:** may need texture-modified foods, thickened liquids, swallow evaluation, and aspiration precautions.
- **NPO:** no food or fluids by mouth; nutrition and hydration may need to be supported in other ways.
- **Nausea and vomiting:** often need small bland meals when allowed, antiemetics, and hydration support.

### Differentiate between enteral and parenteral nutrition

(Enteral uses the gut. Parenteral goes into the bloodstream through a vein.)

- **Enteral nutrition:** uses the gastrointestinal tract, such as tube feeding
- **Parenteral nutrition:** goes into the bloodstream through a vein

### Identify indications for enteral and parenteral nutrition

- **Enteral:** used when the gut works but the client cannot safely eat enough by mouth
- **Parenteral:** used when the gastrointestinal tract cannot be used or cannot absorb enough nutrients

### Identify the benefits of enteral feedings

- Keeps the gut working
- More natural than intravenous nutrition
- Lower infection risk than total parenteral nutrition in many cases
- Usually less expensive

### Discuss potential complications of enteral feeds

- Aspiration
- Diarrhea
- Nausea or vomiting
- Tube displacement
- Tube clogging
- Poor tolerance or high residuals
- Skin breakdown at the tube site

### Identify essential steps in introducing liquid feedings through an indwelling feeding tube

- Confirm the order.
- Confirm placement before first use with **x-ray**.
- Follow policy for placement checks after that.
- Raise the head of the bed 30 to 45 degrees.
- Start the feeding slowly.
- Use the pump as ordered.
- Flush the tube as ordered.

### Explain the rationale for the following assessments

#### X-ray before first enteral feeding

- Confirms the tube is in the correct place before feeding starts.
- This helps prevent feeding into the lungs.

#### Checking for gastric residual volumes

- Helps assess tolerance and delayed stomach emptying.

#### Checking gastrointestinal aspirate pH

- Helps support placement checking per policy.

### Describe interventions to prevent aspiration during enteral feeding

- Keep the head of the bed elevated.
- Check tube placement per policy.
- Start feedings slowly.
- Monitor tolerance.
- Give oral care.
- Hold feeding and report concerns if the client shows poor tolerance or aspiration signs.

### Explain why enteral feedings are started at slow rates

- The gastrointestinal tract needs time to adjust.
- Slow rates help lower the risk of nausea, cramping, diarrhea, and aspiration.

### Identify interventions to prevent and treat diarrhea associated with enteral feedings

- Start slowly.
- Use the feeding pump as ordered.
- Avoid contamination of equipment and formula.
- Review medications such as antibiotics.
- Assess for formula intolerance.
- Maintain hydration and skin care.

### Identify benefits of total parenteral nutrition

- Provides complete nutrition when the gut cannot be used
- Can be carefully adjusted to the client’s needs

### Identify potential complications of total parenteral nutrition

- Infection
- Hyperglycemia
- Fluid overload
- Electrolyte imbalance
- Liver problems
- Catheter complications

### Describe methods for preventing complications of total parenteral therapy

- Use strict aseptic technique.
- Use an infusion pump.
- Monitor blood glucose.
- Monitor labs and intake/output.
- Check daily weights.
- Watch the central line site closely.
- Change tubing and dressings per policy.

### Explain the procedure for initiating parenteral nutrition using a central catheter

- Verify the order and solution.
- Confirm central catheter placement before use.
- Use a dedicated line for parenteral nutrition.
- Start the infusion with a pump as ordered.
- Monitor blood glucose, labs, fluid status, and line status closely.

### Explain why it is essential to confirm placement of the catheter before starting the infusion

- Parenteral nutrition is very concentrated.
- If the catheter is not in the correct central vein, serious tissue damage can happen.

## Stress and Coping

(This section is about how the body responds to stress and how people manage it.)

### Explain the body’s natural processes that help keep balance or restore it when under stress

- The body tries to maintain **homeostasis**, which means internal balance.
- Stress activates the **sympathetic nervous system**, also called the fight-or-flight response.
- Hormones such as **adrenaline** and **cortisol** help the body respond.
- The **parasympathetic system** helps the body calm down and recover after the stress passes.

### Review normal coping strategies

- Problem solving
- Talking with supportive people
- Exercise
- Deep breathing
- Prayer or spiritual support
- Journaling
- Rest
- Healthy routines

### Review common defense mechanisms (Table 41-1)

(Defense mechanisms are automatic ways people protect themselves from stress or anxiety.)

- **Denial:** refusing to accept reality
- **Projection:** placing one’s feelings on someone else
- **Displacement:** moving feelings to a safer target
- **Regression:** returning to less mature behavior under stress
- **Rationalization:** making excuses that sound logical
- **Repression:** pushing distressing thoughts out of awareness
- **Sublimation:** changing feelings into a healthy activity
- **Compensation:** strengthening one area to make up for weakness in another

### Explain factors that may affect coping mechanisms

- Physical health
- Fatigue
- Support system
- Finances
- Previous experience
- Culture
- Personal beliefs
- Developmental stage

### Identify nursing interventions for clients with ineffective coping mechanisms

- Use therapeutic communication.
- Assess stressors and current coping.
- Encourage expression of feelings.
- Reinforce strengths.
- Teach coping skills.
- Involve support people if appropriate.
- Refer to counseling, social work, or spiritual care when needed.
- Maintain safety if the client is at risk.

### Review therapeutic communication techniques from exam 1

- Active listening
- Silence
- Open-ended questions
- Reflection
- Clarification
- Summarizing
- Empathy
- Avoid false reassurance and judgment

## Sexuality

(This section is about respectful assessment of sexual health and awareness of nurse bias.)

### Differentiate between sexual orientation and gender identity

- **Sexual orientation:** who a person is emotionally or sexually attracted to
- **Gender identity:** how a person understands and identifies their own gender

### Identify client risk factors in the area of sexual health

- Risk for sexually transmitted infections
- Multiple partners
- Violence or abuse
- Chronic illness
- Medication effects
- Body image concerns
- Lack of privacy
- Older adult myths
- Cultural stigma

### Identify the impact of your personal attitudes, beliefs, and biases related to sexuality

- Bias can make assessment incomplete.
- Bias can make the client feel judged or unsafe.
- Nurses must provide respectful, nonjudgmental care even when personal beliefs differ.

### Discuss basic strategies for discussing sexual health with a client

- Ensure privacy.
- Use calm professional language.
- Do not assume orientation or gender identity.
- Ask permission before discussing sensitive topics.
- Normalize the topic.
- Listen without judgment.

### PLISSIT model Table 40-4

(PLISSIT is a simple step-by-step framework for talking about sexual health concerns.)

- **P - Permission:** give the client permission to talk about sexual concerns
- **LI - Limited Information:** give basic facts
- **SS - Specific Suggestions:** give practical advice related to the concern

## Perioperative Nursing

(This section is about preoperative, intraoperative, and postoperative nursing care.)

### Identify risk factors that affect a client’s response to surgery

- Age
- Poor nutrition
- Obesity
- Smoking
- Chronic disease
- Infection
- Medications
- Allergies
- Alcohol or drug use
- Fluid and electrolyte imbalance

### Explain the role and responsibilities of the RN in the preoperative surgical phase

(Preoperative nursing focuses on assessment, teaching, consent, and safe preparation.)

#### Assessment

##### Identify priority pre-operative assessments

- Confirm identity, procedure, and site.
- Check allergies.
- Check NPO status.
- Review vital signs and relevant lab results.
- Assess baseline respiratory, cardiac, and neurological status.
- Review medications and past health history.

###### Latex allergy

- Important because latex exposure can cause a serious reaction during surgery.

###### Iodine allergy

- Important because skin prep or contrast-related products may be used.

##### Past anesthesia reactions (personal or family)

- Ask about severe nausea, difficult intubation, or malignant hyperthermia.

###### Malignant hyperthermia

- Malignant hyperthermia is a rare but life-threatening reaction to certain anesthetic drugs.
- It can cause rapid temperature rise, muscle rigidity, and serious instability.

##### Medication history

- Ask about prescription drugs.
- Ask about over-the-counter drugs.
- Ask about herbs and supplements.
- Ask about alcohol or recreational drug use.

###### Explain risks associated with aspirin or anticoagulant therapy

- These drugs increase bleeding risk during and after surgery.

###### Identify herbal supplements that may increase risks associated with surgery

- Garlic
- Ginkgo
- Ginseng
- Some other supplements can also increase bleeding or interact with anesthesia.

##### Client learning and readiness to learn

- Assess anxiety, pain, language, and ability to understand.

###### Pre-op instructions (NPO, medications)

- Teach NPO instructions clearly.
- Teach which medicines to take or hold, based on orders.
- Explain basic preparation steps.

###### Post-op expectations (pain control, coughing, deep breathing, ambulation)

- Teach these before surgery because the client may be drowsy later.

##### Informed consent

- The provider explains the procedure, risks, benefits, and alternatives.
- The nurse checks that the form is signed and reports if the client seems confused or still has questions.

### Differentiate between diagnostic, palliative, ablative, constructive, cosmetic, and reconstructive surgeries

(These words tell you the main reason the surgery is being done.)

- **Diagnostic:** done to find the cause of a problem
- **Palliative:** done to relieve symptoms
- **Ablative:** removes diseased tissue or a body part
- **Constructive:** restores function
- **Cosmetic:** improves appearance
- **Reconstructive:** repairs or rebuilds after injury, disease, or surgery

### Explain the role and responsibilities of the RN in the intraoperative surgical phase

- Protect safety
- Maintain asepsis
- Position the client safely
- Monitor the client and equipment
- Perform counts as required
- Document and communicate clearly

### Explain the Joint Commission's Universal Protocol that is done before the surgery

(This is the safety check the team does before surgery starts.)

- Confirm the **correct patient**
- Confirm the **correct procedure**
- Confirm the **correct site**
- Mark the site when needed
- Perform a **time-out** before incision

### Identify interventions to prevent injury to the client

#### Positioning

- Pad pressure points.
- Keep the body aligned.
- Avoid nerve injury.

#### Prevention of hypothermia

- Use warm blankets or warming devices as ordered.
- Use warmed fluids when indicated.

#### Airway management and effects of anesthesia

- Remember anesthesia lowers protective reflexes.
- Support airway and breathing carefully.

#### Surgical asepsis

- Keep sterile items sterile.

### Describe basic principles of surgical aseptic technique

- Sterile touches sterile.
- If there is doubt, it is contaminated.
- Keep the sterile field above waist level and in sight.
- Moisture can contaminate the field.
- The edges of a sterile field are considered contaminated.

### Explain the role and responsibilities of the RN in the postoperative surgical phase

- Receive a clear handoff report.
- Assess airway, breathing, and circulation first.
- Monitor for complications.
- Manage pain.
- Support mobility, lung expansion, and circulation.
- Protect the wound and promote healing.

### Explain how clear communication contributes to client safety

- Clear handoff communication helps prevent missed problems, treatment delay, and medication or procedure errors.

### Prioritize client care after anesthesia (ABCs)

- **Airway first**
- Then **breathing**
- Then **circulation**

#### Respiratory

##### Identify potential respiratory postoperative complications

- Airway obstruction
- Aspiration
- Atelectasis
- Pneumonia
- Hypoxemia
- Oversedation

##### Explain early signs of hypoxia and why a post-surgical client is at risk for this

- Early signs include restlessness, anxiety, tachycardia, tachypnea, and low oxygen saturation.
- Post-surgical clients are at risk because of anesthesia, pain, shallow breathing, immobility, and opioid use.

##### Identify respiratory breath sounds associated with atelectasis and pneumonia

- Crackles are common.
- Decreased breath sounds may occur with poor lung expansion.

##### Explain interventions to prevent complications

#### Aspiration/airway obstruction

##### Appropriate positioning

- Side-lying or head elevated as appropriate helps protect the airway.

#### Atelectasis/pneumonia

##### Coughing and deep breathing

- Helps open the lungs and clear secretions.

##### Incentive spirometry

- Encourages slow deep breaths.

##### Hydration

- Helps thin secretions.

##### Early ambulation

- Improves lung expansion and circulation.

##### Suctioning

- Helps when the client cannot clear secretions well.

#### Hypoxemia

##### Oximetry monitoring

- Tracks oxygen saturation.

##### Oxygen therapy

- Supports oxygen delivery.

##### Sedation monitoring

- Helps find oversedation before breathing worsens.

#### Circulation

##### Identify circulatory postoperative complications

- Bleeding
- Hemorrhage
- Hypovolemia
- Shock
- Thrombus formation

##### Identify common signs and symptoms of bleeding or hemorrhage

- Tachycardia
- Low blood pressure
- Pale cool skin
- Rising drainage
- Swelling
- Restlessness
- Low urine output

### Explain interventions when these occur

- Assess immediately.
- Maintain airway, breathing, and circulation.
- Apply oxygen if needed.
- Check the wound and drains.
- Notify the provider or rapid response as needed.
- Give fluids or blood products as ordered.

### Prioritize care for the client receiving blood products

- Verify the blood correctly with another licensed person per policy.
- Monitor vital signs.
- Stay with the client closely at the start.
- Stop the transfusion immediately if a reaction is suspected.

### Identify signs and symptoms of a hemolytic transfusion reaction

- Fever
- Chills
- Low back pain
- Chest tightness
- Shortness of breath
- Hypotension
- Dark urine

#### Thrombus formation: Explain the purpose for the following preventive interventions

##### Early ambulation

- Improves venous blood flow

##### Sequential compression device

- Squeezes the legs to reduce venous stasis

##### Leg exercises

- Promote circulation while in bed

##### Hydration

- Helps prevent blood from becoming too concentrated

##### Anticoagulant therapy

- Helps lower clot formation risk when ordered

#### Fluid and electrolytes

##### Review priority nursing assessments and interventions to prevent and identify fluid and electrolyte abnormalities

- Monitor vital signs.
- Monitor intake and output.
- Check daily weight.
- Review lab results.
- Watch mental status, edema, lung sounds, and urine output.

##### Review intravenous fluids and how they relate to the surgical client

- Intravenous fluids replace losses and support blood pressure and hydration.
- Too much can cause overload.
- Too little can worsen poor perfusion.

#### Thermoregulation

##### Define malignant hyperthermia

- Malignant hyperthermia is a dangerous reaction to certain anesthetic drugs.
- It causes very high temperature, muscle rigidity, fast heart rate, and rising carbon dioxide levels.

##### Explain the significance of an elevated temperature after surgery

- It may suggest infection, atelectasis, dehydration, or malignant hyperthermia depending on timing and severity.

###### Identify additional assessments when a post-op client demonstrates an elevated temperature

- Assess the timing after surgery.
- Assess lungs and breath sounds.
- Assess the wound and drainage.
- Assess urine and other infection signs.
- Assess hydration and fluid status.

### Neurological function (patient’s level of consciousness (LOC), orientation, and responsiveness after surgery)

- Assess level of consciousness.
- Check orientation.
- Check response to voice and touch.
- Compare with expected recovery after anesthesia.

### Explain why neurologic function assessment is important following surgery

- It helps detect oversedation, hypoxia, stroke-like changes, or delayed recovery from anesthesia.

### Identify priority nursing assessments following surgery

- Airway
- Breathing
- Oxygen saturation
- Circulation
- Pain
- Level of consciousness
- Wound and dressing
- Drains
- Urine output
- Temperature

### Identify priority nursing interventions following surgery

- Maintain airway and safe positioning.
- Monitor vital signs and oxygenation.
- Control pain safely.
- Encourage deep breathing and coughing.
- Encourage early ambulation when allowed.
- Check wound, dressings, drains, and urine output.
- Protect the client from falls.

### Skin Integrity and wound care

### Review concepts of wound assessment and care from exam 2 content

- Assess wound edges, drainage, swelling, redness, warmth, and pain.
- Watch for approximation and signs of infection.
- Use correct dressing care and asepsis.

### Identify risks for impaired wound healing related to obesity and surgery

- Fat tissue has less blood flow.
- The incision may have more tension.
- Infection risk is higher.

### Demonstrate interventions to prevent dehiscence and evisceration

(Dehiscence means a wound opens. Evisceration means organs push out through the wound.)

#### Splinting

- Support the incision during coughing, deep breathing, or movement.

### Identify signs and symptoms of an infected wound

- Redness
- Warmth
- Swelling
- Pain
- Purulent drainage
- Bad odor
- Fever
- Delayed healing

### GI function and nutrition

#### Identify priority assessments

- Bowel sounds
- Abdominal distention
- Nausea and vomiting
- Passage of gas
- Tolerance of diet

### Explain paralytic ileus

- Paralytic ileus means the bowel is not moving normally.
- It can happen after surgery, anesthesia, or opioid use.
- The client may have distention, nausea, vomiting, and lack of flatus.

### Explain diet progression after being NPO

(NPO means nothing by mouth.)

- The diet often starts with clear liquids.
- It is advanced slowly as tolerated and as bowel function returns.

### Explain how ambulation stimulates peristalsis

(Peristalsis is the wave-like squeezing movement that pushes food through the intestines.)

- Movement helps wake up the bowel and improve peristalsis.

### Urinary elimination

### Explain the effects of anesthesia on the bladder

- Anesthesia can reduce bladder sensation and bladder emptying.
- This can lead to urinary retention.

### Identify priority assessments

- Time of last void
- Bladder distention
- Urine output
- Discomfort
- Catheter patency if present

### Comfort

### Identify signs and symptoms of pain (use content from exam 2)

- Client report of pain
- Grimacing
- Guarding
- Restlessness
- Increased heart rate or blood pressure
- Shallow breathing

### Review principles of analgesic administration

- Assess before and after giving pain medicine.
- Give medication on time when needed.
- Use the safest route and dose ordered.
- Watch for oversedation and slowed breathing.
- Combine medication with non-drug comfort measures when appropriate.

### Discuss relationship of pain to function and complications of immobility

- Poor pain control reduces deep breathing, coughing, movement, and sleep.
- This raises the risk for atelectasis, pneumonia, clots, constipation, and slower healing.
