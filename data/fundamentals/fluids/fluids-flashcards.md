# Fluids Flashcards

These flashcards follow the fluids guide and use a simple, exam-friendly style.

## How to use this deck

- **Front** = the question side.
- **Back** = the answer side.
- **Why it matters** = a short reason this card matters for tests or clinical thinking.

## Processes that regulate fluid and electrolyte distribution

### Card 1

**Front:** What do osmosis, diffusion, and filtration mean?

**Back:**
- **Osmosis:** water movement across a membrane.
- **Diffusion:** particle movement from higher to lower concentration.
- **Filtration:** fluid movement caused by pressure.

**Why it matters:** These three processes explain many fluid-shift questions.

### Card 2

**Front:** How do ADH, RAAS, and thirst help regulate fluid balance?

**Back:**
- **ADH:** helps the kidneys hold on to water.
- **RAAS:** helps the body keep sodium and water and raise blood volume.
- **Thirst:** tells the person to drink more fluid.

**Why it matters:** These are the body's major fluid-balance controls.

### Card 3

**Front:** Why are sodium, potassium, and calcium high-yield electrolytes?

**Back:**
- **Sodium:** strongly affects fluid balance and neurologic status.
- **Potassium:** strongly affects muscles and heart rhythm.
- **Calcium:** strongly affects nerves, muscles, and heart contraction.

**Why it matters:** These are the electrolyte patterns most often tested.

## Common fluid and electrolyte imbalances

### Card 1

**Front:** What are common risk factors and signs of fluid volume deficit?

**Back:**
- Risks: vomiting, diarrhea, fever, sweating, diuretics, poor intake, bleeding.
- Signs: dry mucous membranes, thirst, poor skin turgor, tachycardia, low blood pressure, low urine output, weight loss, dizziness.

**Why it matters:** Fluid deficit is a classic nursing assessment problem.

### Card 2

**Front:** What are common risk factors and signs of fluid volume excess?

**Back:**
- Risks: heart failure, kidney failure, liver disease, too much IV fluid, high sodium intake.
- Signs: edema, weight gain, crackles, shortness of breath, high blood pressure, bounding pulse, swollen neck veins.

**Why it matters:** This helps you recognize overload quickly.

### Card 3 (Compare)

**Front:** What are common findings of hyponatremia and hypernatremia?

**Back:**
- **Hyponatremia:** headache, nausea, confusion, lethargy, seizures if severe.
- **Hypernatremia:** thirst, restlessness, weakness, confusion, irritability, neurologic changes.

**Why it matters:** Sodium problems often show up through mental-status changes.

### Card 4 (Compare)

**Front:** What are common findings of hypokalemia and hyperkalemia?

**Back:**
- **Hypokalemia:** weakness, fatigue, constipation, weak bowel activity, dysrhythmias.
- **Hyperkalemia:** weakness, tingling in some cases, dangerous dysrhythmias, ECG changes.

**Why it matters:** Potassium problems are high-risk because of the heart.

### Card 5 (Compare)

**Front:** What are common findings of hypocalcemia and hypercalcemia?

**Back:**
- **Hypocalcemia:** tingling, muscle cramps, tetany, hyperactive reflexes, seizures.
- **Hypercalcemia:** weakness, constipation, confusion, decreased reflexes, dysrhythmias in severe cases.

**Why it matters:** Calcium problems are often tested through nerve and muscle findings.

### Card 6

**Front:** What do positive Chvostek's and Trousseau's signs suggest?

**Back:**
- Both suggest **hypocalcemia**.
- **Chvostek's sign:** facial twitching when the facial nerve is tapped.
- **Trousseau's sign:** hand spasm when the blood pressure cuff is inflated.

**Why it matters:** These named signs are classic exam material.

### Card 7

**Front:** What are the main nursing priorities for fluid and electrolyte imbalances?

**Back:**
- Reassess vital signs.
- Check daily weight and intake and output.
- Review lab values.
- Watch heart rhythm when potassium or calcium is abnormal.
- Watch mental status when sodium is abnormal.
- Treat the cause, not only the number.

**Why it matters:** This card turns physiology into nursing action.

## Parenteral therapy

### Card 1

**Front:** What is the purpose of parenteral therapy?

**Back:**
- To replace fluids and electrolytes
- To give medications
- To give blood products
- To give nutrition when the gut cannot be used
- To provide a fast route when quick treatment is needed

**Why it matters:** Parenteral therapy is about access, speed, and support.

### Card 2 (Compare)

**Front:** How do isotonic, hypotonic, and hypertonic solutions differ?

**Back:**
- **Isotonic:** stays mainly in the bloodstream.
- **Hypotonic:** moves water into cells.
- **Hypertonic:** pulls water into the bloodstream from cells.

**Why it matters:** Solution type helps predict where the fluid will go.

### Card 3

**Front:** What are common examples of isotonic, hypotonic, and hypertonic IV solutions?

**Back:**
- **Isotonic:** 0.9% sodium chloride, lactated Ringer's
- **Hypotonic:** 0.45% sodium chloride
- **Hypertonic:** 3% sodium chloride

**Why it matters:** Exams often ask you to match the solution with the category.

### Card 4

**Front:** What are common complications of parenteral therapy?

**Back:**
- Inaccurate infusion rate
- Infiltration
- Extravasation
- Phlebitis
- Infection
- Fluid overload
- Air embolism

**Why it matters:** These are common IV-therapy safety questions.

### Card 5

**Front:** What are the priority assessments for a patient receiving parenteral therapy?

**Back:**
- Check the IV site for redness, swelling, pain, leakage, coolness, or warmth.
- Confirm the correct fluid, rate, and tubing setup.
- Assess lung sounds, edema, vital signs, and oxygenation.
- Monitor intake and output, daily weight, and lab values.
- Check whether the therapy is helping.

**Why it matters:** Good IV assessment prevents complications from becoming severe.

### Card 6 (Safety)

**Front:** How do infiltration, extravasation, and phlebitis differ?

**Back:**
- **Infiltration:** regular IV fluid leaks into tissue.
- **Extravasation:** a tissue-damaging fluid or drug leaks into tissue.
- **Phlebitis:** the vein becomes irritated or inflamed.

**Why it matters:** These problems sound alike, but the nursing response is not exactly the same.

### Card 7

**Front:** What nursing actions are important when IV complications are suspected?

**Back:**
- Stop or slow the infusion when needed and follow policy and orders.
- Remove the IV if infiltration, phlebitis, or infection requires it.
- Elevate the extremity when appropriate for infiltration.
- Protect the tissue and follow policy for extravasation.
- Report serious changes quickly.

**Why it matters:** Action questions usually focus on what the nurse does right away.

## High-yield scenarios

### Card 1 (Priority)

**Front:** A client receiving a large amount of IV fluid now has crackles, shortness of breath, high blood pressure, and swollen neck veins. What problem should the nurse suspect?

**Back:**
- Think about **fluid overload** or **fluid volume excess**.
- Assess breathing and oxygenation right away.
- Follow orders and policy for slowing or stopping the fluid if appropriate.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic IV-fluid complication scenario.

### Card 2 (Red Flag)

**Front:** A client with low sodium becomes confused and starts having seizure activity. Why is this so concerning?

**Back:**
- Severe **hyponatremia** can cause serious neurologic problems.
- The nurse should treat this as urgent, protect safety, and monitor neurologic status closely.

**Why it matters:** Sodium problems are dangerous because the brain is very sensitive to them.

### Card 3 (Safety)

**Front:** Why must IV potassium be given carefully and never too fast?

**Back:**
- Potassium strongly affects cardiac rhythm.
- Giving IV potassium too fast can cause dangerous dysrhythmias.
- Heart rhythm and kidney function must be monitored.

**Why it matters:** This is one of the most important medication-safety facts in fluids and electrolytes.
