# Fluids Application Questions

These questions help you practice NCLEX-style thinking for fluids and electrolytes. The language is simple, but the situations are realistic and focus on priority, safety, and clinical judgment.

## How to use this file

- Try to answer each question before reading the answer.
- Pay close attention to words like **first**, **best**, **priority**, and **report immediately**.
- For **SATA** questions, choose only the options that are fully correct.
- If you miss a question, reread the explanation and connect it back to the guide.

## Describe processes that regulate fluid and electrolyte distribution

### Question 1 (Physiology)

**Question:** A client has had several days of vomiting and poor oral intake. The nurse notes low urine output and concentrated urine. Which body response is most likely helping the kidneys conserve water?

A. Increased antidiuretic hormone (ADH) release  
B. Decreased aldosterone release  
C. Decreased thirst response  
D. Increased diffusion of sodium into cells

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:** Low urine output with concentrated urine is the clue here. That pattern means the body is trying to save water during volume loss, and ADH tells the kidneys to hold on to water. Aldosterone affects sodium and volume too, but the urine-concentrating clue points more directly to ADH.

### Question 2 (Assessment)

**Question:** A client with heart failure has dependent edema in both legs. Which process best explains fluid moving from the blood vessels into the tissues because of increased pressure in the capillaries?

A. Diffusion  
B. Filtration  
C. Osmosis  
D. Active transport

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:** The important clue is increased capillary pressure from heart failure. When pressure inside the capillary rises, fluid gets pushed out into the tissues, which is filtration and explains the edema. Osmosis can be tempting, but this question is about pressure, not water moving toward a higher concentration.

## Describe common fluid and electrolyte imbalances

### Question 3 (Priority)

**Question:** Which assessment finding is most concerning for fluid volume deficit in an older adult with diarrhea?

A. Dry lips and thirst  
B. Weight loss of 2 kg in 2 days  
C. Heart rate 118/min, blood pressure 88/54 mm Hg, urine output 15 mL/hr  
D. Mild weakness when walking to the bathroom

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:** This set of findings shows more than simple dehydration. Tachycardia, hypotension, and urine output of 15 mL/hr point to poor organ perfusion and possible shock. On NCLEX-style questions, unstable blood pressure and very low urine output matter more than dry lips or mild weakness.

### Question 4 (Recognition)

**Question:** A client receiving large-volume IV fluids becomes short of breath. Which additional finding most strongly supports fluid volume excess?

A. Flat neck veins  
B. Crackles and a bounding pulse  
C. Weight loss and poor skin turgor  
D. Dark, concentrated urine

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:** Shortness of breath plus crackles is the big clue. That pattern means fluid is backing up into the lungs, and a bounding pulse fits fluid volume excess. Flat neck veins and dark urine would point more toward volume deficit.

### Question 5 (Compare/Contrast)

**Question:** Which finding best fits **hyponatremia** rather than hypernatremia?

A. Severe thirst and dry mucous membranes  
B. Restlessness after not drinking enough water  
C. Headache, nausea, and increasing confusion  
D. Hot, dry skin after heavy sweating

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:** The key clue is the neurologic change. Low sodium can cause water to shift into brain cells, leading to headache, nausea, confusion, and even seizures. Strong thirst and dry mucous membranes sound more like hypernatremia from water loss.

### Question 6 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** A client asks how to lower the chance of hypernatremia during a fever with poor appetite. Which response is best?

A. "Drink small amounts of water often unless you were told to limit fluids."  
B. "Choose salty foods instead of fluids."  
C. "Avoid fluids until the fever is gone."  
D. "Take extra diuretics so you do not swell."

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:** Think about what usually drives hypernatremia: losing more water than sodium. Drinking small amounts of water often is the safest teaching point when fluids are not restricted. Salty foods, avoiding fluids, or extra diuretics would push the client further toward dehydration.

### Question 7 (Report to Provider)

**Question:** Which client with hypokalemia should the nurse report to the provider first?

A. Potassium 3.3 mEq/L with mild leg cramps  
B. Potassium 3.1 mEq/L with constipation  
C. Potassium 2.8 mEq/L with an irregular pulse  
D. Potassium 3.4 mEq/L with poor appetite

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:** An irregular pulse turns this into a priority problem. Potassium of 2.8 mEq/L can trigger dangerous dysrhythmias, so this client needs provider follow-up first. Constipation or leg cramps matter, but they are not as urgent as a cardiac sign.

### Question 8 (Priority)

**Question:** A client with kidney failure has muscle weakness and peaked T waves on the ECG. Which electrolyte problem should the nurse suspect?

A. Hypokalemia  
B. Hyperkalemia  
C. Hypocalcemia  
D. Hypernatremia

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:** Peaked T waves are one of the classic ECG clues for high potassium. Kidney failure also matters, because the kidneys may not clear potassium well. When you see weakness plus peaked T waves, hyperkalemia should move to the top of your list.

### Question 9 (Assessment)

**Question:** During assessment, which finding is most suggestive of hypocalcemia?

A. Decreased reflexes and constipation  
B. Facial twitching when the cheek is tapped  
C. Warm, flushed skin and a bounding pulse  
D. Intense thirst with increased urine output

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:** Facial twitching when the cheek is tapped is Chvostek's sign. That happens because low calcium makes nerves and muscles more excitable, so twitching and tingling are common clues. Decreased reflexes and constipation would steer you more toward hypercalcemia.

## Discuss appropriate interventions for common fluid and electrolyte imbalances

### Question 10 (SATA)

**Question:** The nurse is caring for a client with severe hyponatremia and increasing confusion. Which nursing actions are appropriate? **Select all that apply.**

A. Use seizure precautions  
B. Monitor neurologic status closely  
C. Encourage large amounts of free water  
D. Recheck sodium levels as ordered  
E. Ignore the confusion until the next routine lab draw

**Correct answer:** A, B, D

**Explanation:** Increasing confusion is the warning sign here. Severe hyponatremia can cause cerebral edema and seizures, so seizure precautions, close neurologic checks, and repeat sodium monitoring are the right moves. Free water is the trap, because it can drop the sodium even more.

### Question 11 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** A client takes furosemide and has a history of hypokalemia. Which teaching is best?

A. "Stop the medicine when your urine is light yellow."  
B. "Report new muscle weakness or palpitations right away."  
C. "Limit all foods that contain potassium."  
D. "Drink sports drinks only and avoid water."

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:** Furosemide can lower potassium, so the teaching needs to focus on early warning signs. New muscle weakness or palpitations may mean hypokalemia is getting worse and should be reported right away. A common test-day trap is choosing a food answer when the safer teaching is when to call.

### Question 12 (First/Best Action)

**Question:** A client with fluid volume excess has crackles, blood pressure 170/96 mm Hg, and oxygen saturation 89% on room air while IV fluid is infusing. What is the nurse's best first action?

A. Increase the IV rate to improve kidney perfusion  
B. Place the client flat in bed  
C. Slow or stop the IV fluid and raise the head of the bed  
D. Encourage the client to drink more oral fluids

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:** The client is already showing fluid overload and impaired oxygenation. Slowing or stopping the IV removes the source of the problem, and raising the head of the bed helps breathing right away. Giving more fluid or laying the client flat would make the situation worse.

### Question 13 (SATA)

**Question:** Which findings in clients with fluid or electrolyte problems should the nurse report to the provider immediately? **Select all that apply.**

A. New seizure activity in a client with hyponatremia  
B. Peaked T waves in a client with high potassium  
C. Mild dry mouth in a client with fluid volume deficit  
D. Laryngeal stridor in a client with hypocalcemia  
E. Mild ankle swelling after standing all day

**Correct answer:** A, B, D

**Explanation:** Seizures, peaked T waves, and laryngeal stridor are the danger clues in this group. They point to brain injury risk, cardiac instability, or airway compromise, so those are the findings to report now. Mild dry mouth or ankle swelling is not in the same priority category.

## Explain the purpose of parenteral therapy

### Question 14 (Best Explanation)

**Question:** A client has persistent vomiting, is NPO, and has blood pressure 92/58 mm Hg with poor urine output. Why is parenteral therapy most appropriate?

A. It is the safest long-term method for all clients  
B. It can replace fluids and electrolytes quickly when the oral route is not useful  
C. It prevents all electrolyte problems immediately  
D. It removes the need for ongoing assessment

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:** The client cannot keep fluids down and is already showing signs of poor perfusion. IV therapy is the best option because it can replace fluids and electrolytes quickly when the oral route is not working. It helps stabilize the client, but ongoing assessment is still essential.

## Differentiate between types of parenteral solutions

### Question 15 (Solution Selection)

**Question:** Which IV solution is most appropriate for a client with fluid volume deficit from vomiting who needs intravascular volume expansion?

A. 0.45% sodium chloride  
B. 3% sodium chloride  
C. 0.9% sodium chloride  
D. D10W

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:** The goal is to expand intravascular volume, not shift water into cells. Normal saline is isotonic, so it stays mostly in the vascular space and is a solid first choice for hypovolemia from vomiting. A hypotonic fluid like 0.45% sodium chloride is a common trap because it does not support circulation as well at first.

### Question 16 (Safety)

**Question:** The nurse questions an order for 0.45% sodium chloride for which client?

A. A client with hypernatremia and dry mucous membranes  
B. A client with a recent head injury and suspected increased intracranial pressure  
C. A client who needs careful free-water replacement  
D. A client with cellular dehydration and stable blood pressure

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:** The safety clue is the recent head injury with possible increased intracranial pressure. Because 0.45% sodium chloride is hypotonic, it can move water into cells and worsen cerebral edema. When the brain is already at risk, hypotonic fluids should make you pause.

### Question 17 (Priority Follow-Up)

**Question:** A client is receiving 3% sodium chloride for severe symptomatic hyponatremia. Which assessment finding requires the most immediate follow-up?

A. Crackles and rising blood pressure  
B. Mild thirst  
C. Urine output 40 mL/hr  
D. Slightly dry lips

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:** Hypertonic saline can help severe hyponatremia, but it needs close watching. Crackles with a rising blood pressure suggest the fluid is now pulling too much volume into the bloodstream and causing overload. Breathing-related changes matter far more than mild thirst or slightly dry lips.

## Identify potential complications of parenteral therapy

### Question 18 (Complication Recognition)

**Question:** A peripheral IV site is cool, pale, swollen, and uncomfortable. The infusion is running slowly. Which complication is most likely?

A. Phlebitis  
B. Infection  
C. Infiltration  
D. Air embolism

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:** Cool, pale, swollen tissue with a slowed infusion is the classic infiltration picture. It means the IV fluid is leaking into the surrounding tissue instead of staying in the vein. Phlebitis is the tempting wrong answer, but that site is usually warm, red, and tender.

### Question 19 (First/Best Action)

**Question:** A vesicant medication is infusing and the IV site becomes painful, swollen, and blistered. What is the nurse's best first action?

A. Remove the catheter immediately and apply pressure  
B. Stop the infusion but leave the catheter in place  
C. Flush the line to clear the medication  
D. Massage the area to spread the drug

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:** Pain, swelling, and blistering during a vesicant infusion should make you think extravasation. The first move is to stop the drug, but leave the catheter in place in case aspiration or an antidote is needed. Flushing or massaging is unsafe because it can spread the medication deeper into the tissue.

### Question 20 (SATA)

**Question:** Which findings are most consistent with **phlebitis** rather than infiltration? **Select all that apply.**

A. Redness and warmth along the vein  
B. A tender, cordlike feeling at the vein  
C. Cool, blanched swelling around the site  
D. Pain at the insertion site  
E. Slowed infusion with pale tissue around the site

**Correct answer:** A, B, D

**Explanation:** Phlebitis is inflammation of the vein itself, so think red, warm, tender, and cordlike. Those findings fit A, B, and D. Cool, blanched swelling is the trap here because it points to infiltration, not phlebitis.

## Identify priority assessments and nursing actions

### Question 21 (Priority Assessment)

**Question:** Thirty minutes after IV fluids are started for a client with heart failure, which assessment is the priority to help detect harm early?

A. Lung sounds and oxygen saturation  
B. Hair and scalp condition  
C. Bowel sounds in all four quadrants  
D. Hand grasp strength

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:** Heart failure makes this client vulnerable to pulmonary congestion soon after IV fluids begin. Lung sounds and oxygen saturation help you catch early respiratory harm, which is the priority. Hair, bowel sounds, and hand grasp do not tell you as quickly if the fluid is causing trouble.

### Question 22 (Priority Action)

**Question:** While managing a central IV line, the nurse notices air has entered the tubing and the client suddenly reports chest pain and shortness of breath. What is the nurse's first action?

A. Clamp the line to stop more air from entering  
B. Flush the line quickly with normal saline  
C. Remove the catheter and place the client flat  
D. Ask the client to take deep breaths and wait

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:** Once air enters the tubing and the client has chest pain with shortness of breath, the first job is to stop more air from getting in. Clamping the line removes the immediate source of harm. After that, the nurse can give oxygen, position the client, and call for help.

## Mixed high-yield priority practice

### Question 23 (Report to Provider)

**Question:** The nurse reviews morning labs and assessments. Which client should the nurse report to the provider first?

A. Sodium 132 mEq/L with mild nausea after bowel prep  
B. Sodium 129 mEq/L with leg cramps and fatigue  
C. Sodium 118 mEq/L with new confusion and vomiting  
D. Sodium 134 mEq/L with dry mouth after missing breakfast

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:** The sodium level is low, but the new confusion and vomiting are the real red flags. Those symptoms suggest severe symptomatic hyponatremia with neurologic involvement, which makes this client unstable. Mild nausea or dry mouth without serious changes can wait.

### Question 24 (Unstable vs Stable)

**Question:** Which client with an electrolyte imbalance is most unstable?

A. Potassium 3.2 mEq/L with constipation  
B. Calcium 7.8 mg/dL with tingling around the mouth  
C. Calcium 7.1 mg/dL with laryngeal stridor  
D. Sodium 147 mEq/L with thirst

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:** Laryngeal stridor is the clue that decides this question. Severe hypocalcemia can irritate nerves and muscles enough to threaten the airway, and airway problems always rise to the top. Tingling, constipation, or thirst are more stable findings.

### Question 25 (Provider Notification Threshold)

**Question:** An adult client is receiving IV fluids after surgery. Which urine output finding should the nurse report immediately?

A. 20 mL/hr for the last 2 hours  
B. 45 mL/hr during the last hour  
C. 350 mL in the last 8 hours  
D. 500 mL in the last 12 hours

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:** Twenty mL/hr for 2 hours is persistently below the usual adult minimum of about 30 mL/hr. That can mean poor kidney perfusion or early kidney injury, so it needs prompt reporting. The other options are less concerning because the output is not as clearly unsafe.

### Question 26 (Solution Safety)

**Question:** Which IV fluid order should the nurse question for a client with a severe head injury and suspected increased intracranial pressure?

A. 0.9% sodium chloride  
B. D5W  
C. 3% sodium chloride  
D. Normal saline with prescribed IV medication

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:** D5W looks harmless at first glance, but after the dextrose is used it acts like a hypotonic fluid. That lets water move into brain cells and can worsen cerebral edema. In head injury questions, hypotonic solutions are a common safety trap.

### Question 27 (Solution Selection)

**Question:** Which client is the best candidate for 0.45% sodium chloride?

A. A client with hypernatremia, cellular dehydration, and stable blood pressure  
B. A client with traumatic brain injury and worsening headache  
C. A client with vomiting, blood pressure 82/46 mm Hg, and tachycardia  
D. A client with severe hyponatremia and seizure activity

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:** The best clue is stable blood pressure in a client with hypernatremia and cellular dehydration. 0.45% sodium chloride is hypotonic, so it helps replace free water in a stable client. It is not the right first choice for shock, severe hyponatremia, or brain injury.

### Question 28 (First/Best Action)

**Question:** A peripheral IV infusing normal saline becomes cool, swollen, pale, and uncomfortable. The nurse stops the infusion. What should the nurse do next?

A. Remove the catheter and elevate the extremity  
B. Flush the catheter with normal saline  
C. Massage the area to spread the fluid  
D. Restart the infusion at a slower rate

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:** Cool, swollen, pale discomfort around the site matches infiltration. After stopping the infusion, the next safe step is to remove the catheter and protect the tissue, often by elevating the extremity per policy. Flushing, massaging, or restarting the line would worsen the leak.

### Question 29 (Priority Follow-Up)

**Question:** A central line tubing connection becomes loose, and the client suddenly develops chest pain and dyspnea. The nurse clamps the line. What should the nurse do next?

A. Place the client on the left side with the head lower and apply oxygen  
B. Remove the central line immediately  
C. Flush the line with normal saline  
D. Ask the client to cough and sit upright

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:** After clamping the line, the next priority is limiting the effects of the air embolism and supporting oxygenation. Placing the client on the left side with the head lower helps keep the air from moving forward, and oxygen should be applied while help is called. Removing or flushing the line is not the safe next step.

### Question 30 (SATA)

**Question:** A vesicant medication has infiltrated into the tissue and extravasation is suspected. Which nursing actions are appropriate? **Select all that apply.**

A. Stop the infusion right away  
B. Leave the catheter in place at first  
C. Flush the line with saline to dilute the medication  
D. Try to aspirate residual medication if policy or orders direct it  
E. Elevate the extremity and follow antidote or policy guidance  
F. Massage the area firmly

**Correct answer:** A, B, D, E

**Explanation:** Extravasation is a tissue-injury emergency, so the goal is to stop the drug and limit deeper damage. That is why stopping the infusion, keeping the catheter in place at first, aspirating if directed, and following antidote or elevation guidance are appropriate. Flushing or massaging is the trap because it can push the vesicant farther into the tissue.

### Question 31 (Medication Safety)

**Question:** The provider orders IV potassium chloride for a client with potassium 2.9 mEq/L. Which finding is most important to report before starting the infusion?

A. Urine output 15 mL/hr  
B. Leg cramps  
C. Heart rate 92/min  
D. Potassium 2.9 mEq/L

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:** The safety clue is the urine output, not the potassium order itself. If the client is making only 15 mL/hr, the kidneys may not clear added potassium well, and the level can rise to a dangerous point. Replacing potassium with poor urine output can set up a serious dysrhythmia.

### Question 32 (Who to Assess First)

**Question:** After shift report, which client should the nurse assess first?

A. A client with sodium 129 mEq/L and a mild headache who is alert and talking  
B. A client with potassium 6.1 mEq/L, muscle weakness, and peaked T waves  
C. A client with calcium 10.9 mg/dL and constipation  
D. A client with sodium 149 mEq/L and thirst after being NPO

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:** Peaked T waves make this the priority. Potassium 6.1 mEq/L with weakness and ECG changes points to an immediate risk for life-threatening dysrhythmias. The other clients need care, but this one is the most unstable right now.

### Question 33 (Who to Assess First)

**Question:** The nurse is rounding on four clients receiving IV therapy. Which client should the nurse assess first?

A. A client with a cool, swollen forearm and slowed infusion  
B. A client with redness and tenderness along a peripheral vein  
C. A client with sudden shortness of breath after air is seen in the central line tubing  
D. A client with mild ankle edema after a salty meal

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:** Sudden shortness of breath after air is seen in central line tubing should make you think air embolism first. That is a breathing and circulation emergency, so it outranks local IV problems like infiltration or phlebitis. NCLEX-style priority questions reward spotting the client who can crash fastest.

### Question 34 (Best Action)

**Question:** A peripheral IV site is red, warm, and tender, and the vein feels cordlike. What is the nurse's best action?

A. Remove the IV and apply a warm compress  
B. Continue the infusion and reassess in 30 minutes  
C. Flush the line with normal saline to clear the vein  
D. Massage the site and place a cold pack

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:** Redness, warmth, tenderness, and a cordlike vein fit phlebitis. The safest response is to stop using that vein, remove the IV, and give supportive care such as a warm compress if policy allows. Continuing the infusion, flushing, or massaging would increase the irritation.
