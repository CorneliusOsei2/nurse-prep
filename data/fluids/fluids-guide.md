# Fluids Guide

This guide is written in a friendly, plain-English style, but it still keeps the important nursing facts correct.

## Describe processes that regulate fluid and electrolyte distribution

(This section explains how water and dissolved particles move through the body.)

### Osmosis

(Osmosis is water movement.)

- Water moves across a membrane.
- Water moves toward the side with more dissolved particles.
- This helps explain why fluid shifts happen between blood vessels and cells.

### Diffusion

(Diffusion is particle movement.)

- Solutes move from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration.
- This helps oxygen, carbon dioxide, and electrolytes move where they are needed.

### Filtration

(Filtration is fluid movement caused by pressure.)

- Fluid moves because pressure pushes it.
- Hydrostatic pressure pushes fluid out of blood vessels.
- This matters in edema and fluid overload questions.

### Hormonal regulation

(The body uses hormones and thirst to help control water and sodium.)

#### Antidiuretic hormone (ADH)

(ADH helps the body hold on to water.)

- ADH tells the kidneys to reabsorb more water.
- More ADH usually means less urine output.

#### Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS)

(RAAS helps raise blood volume and blood pressure.)

- RAAS causes the body to hold sodium and water.
- This helps increase blood volume when the body thinks blood flow is low.

#### Thirst

(Thirst is the body's signal to drink more.)

- Thirst increases when the body needs more fluid.
- It is one of the body's simplest fluid-balance controls.

### Why electrolytes matter

(Electrolytes help nerves, muscles, fluid balance, and heart rhythm work correctly.)

- **Sodium** strongly affects fluid balance and neurologic status.
- **Potassium** strongly affects muscle and heart function.
- **Calcium** strongly affects nerves, muscles, and heart contraction.

## Describe common fluid and electrolyte imbalances

(This section covers the big problems most nursing courses focus on.)

### Fluid volume deficit

(Fluid volume deficit means the body does not have enough fluid.)

#### Risk factors

- Vomiting
- Diarrhea
- Fever
- Sweating
- Diuretics
- Poor fluid intake
- Bleeding

#### Common clinical manifestations

- Dry mouth and dry mucous membranes
- Thirst
- Poor skin turgor
- Tachycardia
- Low blood pressure or blood pressure that drops when standing
- Dark concentrated urine
- Low urine output
- Weight loss
- Weakness or dizziness

#### Appropriate interventions

- Replace fluids carefully.
- Encourage oral fluids if safe and allowed.
- Monitor blood pressure, pulse, intake and output, and daily weight.
- Watch mental status and urine output closely.
- Treat the cause, such as vomiting, diarrhea, or bleeding.

### Fluid volume excess

(Fluid volume excess means the body is holding too much fluid.)

#### Risk factors

- Heart failure
- Kidney failure
- Liver disease
- Too much IV fluid
- High sodium intake

#### Common clinical manifestations

- Edema
- Weight gain
- Crackles
- Shortness of breath
- High blood pressure
- Bounding pulse
- Jugular venous distention (swollen neck veins)

#### Appropriate interventions

- Restrict fluids if ordered.
- Limit sodium if ordered.
- Monitor daily weight, lung sounds, intake and output, and edema.
- Elevate swollen extremities when appropriate.
- Give diuretics as ordered.
- Slow or stop IV fluids if the situation and orders require it.

### Hyponatremia

(Hyponatremia means low sodium.)

#### Risk factors

- Too much free water
- Diuretics
- Vomiting or diarrhea
- Kidney problems
- Conditions that dilute the sodium level

#### Common clinical manifestations

- Headache
- Nausea
- Confusion
- Lethargy
- Muscle cramps
- Seizures if severe

#### Appropriate interventions

- Restrict free water if ordered.
- Replace sodium carefully if ordered.
- Monitor neurologic status closely.
- Recheck labs.
- Use seizure precautions when sodium is very low.

### Hypernatremia

(Hypernatremia means high sodium.)

#### Risk factors

- Water loss
- Fever
- Sweating
- Diarrhea
- Not drinking enough water
- Too much sodium intake or sodium-rich IV therapy

#### Common clinical manifestations

- Thirst
- Restlessness
- Weakness
- Confusion
- Irritability
- Neurologic changes if severe

#### Appropriate interventions

- Replace fluids carefully.
- Monitor neurologic status.
- Recheck sodium levels.
- Treat the cause of water loss or excess sodium.

### Hypokalemia

(Hypokalemia means low potassium.)

#### Risk factors

- Vomiting
- Diarrhea
- Diuretics
- Poor intake
- Gastrointestinal suction

#### Common clinical manifestations

- Muscle weakness
- Fatigue
- Constipation
- Weak bowel activity
- Dysrhythmias

#### Appropriate interventions

- Replace potassium as ordered.
- Monitor heart rhythm.
- Check kidney function and urine output before giving potassium.
- Encourage potassium-rich foods when appropriate.
- Never give IV potassium too fast.

### Hyperkalemia

(Hyperkalemia means high potassium.)

#### Risk factors

- Kidney failure
- Too much potassium intake or replacement
- Cell breakdown
- Certain medications

#### Common clinical manifestations

- Muscle weakness
- Tingling in some cases
- Dangerous dysrhythmias
- ECG changes may occur

#### Appropriate interventions

- Monitor cardiac rhythm closely.
- Stop potassium sources if appropriate.
- Give ordered treatments to lower potassium.
- Watch for life-threatening rhythm changes.

### Hypocalcemia

(Hypocalcemia means low calcium.)

#### Risk factors

- Low calcium intake
- Kidney disease
- Low vitamin D
- Thyroid or parathyroid problems

#### Common clinical manifestations

- Tingling
- Muscle cramps
- Tetany
- Hyperactive reflexes
- Seizures
- Positive Chvostek's sign
- Positive Trousseau's sign

#### Appropriate interventions

- Replace calcium as ordered.
- Monitor neurologic and muscle symptoms.
- Use seizure precautions when needed.
- Recheck labs.

### Hypercalcemia

(Hypercalcemia means high calcium.)

#### Risk factors

- Hyperparathyroidism
- Cancer
- Long periods of immobility
- Excess calcium intake in some cases

#### Common clinical manifestations

- Weakness
- Constipation
- Confusion
- Decreased reflexes
- Dysrhythmias in severe cases

#### Appropriate interventions

- Increase fluids if safe and ordered.
- Encourage movement when possible.
- Monitor mental status and rhythm.
- Treat the cause.

## Discuss appropriate interventions for common fluid and electrolyte imbalances

(This is the big nursing-action section.)

### General nursing priorities

- Reassess vital signs.
- Monitor daily weight.
- Measure intake and output accurately.
- Review laboratory values.
- Watch heart rhythm when potassium or calcium is abnormal.
- Watch mental status when sodium is abnormal.
- Treat the cause, not just the numbers.

### Patient teaching

- Teach signs of too little or too much fluid.
- Teach food sources of sodium, potassium, and calcium.
- Teach patients to take medicines such as diuretics exactly as ordered.
- Teach when to report weakness, confusion, swelling, shortness of breath, or very low urine output.

## Explain the purpose of parenteral therapy

(Parenteral therapy means fluids or medications are given by vein instead of by mouth.)

- Replaces fluids and electrolytes.
- Gives medications.
- Gives blood products.
- Gives nutrition when the gut cannot be used.
- Provides a fast route when quick treatment is needed.

## Differentiate between types of parenteral solutions

(This section explains how different IV fluids move water in the body.)

### Isotonic solutions

(Isotonic solutions stay mainly in the bloodstream.)

- Used to expand circulating volume.
- Commonly used for fluid volume deficit.
- Examples include **0.9% sodium chloride** and **lactated Ringer's**.

### Hypotonic solutions

(Hypotonic solutions move water from the bloodstream into the cells.)

- Used carefully when cells need more water.
- Can lower circulating volume if used inappropriately.
- Example: **0.45% sodium chloride**.

### Hypertonic solutions

(Hypertonic solutions pull water into the bloodstream from the cells.)

- Used carefully for certain severe problems such as serious hyponatremia.
- Can raise circulating volume quickly.
- Example: **3% sodium chloride**.

## Identify potential complications of parenteral therapy

(These are common IV therapy problems.)

### Inaccurate infusion rate

- Too fast can cause fluid overload.
- Too slow can delay needed treatment.

### Infiltration

(Infiltration means regular IV fluid leaks into the tissue.)

- The site may become cool, pale, swollen, or uncomfortable.

### Extravasation

(Extravasation means a tissue-damaging fluid or medication leaks into the tissue.)

- This can cause severe tissue injury.

### Phlebitis

(Phlebitis means the vein is irritated or inflamed.)

- The site may become warm, red, tender, or streaked.

### Infection

- Infection can stay local or spread through the body.
- Risk rises with poor aseptic technique or long device use.

### Fluid overload

- Too much IV fluid can lead to edema, crackles, dyspnea, and high blood pressure.

### Air embolism

- Air enters the bloodstream through the IV system.
- This is uncommon but dangerous.

## Identify priority assessments and nursing actions

(These are the most important bedside checks and actions.)

### Priority assessments

- Check the IV site for redness, swelling, pain, leakage, coolness, or warmth.
- Confirm the correct fluid, rate, and tubing setup.
- Assess lung sounds, edema, vital signs, and oxygenation.
- Monitor intake and output.
- Monitor daily weight.
- Review lab values.
- Check whether the therapy is helping the patient.

### Priority nursing actions

- Stop or slow the infusion when a serious complication is suspected and follow policy and orders.
- Remove the IV when infiltration, phlebitis, or infection requires it.
- Elevate the extremity when appropriate for infiltration.
- Protect the tissue and follow policy for extravasation.
- Use aseptic technique.
- Report serious changes quickly, especially signs of overload, severe electrolyte imbalance, or tissue injury.

## Quick Memorization Points

(Use these short facts for fast review.)

- **Daily weight** is one of the best ways to track fluid gain or loss.
- **Sodium** problems often show up in the brain first.
- **Potassium** problems are high-yield because they affect the heart.
- **Calcium** problems often affect nerves and muscles.
- **Isotonic** = stays mainly in the bloodstream.
- **Hypotonic** = moves water into cells.
- **Hypertonic** = pulls water into the bloodstream.
- **Infiltration** = regular IV fluid in tissue.
- **Extravasation** = tissue-damaging IV fluid or drug in tissue.

## Study Tips

(Use these to study actively instead of just rereading.)

- For each imbalance, ask: **What causes it? What do I see? What does the nurse do?**
- Compare the look-alike pairs: **fluid deficit vs fluid excess**, **hyponatremia vs hypernatremia**, **hypokalemia vs hyperkalemia**.
- Memorize the danger points first: **confusion, seizures, dysrhythmias, crackles, shortness of breath, and very low urine output**.
