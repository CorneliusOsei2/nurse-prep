## Card 1 — Recall
**Q: What is the therapeutic blood level range for lithium?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**0.6 to 1.2 mEq/L**

- Above 1.5 mEq/L = **toxic**
- Above 2.0 mEq/L = **life-threatening**
- Draw the level 8 to 12 hours after the last dose (morning trough).

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* The safe range is very narrow. Just a small increase can be dangerous. That's why we monitor lithium levels so carefully.
</details>

---

## Card 2 — Safety
**Q: A patient on sertraline (Zoloft) is started on tramadol for pain. The nurse finds the patient agitated, tremoring, with a fever of 103.4°F and hyperreflexia. What is happening?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Serotonin syndrome!**

Both sertraline (SSRI) and tramadol increase serotonin. Together, they caused too much serotonin.

Key signs:
- Agitation
- Hyperthermia (fever)
- Tremor and clonus
- Hyperreflexia
- Tachycardia

**Action:** Stop both serotonin drugs immediately. Notify the provider. Supportive care and possible cyproheptadine.
</details>

---

## Card 3 — Compare/Contrast
**Q: What is the MAIN difference in side effects between typical (first-generation) and atypical (second-generation) antipsychotics?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

- **Typical antipsychotics** (like haloperidol) → HIGH risk of **EPS** (movement problems: dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia)
- **Atypical antipsychotics** (like olanzapine) → HIGH risk of **metabolic syndrome** (weight gain, high blood sugar, high cholesterol)

> 💡 *Think of it this way:*
> - Typicals = worry about **moving** (EPS)
> - Atypicals = worry about **metabolism** (weight, sugar, cholesterol)

Both can cause NMS, but it's more common with typicals.
</details>

## Card 4 — Patient Teaching
**Q: A patient with bipolar disorder on lithium asks, "Can I take ibuprofen for my headache?" What do you teach?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**"No — avoid ibuprofen and other NSAIDs while taking lithium."**

- NSAIDs decrease the kidneys' ability to remove lithium.
- This causes lithium levels to rise → toxicity risk.
- **Use acetaminophen (Tylenol) instead** for mild pain.
- Always check with the provider before taking any new medication.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Lithium HATES NSAIDs. They trap lithium in the body.
</details>

## Card 5 — Safety (Emergency)
**Q: A patient on haloperidol (Haldol) suddenly has a temperature of 105°F, severe "lead-pipe" muscle rigidity, altered consciousness, and a CK level of 15,000. What is happening, and what is the FIRST action?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)**

This is a **medical emergency!**

**FIRST action:** **Stop the antipsychotic immediately.**

Then:
- Notify the provider STAT
- Transfer to ICU
- Cooling measures
- IV fluids
- Dantrolene (muscle relaxant) and/or bromocriptine (dopamine agonist)
- Monitor CK and kidney function (risk of rhabdomyolysis)

Remember NMS = "FEVER": **F**ever, **E**ncephalopathy, **V**ital sign instability, **E**levated CK, **R**igidity
</details>

## Card 6 — Compare/Contrast
**Q: How do you tell the difference between serotonin syndrome and NMS?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

| Feature | Serotonin Syndrome | NMS |
|---|---|---|
| Cause | Serotonin drugs (SSRIs, MAOIs, tramadol) | Antipsychotics |
| Onset | Fast — **hours** | Slow — **days to weeks** |
| Reflexes | **Hyperreflexia** | Normal/decreased |
| Clonus | **Yes** | No |
| CK level | May be elevated | **Very elevated** |
| Key feature | Twitchy and hyperactive | Stiff as a board |

> 💡 Both have fever and rigidity. Look for **clonus and hyperreflexia** = serotonin syndrome. Look for **lead-pipe rigidity and sky-high CK** = NMS.
</details>

## Card 7 — Patient Teaching
**Q: A patient just started fluoxetine (Prozac) for depression. After 5 days, they say, "This medicine isn't working. I want to stop." What do you teach?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**"Please don't stop yet! SSRIs take 2 to 4 weeks to reach full effect."**

Key teaching points:
1. It takes time for the brain to adjust to the higher serotonin levels.
2. Keep taking the medication every day as prescribed.
3. Do NOT stop suddenly — this can cause discontinuation syndrome (dizziness, nausea, irritability, "brain zaps").
4. If you still feel no improvement after 4 to 6 weeks, the doctor may adjust the dose or try a different medication.
5. Report any worsening mood, agitation, or thoughts of self-harm right away.
</details>

## Card 8 — Patient Teaching (Tyramine)
**Q: A patient on phenelzine (MAOI) asks what foods they should avoid. What do you teach?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Avoid foods high in tyramine:**
- 🧀 Aged cheeses (cheddar, Swiss, blue, Parmesan)
- 🥩 Cured/smoked meats (salami, pepperoni, bacon, hot dogs)
- 🍺 Beer and red wine
- 🫘 Soy sauce, miso, tofu
- 🫒 Pickled or fermented foods (sauerkraut, kimchi)
- 🍌 Overripe bananas and avocados
- Fava beans, broad beans

**Why?** MAOIs block the enzyme that breaks down tyramine. Too much tyramine → massive release of norepinephrine → **hypertensive crisis** (dangerously high blood pressure).

**Warning signs:** Sudden severe headache, stiff neck, nausea, sweating → **Go to the ER immediately!**
</details>

## Card 9 — Application
**Q: A patient on haloperidol suddenly has their eyes rolling upward and their jaw locked shut. What is happening and what do you give?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Acute dystonia** — a type of EPS (extrapyramidal symptom).

This is a sudden, involuntary muscle spasm. The eyes rolling up is called **oculogyric crisis**.

**Give immediately:**
- **Diphenhydramine (Benadryl)** IV or IM, OR
- **Benztropine (Cogentin)** IV or IM

These anticholinergic drugs counteract the dopamine blockade and relieve the spasm quickly.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Dystonia is the EPS **emergency.** Don't wait — give the anticholinergic right away!
</details>

## Card 10 — Recall
**Q: What labs do you monitor for a patient on lithium? Name at least 4.**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

1. **Lithium blood level** (0.6–1.2 mEq/L) — most important!
2. **BUN and creatinine** — kidney function (lithium is cleared by kidneys)
3. **TSH** (thyroid function) — lithium can cause hypothyroidism
4. **Sodium / electrolytes** — low sodium increases lithium levels
5. **ECG** — at baseline (heart rhythm)

> 💡 Remember: Lithium needs regular lab work. It's one of those drugs where "blood draws save lives."
</details>

## Card 11 — Safety
**Q: A patient on lithium has been vomiting and having diarrhea for 2 days. Why is this dangerous?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Dehydration and fluid loss increase lithium levels → risk of lithium toxicity!**

When the body loses fluid:
- The kidneys try to hold on to sodium and water.
- Lithium is reabsorbed right along with sodium.
- Lithium levels rise → toxicity.

**What to do:**
- Hold the lithium dose.
- Notify the provider.
- Encourage fluids (if able to drink).
- Check a lithium level.
- Watch for toxicity signs: coarse tremor, confusion, slurred speech, seizures.
</details>

## Card 12 — Application
**Q: A patient on a benzodiazepine is found unresponsive with a respiratory rate of 6. What is the reversal agent?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Flumazenil (Romazicon)**

- Given IV.
- Reverses the sedative effects of benzodiazepines.
- Works quickly but wears off fast — monitor for re-sedation.

**Important caution:** Flumazenil can cause **seizures** in patients who have been on long-term benzodiazepines or who also took drugs that lower the seizure threshold.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:*
> - Benzodiazepine overdose → **flumazenil**
> - Opioid overdose → **naloxone**
> Both are reversal agents, but for different drug classes.
</details>

## Card 13 — Priority
**Q: A nurse is caring for four patients. Who should be assessed FIRST?**
- A) Patient on lithium with a fine hand tremor
- B) Patient on sertraline who reports nausea
- C) Patient on haloperidol with fever of 104°F and muscle rigidity
- D) Patient on alprazolam who feels drowsy

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**C) Patient on haloperidol with fever of 104°F and muscle rigidity**

This is **NMS** (Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome) — a life-threatening emergency!

- A) Fine tremor is a common, expected side effect of therapeutic lithium levels — not urgent.
- B) Nausea is a common SSRI side effect — not urgent.
- C) ✅ **This is the emergency.** High fever + rigidity + antipsychotic = NMS until proven otherwise.
- D) Drowsiness is an expected effect of benzodiazepines — not urgent.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* On priority questions, always look for the **life-threatening** situation first. NMS can be fatal if not treated immediately.
</details>

## Card 14 — Patient Teaching
**Q: What should you teach the parents of a 16-year-old who was just started on an SSRI for depression?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**The black box warning: SSRIs can increase suicidal thoughts in adolescents, especially early in treatment.**

Teach parents to:
- Watch closely for mood changes, agitation, new anxiety, or self-harm talk — especially in the **first 1 to 2 weeks** and when the dose changes.
- Keep all follow-up appointments.
- Do NOT stop the medication suddenly.
- Call the provider immediately if the teen seems more agitated, talks about death, or shows behavioral changes.
- Ensure medications are stored safely and the teen cannot access extra pills.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* The medication may give energy before it improves mood. More energy + still hopeless = higher risk.
</details>

## Card 15 — Recall
**Q: Name the four types of EPS (extrapyramidal symptoms) in order from earliest to latest onset.**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

1. **Acute dystonia** — hours to days (muscle spasms, neck twisting, eye rolling)
2. **Akathisia** — days to weeks (restlessness, can't sit still)
3. **Parkinsonism** — weeks to months (shuffling gait, rigidity, mask-like face, tremor)
4. **Tardive dyskinesia** — months to years (lip smacking, tongue movements, facial grimacing)

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* **D-A-P-T** in order of onset. Dystonia comes first and is an emergency. Tardive dyskinesia comes last and may be **irreversible.**
</details>

## Card 16 — Compare/Contrast
**Q: Compare SSRIs and MAOIs — which is first-line, and why?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**SSRIs are first-line.** MAOIs are last resort.

| Feature | SSRIs | MAOIs |
|---|---|---|
| First-line? | ✅ Yes | ❌ No |
| Diet restrictions | None | Strict tyramine diet |
| Drug interactions | Some (serotonin syndrome) | MANY (very dangerous) |
| Side effects | Moderate (GI, sexual) | Many + hypertensive crisis risk |
| Patient adherence | Easier | Very hard (diet + drug rules) |

MAOIs are used only when other antidepressants have failed, because:
- Tyramine diet is hard to follow.
- Many dangerous drug interactions.
- Hypertensive crisis is life-threatening.
</details>

## Card 17 — Safety
**Q: A patient on an MAOI ate aged cheddar cheese at a party. They now have a sudden, severe occipital headache and their blood pressure is 220/120 mmHg. What is happening?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Hypertensive crisis from tyramine!**

The aged cheese is high in tyramine. The MAOI blocked the enzyme that breaks tyramine down. This caused a massive surge of norepinephrine → dangerously high blood pressure.

**This is a medical emergency!**
- Notify the provider STAT.
- Prepare for IV **phentolamine** (alpha-blocker to lower BP).
- Monitor vital signs continuously.
- The patient may need ICU care.
- Risk of stroke if not treated quickly.
</details>

## Card 18 — Application

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**HOLD the dose!**

- Therapeutic range: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L.

**What to do:**
1. Hold the lithium.
2. Notify the provider immediately.
3. Assess the patient for toxicity signs (coarse tremor, confusion, vomiting, slurred speech).
4. Check hydration status.
5. Expect orders for IV fluids and repeat lithium levels.

## Card 19 — Patient Teaching
**Q: A patient on lithium is going on a beach vacation. What safety teaching is most important?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**"Stay very well hydrated — dehydration can cause lithium toxicity!"**

On a hot beach vacation:
- Sweating causes fluid and sodium loss.
- Dehydration leads to lithium building up in the blood.
- This can reach toxic levels.

**Teach the patient to:**
- Drink **plenty of water** (8–10 glasses or more in the heat).
- Keep sodium intake consistent — don't skip salty foods.
- Avoid excessive exercise in the heat.
- If they feel a strong tremor, nausea, confusion, or slurred speech → seek medical help right away.
- Take the lithium dose as usual — but watch for warning signs.
</details>

## Card 22 — Safety
**Q: Why must you wait at least 2 weeks between stopping an SSRI and starting an MAOI?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**To prevent serotonin syndrome!**

- SSRIs take time to leave the body completely.
- If an MAOI is started while SSRI is still in the system, serotonin levels can skyrocket.
- This causes life-threatening serotonin syndrome.

**Special note:** For **fluoxetine (Prozac)**, wait **5 weeks** because it has a very long half-life and stays in the body much longer than other SSRIs.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* You need a "washout period" — time for one drug to completely leave before the next one starts. It's like draining the pool before refilling it with different water.
</details>

## Card 23 — Application (Metabolic Monitoring)
**Q: A patient has been on olanzapine (Zyprexa) for 3 months. What should the nurse be monitoring?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Metabolic monitoring — weight, blood sugar, and cholesterol!**

Atypical antipsychotics (especially olanzapine) cause metabolic syndrome:
- ⚖️ **Weight** — at every visit (olanzapine causes significant weight gain)
- 🩸 **Fasting blood glucose / HbA1c** — at baseline, 12 weeks, then yearly
- 🩸 **Fasting lipid panel** — at baseline, 12 weeks, then yearly
- 🩺 **Blood pressure** — at every visit
- 📏 **Waist circumference** — periodically

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Atypicals = metabolic. Think "weight, sugar, cholesterol" every time you see olanzapine, risperidone, or quetiapine.
</details>

## Card 27 — Safety
**Q: A patient on sertraline (Zoloft) tells you they also take St. John's Wort, an herbal supplement. What is the risk?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Serotonin syndrome!**

- St. John's Wort is an herbal supplement that **increases serotonin** levels.
- Sertraline (an SSRI) **also increases serotonin**.
- Taking both together = too much serotonin = **serotonin syndrome**.

**What to do:**
- Tell the patient to **stop the St. John's Wort** and notify their provider.
- Teach: Always tell your doctor about **all** supplements, herbs, and over-the-counter products.
- Watch for symptoms: agitation, tremor, fever, rapid heart rate, hyperreflexia, clonus.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* "Natural" does not mean "safe to combine." St. John's Wort acts like an antidepressant — two serotonin boosters together is dangerous.
</details>

## Card 28 — Application
**Q: A teenager on an SSRI seems more energized lately but tells the nurse, "It won't matter soon — I won't be here anymore." What is happening?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**This is a suicidal warning sign — take it seriously RIGHT NOW.**

Here's what's likely happening:
- The SSRI is starting to give the teen **more energy and motivation**.
- But the **hopelessness and sadness haven't lifted yet**.
- More energy + still feeling hopeless = the teen now has the **energy to act on suicidal thoughts**.

**This is the most dangerous time in early SSRI treatment.**

**Immediate actions:**
1. Stay with the patient. Do not leave them alone.
2. Ask directly: "Are you thinking about hurting yourself?"
3. Notify the provider STAT.
4. Initiate suicide precautions per facility policy.
5. Document everything.

> 💡 *Black box warning reminder:* SSRIs can increase suicidal thinking in children and adolescents, especially in the **first few weeks** and during dose changes.
</details>

## Card 29 — Compare
**Q: How are SNRIs different from SSRIs? When might a provider choose an SNRI instead?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

| Feature | SSRIs | SNRIs |
|---|---|---|
| What they boost | **Serotonin** only | **Serotonin + norepinephrine** |
| Examples | Fluoxetine, sertraline | Venlafaxine, duloxetine |
| Pain relief? | Not really | **Yes** — norepinephrine helps with pain |
| First-line for depression? | ✅ Yes | ✅ Also yes |

**A provider might choose an SNRI when:**
- The patient has **depression AND chronic pain** (like fibromyalgia or neuropathy).
- An SSRI alone didn't work well enough.
- The patient also has **anxiety and fatigue** (norepinephrine can boost energy).

**Duloxetine (Cymbalta)** is commonly used for both depression and nerve pain.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* SSRI = one key (serotonin). SNRI = two keys (serotonin + norepinephrine). Two keys open more doors — including the pain door.
</details>

## Card 30 — Patient Teaching
**Q: A patient asks, "I've been on my SSRI for a year and feel great. Can I just stop taking it?" What do you teach?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**"No — never stop an SSRI suddenly! It must be tapered slowly."**

Stopping suddenly can cause **discontinuation syndrome:**
- 😵 Dizziness and lightheadedness
- ⚡ "Brain zaps" (electric shock feelings in the head)
- 😠 Irritability and mood swings
- 🤢 Nausea
- 😴 Insomnia or vivid dreams
- 🤧 Flu-like symptoms

**Teach the patient:**
1. Talk to your provider first — they will create a **gradual taper plan**.
2. The dose is reduced slowly over weeks to months.
3. Feeling good does NOT mean you no longer need the medication — it means the medication is working!
4. If you do taper off, watch for returning symptoms of depression.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Feeling better is not the same as being cured. The SSRI is the reason you feel better. Think of it like glasses — you see fine WITH them, but that doesn't mean your eyes are fixed.
</details>

## Card 31 — Safety
**Q: A patient on an SSRI is prescribed tramadol for pain after a procedure. What is the concern?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Serotonin syndrome risk!**

- SSRIs increase serotonin.
- Tramadol **also** increases serotonin (in addition to being a pain reliever).
- Together, they can push serotonin to dangerous levels.

**What should the nurse do?**
1. **Question the order** — call the provider before giving tramadol.
2. Ask if an alternative pain medication can be used (like acetaminophen or a non-serotonergic option).
3. If both must be used, monitor closely for serotonin syndrome signs: agitation, tremor, fever, clonus, rapid heart rate.

**Watch for symptoms within hours of combining the two drugs.**

> 💡 *NCLEX tip:* Tramadol is a sneaky serotonin drug. Many students forget it's not "just a pain med." Always think about serotonin syndrome when you see tramadol + any serotonin drug.
</details>

## Card 32 — Patient Teaching
**Q: Name 5 foods a patient on an MAOI must avoid, and explain what happens if they eat them.**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Five high-tyramine foods to avoid:**

1. 🧀 **Aged cheeses** (cheddar, Swiss, blue cheese, Parmesan)
2. 🥩 **Cured or smoked meats** (salami, pepperoni, summer sausage)

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* The more aged, cured, or fermented a food is, the more tyramine it has. Fresh is safe. Old and funky is dangerous on MAOIs.

## Card 33 — Safety
**Q: How long must you wait between stopping an MAOI and starting an SSRI? Why?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Wait at least 2 weeks (14 days).**

**Why?**
- MAOIs irreversibly block the MAO enzyme.
- The body needs about 2 weeks to make **new MAO enzymes**.
- If you start an SSRI before the MAOI is fully gone, serotonin will build up with no way to break it down → **serotonin syndrome**.

**And the reverse?**
- Switching from an SSRI to an MAOI → also wait **at least 2 weeks**.
- Switching from **fluoxetine** (Prozac) to an MAOI → wait **5 weeks** because fluoxetine has a very long half-life.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* This "washout period" is like cleaning a gas tank before switching fuel types. If you mix them, the engine (your brain) could explode.
</details>

2. **Notify the provider immediately.**
3. **Assess the patient** for toxicity signs: coarse tremor, nausea/vomiting, confusion, slurred speech, muscle twitching.
4. Expect orders for IV fluids and repeat lithium levels.
5. Monitor kidney function (BUN, creatinine).

> 💡 *NCLEX rule:* If a lithium level is above 1.5 mEq/L, **always hold and call.** Giving more lithium to a toxic patient could be fatal. When in doubt — hold and notify!
</details>

## Card 36 — Lab
**Q: A patient on lithium has been vomiting for 2 days. Why is this especially dangerous?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Because vomiting causes dehydration, and dehydration raises lithium levels → toxicity!**

Here's the chain of events:
1. Vomiting → fluid loss → **dehydration**
2. Dehydration → kidneys try to save water and sodium
3. Lithium is reabsorbed right along with sodium
4. Lithium level **rises** → moves toward **toxic range**

**This creates a vicious cycle:**
- Lithium toxicity itself causes **more nausea and vomiting** → even more dehydration → even higher lithium levels.

**What to do:**
- Hold the lithium dose.
- Notify the provider.
- Check a lithium level STAT.
- Start IV fluids if ordered.
- Monitor for worsening toxicity: confusion, coarse tremor, seizures.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Anything that dehydrates the body (vomiting, diarrhea, heavy sweating, fever) is a red flag for lithium patients. Dehydration is lithium's worst enemy.
</details>

## Card 37 — Safety
**Q: A patient on lithium asks, "Can I take ibuprofen for my knee pain?" What do you say?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**"No — ibuprofen is not safe with lithium. Let's find you something else."**

**Why not?**
- Ibuprofen is an NSAID (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug).
- NSAIDs reduce blood flow to the kidneys.
- This means the kidneys **can't get rid of lithium** as well.
- Lithium builds up → **toxicity risk**.

**Safe alternative:** Use **acetaminophen (Tylenol)** for mild to moderate pain.

**Other NSAIDs to avoid:**
- Naproxen (Aleve)
- Aspirin (high doses)
- Celecoxib (Celebrex)

Always tell the patient: **"Check with your provider before taking ANY new medication — even over-the-counter ones."**

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Lithium and NSAIDs are enemies. NSAIDs trap lithium in the body. Remember: **L**ithium **L**oves to stay when NSAIDs are around.
</details>

## Card 38 — Application
**Q: A patient on lithium has a sodium level of 128 mEq/L (normal: 135–145). What is the risk?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Low sodium increases the risk of lithium toxicity!**

**Here's why:**
- Lithium and sodium are handled similarly by the kidneys.
- When sodium is low, the kidneys try to **hold on to sodium** — and they grab **lithium** too.
- Lithium gets reabsorbed instead of excreted.
- Lithium level rises → **toxicity**.

**What to do:**
1. Notify the provider immediately.
2. Check a lithium level STAT.
3. Assess for lithium toxicity signs: nausea, coarse tremor, confusion, slurred speech.
4. Expect orders to correct sodium and possibly hold lithium.
5. Monitor I&O and electrolytes closely.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Lithium follows sodium. Low sodium = lithium gets stuck in the body. That's why we tell patients to keep their salt intake **consistent** — never go on a sudden low-sodium diet while taking lithium.
</details>

## Card 39 — Patient Teaching
**Q: What do you teach a patient about fluid and salt intake while taking lithium?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**"Keep your fluids and salt steady every day. Don't make big changes without telling your doctor."**

**Key teaching points:**

💧 **Fluids:**
- Drink **8 to 10 glasses of water** every day.
- Drink more in hot weather or if you exercise.
- Avoid dehydration — it raises lithium levels.
- Don't drink huge amounts of water in one sitting either — this can dilute sodium.

🧂 **Salt (sodium):**
- Keep your salt intake **consistent** — about the same every day.
- Do NOT start a low-sodium diet without telling your provider.
- Low sodium → kidneys hold on to lithium → toxicity risk.
- Don't suddenly eat a lot more salt either — this can lower lithium levels and reduce its effectiveness.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Lithium is like a roommate who copies everything sodium does. If sodium leaves (low salt, dehydration), lithium stays behind and builds up. Keep things steady!
</details>

## Card 40 — Safety
**Q: A patient on haloperidol suddenly has their neck twisted to one side and they are in obvious distress. This is acute dystonia. What do you give?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Give an anticholinergic medication RIGHT AWAY:**

- **Diphenhydramine (Benadryl)** 25–50 mg IV or IM, OR
- **Benztropine (Cogentin)** 1–2 mg IV or IM

**Why these drugs?**
- Haloperidol blocks dopamine.
- Too little dopamine → the muscles spasm uncontrollably.
- Anticholinergics help restore the dopamine-acetylcholine balance and relax the muscles.

**Key points:**
- Acute dystonia usually happens within the **first few days** of starting an antipsychotic.
- It is **scary but treatable** — the medication works within minutes.
- The patient may also have jaw clenching, tongue protrusion, or eyes rolling upward (oculogyric crisis).
- After treating, notify the provider to discuss changing the medication or adding a standing anticholinergic.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Dystonia = dopamine too low. Anticholinergic = restores the balance. Benadryl or Cogentin to the rescue!
</details>

## Card 41 — Compare
**Q: What is the difference between EPS and tardive dyskinesia (TD)? Which one is reversible?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

| Feature | EPS (early types) | Tardive Dyskinesia (TD) |
|---|---|---|
| When it appears | **Early** — days to months | **Late** — months to years |
| Types | Dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism | Involuntary facial/body movements |
| What it looks like | Muscle spasms, restlessness, stiffness | Lip smacking, tongue darting, grimacing, finger movements |
| Reversible? | **Usually YES** — if caught early and treated | **Often NO** — may be permanent |
| Treatment | Anticholinergics (Benadryl, Cogentin) | Stop or change the drug; valbenazine (Ingrezza) may help |

**The big difference:**
- EPS types (dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism) usually **go away** when the drug is stopped or an anticholinergic is given.
- Tardive dyskinesia may be **irreversible**, even after the antipsychotic is stopped.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* EPS is the early warning. TD is the permanent damage. That's why we monitor for EPS carefully — to prevent TD from ever developing.
</details>

## Card 42 — Application
**Q: A patient on olanzapine (Zyprexa) has gained 20 pounds in 3 months. Their fasting glucose is 210 mg/dL. What is happening?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Metabolic syndrome — a well-known side effect of atypical antipsychotics.**

**What's going on:**
- Olanzapine is one of the **worst** atypical antipsychotics for metabolic effects.
- It causes significant **weight gain** and **insulin resistance**.
- Fasting glucose of 210 mg/dL is in the **diabetic range** (normal is under 100).

**Nursing actions:**
1. Notify the provider — the patient may be developing **type 2 diabetes**.
2. Expect orders for HbA1c and fasting lipid panel.
3. Discuss the findings with the provider — a **medication change** may be needed (to an antipsychotic with fewer metabolic effects, like aripiprazole/Abilify).
4. Teach about healthy diet and exercise.
5. Continue monitoring weight, glucose, and lipids regularly.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Olanzapine feeds the body's fat and sugar problems. Weight gain + high glucose on an atypical antipsychotic = metabolic syndrome until proven otherwise. Always monitor!
</details>

## Card 43 — Lab
**Q: What labs do you monitor for a patient on an atypical antipsychotic? List at least 4.**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**At least these labs and assessments:**

1. 🩸 **Fasting blood glucose / HbA1c** — screen for diabetes
2. 🩸 **Fasting lipid panel** (cholesterol, triglycerides) — screen for high cholesterol
3. ⚖️ **Weight and BMI** — at every visit (track weight gain)

5. 📏 **Waist circumference** — periodically (central obesity is a risk factor)
6. 🩸 **CBC** — some atypicals (especially clozapine) can cause low white blood cells

- Baseline (before starting the drug)
- At 12 weeks
- Then at least yearly — or more often if abnormal

**Extra for clozapine:** Must have regular **ANC (absolute neutrophil count)** monitoring due to risk of **agranulocytosis** (dangerously low white blood cells).

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Atypical antipsychotic = think **metabolic panel every time.** Weight, sugar, cholesterol, blood pressure. These drugs can quietly cause diabetes and heart disease.

## Card 45 — Patient Teaching
**Q: A patient on risperidone (Risperdal) is a truck driver. What safety concern should you discuss?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Sedation, drowsiness, and impaired reaction time!**

Risperidone and other antipsychotics can cause:
- 😴 **Sedation and drowsiness** — especially when first starting or increasing the dose
- 😵‍💫 **Dizziness and orthostatic hypotension** (feeling faint when standing up)

**What to teach:**

2. This is especially important for a truck driver — drowsiness behind the wheel is life-threatening.
3. These effects are usually worst in the **first few weeks** and may get better over time.
4. Avoid alcohol — it makes sedation much worse.
5. Talk to your provider about timing the dose at bedtime if drowsiness is a problem.
6. Report any ongoing sedation — the provider may adjust the dose or try a less sedating medication.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Antipsychotics can make you sleepy. Sleepy + truck = disaster. Always ask about the patient's job and daily activities when teaching about sedating medications!

## Card 46 — Safety
**Q: A patient is on both lorazepam (Ativan) and morphine. What is the biggest danger?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Respiratory depression — this combination can stop breathing!**

**Why?**
- Lorazepam (benzodiazepine) depresses the CNS → slows breathing.
- Morphine (opioid) also depresses the CNS → slows breathing.
- Together, the effects are **additive** — even at normal doses, the combination can slow breathing to a dangerous level.

**Nursing priorities:**
1. Monitor **respiratory rate** closely — at least every 1–2 hours.
2. Monitor **oxygen saturation** (pulse oximetry).
3. Assess **level of consciousness** — excessive sedation is a warning sign.
4. Have **naloxone** (for morphine) and **flumazenil** (for lorazepam) available.
5. Hold either medication and notify the provider if RR drops below 12 or the patient is hard to arouse.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Benzos + opioids = **double CNS depression.** Two brakes on breathing at the same time. The FDA has a black box warning about this combination. Always, always watch the breathing!
</details>

## Card 47 — Antidote
**Q: What drug reverses benzodiazepines? What is the important warning about using it in patients with a seizure history?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Flumazenil (Romazicon)** is the reversal agent for benzodiazepines.

**How it works:**
- It competes with benzodiazepines at the GABA receptor.
- Given IV — works within 1–2 minutes.
- Wears off in about 1 hour — so **re-sedation can happen**. Keep monitoring!

**⚠️ Important warning — seizure risk:**
- Patients who have taken benzodiazepines **long-term** may have physical dependence.
- If you suddenly reverse the benzo with flumazenil, it's like **abruptly stopping** the drug.
- This can trigger **seizures** — especially in patients with a seizure history or epilepsy.
- Flumazenil is **used cautiously** (or avoided) in these patients.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Flumazenil rips the benzo off the receptor. If the brain has been depending on that benzo to prevent seizures, taking it away suddenly can cause a seizure. Use with caution!
</details>

## Card 48 — Application
**Q: An elderly patient on alprazolam (Xanax) falls and hits their head at night. What changes might help prevent this?**

<details>

**Changes to consider:**

- Hourly rounding at night.
- Assist with ambulation.
- Educate patient to call for help before getting up.

## Card 49 — Patient Teaching
**Q: What do you teach a patient about stopping a benzodiazepine after taking it for several months?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**"Never stop suddenly! Benzodiazepines must be tapered off slowly."**

**Why?**
- After months of use, the brain becomes **physically dependent** on the drug.
- Stopping suddenly can cause a life-threatening withdrawal reaction.

**Abrupt withdrawal symptoms:**
- 🚨 **Seizures** — the most dangerous complication!
- Rebound anxiety (worse than the original anxiety)
- Insomnia
- Tremors and sweating
- Irritability and agitation
- In severe cases: hallucinations and delirium

**What to teach:**
1. Your provider will create a **slow taper plan** — reducing the dose gradually over weeks or months.
2. Never skip or double doses.
3. Do not share your medication or take extra.
4. Report any new symptoms during the taper right away.
5. There are other, non-addictive options for long-term anxiety management.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Stopping a benzo cold turkey after months is like slamming on the brakes at full speed — the brain crashes. A slow taper is like gently slowing down. Always taper!
</details>

## Card 50 — Hold Parameter
**Q: A patient on lorazepam (Ativan) is very sedated, hard to wake up, and has a respiratory rate of 10. What do you do?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**This is oversedation — act immediately!**

**Step-by-step:**
1. **Hold the next lorazepam dose** — do not give more.
2. **Stimulate the patient** — call their name, gently shake, sternal rub if needed.
3. **Check oxygen saturation** — apply pulse ox.
4. **Position for airway** — raise the head of the bed or put in a side-lying position.
5. **Notify the provider STAT.**
6. Have **flumazenil (Romazicon)** available in case respiratory depression worsens.
7. Monitor vital signs every 5–15 minutes.
8. Be prepared to assist with ventilation (bag-valve mask) if the RR drops further.

**Key parameters to remember:**
- Hold benzodiazepines if RR is below **12** breaths/min.
- RR of 10 with sedation = the patient is heading toward respiratory failure.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Sedated + slow breathing = DANGER. The brain is too sedated to breathe properly. Hold the drug, support the airway, and call for help!
</details>

## Card 51 — Compare
**Q: Serotonin syndrome vs. Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS) — quick comparison: cause, symptoms, treatment.**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

| Feature | Serotonin Syndrome | NMS |
|---|---|---|
| **Cause** | Serotonin drugs (SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, tramadol) | Antipsychotics (especially typicals) |
| **Onset** | **Fast** — within hours | **Slow** — days to weeks |
| **Temperature** | High fever | Very high fever |
| **Muscles** | Clonus, tremor, **hyperreflexia** | "Lead-pipe" rigidity, **NO clonus** |
| **Mental state** | Agitation, confusion | Confusion, altered consciousness |
| **Key lab** | CK may be elevated | CK **very** elevated |
| **Treatment** | Stop serotonin drugs, **cyproheptadine**, cooling | Stop antipsychotic, **dantrolene** + **bromocriptine**, cooling |

**The easiest way to tell them apart on a test:**
- **Clonus + hyperreflexia + fast onset** → Serotonin syndrome
- **Lead-pipe rigidity + sky-high CK + slow onset** → NMS

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Serotonin syndrome is **twitchy and fast.** NMS is **stiff and slow.** Both are emergencies — stop the drug and cool the patient!
</details>

## Card 52 — Priority
**Q: A patient takes lithium and furosemide (Lasix). They develop nausea, tremor, and confusion. What is the priority concern?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**Lithium toxicity! This is the priority concern.**

**Here's the connection:**
- **Furosemide** is a loop diuretic — it makes the kidneys excrete sodium and water.
- When the body loses sodium, the kidneys **reabsorb lithium** in place of sodium.
- Lithium levels rise → **toxicity**.

**Nausea + tremor + confusion = classic signs of lithium toxicity.**

**Priority actions:**
1. **Hold the lithium** — do not give the next dose.
2. **Notify the provider STAT.**
3. **Check a lithium level** immediately.
4. Start IV fluids (normal saline) as ordered to rehydrate and help clear lithium.
5. Monitor vital signs, neuro status, and kidney function.
6. The provider may also hold or change the furosemide.

> 💡 *NCLEX tip:* Lithium + any diuretic (especially loop diuretics like furosemide) = toxicity risk. This is a very commonly tested drug interaction. When you see lithium + diuretic on a test, immediately think: **"lithium level is going up!"**
</details>

## Card 53 — Application
**Q: A depressed patient is started on an SSRI and a benzodiazepine at the same time. Why would the provider prescribe both?**

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer</summary>

**The benzodiazepine is a "bridge" — it provides immediate relief while waiting for the SSRI to kick in.**

**Here's the logic:**
- SSRIs take **2 to 4 weeks** to start working.
- During those weeks, the patient still has anxiety, panic, or insomnia.
- A benzodiazepine works **within 30 minutes to 1 hour** — fast relief!

**The plan:**
1. Start both medications together.
2. The benzo controls symptoms **right now** (the bridge).
3. Once the SSRI reaches full effect (2–4 weeks), the provider will **taper the benzo off**.
4. The patient continues on the SSRI alone for long-term treatment.

**Why not just use the benzo long-term?**
- Benzodiazepines cause **dependence and tolerance**.
- They are meant for **short-term use only**.
- SSRIs are safer for long-term anxiety and depression management.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* The benzo is the bridge that gets you across the river while the SSRI builds a permanent road. Once the road is built, you don't need the bridge anymore.
</details>
