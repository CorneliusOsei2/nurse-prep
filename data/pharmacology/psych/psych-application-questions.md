## Question 1 — Serotonin Syndrome Recognition

A patient takes sertraline (Zoloft) for depression. The patient also started taking St. John's Wort for "natural mood support." The nurse finds the patient agitated, diaphoretic (sweating), with a temperature of 103.8°F, muscle rigidity, and hyperreflexia. What should the nurse do FIRST?

- A) Administer acetaminophen for the fever
- B) Discontinue the sertraline and St. John's Wort and notify the provider
- C) Apply cooling blankets and continue monitoring
- D) Obtain a complete blood count (CBC)

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) Discontinue the sertraline and St. John's Wort and notify the provider**

This patient has **serotonin syndrome** — caused by combining two serotonin-boosting substances (sertraline + St. John's Wort).

- **A) Acetaminophen for fever** — Treating the fever alone doesn't address the cause. In serotonin syndrome, the fever comes from excessive serotonin, not an infection. You need to stop the offending drugs.
- **B) ✅ Correct!** The FIRST action is to stop the drugs causing the problem and notify the provider. This is the priority because serotonin syndrome can be fatal.
- **C) Cooling blankets** — This is supportive care and may be done, but it's not the FIRST action. You must stop the cause first.
- **D) CBC** — A CBC won't help diagnose serotonin syndrome. This is not the priority.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* When you see an emergency caused by a drug, the first action is usually to **stop the drug** and **notify the provider**. Supportive care comes after.
</details>

## Question 2 — NMS Emergency Management

A patient receiving haloperidol (Haldol) for schizophrenia develops a temperature of 106°F, severe generalized muscle rigidity, altered level of consciousness, and diaphoresis. The creatine kinase (CK) level is 12,000 U/L. Which action should the nurse take FIRST?

- A) Administer the next scheduled dose of haloperidol
- B) Discontinue haloperidol and notify the provider immediately
- C) Apply warm blankets to relieve the muscle rigidity
- D) Prepare to administer an SSRI to counteract the dopamine blockade

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) Discontinue haloperidol and notify the provider immediately**

This is **Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)** — a life-threatening emergency caused by dopamine-blocking drugs like haloperidol.

- **A) Give the next dose** — Absolutely NOT! This would make NMS worse. The drug that caused the problem must be STOPPED.
- **B) ✅ Correct!** Stop the antipsychotic immediately. NMS is fatal if not treated. The provider will likely order dantrolene (muscle relaxant) and/or bromocriptine (dopamine agonist), plus ICU transfer.
- **C) Warm blankets** — NO! The patient has a dangerously high fever (106°F). You would apply COOLING measures, not warm blankets. Rigidity is from the NMS, not from being cold.
- **D) SSRI** — No. SSRIs affect serotonin, not dopamine. An SSRI would not treat NMS and could cause additional problems.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* NMS = stop the antipsychotic FIRST. Everything else comes after that critical step.
</details>

## Question 3 — Lithium Toxicity with Lab Values

A patient with bipolar disorder takes lithium carbonate. The morning lithium level is 2.1 mEq/L. The patient reports feeling nauseated, confused, and has a coarse hand tremor. Which action should the nurse take?

- A) Administer the scheduled morning dose of lithium
- B) Hold the lithium, notify the provider, and prepare for possible IV fluids
- C) Give the lithium with food to reduce nausea
- D) Document the findings and recheck the level in 24 hours

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) Hold the lithium, notify the provider, and prepare for possible IV fluids**

The lithium level of 2.1 mEq/L is **toxic** (therapeutic range is 0.6–1.2; above 1.5 is toxic; above 2.0 is life-threatening). The symptoms confirm toxicity.

- **A) Give the dose** — NEVER give lithium when the level is toxic! This could be fatal.
- **B) ✅ Correct!** Hold the drug, notify the provider urgently, hydrate the patient with IV fluids to help the kidneys clear the lithium. If the level is this high, dialysis may be needed.
- **C) Give with food** — The nausea is from toxicity, not from the drug irritating the stomach. Giving more lithium would worsen toxicity.
- **D) Document and recheck in 24 hours** — This is too slow! A level of 2.1 is a medical emergency. Waiting 24 hours could result in seizures, coma, or death.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* If a question gives you a lab value that is clearly out of range AND the patient has matching symptoms, you need to act NOW — not "wait and see."
</details>

## Question 4 — SSRI Patient Teaching

A nurse is teaching a patient who was just prescribed escitalopram (Lexapro) for generalized anxiety disorder. Which statement by the patient shows they need MORE teaching?

- A) "I should take this medication at the same time every day."
- B) "I should feel better within the first 2 to 3 days."
- C) "I should not stop taking this suddenly without talking to my doctor."
- D) "I should report any new thoughts of self-harm to my doctor."

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) "I should feel better within the first 2 to 3 days."**

This statement is INCORRECT — the patient needs more teaching.

- **A) Same time every day** — Correct understanding. SSRIs should be taken consistently.
- **B) ✅ Needs more teaching!** SSRIs take **2 to 4 weeks** to reach full effect, NOT 2 to 3 days. If the patient expects immediate results, they may stop the medication too soon.
- **C) Don't stop suddenly** — Correct understanding. Abrupt discontinuation causes discontinuation syndrome.
- **D) Report self-harm thoughts** — Correct understanding. This aligns with the black box warning, especially in younger patients.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* "Which statement needs more teaching?" means find the WRONG statement. The wrong statement is the correct answer. It can feel backward — read carefully!
</details>

## Question 5 — Tyramine Crisis with MAOIs

A patient taking tranylcypromine (Parnate) calls the clinic reporting a sudden, severe headache at the back of the head, a stiff neck, nausea, and sweating. What should the nurse instruct the patient to do?

- A) Take acetaminophen and rest
- B) Go to the emergency department immediately
- C) Take an extra dose of the medication
- D) Apply a cold compress to the forehead and lie down

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) Go to the emergency department immediately**

These are classic signs of a **hypertensive crisis** from tyramine interaction with an MAOI. The occipital headache (back of head) is the hallmark sign.

- **A) Acetaminophen and rest** — This is NOT just a headache. This is a hypertensive emergency that can lead to a stroke. Acetaminophen won't help.
- **B) ✅ Correct!** This patient needs emergency care NOW. Their blood pressure may be dangerously high. They may need IV phentolamine to lower it.
- **C) Extra dose** — Absolutely not! The MAOI is part of the problem. More drug = worse crisis.
- **D) Cold compress and lie down** — This is way too casual for a potentially life-threatening situation. A stroke could happen at any moment.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* MAOI + sudden severe headache (especially in the back of the head) = hypertensive crisis = **911/ER NOW.**
</details>

## Question 6 — Benzodiazepine Overdose / Flumazenil

A patient in the emergency department is brought in after taking a large amount of alprazolam (Xanax). The patient is barely responsive with a respiratory rate of 7 breaths per minute and oxygen saturation of 84%. The nurse prepares to administer flumazenil. Which patient history finding would cause the nurse to question this order?

- A) The patient has a history of hypertension
- B) The patient has been taking alprazolam daily for 5 years
- C) The patient weighs 180 pounds
- D) The patient is 45 years old

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) The patient has been taking alprazolam daily for 5 years**

**Flumazenil can cause seizures in patients who are physically dependent on benzodiazepines** — and 5 years of daily use means this patient is very likely dependent.

- **A) Hypertension** — Not a contraindication for flumazenil. Blood pressure is not the concern here.
- **B) ✅ Correct!** Long-term benzodiazepine use causes physical dependence. Suddenly reversing the drug with flumazenil can trigger life-threatening seizures. The nurse should question this order and discuss with the provider.
- **C) Weight** — A patient's weight may affect dosing but is not a reason to withhold flumazenil.
- **D) Age** — 45 years old is not a contraindication.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* "Question this order" means you're looking for a contraindication or safety concern. Always think about what could go WRONG with the intervention.
</details>

## Question 7 — EPS Recognition and Treatment

A nurse is caring for a patient who started haloperidol 2 days ago. The patient is pacing in the hallway, repeatedly standing up and sitting down, and states, "I feel like I need to move. I can't sit still. Something is wrong inside me." What EPS is this, and what should the nurse do?

- A) Tardive dyskinesia — stop the medication permanently
- B) Acute dystonia — administer diphenhydramine immediately
- C) Akathisia — notify the provider for possible dose reduction or medication change
- D) Parkinsonism — administer levodopa

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ C) Akathisia — notify the provider for possible dose reduction or medication change**

Akathisia is an intense inner restlessness — the patient feels compelled to keep moving. It typically appears days to weeks after starting an antipsychotic.

- **A) Tardive dyskinesia** — TD involves involuntary facial movements (lip smacking, tongue darting). This patient is restless, not having involuntary facial movements. Also, TD takes months to years to develop.
- **B) Acute dystonia** — Dystonia is sudden muscle spasms (neck twisting, jaw locking). This patient can move freely — they just can't STOP moving. Different problem.
- **C) ✅ Correct!** The hallmark of akathisia is the subjective feeling of needing to move combined with observable restlessness. Treatment includes dose reduction, switching medications, or adding a beta-blocker (propranolol) or benzodiazepine.
- **D) Parkinsonism with levodopa** — Parkinsonism shows rigidity and slow movements, not restlessness. Also, levodopa is not used for antipsychotic-induced parkinsonism (benztropine is used instead).

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* The patient says "I can't sit still" — that's the key phrase for akathisia. If they said "my jaw is locked," it would be dystonia.
</details>

## Question 8 — Metabolic Monitoring with Atypical Antipsychotics

A patient has been taking olanzapine (Zyprexa) for 12 weeks. Which assessment findings would concern the nurse the most?

- A) The patient gained 2 pounds since starting the medication
- B) The patient reports occasional drowsiness in the morning
- C) The patient's fasting blood glucose is 210 mg/dL and they have gained 25 pounds
- D) The patient reports dry mouth

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ C) Fasting blood glucose of 210 mg/dL and 25-pound weight gain**

Olanzapine is well-known for causing **metabolic syndrome**, and these findings are alarming.

- **A) 2-pound gain** — Minor weight gain can happen with many medications. This is within normal fluctuation and not concerning on its own.
- **B) Morning drowsiness** — Olanzapine is sedating. Occasional drowsiness is an expected side effect.
- **C) ✅ Most concerning!** A fasting glucose of 210 mg/dL is in the **diabetic range** (normal is below 100). Combined with 25 pounds of weight gain in 12 weeks, this suggests the patient is developing metabolic syndrome, possibly type 2 diabetes. The provider must be notified.
- **D) Dry mouth** — An anticholinergic side effect that is common and not dangerous.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* "Most concerning" questions want you to find the abnormal finding that needs action. Compare the severity of each option.
</details>

## Question 9 — Hold-or-Give Decision (Lithium)

A patient is scheduled for their morning lithium dose. The nurse reviews the chart and notes: the patient had diarrhea three times overnight, the morning lithium level is 1.6 mEq/L, and the patient reports feeling "shaky and confused." What should the nurse do?

- A) Give the lithium dose with extra water
- B) Give half the dose and recheck the level in 4 hours
- C) Hold the dose, notify the provider, and assess for toxicity
- D) Give the dose and draw a repeat lithium level in 1 hour

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ C) Hold the dose, notify the provider, and assess for toxicity**

Everything here points to **lithium toxicity**: the level is above 1.5 (toxic range), the patient has diarrhea (causes dehydration → raises lithium), and they are confused and shaky.

- **A) Give with extra water** — NO. The level is already toxic. Giving more lithium could be fatal.
- **B) Give half the dose** — NO. Any additional lithium when the level is already toxic is dangerous.
- **C) ✅ Correct!** Hold the lithium. Notify the provider immediately. Assess for other toxicity signs (coarse tremor, slurred speech, vomiting, seizures). Expect orders for IV fluids and repeat labs.
- **D) Give and recheck** — NO. You never give a drug when the level is in the toxic range.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* If the lab value is toxic + the patient has symptoms of toxicity → HOLD the drug and NOTIFY. Never give more of a toxic drug!
</details>

## Question 10 — Priority / First Action (SSRI Suicide Risk)

A 19-year-old patient started fluoxetine (Prozac) for depression 1 week ago. During a follow-up visit, the patient's mother says, "She seems more agitated than before. She's been pacing and talking about 'ending it all.'" What is the nurse's FIRST action?

- A) Reassure the mother that agitation is a normal side effect
- B) Assess the patient directly for suicidal ideation and ensure safety
- C) Discontinue the fluoxetine immediately
- D) Schedule a follow-up appointment for next week

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) Assess the patient directly for suicidal ideation and ensure safety**

This is a **critical safety situation**. The patient is showing signs consistent with the SSRI black box warning — increased agitation and suicidal talk early in treatment.

- **A) Reassure that it's normal** — Agitation CAN happen with SSRIs, but "talking about ending it all" is NEVER something to dismiss. This must be assessed immediately.
- **B) ✅ Correct!** Always assess the patient directly. Ask about suicidal thoughts, plans, and means. Ensure the patient's immediate safety. Then notify the provider.
- **C) Discontinue immediately** — This is a provider decision, not a nursing decision. Also, abrupt discontinuation can cause withdrawal symptoms. You need to assess first and then contact the provider.
- **D) Schedule for next week** — Way too slow! This patient could be in danger RIGHT NOW.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* When a question asks "FIRST action" and patient safety is at risk, the answer is almost always **assess the patient**. Assessment comes before intervention.
</details>

## Question 11 — SATA (Select All That Apply) — Lithium Teaching

A nurse is teaching a patient who is starting lithium for bipolar disorder. Which instructions should the nurse include? **Select all that apply.**

- A) "Drink 8 to 10 glasses of water every day."
- B) "Follow a low-sodium diet to prevent fluid retention."
- C) "Use acetaminophen instead of ibuprofen for pain."
- D) "Get regular blood tests to check your lithium level."
- E) "Take the lithium only when you feel your mood changing."
- F) "Report severe tremor, confusion, or vomiting to your doctor right away."

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ A, C, D, F**

- **A) ✅ Correct!** Adequate hydration is essential. Dehydration concentrates lithium in the blood → toxicity.
- **B) ❌ Incorrect!** A low-sodium diet is DANGEROUS with lithium. When sodium is low, the kidneys hold onto lithium instead → toxicity. The patient should maintain a **consistent, normal sodium intake.**
- **C) ✅ Correct!** NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) decrease lithium excretion → toxicity risk. Acetaminophen is the safe alternative.
- **D) ✅ Correct!** Lithium has a very narrow therapeutic range. Regular blood level monitoring is essential for safety.
- **E) ❌ Incorrect!** Lithium must be taken consistently every day, NOT just when symptoms appear. It is a maintenance medication that works by keeping a steady level in the blood.
- **F) ✅ Correct!** These are signs of lithium toxicity and need immediate medical attention.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* For SATA questions, evaluate EACH option independently — don't let one answer influence another. Ask yourself: "Is this TRUE on its own?"
</details>

## Question 12 — Benzodiazepine Safety in Elderly

An 82-year-old patient is prescribed lorazepam (Ativan) for anxiety before a medical procedure. Which nursing action is MOST important?

- A) Teach the patient about the risk of addiction
- B) Implement fall precautions and monitor respiratory status closely
- C) Administer the medication on an empty stomach for faster absorption
- D) Encourage the patient to walk in the hallway after administration

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) Implement fall precautions and monitor respiratory status closely**

Elderly patients are at HIGH risk for falls and respiratory depression from benzodiazepines.

- **A) Teach about addiction** — While addiction education is important, this is a single dose before a procedure. The immediate safety concern is the physical risk of falls and respiratory depression.
- **B) ✅ Correct!** In elderly patients, benzodiazepines cause excessive sedation, confusion, and impaired balance. Falls can cause hip fractures and head injuries. Respiratory depression is also a concern. Safety measures are the priority.
- **C) Empty stomach** — This is not a standard recommendation and could cause faster, more intense sedation — which is not what we want in an elderly patient.
- **D) Walk in the hallway** — NO! After benzodiazepines, the patient should NOT be walking unassisted. This increases fall risk dramatically.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Elderly + benzodiazepine = fall risk and respiratory risk. Safety first!
</details>

## Question 13 — SATA — Serotonin Syndrome

Which combinations put a patient at risk for serotonin syndrome? **Select all that apply.**

- A) Sertraline (SSRI) + tramadol
- B) Fluoxetine (SSRI) + phenelzine (MAOI)
- C) Haloperidol + olanzapine
- D) Sertraline (SSRI) + St. John's Wort
- E) Lithium + ibuprofen
- F) Venlafaxine (SNRI) + sumatriptan (triptan)

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ A, B, D, F**

- **A) ✅ SSRI + tramadol** — Both increase serotonin. Tramadol has serotonergic effects. High risk!
- **B) ✅ SSRI + MAOI** — This is the MOST dangerous combination. Both massively increase serotonin. Must wait 2+ weeks between them.
- **C) ❌ Haloperidol + olanzapine** — Both are antipsychotics that block dopamine. This combination risks excessive dopamine blockade (more NMS risk, not serotonin syndrome).
- **D) ✅ SSRI + St. John's Wort** — St. John's Wort has serotonergic effects. Combining with an SSRI increases serotonin syndrome risk.
- **E) ❌ Lithium + ibuprofen** — This combination increases lithium toxicity risk (NSAIDs reduce lithium excretion). It is dangerous, but it causes lithium toxicity, NOT serotonin syndrome.
- **F) ✅ SNRI + triptan** — Triptans (migraine drugs) have serotonergic effects. Combined with an SNRI, there is serotonin syndrome risk.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Serotonin syndrome needs TWO drugs that BOTH increase serotonin. If neither drug affects serotonin, it's not serotonin syndrome.
</details>

## Question 14 — EPS Emergency — Dystonia

A patient who received their first dose of haloperidol 4 hours ago suddenly develops neck twisting to one side and difficulty swallowing. The patient appears frightened. What should the nurse do FIRST?

- A) Reassure the patient that this is temporary and will go away on its own
- B) Administer diphenhydramine (Benadryl) IM as ordered for acute dystonia
- C) Position the patient supine and apply a warm compress to the neck
- D) Administer the next dose of haloperidol early to stabilize dopamine levels

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) Administer diphenhydramine (Benadryl) IM as ordered for acute dystonia**

This is **acute dystonia** — a type of EPS that occurs within hours to days of starting an antipsychotic. It's an emergency because it's painful, frightening, and can compromise the airway if the throat muscles are involved.

- **A) Reassure and wait** — While reassurance is kind, this will NOT go away on its own without treatment. The patient is in pain and potentially at risk for airway compromise (difficulty swallowing!). Action is needed NOW.
- **B) ✅ Correct!** Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) or benztropine (Cogentin) given IM or IV provides rapid relief from dystonia. This is the standard, first-line treatment.
- **C) Supine position and warm compress** — This does not treat the cause. If the patient is having trouble swallowing, lying flat could increase aspiration risk.
- **D) More haloperidol** — Absolutely NOT! Haloperidol caused this problem. More would make it worse.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* Dystonia + antipsychotic = give diphenhydramine or benztropine IMMEDIATELY. This is one of those "act now" situations.
</details>

## Question 15 — MAOI Drug Interaction

A patient taking phenelzine (MAOI) goes to the urgent care clinic for a cold. The provider prescribes pseudoephedrine (a decongestant). The nurse should:

- A) Administer the pseudoephedrine as ordered
- B) Question the order because pseudoephedrine combined with an MAOI can cause hypertensive crisis
- C) Hold the phenelzine until the cold resolves
- D) Give the pseudoephedrine with food to reduce side effects

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) Question the order because pseudoephedrine combined with an MAOI can cause hypertensive crisis**

Pseudoephedrine is a sympathomimetic drug — it releases norepinephrine. With MAO blocked, the norepinephrine cannot be broken down, causing a dangerous blood pressure spike.

- **A) Administer as ordered** — Not safe! This combination could cause a hypertensive crisis (stroke risk). The nurse has a duty to question unsafe orders.
- **B) ✅ Correct!** The nurse must advocate for patient safety. MAOIs interact with sympathomimetics, tyramine-containing foods, and many other drugs. The nurse should contact the provider to discuss a safer alternative.
- **C) Hold the MAOI** — You wouldn't stop the MAOI just for a cold. And you can't just stop an MAOI abruptly anyway.
- **D) Give with food** — Taking pseudoephedrine with food does NOT prevent the dangerous interaction with an MAOI.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* MAOI patients must be very careful with ANY new medication, including over-the-counter cold medicines. When in doubt, the nurse should always question and verify.
</details>

## Question 16 — Atypical Antipsychotic Side Effects

A patient who has been on risperidone (Risperdal) for 6 months reports breast tenderness and milky nipple discharge. What is the most likely cause?

- A) An allergic reaction to risperidone
- B) Elevated prolactin levels caused by risperidone
- C) Serotonin syndrome
- D) Neuroleptic malignant syndrome

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) Elevated prolactin levels caused by risperidone**

Risperidone is known for causing **hyperprolactinemia** (elevated prolactin) more than other atypical antipsychotics. Dopamine normally inhibits prolactin release. Blocking dopamine → prolactin rises.

- **A) Allergic reaction** — Allergic reactions typically involve rash, hives, itching, swelling, or breathing difficulty — not breast changes.
- **B) ✅ Correct!** High prolactin causes galactorrhea (milky discharge), breast tenderness/enlargement, menstrual irregularities, and sexual dysfunction. The nurse should notify the provider — the medication may need to be changed.
- **C) Serotonin syndrome** — Serotonin syndrome involves agitation, fever, tremor, and hyperreflexia. Breast symptoms are not a feature.
- **D) NMS** — NMS involves high fever, muscle rigidity, and altered consciousness. Breast discharge is not an NMS symptom.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Risperidone = "Risper-PROLACTIN-one" — it's the atypical most associated with high prolactin.
</details>

## Question 17 — SATA — Benzodiazepine Teaching

**✅ A, C, D**

## Question 18 — Hold-or-Give Decision (Benzodiazepine)

A patient is scheduled to receive lorazepam (Ativan) 1 mg IV for anxiety. The nurse assesses the patient and finds: respiratory rate 10 breaths/min, oxygen saturation 91%, blood pressure 100/60 mmHg, and the patient appears drowsy. What should the nurse do?

- A) Administer the lorazepam as ordered since the patient is anxious
- B) Hold the lorazepam, notify the provider, and continue monitoring
- C) Administer the lorazepam and then elevate the head of the bed
- D) Give half the ordered dose to reduce the risk

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) Hold the lorazepam, notify the provider, and continue monitoring**

The patient already has **respiratory depression** (RR 10, O2 sat 91%, drowsy). Giving a benzodiazepine — which further depresses respiration — could be fatal.

- **A) Give as ordered** — NO! The respiratory rate is below 12, the O2 sat is low, and the patient is already sedated. Giving lorazepam could cause respiratory arrest.
- **B) ✅ Correct!** Hold the drug when respiratory parameters are compromised. A respiratory rate below 12 and low oxygen saturation are hold parameters for benzodiazepines. Notify the provider of the findings.
- **C) Give and elevate HOB** — Elevating the head of bed may help breathing slightly, but it won't prevent respiratory depression from the drug. Don't give the drug!
- **D) Give half the dose** — Nurses cannot modify the dose without a provider's order. Also, even half a dose could worsen this patient's already compromised respiratory status.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* Always check respiratory rate and level of consciousness BEFORE giving any CNS depressant. If RR is below 12 or the patient is already very drowsy, HOLD and notify.
</details>

## Question 19 — TCA Overdose

A patient is brought to the emergency department after intentionally ingesting a large amount of amitriptyline (a TCA). The ECG shows a widened QRS complex. What is the priority treatment?

- A) Activated charcoal
- B) Flumazenil
- C) Sodium bicarbonate
- D) Naloxone

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ C) Sodium bicarbonate**

TCA overdose is **cardiotoxic** — it causes widened QRS, arrhythmias, and can be fatal. Sodium bicarbonate is the specific treatment for TCA-induced cardiac toxicity.

- **A) Activated charcoal** — May be used if the patient presents very early after ingestion (within 1-2 hours) and is alert enough to swallow safely. But with cardiac changes already present, sodium bicarbonate is the priority.
- **B) Flumazenil** — This reverses benzodiazepines, NOT TCAs. Wrong reversal agent.
- **C) ✅ Correct!** Sodium bicarbonate narrows the QRS complex and treats the cardiac toxicity of TCA overdose. This is the first-line treatment for TCA-induced cardiac conduction abnormalities.
- **D) Naloxone** — This reverses opioids, NOT TCAs. Wrong reversal agent.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* TCA overdose → heart problems (wide QRS) → sodium bicarbonate. Know your reversal agents: flumazenil = benzos, naloxone = opioids, sodium bicarb = TCAs.
</details>

## Question 20 — SATA — NMS Signs

A nurse suspects a patient may be developing Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS). Which findings support this suspicion? **Select all that apply.**

- A) Temperature of 105.2°F
- B) Severe "lead-pipe" muscle rigidity
- C) Hyperreflexia and clonus
- D) Altered level of consciousness
- E) Creatine kinase (CK) of 18,000 U/L
- F) Diaphoresis and tachycardia

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ A, B, D, E, F**

- **A) ✅ High fever** — One of the hallmark signs of NMS. Temperatures above 104°F are common.
- **B) ✅ Lead-pipe rigidity** — A classic NMS finding. The muscles are extremely stiff throughout the range of motion.
- **C) ❌ Hyperreflexia and clonus** — These are signs of **serotonin syndrome**, NOT NMS! In NMS, reflexes may be normal or decreased. This is a key differentiator.
- **D) ✅ Altered consciousness** — Encephalopathy (confusion, delirium, even coma) is part of NMS.
- **E) ✅ Elevated CK** — Massively elevated CK indicates muscle breakdown (rhabdomyolysis) from severe rigidity. This is characteristic of NMS.
- **F) ✅ Diaphoresis and tachycardia** — Autonomic instability (sweating, blood pressure changes, fast heart rate) is a feature of NMS.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* The key to telling NMS from serotonin syndrome on a SATA: **hyperreflexia and clonus = serotonin syndrome** (not NMS). **Lead-pipe rigidity with very high CK = NMS.**
</details>

## Question 21 — Patient Teaching — Discontinuation

A patient on paroxetine (Paxil, an SSRI) tells the nurse, "I feel great now! I'm going to stop my medication today since I don't need it anymore." What is the nurse's BEST response?

- A) "That's wonderful that you feel better! Go ahead and stop."
- B) "You should never stop this medication — you'll be on it for life."
- C) "I'm glad you feel better! But please talk to your doctor before stopping. This medication needs to be reduced gradually to avoid withdrawal symptoms."
- D) "Stop the medication and restart it only if your symptoms come back."

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ C) "I'm glad you feel better! But please talk to your doctor before stopping. This medication needs to be reduced gradually to avoid withdrawal symptoms."**

- **A) Go ahead and stop** — Dangerous! Abruptly stopping an SSRI can cause **discontinuation syndrome**: dizziness, nausea, flu-like symptoms, irritability, insomnia, electric shock sensations ("brain zaps"). The depression may also return.
- **B) On it for life** — This is not necessarily true and is discouraging. Some patients do discontinue SSRIs successfully with their provider's guidance. Telling them "for life" is not accurate or helpful.
- **C) ✅ Correct!** Acknowledges the patient's feelings positively, provides accurate information, and directs them to the provider for a safe tapering plan.
- **D) Stop and restart** — This "start-stop" approach is not recommended. It increases the risk of discontinuation syndrome and relapse.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* The answer that educates the patient AND directs them to the provider is usually the best nursing response. Never encourage abrupt stopping of SSRIs, SNRIs, or benzodiazepines.
</details>

## Question 22 — Lithium and Dehydration

A patient on lithium reports starting a new exercise routine that involves running 5 miles daily in hot weather. Which nursing concern is the HIGHEST priority?

- A) The exercise may cause insomnia
- B) The patient will lose weight, making the lithium dose too high
- C) Excessive sweating can cause dehydration and sodium loss, raising lithium levels to toxic range
- D) Exercise increases serotonin, which may interact with lithium

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ C) Excessive sweating can cause dehydration and sodium loss, raising lithium levels to toxic range**

- **A) Insomnia from exercise** — Exercise may actually improve sleep. This is not a lithium-specific concern and is not the highest priority.
- **B) Weight loss and dose** — While extreme weight loss could theoretically affect drug distribution, this is not the primary concern. The immediate danger is dehydration.
- **C) ✅ Correct!** Running in hot weather causes heavy sweating → loss of fluid and sodium → dehydration → kidneys retain lithium → lithium levels rise → toxicity! This is a real and immediate safety concern.
- **D) Exercise and serotonin** — While exercise does affect neurotransmitters, this is not a clinically significant interaction with lithium on the NCLEX level.

**Teach the patient:**
- Drink EXTRA fluids before, during, and after exercise.
- Replace electrolytes.
- Monitor for toxicity signs: coarse tremor, nausea, confusion.
- Consider exercising during cooler parts of the day.
</details>

## Question 23 — SATA — Antipsychotic Monitoring

A patient is being started on olanzapine (Zyprexa). Which baseline assessments should the nurse perform? **Select all that apply.**

- A) Fasting blood glucose
- B) Lithium level
- C) Body weight and BMI
- D) Fasting lipid panel
- E) Blood pressure
- F) Thyroid function (TSH)

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ A, C, D, E**

- **A) ✅ Fasting blood glucose** — Atypical antipsychotics increase the risk of hyperglycemia and diabetes. Baseline glucose is essential.
- **B) ❌ Lithium level** — Lithium levels are monitored for patients taking lithium, not olanzapine. These are different medications.
- **C) ✅ Weight and BMI** — Olanzapine causes significant weight gain. Baseline weight is needed to track changes.
- **D) ✅ Fasting lipid panel** — Atypicals increase the risk of dyslipidemia (high cholesterol and triglycerides).
- **E) ✅ Blood pressure** — Baseline BP is needed because atypical antipsychotics can cause orthostatic hypotension.
- **F) ❌ TSH** — Thyroid monitoring is important for **lithium**, not olanzapine. Lithium can cause hypothyroidism; olanzapine does not typically affect the thyroid.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Atypical antipsychotic baseline = **metabolic panel** (weight, glucose, lipids, BP). Lithium baseline = **kidney, thyroid, sodium, lithium level.**
</details>

## Question 24 — Priority — Multiple Patients

The nurse receives report on four patients. Which patient should the nurse assess FIRST?

- A) A patient on sertraline who reports feeling nauseated after taking the morning dose
- B) A patient on lithium whose morning lithium level is 1.0 mEq/L and reports a fine hand tremor
- C) A patient on diazepam who has a respiratory rate of 8 breaths per minute and is difficult to arouse
- D) A patient on risperidone who gained 3 pounds this week

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ C) Patient on diazepam with RR of 8 and difficult to arouse**

- **A) Nausea on sertraline** — GI upset is a common SSRI side effect. Not urgent.
- **B) Lithium at 1.0 with fine tremor** — A level of 1.0 is within the therapeutic range (0.6–1.2). Fine tremor is an expected side effect at therapeutic levels. This patient is stable.
- **C) ✅ FIRST!** A respiratory rate of 8 is dangerously low (normal is 12–20). The patient is difficult to arouse. This suggests **benzodiazepine toxicity or overdose.** This is an airway and breathing emergency! The nurse may need to use a bag-valve mask, call a rapid response, and prepare flumazenil.
- **D) Weight gain on risperidone** — A 3-pound weekly gain warrants monitoring and reporting, but it is not an immediate life threat.

> 💡 *Test-taking tip:* Use the **ABCs** (Airway, Breathing, Circulation). A respiratory rate of 8 is a breathing emergency. Always assess this patient first.
</details>

## Question 25 — Application — Switching Antidepressants

A patient is being switched from fluoxetine (Prozac, an SSRI) to phenelzine (Nardil, an MAOI). The provider orders to stop the fluoxetine today and start phenelzine tomorrow. The nurse should:

- A) Carry out the order as written
- B) Question the order because there should be at least a 5-week washout period between fluoxetine and an MAOI
- C) Question the order because there should be a 2-week washout but not longer
- D) Start both medications at the same time at lower doses

<details>
<summary>🔑 Answer and Explanation</summary>

**✅ B) Question the order because there should be at least a 5-week washout period between fluoxetine and an MAOI**

- **A) Carry out the order** — NO! Starting an MAOI the day after stopping fluoxetine could cause fatal serotonin syndrome. This is a dangerous order.
- **B) ✅ Correct!** Fluoxetine has a very long half-life (the drug and its active metabolite stay in the body for weeks). A **5-week washout** is required before starting an MAOI. For other SSRIs, 2 weeks is sufficient, but fluoxetine needs 5 weeks because of its long half-life.
- **C) 2-week washout** — 2 weeks is correct for MOST SSRIs, but **fluoxetine specifically requires 5 weeks** because of its long half-life. Close, but not safe enough for this specific drug.
- **D) Both at the same time** — NEVER! Combining an SSRI and MAOI simultaneously is one of the most dangerous drug combinations — guaranteed serotonin syndrome risk.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Fluoxetine is the SSRI that hangs around the longest. It needs the longest washout (5 weeks) before starting an MAOI. Other SSRIs need only 2 weeks. This is a commonly tested detail!
</details>
