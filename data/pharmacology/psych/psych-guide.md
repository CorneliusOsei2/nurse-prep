## 1️⃣ SSRIs — Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors

### Key Drugs
- **Fluoxetine (Prozac)**
- **Sertraline (Zoloft)**
- **Escitalopram (Lexapro)**

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* SSRIs are the "go-to" first choice for depression and anxiety. They are the most commonly prescribed antidepressants.

### How They Work (Simple Version)
- Your brain has a chemical called **serotonin** that helps with mood.
- Normally, after serotonin does its job, the brain takes it back (reuptakes it).
- SSRIs **block that reuptake** — so serotonin stays around longer.
- More serotonin available = better mood over time.

### Indications (When We Use Them)
- Major depression
- Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD)
- Panic disorder
- Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)
- Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)
- Social anxiety

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- Is the patient also taking an **MAOI**? → **Do NOT give together** (wait at least 2 weeks between them).
- Is the patient taking other serotonin-boosting drugs? (tramadol, St. John's Wort, triptans) → Risk of serotonin syndrome.
- Ask about **suicidal thoughts** — especially in young patients.
- Check current medications for interactions.
- Pregnancy status — some SSRIs are safer than others in pregnancy.

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **It takes 2 to 4 weeks to feel the full effect.** This is very important to teach patients!
- Watch for **serotonin syndrome** — especially if they take more than one serotonin drug.
- Watch for increased **suicidal thinking** early in treatment (first few weeks), especially in adolescents and young adults.
- Side effects to monitor:
  - GI upset (nausea, diarrhea)
  - Sexual problems (low desire, trouble with orgasm)
  - Insomnia or drowsiness
  - Weight changes
  - Headache

### Hold Parameters
- Hold and contact the provider if the patient shows signs of **serotonin syndrome**.
- Hold if the patient is starting an **MAOI** (need a 2-week washout).

### Key Labs
- No routine blood levels needed for SSRIs.
- Monitor sodium in elderly patients (can cause low sodium — a condition called SIADH).

### ⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY — Serotonin Syndrome

> 🚨 This is a **life-threatening emergency.** Know these signs!

**What causes it?** Too much serotonin, usually from combining serotonin drugs.

**Dangerous combinations:**
- SSRI + MAOI (most dangerous!)
- SSRI + tramadol
- SSRI + St. John's Wort
- SSRI + other SSRIs or SNRIs
- SSRI + triptans (migraine drugs)

**Signs and symptoms — remember "HARMT":**
- **H**yperthermia (high fever)
- **A**gitation, confusion, restlessness
- **R**apid heart rate (tachycardia)
- **M**uscle rigidity, tremor, clonus (rhythmic muscle jerking)
- **T**remor, hyperreflexia (overactive reflexes)

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* The body is "overheating and overstimulated" — everything is going too fast.

**What to do:**
- Stop the serotonin drug immediately.
- Notify the provider — this is urgent!
- Supportive care: cooling, fluids, monitoring.
- Cyproheptadine may be given (serotonin antagonist).

### ⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY — Black Box Warning (Suicide Risk)

- SSRIs can **increase suicidal thoughts** in children, adolescents, and young adults (under 25) in the first weeks of treatment.
- This does NOT mean we avoid the drug — it means we **monitor closely.**
- Teach families to watch for mood changes, agitation, new anxiety, or talk of self-harm.
- Most dangerous time: first 1 to 2 weeks, or when the dose changes.

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* The medication may give the person energy before it improves their mood. A depressed person who now has more energy but still feels hopeless may be at higher risk.

### Patient Teaching
- "This medication takes 2 to 4 weeks to work. Don't stop taking it because you feel no change in the first week."
- "Do NOT stop suddenly — taper slowly with your doctor." (Stopping suddenly causes **discontinuation syndrome**: dizziness, nausea, flu-like feeling, irritability, "brain zaps.")
- "Avoid alcohol."
- "Tell your doctor about ALL other medications, including herbal supplements."
- "If you feel more anxious, agitated, or have thoughts of harming yourself, contact your provider right away."
- "Take at the same time every day."

### Drug Interactions
| Interacting Drug | What Happens |
|---|---|
| MAOIs | Serotonin syndrome — life-threatening! |
| Tramadol | Serotonin syndrome risk |
| St. John's Wort | Serotonin syndrome risk |
| Triptans | Serotonin syndrome risk |
| Blood thinners (warfarin) | Increased bleeding risk |
| NSAIDs | Increased GI bleeding risk |

### Contraindications
- Concurrent MAOI use (or within 2 weeks of stopping MAOI)
- Known allergy to the drug

## 2️⃣ SNRIs — Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors

### Key Drugs
- **Venlafaxine (Effexor)**
- **Duloxetine (Cymbalta)**

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* SNRIs are like SSRIs PLUS a bonus. They boost TWO chemicals — serotonin AND norepinephrine. This makes them helpful for mood AND pain.

### How They Work (Simple Version)
- They block the reuptake of **both serotonin and norepinephrine**.
- More of both chemicals stay in the brain.
- Serotonin helps mood; norepinephrine helps energy, focus, and pain control.

### Indications
- Major depression
- Generalized anxiety disorder
- Neuropathic pain (nerve pain) — especially duloxetine
- Fibromyalgia — duloxetine
- Chronic musculoskeletal pain

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- Blood pressure (SNRIs can raise it).
- Is the patient on an MAOI? → Do NOT give together.
- Check for other serotonin drugs → serotonin syndrome risk.
- Liver and kidney function (duloxetine is processed by the liver).

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- Blood pressure — can increase, especially at higher doses of venlafaxine.
- Same side effects as SSRIs: nausea, headache, dizziness, sexual dysfunction, insomnia.
- Serotonin syndrome signs (same as SSRIs).
- Suicidal thoughts — same black box warning as SSRIs.
- Takes 2 to 4 weeks for full effect.

### Hold Parameters
- Hold for signs of serotonin syndrome.
- Hold if blood pressure is significantly elevated — notify provider.
- Hold if starting an MAOI.

### Key Labs
- Blood pressure monitoring (regular checks).
- No routine blood levels.
- Liver function if taking duloxetine.

### Patient Teaching
- Same as SSRIs: takes weeks to work, don't stop suddenly, watch for mood changes.
- "Have your blood pressure checked regularly."
- "Report headaches, chest pain, or vision changes" (could be from high BP).
- "This medication can also help with pain — it does double duty!"

### Drug Interactions
- Same serotonin syndrome risks as SSRIs (MAOIs, tramadol, St. John's Wort).
- Caution with other drugs that raise blood pressure.

### Contraindications
- Concurrent MAOI use.
- Duloxetine: avoid in severe liver disease or heavy alcohol use.
- Uncontrolled narrow-angle glaucoma (both can worsen it).

## 3️⃣ TCAs — Tricyclic Antidepressants (Brief Review)

### Key Drug
- **Amitriptyline (Elavil)**

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* TCAs are the "old-school" antidepressants. They work, but they have a LOT of side effects. That's why SSRIs replaced them as first-line. But NCLEX still tests them!

### How They Work (Simple Version)
- They block reuptake of serotonin and norepinephrine (like SNRIs).
- But they ALSO block other receptors — which causes many extra side effects.

### Indications
- Depression (not first-line anymore)
- Chronic pain, migraines
- Insomnia (at low doses)
- Bedwetting in children (sometimes)

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- **ECG/heart rhythm** — TCAs affect the heart.
- Ask about suicidal thoughts — TCAs are **very dangerous in overdose**.
- Blood pressure (can cause low blood pressure when standing — orthostatic hypotension).

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **Anticholinergic side effects** — remember "Can't see, can't pee, can't spit, can't... go to the bathroom":
  - Dry mouth
  - Blurred vision
  - Constipation
  - Urinary retention
  - Confusion (especially in elderly)
- Drowsiness and sedation
- Weight gain
- Orthostatic hypotension (stand up slowly!)
- **Cardiac effects** — arrhythmias, prolonged QT

### ⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY — TCA Overdose
- TCAs are **cardiotoxic in overdose** — can cause fatal heart rhythms.
- This is why they are risky in suicidal patients.
- Signs of overdose: altered consciousness, seizures, wide QRS on ECG, hypotension.
- Treatment: sodium bicarbonate for cardiac effects.

### Patient Teaching
- "Stand up slowly to avoid dizziness."
- "This may make you very drowsy — take at bedtime."
- "Drink water, chew gum for dry mouth."
- "Do NOT take extra pills — overdose is very dangerous."
- "Keep this medication locked up, away from children."

### Drug Interactions
- MAOIs — serotonin syndrome.
- Other drugs that cause drowsiness (additive sedation).
- Alcohol — do not combine.

### Contraindications
- Recent heart attack (MI).
- Concurrent MAOI use.
- Use with caution in patients at risk for suicide.

## 4️⃣ MAOIs — Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (Brief Review)

### Key Drugs
- **Phenelzine (Nardil)**
- **Tranylcypromine (Parnate)**

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* MAOIs are powerful but tricky. They stop the enzyme (MAO) that breaks down serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine. This means MORE of all these chemicals. But it also means certain foods and drugs become DANGEROUS.

### How They Work (Simple Version)
- MAO is an enzyme that breaks down mood chemicals in the brain.
- MAOIs block this enzyme → mood chemicals build up → mood improves.
- BUT — MAO also breaks down **tyramine** (found in certain foods). If you block MAO, tyramine builds up too → dangerous blood pressure spike.

### Indications
- Treatment-resistant depression (when other drugs haven't worked)
- Atypical depression
- Rarely used as first-line because of the many restrictions

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- **Complete medication list** — many dangerous interactions!
- Make sure patient is NOT taking SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, meperidine, or other serotonin drugs.
- There must be at least **2 weeks** between stopping an SSRI and starting an MAOI (5 weeks for fluoxetine because it stays in the body longer).
- Blood pressure.
- Make sure the patient understands and can follow the tyramine diet.

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **Blood pressure** — watch for hypertensive crisis.
- Signs of serotonin syndrome.
- Orthostatic hypotension (at normal doses).
- Insomnia, weight gain.

### ⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY — Tyramine and Hypertensive Crisis

> 🚨 This is life-threatening. Patients MUST follow the tyramine-restricted diet.

**What is tyramine?** A substance in certain aged, fermented, or cured foods. Normally, MAO in the gut breaks it down. When MAO is blocked, tyramine is not broken down, and it causes a massive release of norepinephrine → blood pressure skyrockets.

**Foods to AVOID (high tyramine):**
- 🧀 Aged cheeses (cheddar, Swiss, blue, Parmesan)
- 🥩 Cured and smoked meats (salami, pepperoni, bacon, hot dogs)
- 🍺 Beer and red wine
- 🫘 Soy sauce, miso, tofu
- 🫒 Pickled or fermented foods (sauerkraut, kimchi)
- 🍌 Overripe bananas and avocados
- 🍫 Chocolate (in large amounts)
- Fava beans, broad beans

**Signs of hypertensive crisis:**
- Sudden, severe headache (especially in the back of the head — occipital)
- Stiff neck
- Nausea, vomiting
- Sweating
- Very high blood pressure
- Fast or slow heart rate
- Chest pain
- Can lead to stroke!

**What to do:**
- Stop the food/drug causing the problem.
- This is a medical emergency — notify the provider immediately.
- Phentolamine (alpha-blocker) may be given IV to lower BP.

### Patient Teaching
- "Follow the tyramine-restricted diet strictly — no aged cheese, cured meats, beer, or soy sauce."
- "Carry a list of foods to avoid with you at all times."
- "If you get a sudden, severe headache, go to the emergency room immediately."
- "Tell EVERY doctor, dentist, and pharmacist you take an MAOI."
- "Wait at least 2 weeks after stopping this drug before starting another antidepressant."
- "Do NOT take cold medicines without checking — many contain drugs that interact with MAOIs."

### Drug Interactions
| Interacting Drug | What Happens |
|---|---|
| SSRIs, SNRIs | Serotonin syndrome — deadly! Wait 2+ weeks between. |
| Meperidine (Demerol) | Serotonin syndrome — can be fatal! |
| TCAs | Serotonin syndrome risk |
| Sympathomimetics (pseudoephedrine) | Hypertensive crisis |
| Tyramine-rich foods | Hypertensive crisis |

### Contraindications
- Concurrent SSRI, SNRI, TCA, or meperidine use.
- Pheochromocytoma.
- Patients who cannot follow the tyramine diet.
- Liver disease.

## 5️⃣ Lithium — The Gold Standard Mood Stabilizer

### Key Drug
- **Lithium (Lithobid, Eskalith)**

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Lithium is like a "mood thermostat" for bipolar disorder. It keeps the highs from going too high and the lows from going too low. But it's a very "picky" drug — the difference between a helpful dose and a toxic dose is VERY small.

### How It Works (Simple Version)
- We don't fully understand exactly how lithium works.
- It seems to stabilize nerve cell membranes and affect neurotransmitters.
- The key thing: it evens out mood swings in bipolar disorder.

### Indications
- **Bipolar disorder** — first-line treatment for mania and maintenance.
- Prevention of manic and depressive episodes.
- Sometimes used for severe depression that doesn't respond to other treatments.

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- **Kidney function** (BUN, creatinine) — lithium is cleared by the kidneys.
- **Thyroid function** (TSH, T3, T4) — lithium can cause hypothyroidism.
- **Baseline lithium level**.
- **Electrolytes**, especially **sodium** — low sodium raises lithium levels!
- **ECG** — lithium can affect heart rhythm.
- **Pregnancy test** — lithium causes birth defects (Category D).
- Hydration status.

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **Lithium blood levels** — this is the #1 priority!
  - **Therapeutic range: 0.6 to 1.2 mEq/L**
  - **Toxic: above 1.5 mEq/L**
  - **Life-threatening: above 2.0 mEq/L**
- Draw lithium levels **8 to 12 hours after the last dose** (usually a morning trough).
- Early side effects (often mild and may improve):
  - Fine hand tremor (normal at therapeutic levels)
  - Increased thirst and urination
  - Mild nausea
  - Slight weight gain
- Signs the level is getting high — watch carefully!

### ⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY — Lithium Toxicity

> 🚨 Lithium has a **very narrow therapeutic range.** A little too much can be dangerous.

**What causes lithium to build up?**
- Dehydration (sweating, vomiting, diarrhea, fever)
- Low sodium intake or low-sodium diet
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) — they decrease lithium excretion
- ACE inhibitors and diuretics (especially thiazides)
- Kidney problems

**Signs of lithium toxicity — they get worse as the level rises:**

| Level | Signs |
|---|---|
| Mild (1.5–2.0) | **Coarse** hand tremor (not fine!), nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, drowsiness, muscle weakness |
| Moderate (2.0–2.5) | Confusion, slurred speech, blurred vision, severe GI symptoms, muscle twitching, irregular heartbeat |
| Severe (above 2.5) | Seizures, coma, kidney failure, cardiovascular collapse, death |

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Fine tremor = probably okay. **Coarse** tremor = getting toxic — check that level NOW!

**What to do for toxicity:**
- **Hold the lithium!** Do NOT give the next dose.
- Draw a lithium level STAT.
- Notify the provider immediately.
- IV fluids for hydration.
- Monitor cardiac rhythm.
- Hemodialysis for severe toxicity.
- There is **no antidote** — we manage with supportive care and removal.

### Hold Parameters
- **Hold lithium if:**
  - Patient has vomiting, diarrhea, or is dehydrated.
  - Lithium level is above 1.5 mEq/L.
  - Patient shows signs of toxicity (coarse tremor, confusion, slurred speech).
  - Patient is about to have surgery or an imaging test with contrast dye.

### Key Labs
| Lab | When | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Lithium level | Every 1-2 weeks initially, then every 1-3 months | Keep in therapeutic range |
| BUN/Creatinine | Baseline, then every 2-3 months | Lithium can harm kidneys |
| Thyroid (TSH) | Baseline, then every 6 months | Lithium can cause hypothyroidism |
| Sodium/Electrolytes | Regular checks | Low sodium increases lithium levels |
| ECG | Baseline | Heart rhythm check |

### Patient Teaching
- "Take your lithium at the same time every day."
- "Drink **8 to 10 glasses of water** every day — stay well hydrated!"
- "Eat a **normal, consistent amount of salt** (sodium). Do NOT start a low-sodium diet without talking to your doctor."
- "**Avoid NSAIDs** like ibuprofen and naproxen — they make lithium levels go up. Use acetaminophen (Tylenol) for pain instead."
- "Get your blood drawn for lithium levels on schedule — this is very important."
- "If you are vomiting, have diarrhea, or are sweating a lot, drink extra fluids and call your doctor."
- "Watch for these warning signs: bad tremor, confusion, vomiting, trouble speaking — go to the ER."
- "Do NOT get pregnant while taking lithium — it can harm the baby."
- "Avoid caffeine in excess — it can lower lithium levels."

### Drug Interactions
| Interacting Drug | What Happens |
|---|---|
| NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) | Increase lithium levels → toxicity risk |
| Thiazide diuretics | Increase lithium levels → toxicity risk |
| ACE inhibitors | Increase lithium levels → toxicity risk |
| Caffeine | Decreases lithium levels (less effective) |
| Sodium changes | Low sodium → high lithium; high sodium → low lithium |

### Contraindications
- Severe kidney disease.
- Severe dehydration.
- Pregnancy (especially first trimester — Ebstein's anomaly, a heart defect).
- Sodium-depleted patients.

## 6️⃣ Typical (First-Generation) Antipsychotics

### Key Drug
- **Haloperidol (Haldol)**

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Haloperidol is a strong, older antipsychotic. It works well for psychosis and acute agitation, but it has serious movement-related side effects (EPS). Think "Haldol = high EPS risk."

### How It Works (Simple Version)
- It blocks **dopamine receptors** in the brain.
- Too much dopamine activity is linked to psychotic symptoms (hallucinations, delusions).
- Blocking dopamine reduces these symptoms.
- But dopamine also controls movement — so blocking it can cause movement problems.

### Indications
- Schizophrenia
- Acute psychosis
- Severe agitation
- Tourette syndrome (sometimes)
- Nausea (in some settings)

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- **ECG** — haloperidol can prolong QT interval (heart rhythm problem).
- Is the patient already taking other drugs that prolong QT?
- Blood pressure.
- Current level of consciousness.
- Is the patient already experiencing movement problems?
- Check for history of NMS.

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **EPS** — this is the big concern with typical antipsychotics.
- Blood pressure (can cause orthostatic hypotension).
- Level of sedation.
- Heart rhythm (QT prolongation).
- Anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention).
- Prolactin levels may increase (can cause breast enlargement, milk production, menstrual changes).

### ⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY — Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS)

> 🚨 These are movement disorders caused by dopamine blockade. Know all four types!

| Type | When It Appears | What It Looks Like | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Acute dystonia** | Hours to days | Sudden muscle spasms — neck twisting, jaw locking, tongue protrusion, eyes rolling up (oculogyric crisis) | **Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) IV/IM** or **benztropine (Cogentin)** — give right away! |
| **Akathisia** | Days to weeks | Restlessness — the patient cannot sit still, pacing, rocking, feeling of inner agitation | Reduce dose, beta-blockers (propranolol), benzodiazepines |
| **Parkinsonism** | Weeks to months | Shuffling gait, mask-like face, rigidity, bradykinesia, pill-rolling tremor — looks like Parkinson's disease | Benztropine, trihexyphenidyl |
| **Tardive dyskinesia (TD)** | Months to years | Involuntary movements of the face — lip smacking, tongue darting, chewing, facial grimacing | **May be irreversible!** Stop or switch the drug. Valbenazine or deutetrabenazine may help. |

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Dystonia is the **emergency** — a patient suddenly can't open their jaw or their neck is twisted. Give diphenhydramine or benztropine IMMEDIATELY. Tardive dyskinesia is the **long-term** problem that may never go away.

### ⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY — Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)

> 🚨 This is a **medical emergency!** It can happen with ANY antipsychotic but is more common with typicals.

**Signs — remember "FEVER":**
- **F**ever (very high — over 104°F / 40°C)
- **E**ncephalopathy (altered consciousness, confusion)
- **V**ital sign instability (unstable BP, tachycardia, tachypnea)
- **E**levated enzymes (CK/CPK — from muscle breakdown)
- **R**igidity (severe "lead-pipe" muscle rigidity)

**Other signs:** Profuse sweating, tremor, incontinence.

**What to do:**
- **STOP the antipsychotic IMMEDIATELY.**
- This is a medical emergency — notify the provider STAT.
- Transfer to ICU if needed.
- Cooling measures for fever.
- IV fluids.
- **Dantrolene** (muscle relaxant) and/or **bromocriptine** (dopamine agonist) may be given.
- Monitor CK levels, kidney function (muscle breakdown can cause kidney damage — rhabdomyolysis).

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* NMS and serotonin syndrome look similar (both have fever and rigidity), but the CAUSE is different. NMS = dopamine blockade (from antipsychotics). Serotonin syndrome = too much serotonin (from serotonin drugs). NMS usually develops over days; serotonin syndrome can happen within hours.

### Patient Teaching
- "Report any unusual muscle stiffness, spasms, or movements right away."
- "Stand up slowly — this drug can make you dizzy."
- "Avoid alcohol."
- "Use sunscreen — this drug can make you more sensitive to the sun."
- "If you develop a high fever with stiffness and confusion, this is an emergency — go to the ER."

### Drug Interactions
- Other QT-prolonging drugs → dangerous heart rhythms.
- CNS depressants (alcohol, opioids, benzodiazepines) → increased sedation.
- Anticholinergic drugs → additive effects.

### Contraindications
- Parkinson's disease (blocking dopamine will make it much worse).
- Prolonged QT interval.
- Known NMS history with this drug.
- CNS depression / coma.

## 7️⃣ Atypical (Second-Generation) Antipsychotics

### Key Drugs
- **Olanzapine (Zyprexa)**
- **Risperidone (Risperdal)**
- **Quetiapine (Seroquel)**

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Atypical antipsychotics are "newer and kinder." They cause FEWER movement problems (EPS) than the typicals. But they bring a different problem — **metabolic syndrome** (weight gain, diabetes, high cholesterol).

### How They Work (Simple Version)
- They block both **dopamine and serotonin receptors**.
- The serotonin blockade is what reduces the EPS risk compared to typicals.
- They treat psychosis, mania, and are also used for depression and mood stabilization.

### Indications
- Schizophrenia
- Bipolar disorder (mania and maintenance)
- Treatment-resistant depression (add-on therapy)
- Agitation and behavioral disturbances
- Quetiapine: also used for insomnia and anxiety (off-label)

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- **Baseline weight and BMI**
- **Fasting blood glucose** and **HbA1c**
- **Fasting lipid panel** (cholesterol, triglycerides)
- Blood pressure
- Waist circumference
- ECG (some can prolong QT)
- History of diabetes or metabolic syndrome

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **Weight** — weigh regularly! Olanzapine especially causes significant weight gain.
- **Blood glucose** — watch for hyperglycemia (increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue).
- **Lipids** — monitor for dyslipidemia.
- Blood pressure.
- EPS — less common but still possible, especially with risperidone at higher doses.
- NMS — still a risk (less than with typicals, but it can happen).
- Sedation, especially with quetiapine and olanzapine.
- Prolactin elevation — especially with risperidone.

### ⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY — Metabolic Syndrome

> ⚠️ This is the main concern with atypical antipsychotics!

**What is metabolic syndrome?** A cluster of problems that increase the risk of heart disease and diabetes:
- Weight gain (especially belly fat)
- High blood sugar (can develop type 2 diabetes)
- High cholesterol and triglycerides
- High blood pressure

**Monitoring schedule:**
- Weight: at every visit
- Fasting glucose and lipids: at baseline, 12 weeks, then annually
- Blood pressure: at every visit

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Typicals → worry about **movement** (EPS). Atypicals → worry about **metabolism** (weight, sugar, cholesterol).

### Hold Parameters
- Hold for signs of NMS.
- Hold for significantly elevated blood glucose — notify provider.
- Hold if patient is excessively sedated or has very low blood pressure.

### Key Labs
| Lab | When | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Fasting glucose / HbA1c | Baseline, 12 weeks, then yearly | Diabetes screening |
| Fasting lipid panel | Baseline, 12 weeks, then yearly | Cholesterol monitoring |
| Weight/BMI | Every visit | Track weight gain |
| Prolactin (if symptoms) | As needed | Risperidone can raise it |

### Patient Teaching
- "This medication may make you gain weight. Eat healthy foods and stay active."
- "Report increased thirst, frequent urination, or blurred vision — these could be signs of high blood sugar."
- "This drug can make you very drowsy — avoid driving until you know how it affects you."
- "Do NOT stop this medication suddenly without talking to your doctor."
- "Still report unusual movements, stiffness, or fever."
- "Keep all your lab appointments — we need to check your blood sugar and cholesterol."

### Drug Interactions
- CNS depressants → increased sedation and respiratory depression.
- Drugs that lower blood pressure → additive hypotension.
- CYP450 interactions vary by drug (ask the pharmacist!).

### Contraindications
- Known allergy.
- Elderly patients with dementia — **black box warning** for increased risk of death.
- Severe liver impairment (some agents).
- Use caution in patients with diabetes or metabolic syndrome.

## 8️⃣ Benzodiazepines

### Key Drugs
- **Lorazepam (Ativan)**
- **Diazepam (Valium)**
- **Alprazolam (Xanax)**

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Benzodiazepines are "chill pills" — they calm the brain down quickly. They boost a natural brain chemical called GABA that slows things down. They work FAST (unlike antidepressants which take weeks). But they come with big risks: dependence, falls, and breathing problems.

### How They Work (Simple Version)
- **GABA** is the brain's main "slow down" chemical.
- Benzodiazepines make GABA work BETTER → brain activity slows down.
- This reduces anxiety, stops seizures, relaxes muscles, and causes sedation.

### Indications
- Anxiety disorders (short-term)
- Panic attacks
- Seizures (status epilepticus — lorazepam, diazepam)
- Alcohol withdrawal (to prevent seizures — lorazepam, chlordiazepoxide)
- Insomnia (short-term)
- Procedural sedation
- Muscle spasms

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- **Respiratory rate and oxygen saturation** — benzodiazepines can slow breathing.
- **Level of consciousness** — are they already sedated?
- Is the patient on **opioids**? → Very dangerous combination! Both cause respiratory depression.
- Blood pressure.
- Is the patient elderly? → Higher fall risk and sensitivity.
- History of substance abuse (high addiction potential).
- Liver function (most are processed by the liver).

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **Respiratory rate** — the #1 concern! Count respirations before AND after.
- **Level of sedation** — excessive drowsiness.
- **Falls** — especially in elderly patients. Raise side rails, assist with ambulation.
- Blood pressure (may drop).
- Confusion, especially in elderly.
- Signs of dependence over time.
- Paradoxical reactions (rare): agitation, aggression, excitement instead of calm.

### Hold Parameters
- **Hold if respiratory rate is below 12 breaths per minute.**
- Hold if the patient is excessively sedated (hard to arouse).
- Hold if oxygen saturation is low.
- Hold if the patient just received opioids (check with the provider first).

### ⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY — Respiratory Depression

> 🚨 The most dangerous risk, especially when combined with opioids!

- Benzodiazepines + opioids = **greatly increased risk of respiratory depression and death.**
- The FDA has a **black box warning** about this combination.
- Always check what other medications the patient is taking.
- Have resuscitation equipment nearby when giving IV benzodiazepines.

### ⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY — Flumazenil (The Reversal Agent)

- **Flumazenil (Romazicon)** reverses the effects of benzodiazepines.
- Used for benzodiazepine overdose.
- Given IV — works quickly but wears off fast (may need repeated doses).
- **Caution:** Can cause **seizures** in patients who have been taking benzodiazepines long-term or who also took seizure-threshold-lowering drugs.
- Monitor closely after giving — the benzodiazepine may outlast flumazenil (re-sedation risk).

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* Flumazenil for benzodiazepines is like naloxone for opioids — it's the reversal agent. But remember: flumazenil can cause seizures!

### ⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY — Do NOT Stop Abruptly

- After prolonged use, stopping benzodiazepines suddenly can cause:
  - **Seizures** (most dangerous!)
  - Rebound anxiety
  - Insomnia
  - Tremors
  - Irritability
  - Sweating
- **Always taper gradually** under medical supervision.

### Key Labs
- No routine blood levels needed.
- Monitor liver function if on long-term therapy.
- Monitor respiratory status (not a "lab" but critically important).

### Patient Teaching
- "This medication can make you very drowsy — do NOT drive or operate heavy machinery."
- "Do NOT drink alcohol while taking this — it can be life-threatening."
- "Do NOT take this with opioid pain medications unless your doctor specifically says so."
- "Do NOT stop this medication suddenly if you've been taking it for more than a few weeks — you must taper slowly."
- "This medication can be habit-forming. Take only as prescribed."
- "If you are elderly, be very careful when walking — use handrails, get up slowly."
- "Keep this medication in a safe place — others should not take it."
- "If you have trouble breathing, feel very weak, or can't stay awake, get emergency help."

### Drug Interactions
| Interacting Drug | What Happens |
|---|---|
| Opioids | Severe respiratory depression — can be fatal! Black box warning. |
| Alcohol | Severe CNS depression — can be fatal! |
| Other sedatives (antihistamines, muscle relaxants) | Additive sedation |
| Grapefruit juice | May increase levels of some benzodiazepines |

### Contraindications
- Severe respiratory depression.
- Acute narrow-angle glaucoma (most benzodiazepines).
- Sleep apnea (untreated).
- Myasthenia gravis.
- Pregnancy (risk of fetal harm — Category D).
- Known allergy.

## 🧠 Memory Helpers

### Serotonin Syndrome vs. NMS — How to Tell Them Apart

| Feature | Serotonin Syndrome | NMS |
|---|---|---|
| Cause | Serotonin drugs | Antipsychotics |
| Onset | Fast (hours) | Slow (days to weeks) |
| Reflexes | **Hyperreflexia** (overactive) | Normal or decreased |
| Clonus | **YES** (rhythmic jerking) | No |
| Muscle | Rigidity + tremor | **Lead-pipe rigidity** |
| Pupils | Dilated | Normal |
| GI symptoms | Diarrhea common | Less common |
| Lab | — | **Elevated CK/CPK** |

> 💡 *Think of it this way:*
> - Serotonin syndrome = **"hyperactive and twitchy"** (clonus, hyperreflexia)
> - NMS = **"stiff as a board"** (lead-pipe rigidity, elevated CK)

### Lithium — "Lithium Loves" and "Lithium Hates"

**Lithium LOVES (keep these consistent):**
- 💧 Water (stay hydrated)
- 🧂 Salt/sodium (keep intake steady)
- 🩸 Regular blood draws

**Lithium HATES (these raise the level — danger!):**
- 💊 NSAIDs
- 💧 Dehydration
- 🧂 Low sodium
- 💊 Thiazide diuretics
- 💊 ACE inhibitors
