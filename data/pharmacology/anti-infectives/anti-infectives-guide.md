## 1. Penicillins

### Drugs to Know
- **Amoxicillin** (oral — very common)
- **Ampicillin** (oral or IV)

### What Are They Used For? (Indications)
- Many types of bacterial infections
- Ear infections (otitis media)
- Respiratory infections (pneumonia, bronchitis)
- Urinary tract infections (UTIs)
- Skin infections
- Ampicillin is also used for meningitis and endocarditis

> These are called **broad-spectrum** penicillins. That means they work against many different kinds of bacteria.

### How Do They Work? (Mechanism — Simple Version)
- Think of bacteria like tiny creatures with a protective wall around them.
- Penicillins **break that wall**. Without their wall, the bacteria die.
- *Think of it this way:* Imagine popping a water balloon — the wall breaks and everything falls apart. That is what penicillins do to bacteria.

### What to Check BEFORE Giving

| Check This | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Allergy history** ⚠️ | This is the #1 thing! Ask: "Are you allergic to penicillin or any antibiotics?" Penicillin allergy is one of the most common drug allergies. |
| Kidney function (BUN, creatinine) | The drug leaves the body through the kidneys. If kidneys are not working well, the drug can build up. |
| Current medications | Penicillins can interact with other drugs (like blood thinners). |
| Pregnancy/breastfeeding status | Generally safe, but always check. |

### What to Watch AFTER Giving

- **Allergic reactions** — This is the big one!
  - **Mild:** Rash, itching, hives
  - **Severe (anaphylaxis):** Throat swelling, trouble breathing, wheezing, drop in blood pressure, fast heart rate
  - ⚠️ Anaphylaxis is a medical emergency! Stop the drug. Call for help. Give **epinephrine** first.
- **GI upset** — Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (very common)
- **Superinfection** — Watch for signs of new infection like oral thrush (white patches in mouth) or watery diarrhea (could be C. diff)

### Key Labs
- BUN and creatinine (kidney function)
- CBC (white blood cells — is the infection getting better?)

### Patient Teaching
- ✅ "Finish ALL your pills, even if you feel better. Stopping early can make the bacteria come back stronger."
- ✅ "Take with or without food. If your stomach is upset, try taking it with a small snack."
- ✅ "Call your doctor right away if you get a rash, hives, swelling, or trouble breathing."
- ✅ "Diarrhea is common. But if it is very watery or has blood, tell your doctor right away." (Could be C. diff.)
- ✅ "If you are on birth control pills, use a backup method. Antibiotics can sometimes make them less effective."

### Drug Interactions
- **Warfarin** (blood thinner) — may increase bleeding risk
- **Methotrexate** — penicillins can increase methotrexate levels (toxicity risk)
- **Allopurinol** + ampicillin — increased risk of skin rash

### Contraindications
- **Known penicillin allergy** (especially history of anaphylaxis)
- Use with caution in patients with kidney problems

## 2. Cephalosporins

### Drugs to Know
- **Cephalexin (Keflex)** — 1st generation (oral)
- **Ceftriaxone (Rocephin)** — 3rd generation (IV/IM)

> **Quick tip:** Cephalosporins are organized by "generations" (1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th). Higher generations generally work against more types of bacteria, especially gram-negative ones.

### What Are They Used For? (Indications)
- **Cephalexin (1st gen):** Skin infections, UTIs, bone infections, strep throat
- **Ceftriaxone (3rd gen):** Meningitis, pneumonia, gonorrhea, serious infections, surgical prophylaxis

### How Do They Work? (Mechanism — Simple Version)
- Very similar to penicillins! They also **break the bacterial cell wall**.
- *Think of it this way:* Cephalosporins are like cousins of penicillins. They come from the same family and work the same way.

### What to Check BEFORE Giving

| Check This | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Penicillin allergy history** ⚠️ | Because they are "cousins," there is a **cross-allergy risk**. About 1–2% of patients with a penicillin allergy may also react to cephalosporins. If the patient had **anaphylaxis** to penicillin, do NOT give cephalosporins without careful evaluation. |
| Kidney function (BUN, creatinine) | Dose may need to be adjusted. |
| Liver function (for ceftriaxone) | Ceftriaxone is processed partly by the liver. |
| Age of patient (for ceftriaxone) | Special caution in neonates (newborns). |

### What to Watch AFTER Giving

- **Allergic reactions** — Same as penicillins (rash to anaphylaxis)
- **GI upset** — Nausea, diarrhea
- **Superinfection / C. diff** — Watch for watery diarrhea
- **Bleeding** (some cephalosporins can affect vitamin K and clotting)

### ⚠️ Critical Safety Alert: Ceftriaxone + Calcium in Neonates
- **Do NOT mix ceftriaxone with calcium-containing IV solutions in neonates (newborns).**
- Why? It can form dangerous crystals in the blood, lungs, and kidneys. This can be **fatal**.
- *Think of it this way:* Ceftriaxone and calcium in a baby's IV = a very dangerous combination. Never mix them.

### Key Labs
- BUN and creatinine (kidney function)
- CBC (monitor infection)
- PT/INR (if patient is also on blood thinners — some cephalosporins affect clotting)

### Patient Teaching
- ✅ "Tell your nurse or doctor if you have ever had a reaction to penicillin."
- ✅ "Finish the full course of medication."
- ✅ "Report any rash, itching, swelling, or trouble breathing right away."
- ✅ "If you get very bad, watery diarrhea, tell your doctor. Do not try to treat it yourself."

### Drug Interactions
- **Calcium-containing IV solutions** (with ceftriaxone in neonates — see above)
- **Warfarin** — may increase bleeding risk
- **Aminoglycosides** — can increase kidney damage risk when used together
- **Alcohol** (some cephalosporins cause a disulfiram-like reaction — nausea, vomiting, flushing)

### Contraindications
- History of **anaphylaxis** to penicillin (use extreme caution or avoid)
- Known cephalosporin allergy
- Ceftriaxone: avoid in neonates receiving calcium IV

## 3. Vancomycin

### Drug to Know
- **Vancomycin** (IV for systemic infections; oral for C. diff)

> Vancomycin is sometimes called "the drug of last resort" because it is saved for serious, resistant infections.

### What Is It Used For? (Indications)
- **MRSA** (methicillin-resistant *Staphylococcus aureus*) — the big one!
- Serious gram-positive infections when other antibiotics fail
- **C. difficile (C. diff) colitis** — but only the **oral** form works for this (because C. diff lives in the gut)

> *Think of it this way:* IV vancomycin fights infections in the **blood and body**. Oral vancomycin fights infections in the **gut**.

### How Does It Work? (Mechanism — Simple Version)
- It stops bacteria from building their cell wall, but in a different way than penicillins.
- *Think of it this way:* If penicillins "pop" the wall, vancomycin "blocks the building supplies" so the wall is never finished.

### What to Check BEFORE Giving

| Check This | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Kidney function (BUN, creatinine) ⚠️ | Vancomycin is very hard on the kidneys (nephrotoxic). |
| Hearing baseline | Vancomycin can damage hearing (ototoxic). |
| Current vancomycin trough level | You need to check levels to make sure the dose is right — not too high, not too low. |
| Allergy history | Ask about past reactions to vancomycin. |
| Other nephrotoxic drugs | Using vancomycin WITH aminoglycosides increases kidney damage risk. |

### What to Watch AFTER Giving

- **Red Man Syndrome** ⚠️
  - What is it? Flushing (redness) of the face, neck, and upper body. Rash, itching, sometimes low blood pressure.
  - Why does it happen? The IV infusion is running **too fast**.
  - What to do? **Slow the infusion rate.** You can also give an antihistamine (like diphenhydramine).
  - ⚠️ **This is NOT a true allergy.** Do not stop the drug permanently. Just slow it down.
  - *Think of it this way:* Red Man Syndrome = "Too fast!" Slow it down and the patient usually feels better.
- **Nephrotoxicity** (kidney damage) — Watch for decreased urine output, rising creatinine
- **Ototoxicity** (hearing damage) — Watch for tinnitus (ringing in ears), hearing loss, dizziness

### Key Labs

| Lab | Why | When to Check |
|---|---|---|
| **Vancomycin trough level** | Make sure the drug level is in the right range (not too high = toxic, not too low = not working) | Draw **right before** the next dose (usually 30 minutes before) |
| BUN and creatinine | Monitor kidney function | Regularly during treatment |
| CBC | Monitor infection | Regularly |
| Hearing tests | Baseline and if symptoms develop | Before treatment starts, then as needed |

> **Trough level timing is a favorite NCLEX topic!** The trough is drawn at the **lowest point** — right before the next dose. This tells you the minimum amount of drug in the blood.

### Patient Teaching
- ✅ "Tell the nurse right away if you feel flushing, itching, or redness during the IV infusion."
- ✅ "Report any ringing in your ears, hearing changes, or dizziness."
- ✅ "Report any decrease in urination."
- ✅ "You will need regular blood tests to check your drug levels and kidney function."

### Drug Interactions
- **Aminoglycosides** (gentamicin, tobramycin) — increased nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity when used together
- **NSAIDs** — can increase kidney damage risk
- **Loop diuretics** (furosemide) — can increase ototoxicity risk

### Contraindications
- Known vancomycin allergy (rare but possible)
- Use with extreme caution in patients with kidney disease or hearing loss

## 4. Aminoglycosides

### Drugs to Know
- **Gentamicin**
- **Tobramycin**

> **Naming tip:** Many aminoglycosides end in **-mycin** or **-micin** (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, neomycin).

### What Are They Used For? (Indications)
- Serious **gram-negative** bacterial infections
- Sepsis (blood infections)
- Pneumonia (hospital-acquired)
- Urinary tract infections (complicated)
- Often used **in combination** with other antibiotics for serious infections

### How Do They Work? (Mechanism — Simple Version)
- They stop bacteria from making the **proteins** they need to survive.
- *Think of it this way:* If bacteria are a factory, aminoglycosides shut down the assembly line. No proteins = bacteria can't function or grow.

### What to Check BEFORE Giving

| Check This | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Kidney function** (BUN, creatinine, GFR) ⚠️ | Aminoglycosides are very nephrotoxic. Dose is often based on kidney function. |
| **Hearing baseline** ⚠️ | Aminoglycosides are very ototoxic. Get a baseline hearing assessment. |
| Weight | Dose is often calculated by weight. |
| Current peak and trough levels | To make sure the dose is safe and effective. |
| Other nephrotoxic or ototoxic drugs | Using them together increases the danger. |
| Hydration status | Dehydration increases toxicity risk. |

### What to Watch AFTER Giving

- **Nephrotoxicity** (kidney damage) ⚠️
  - Signs: Decreased urine output, increased creatinine, increased BUN
  - Usually reversible if caught early
- **Ototoxicity** (hearing and balance damage) ⚠️
  - Signs: Tinnitus (ringing in the ears), hearing loss, dizziness, loss of balance
  - May be **irreversible** — this is very serious
- **Neuromuscular blockade** (rare but serious)
  - Can cause muscle weakness, breathing problems
  - Higher risk with anesthesia or neuromuscular blocking drugs

### Key Labs

| Lab | Why | When to Check |
|---|---|---|
| **Peak level** | Make sure the drug reaches a level that kills bacteria | Draw **30–60 minutes after** the IV infusion ends |
| **Trough level** | Make sure the drug is not building up to toxic levels | Draw **right before** the next dose |
| BUN and creatinine | Monitor kidneys | Every 2–3 days or more often |
| CBC | Monitor infection | Regularly |
| Hearing tests | Monitor for ototoxicity | Baseline and during treatment |

> ⚠️ **NCLEX loves to ask about peak and trough timing!**
> - **Peak** = highest level = drawn AFTER the dose (30–60 min after IV infusion ends)
> - **Trough** = lowest level = drawn BEFORE the next dose

### Patient Teaching
- ✅ "Tell your nurse right away if you notice ringing in your ears, trouble hearing, or dizziness."
- ✅ "Drink plenty of water to help protect your kidneys (unless on fluid restriction)."
- ✅ "Tell the nurse if you are urinating less than usual."
- ✅ "You will need frequent blood tests to check your drug levels and kidney function."

### Drug Interactions
- **Vancomycin** — increased nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity
- **Loop diuretics** (furosemide) — increased ototoxicity
- **NSAIDs** — increased nephrotoxicity
- **Neuromuscular blocking agents** — increased muscle weakness risk

### Contraindications
- Known aminoglycoside allergy
- Use with extreme caution in kidney disease, hearing impairment, dehydration
- Caution with myasthenia gravis (risk of worsened muscle weakness)

## 5. Fluoroquinolones

### Drugs to Know
- **Ciprofloxacin (Cipro)**
- **Levofloxacin (Levaquin)**

> **Naming tip:** Look for the **-floxacin** ending! That tells you it is a fluoroquinolone.

### What Are They Used For? (Indications)
- Urinary tract infections
- Respiratory infections
- Skin infections
- Bone and joint infections
- GI infections
- Anthrax exposure (ciprofloxacin)

### How Do They Work? (Mechanism — Simple Version)
- They stop bacteria from copying their **DNA**, so the bacteria can't multiply.
- *Think of it this way:* Bacteria need to make copies of their "instruction manual" (DNA) to grow. Fluoroquinolones break the copy machine.

### What to Check BEFORE Giving

| Check This | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Age** | Avoid in children and adolescents when possible (can damage growing cartilage and joints). |
| **Tendon problems history** | Higher risk of tendon rupture, especially in people who have had tendon problems before. |
| Kidney function | Dose may need adjustment. |
| Current medications | Many drug interactions (especially with minerals and antacids). |
| History of seizures or CNS disorders | Fluoroquinolones can cause CNS effects (confusion, seizures). |
| Heart history | QT prolongation risk (can cause dangerous heart rhythms). |

### What to Watch AFTER Giving

- **Tendon pain or rupture** ⚠️ — This has a **BLACK BOX WARNING** (the most serious warning the FDA gives)
  - Most common: Achilles tendon (back of the ankle)
  - Higher risk in: Patients over 60, those taking corticosteroids, kidney/heart/lung transplant recipients
  - If patient reports tendon pain → **STOP the drug** and notify the provider
- **Peripheral neuropathy** ⚠️ — BLACK BOX WARNING
  - Numbness, tingling, burning in hands or feet
  - Can be permanent
- **CNS effects** ⚠️ — BLACK BOX WARNING
  - Dizziness, confusion, hallucinations, seizures
- **Photosensitivity** — Increased risk of severe sunburn
- **GI upset** — Nausea, diarrhea

### Key Labs
- BUN and creatinine (kidney function)
- CBC (monitor infection)
- ECG (if QT prolongation risk)

### Patient Teaching
- ✅ "**Stop the medicine and call your doctor immediately** if you have pain, swelling, or tenderness in any tendon (especially the back of your ankle)."
- ✅ "Do not exercise heavily or run while taking this drug."
- ✅ "**Stay out of the sun** as much as possible. Wear sunscreen (SPF 30+) and protective clothing."
- ✅ "Do not take this drug with **calcium, iron, antacids, or dairy products** at the same time. Take it **2 hours before or 6 hours after** these products." (They block the drug from being absorbed.)
- ✅ "Drink plenty of fluids."
- ✅ "Report any numbness, tingling, or burning in your hands or feet."
- ✅ "Report any confusion, dizziness, or mood changes."

### Drug Interactions
- **Calcium, iron, magnesium, zinc, antacids, dairy** — reduce absorption of the fluoroquinolone (take separately!)
- **Warfarin** — increased bleeding risk
- **Theophylline** — increased theophylline levels (toxicity)
- **NSAIDs** — increased seizure risk
- **QT-prolonging drugs** — increased risk of dangerous heart rhythms

### Contraindications
- Known fluoroquinolone allergy
- Children and adolescents (avoid unless no safer option)
- History of tendon disorders related to fluoroquinolones
- Myasthenia gravis (can worsen muscle weakness)

## 6. Macrolides

### Drugs to Know
- **Azithromycin (Zithromax, "Z-pack")**
- **Erythromycin**

> **Naming tip:** Many macrolides end in **-thromycin** or **-mycin** (azithromycin, clarithromycin, erythromycin).

### What Are They Used For? (Indications)
- Upper and lower respiratory infections (bronchitis, pneumonia)
- Ear infections
- Skin infections
- Sexually transmitted infections (chlamydia — azithromycin is the go-to)
- Atypical pneumonia (Mycoplasma, Legionella)
- Pertussis (whooping cough)

> *Think of it this way:* Macrolides are the "respiratory infection specialists." If a patient has a respiratory infection or is allergic to penicillin, macrolides are often the choice.

### How Do They Work? (Mechanism — Simple Version)
- They stop bacteria from making **proteins**, so bacteria can't grow.
- Similar idea to aminoglycosides, but macrolides are much gentler and safer.

### What to Check BEFORE Giving

| Check This | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Heart history / ECG | QT prolongation risk — can cause dangerous heart rhythms. |
| Liver function (LFTs) | Macrolides are processed by the liver. Can cause liver problems. |
| Current medications | Many drug interactions (CYP450 enzyme system). |
| Allergy history | Ask about macrolide allergies. |

### What to Watch AFTER Giving

- **GI upset** — This is very common! Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain.
  - Erythromycin is especially hard on the stomach.
  - Azithromycin is usually better tolerated.
- **QT prolongation** ⚠️ — Watch for irregular heartbeat, dizziness, fainting.
- **Hepatotoxicity** (liver damage) — Watch for jaundice (yellow skin/eyes), dark urine, right upper quadrant pain.
- **Superinfection** — Watch for oral thrush or C. diff.

### Key Labs
- Liver function tests (AST, ALT, bilirubin)
- ECG (if heart risk factors)
- CBC (monitor infection)

### Patient Teaching
- ✅ "Take azithromycin on an empty stomach for best absorption (1 hour before or 2 hours after eating). But if it upsets your stomach, a small snack is okay."
- ✅ "Finish the full course — even the Z-pack, which is only 5 days."
- ✅ "Report any chest pain, fast heartbeat, or dizziness."
- ✅ "Report any yellowing of the skin or eyes."
- ✅ "Report severe diarrhea."

### Drug Interactions
- **QT-prolonging drugs** — increased risk of dangerous heart rhythms
- **Warfarin** — increased bleeding risk
- **Statins** (cholesterol drugs) — increased risk of muscle damage (rhabdomyolysis)
- **CYP450 interactions** — erythromycin especially inhibits many drug-metabolizing enzymes
- **Digoxin** — macrolides can increase digoxin levels (toxicity risk)

### Contraindications
- Known macrolide allergy
- History of QT prolongation or certain heart arrhythmias
- Severe liver disease (use with caution)

## 7. Metronidazole (Flagyl)

### Drug to Know
- **Metronidazole (Flagyl)** — oral or IV

### What Is It Used For? (Indications)
- **Anaerobic bacterial infections** (infections caused by bacteria that live without oxygen — often found in the gut, pelvis, and abscesses)
- **C. difficile (C. diff) colitis** — one of the main treatments
- Bacterial vaginosis
- Trichomoniasis (a sexually transmitted infection)
- H. pylori infection (part of a combination regimen for stomach ulcers)
- Intra-abdominal infections

### How Does It Work? (Mechanism — Simple Version)
- It damages the **DNA** of anaerobic bacteria and certain parasites, killing them.
- *Think of it this way:* Anaerobic bacteria are like creatures that live in the dark. Metronidazole finds them and destroys their blueprint (DNA).

### What to Check BEFORE Giving

| Check This | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Alcohol use** ⚠️ | Patient MUST avoid alcohol during treatment and for at least 48–72 hours after finishing. |
| Liver function | Metronidazole is processed by the liver. |
| Neurological status | Can cause CNS effects (seizures, neuropathy). |
| Current medications | Drug interactions possible. |
| Pregnancy status | Avoid in first trimester if possible. |

### What to Watch AFTER Giving

- **Disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol** ⚠️ — This is the #1 teaching point!
  - If the patient drinks alcohol while taking metronidazole: severe nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramping, headache, flushing
  - *Think of it this way:* Metronidazole + Alcohol = Misery. The body reacts violently. NO alcohol at all!
- **Metallic taste** — Very common. Patient may say food "tastes like metal."
- **Dark or reddish-brown urine** — This is expected and harmless. But warn the patient so they don't get scared!
- **GI upset** — Nausea, diarrhea
- **Peripheral neuropathy** — Numbness, tingling in hands and feet (with long-term use)
- **Seizures** (rare, with high doses or long-term use)

### Key Labs
- Liver function tests (AST, ALT)
- CBC (monitor infection and blood counts — rare risk of low white blood cells)

### Patient Teaching
- ✅ "**Absolutely NO alcohol** while taking this medicine and for at least **48–72 hours after** your last dose. This includes beer, wine, liquor, and some mouthwashes and cough medicines that contain alcohol."
- ✅ "You may notice a **metallic taste** in your mouth. This is normal and will go away after treatment."
- ✅ "Your **urine may look dark or reddish-brown**. This is normal. Do not worry."
- ✅ "Finish the full course of medication."
- ✅ "Report any numbness or tingling in your hands or feet."

### Drug Interactions
- **Alcohol** ⚠️ — disulfiram-like reaction (see above)
- **Warfarin** — increased bleeding risk
- **Lithium** — increased lithium levels (toxicity risk)
- **Disulfiram** — do not use together (increased CNS toxicity)
- **Phenytoin** — metronidazole can increase phenytoin levels

### Contraindications
- Known metronidazole allergy
- First trimester of pregnancy (if possible to avoid)
- Use with caution in liver disease and neurological conditions

## 8. Antifungals (Brief Overview)

### Drug to Know
- **Fluconazole (Diflucan)**

### What Is It Used For? (Indications)
- **Yeast infections** (Candida) — vaginal, oral (thrush), esophageal, systemic
- Prevention of fungal infections in immunocompromised patients

> *Think of it this way:* Fluconazole is the go-to drug when you think "yeast infection."

### How Does It Work? (Mechanism — Simple Version)
- It damages the **cell membrane** of the fungus, causing it to leak and die.
- *Think of it this way:* Fungi have a special protective "skin" (membrane). Fluconazole pokes holes in it.

### What to Check BEFORE Giving

| Check This | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Liver function** (LFTs) ⚠️ | Fluconazole can cause liver damage (hepatotoxicity). |
| Current medications | Many drug interactions through the **CYP450** enzyme system. |
| Heart history | QT prolongation risk. |
| Allergy history | Ask about antifungal allergies. |

### What to Watch AFTER Giving

- **Hepatotoxicity** (liver damage) — Watch for jaundice (yellow skin/eyes), nausea, right upper belly pain, dark urine
- **QT prolongation** — Watch for irregular heartbeat, dizziness, fainting
- **GI upset** — Nausea, abdominal pain, diarrhea
- **Skin reactions** — Rash (rarely severe, like Stevens-Johnson syndrome)

### Key Labs
- **Liver function tests** (AST, ALT, bilirubin) — check before and during treatment
- ECG (if heart risk factors)

### Patient Teaching
- ✅ "Report any yellowing of the skin or eyes, unusual tiredness, or dark urine."
- ✅ "Tell your doctor about ALL other medications you take — fluconazole interacts with many drugs."
- ✅ "For vaginal yeast infections, one dose is usually enough."
- ✅ "If symptoms do not improve, contact your doctor."

### Drug Interactions (CYP450 — many!)
- **Warfarin** — increased bleeding risk
- **Phenytoin** — increased phenytoin levels
- **Cyclosporine** — increased cyclosporine levels (toxicity)
- **Statins** — increased risk of muscle damage
- **Oral hypoglycemics** — increased risk of low blood sugar
- **QT-prolonging drugs** — increased risk of dangerous heart rhythms

### Contraindications
- Known fluconazole allergy
- Severe liver disease (use with caution)
- Co-administration with certain QT-prolonging drugs

## 9. Key Concepts Across ALL Anti-Infectives

> These concepts apply to EVERY anti-infective medication. The NCLEX loves to test these!

### 🔴 Allergies — Always Check First!

- **ALWAYS ask about drug allergies before giving ANY antibiotic.**
- Document the type of reaction (rash? hives? anaphylaxis?).
- Know the signs of **anaphylaxis**:
  - Throat swelling (airway compromise)
  - Wheezing, difficulty breathing
  - Hypotension (low blood pressure)
  - Tachycardia (fast heart rate)
  - Hives, widespread rash
- **Anaphylaxis action steps:**
  1. **Stop the drug immediately**
  2. Maintain the airway
  3. Call for help (Rapid Response or Code)
  4. Give **epinephrine** (first-line treatment)
  5. Prepare for IV fluids, oxygen, and additional medications

### 🔴 Superinfection and C. difficile (C. diff)

- Antibiotics kill bad bacteria BUT also kill **good bacteria** that normally live in your gut, skin, and mouth.
- When good bacteria are gone, harmful organisms can take over. This is called a **superinfection**.
- **C. difficile (C. diff)** is the most dangerous superinfection:
  - Caused by a toxin-producing bacteria that overgrows in the gut
  - Signs: Watery diarrhea (often foul-smelling), abdominal pain, fever
  - Can be life-threatening
  - Treatment: Stop the offending antibiotic if possible. Oral **vancomycin** or **metronidazole** for C. diff.
  - **Strict contact precautions** (gown and gloves, hand washing with soap and water — alcohol-based sanitizer does NOT kill C. diff spores!)
- Other superinfections: Oral thrush (white patches in mouth), vaginal yeast infection

### 🔴 Culture Before Treatment

- **Whenever possible, get cultures (blood, urine, wound, sputum) BEFORE starting antibiotics.**
- Why? Antibiotics can kill bacteria before the lab can identify them. If you give the antibiotic first, the culture may come back negative even though there is an infection.
- *Think of it this way:* Culture first, then treat. Get the evidence before you start fighting.
- **But:** Do NOT delay treatment in a critically ill patient just to wait for cultures. If the patient is septic, start antibiotics immediately after cultures are drawn.

### 🔴 Finish the Full Course

- Teach EVERY patient to take ALL of their antibiotic doses, even if they feel better.
- Stopping early can:
  - Allow surviving bacteria to come back
  - Lead to **antibiotic resistance** (bacteria learn to fight the drug)
- *Think of it this way:* If you only clean half the mess, it comes back worse.

### 🔴 Antibiotic Stewardship

- **Use the right drug, for the right bug, for the right amount of time.**
- Do not use antibiotics for viral infections (colds, flu, most sore throats).
- Using antibiotics when they are not needed contributes to resistance.
- Nurses play a key role in educating patients and questioning inappropriate antibiotic orders.
