## Flashcard 1 — Recall

**Q: What lab do you check to monitor vancomycin levels, and WHEN do you draw it?**

A. CBC with differential, drawn every morning  
B. Vancomycin trough level, drawn right before the next dose  
C. Vancomycin peak level, drawn 2 hours after the dose  
D. Serum creatinine, drawn at any time

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: B**

**A:** You check the **vancomycin trough level**.

Draw it **right before the next dose** (usually 30 minutes before).

The trough shows the **lowest** amount of drug in the blood. This helps make sure the level is not too high (toxic) and not too low (not effective).

> *Think of it this way:* "Trough" sounds like "low" — it is the low point, right before you give more drug.

</details>

## Flashcard 2 — Safety

**Q: What is Red Man Syndrome? Is it a true allergy?**

A. A true IgE-mediated allergic reaction requiring permanent discontinuation of vancomycin  
B. An anaphylactic reaction caused by vancomycin overdose that requires epinephrine  
C. A histamine reaction from infusing vancomycin too fast; it is not a true allergy  
D. A sign of nephrotoxicity indicating the vancomycin trough level is too high

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: C**

**A:** Red Man Syndrome happens when **vancomycin IV is infused too fast**.

Signs:
- Flushing (redness) of the face, neck, and upper body
- Rash and itching
- Sometimes low blood pressure

**It is NOT a true allergy.** It is a histamine reaction from the fast infusion.

What to do:
- **Slow the infusion rate** (do not stop it completely)
- You can give an **antihistamine** (like diphenhydramine)

> *Think of it this way:* Red Man = "Rate problem." Slow the rate and the patient gets better.

</details>

## Flashcard 3 — Compare/Contrast

**Q: Which of the following correctly describes how lab monitoring differs between aminoglycosides and vancomycin?**

A. Aminoglycosides require both peak and trough levels; vancomycin mainly requires trough only  
B. Vancomycin requires both peak and trough levels; aminoglycosides mainly require trough only  
C. Both drugs require only trough levels drawn right before the next dose  
D. Both drugs require only peak levels drawn 30 minutes after the infusion ends

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: A**

**A:**

| | Aminoglycosides | Vancomycin |
|---|---|---|
| **Drug levels** | Both **peak AND trough** | Mainly **trough** |
| **Peak timing** | 30–60 min after IV infusion ends | Not routinely drawn |
| **Trough timing** | Right before next dose | Right before next dose |
| **Kidney labs** | BUN, creatinine | BUN, creatinine |
| **Hearing** | Monitor both | Monitor both |

The big difference: Aminoglycosides need **peak AND trough**. Vancomycin mainly needs **trough**.

Both drugs are nephrotoxic and ototoxic, and using them **together** makes the danger even worse.

</details>

**Q: Your patient is taking metronidazole (Flagyl). What is the most important thing to teach them about this drug?**

A. Take the medication with a high-fat meal to improve absorption

<details>

**A:** **No alcohol!** 🚫🍷

The patient must avoid **all alcohol** while taking metronidazole AND for **48–72 hours after** the last dose.

If they drink alcohol, they will have a **disulfiram-like reaction**:
- Severe nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal cramps
- Headache
- Flushing

Remind them that some **mouthwashes and cough medicines** also contain alcohol!

> *Think of it this way:* "Flagyl + alcohol = feeling awful." Just say no to alcohol during treatment.

## Flashcard 5 — Safety

**Q: Which of the following is correct about the most serious complication of C. difficile and the appropriate infection control measure?**

A. The most serious complication is septic shock; use alcohol-based hand sanitizer after contact  
B. The most serious complication is toxic megacolon; wash hands with soap and water, not alcohol-based sanitizer  
C. The most serious complication is bowel perforation; standard precautions with an N95 mask are required  
D. The most serious complication is dehydration; use droplet precautions with a surgical mask

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: B**

**A:** C. diff can cause **toxic megacolon** (life-threatening swelling of the colon) and even death in severe cases.

To protect other patients:
- Place the patient on **contact precautions** (gown and gloves)
- Wash hands with **soap and water** — **NOT alcohol-based hand sanitizer** (alcohol does not kill C. diff spores!)
- Thorough environmental cleaning with bleach-based products
- Dedicate equipment to the patient if possible

> *Think of it this way:* C. diff spores are tough little survivors. You need good old soap and water and bleach to beat them.

</details>

## Flashcard 6 — Recall

**Q: Which of the following correctly lists signs of ototoxicity and the two drug classes most likely to cause it?**

A. Tinnitus, hearing loss, and dizziness; caused by aminoglycosides and vancomycin  
B. Blurred vision, eye pain, and photophobia; caused by fluoroquinolones and macrolides  
C. Nausea, vomiting, and metallic taste; caused by metronidazole and penicillins  
D. Headache, confusion, and seizures; caused by carbapenems and cephalosporins

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: A**

**A:** Signs of ototoxicity (ear/hearing damage):
- **Tinnitus** (ringing in the ears)
- **Hearing loss**
- **Dizziness / vertigo** (loss of balance)

The two drug classes most associated with ototoxicity:
1. **Aminoglycosides** (gentamicin, tobramycin)
2. **Vancomycin**

⚠️ Using these two together increases the risk even more.

> *Think of it this way:* "Oto" means "ear." If the patient says "my ears are ringing," think ototoxicity and report it right away.

## Flashcard 7 — Patient Teaching

**Q: Your patient is prescribed ciprofloxacin (a fluoroquinolone). What teaching about tendon safety is essential?**

A. Continue exercising as normal but apply ice to tendons after each workout  
B. Take a calcium supplement along with the antibiotic to strengthen tendons  
C. Stop the drug and call the provider immediately if tendon pain, swelling, or tenderness occurs  
D. Report tendon discomfort at the next scheduled clinic visit

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: C**

**A:** Teach the patient:
- ⚠️ **Stop the medicine and call the doctor immediately** if they feel any pain, swelling, or tenderness in a tendon — especially the **Achilles tendon** (back of the ankle).
- **Avoid heavy exercise, running, or jumping** while taking this drug.
- Tendon rupture can happen **during treatment or even weeks after stopping**.

Higher risk patients:
- Over 60 years old
- Taking corticosteroids
- Organ transplant recipients

This is a **BLACK BOX WARNING** — the FDA's most serious safety warning.

> *Think of it this way:* "-floxacin = Flex (tendon) danger." Protect those tendons!

</details>

## Flashcard 8 — Application

**Q: Your patient is receiving IV vancomycin and tells you, "My face and neck feel really hot and red." What do you do FIRST?**

A. Slow the IV infusion rate  
B. Stop the infusion and administer epinephrine  
C. Increase the IV fluid rate to dilute the drug  
D. Administer acetaminophen for the flushing

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: A**

**A:** This sounds like **Red Man Syndrome**.

Your FIRST action: **Slow the IV infusion rate.**

Then:
- Assess the patient further (check vital signs, look at the rash)
- Notify the provider
- An antihistamine (like diphenhydramine) may be given

Do NOT stop the infusion completely (unless the patient is having a true anaphylactic reaction with airway compromise or severe hypotension).

> *Think of it this way:* Red Man = Rate problem. Slow the rate first. It is not a true allergy.

</details>

## Flashcard 9 — Compare/Contrast

**Q: Which of the following correctly describes the key difference between a mild penicillin allergy and anaphylaxis?**

A. A mild rash requires epinephrine; anaphylaxis requires only an antihistamine  
B. Both reactions are equally dangerous and require stopping all beta-lactam antibiotics permanently  
C. Anaphylaxis causes only a localized rash; a mild allergy causes throat swelling and hypotension  
D. A mild rash is not life-threatening and cephalosporins may be given cautiously; anaphylaxis is life-threatening and cephalosporins should generally be avoided

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: D**

**A:**

| Mild Allergy (Rash) | Anaphylaxis |
|---|---|
| Rash, itching, hives | Throat swelling, wheezing, severe breathing difficulty |
| Localized or mild symptoms | Hypotension (low blood pressure), tachycardia |
| Not immediately life-threatening | **Life-threatening emergency** |
| May still receive cephalosporins (with caution) | Should generally **NOT** receive cephalosporins |

**Why it matters:**
- If a patient had a **mild rash** from penicillin, a cephalosporin might be given with careful monitoring.
- If a patient had **anaphylaxis** from penicillin, cephalosporins should be avoided (or given only with extreme caution).

Always ask: "What happened when you took penicillin?" — not just "Are you allergic?"

</details>

## Flashcard 10 — Safety

**Q: Your patient just started a new antibiotic 3 days ago. Now they have watery, foul-smelling diarrhea and abdominal cramping. What should you suspect?**

A. A normal side effect of antibiotic therapy that will resolve on its own  
B. Clostridioides difficile (C. diff) infection  
C. Lactose intolerance triggered by the antibiotic  
D. An allergic reaction to the antibiotic causing gastrointestinal distress

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: B**

**A:** Suspect **C. difficile (C. diff) infection**.

Antibiotics kill good bacteria in the gut, allowing C. diff to overgrow and produce toxins.

Key signs:
- Watery diarrhea (often foul-smelling)
- Abdominal pain and cramping
- Fever
- Can happen during or after antibiotic therapy

Actions:
- Notify the provider
- Collect a stool sample for C. diff toxin testing
- Implement **contact precautions**
- The offending antibiotic may be stopped
- Treatment: oral vancomycin or metronidazole

</details>

## Flashcard 11 — Priority

**Q: A patient receiving IV penicillin suddenly develops throat swelling, wheezing, and a blood pressure of 80/50. What is your FIRST action?**

A. Administer diphenhydramine (Benadryl) IV push  
B. Slow the IV penicillin infusion rate and monitor closely  
C. Stop the IV penicillin infusion immediately  
D. Place the patient in Trendelenburg position and increase IV fluids

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: C**

**A:** This is **anaphylaxis** — a life-threatening emergency!

**FIRST action: STOP the IV penicillin infusion immediately.**

Then:
1. Call for help (Rapid Response / Code)
2. Maintain the airway (position patient flat, elevate legs if hypotensive)
3. Administer **epinephrine** (the #1 drug for anaphylaxis)
4. Prepare for oxygen, IV fluids, and emergency medications
5. Monitor vital signs continuously

> *Think of it this way:* When there is anaphylaxis (a true systemic allergic reaction with airway compromise), STOP the drug first, then save the life. Epinephrine is the hero drug.

</details>

## Flashcard 12 — Recall

**Q: Why should you get cultures BEFORE starting antibiotics?**

A. Antibiotics can kill bacteria before the lab can identify them, causing a false-negative result  
B. Culture specimens cannot be collected while an IV antibiotic is infusing  
C. Antibiotics change the appearance of bacteria under the microscope, making identification impossible  
D. The lab requires a drug-free blood sample to calibrate the sensitivity testing equipment

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: A**

**A:** Because antibiotics can **kill the bacteria before the lab can identify them**.

If you give the antibiotic first, the culture may come back negative — even though there really was an infection. Then you don't know which specific bug is causing the problem.

Getting cultures first helps identify:
- Which bacteria is causing the infection
- Which antibiotics will work best (sensitivity testing)

**Exception:** In a critically ill patient (like sepsis), draw cultures quickly and then **start antibiotics right away**. Do not delay treatment to wait for results.

> *Think of it this way:* "Culture first, treat second — unless the patient is crashing."

</details>

## Flashcard 14 — Application

**Q: The doctor orders cephalexin for your patient. The patient says, "I had a rash when I took penicillin once." What should you do?**

A. Refuse to give the medication because all beta-lactam antibiotics are contraindicated  
B. Administer the cephalexin without any special precautions since the allergy was mild  
C. Give a test dose of penicillin first to see if the patient still reacts  
D. Notify the provider of the penicillin allergy history and monitor the patient closely after the first dose

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: D**

**A:** Cephalosporins (like cephalexin) are "cousins" of penicillins, so there is a **cross-allergy risk** (about 1–2%).

Since the patient had a **mild reaction** (rash, not anaphylaxis):
1. **Document** the penicillin allergy and the type of reaction
2. **Notify the provider** about the penicillin allergy history
3. The provider may decide to proceed with cephalexin with **careful monitoring**
4. Have **emergency equipment** available (epinephrine, crash cart)
5. **Monitor the patient closely** after the first dose

If the patient had **anaphylaxis** to penicillin, cephalosporins would generally be **avoided**.

> *Think of it this way:* Rash = proceed with caution. Anaphylaxis = generally avoid.

</details>

## Flashcard 16 — Safety

**Q: Why should ceftriaxone NOT be mixed with calcium-containing solutions in neonates?**

A. Ceftriaxone can form dangerous crystals with calcium in neonates, causing organ damage or death  
B. Calcium inactivates ceftriaxone, making the antibiotic ineffective against infection  
C. Ceftriaxone increases calcium absorption, leading to dangerously high calcium levels  
D. Mixing them causes an allergic reaction in neonates due to their immature immune systems

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: A**

**A:** In neonates (newborns), ceftriaxone can combine with calcium to form **crystals** (precipitates) in the blood.

These crystals can lodge in:
- Lungs
- Kidneys
- Blood vessels

This can cause **organ damage and even death**.

This is why ceftriaxone should **NEVER** be mixed with calcium-containing IV solutions (like Lactated Ringer's or TPN with calcium) in neonates.

> *Think of it this way:* "Ceftriaxone + calcium in a baby = crystals = crisis." Keep them apart in neonates!

</details>

## Flashcard 17 — Compare/Contrast

**Q: Which of the following correctly explains why oral vancomycin is used for C. diff instead of IV vancomycin?**

A. IV vancomycin is too toxic for the kidneys to use in C. diff patients  
B. Oral vancomycin stays in the gut where C. diff lives; IV vancomycin enters the bloodstream and does not reach the gut effectively  
C. Oral vancomycin is absorbed faster into the bloodstream than IV vancomycin  
D. IV vancomycin kills the normal gut flora needed to fight C. diff

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: B**

**A:**

| Oral Vancomycin | IV Vancomycin |
|---|---|
| Works in the **gut** (GI tract) | Works in the **bloodstream** and body tissues |
| Used for **C. diff** | Used for **MRSA** and serious systemic infections |
| NOT absorbed into the blood | IS absorbed into the blood |

For C. diff, you need the drug **in the gut** where the infection is. That is why **oral** vancomycin is used.

IV vancomycin does NOT reach the gut in effective amounts, so it does NOT work for C. diff.

> *Think of it this way:* C. diff lives in the belly → oral vancomycin goes to the belly. IV vancomycin goes to the blood.

</details>

## Flashcard 18 — Patient Teaching

**Q: Why is it important to teach patients to finish the FULL course of antibiotics?**

A. Completing the full course prevents the medication from causing allergic reactions later  
B. Leftover pills lose their potency and must be taken to avoid wasting medication  
C. Stopping early allows surviving resistant bacteria to regrow and cause a worse, harder-to-treat infection  
D. The full course is needed to prevent the antibiotic from accumulating to toxic levels in the body

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: C**

**A:** If patients stop antibiotics early (because they "feel better"):
- **Surviving bacteria** that were not completely killed can **come back**
- These survivors may become **antibiotic-resistant** — harder to kill next time
- The infection can **return** and be worse than before

**Teach the patient:**
"Even if you feel 100% better, take every single pill. The bacteria that are left are the strongest ones — you need the full course to finish them off."

> *Think of it this way:* "Feeling better ≠ infection gone." The strongest bacteria are the last to die. Finish them off!

</details>

## Flashcard 19 — Application

**Q: Your patient is on gentamicin. The trough level comes back high. What does this mean and what should you do?**

A. The drug is not being cleared fast enough, increasing toxicity risk; hold the dose and notify the provider  
B. The drug level is too low to be effective; give the next dose early  
C. The kidneys are clearing the drug too quickly; increase the dose  
D. This is an expected finding; continue the medication as scheduled

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: A**

**A:** A **high trough level** means the drug is **not being cleared** from the body fast enough. The level is too high before the next dose.

This increases the risk of **toxicity**:
- **Nephrotoxicity** (kidney damage)
- **Ototoxicity** (hearing damage)

What to do:
1. **Hold the next dose** (do not give it)
2. **Notify the provider** immediately
3. Check **kidney function** (BUN, creatinine)
4. Assess for signs of toxicity (hearing changes, decreased urine output)
5. The provider may adjust the dose or extend the dosing interval

> *Think of it this way:* High trough = too much drug hanging around = danger. Hold and report.

</details>

## Flashcard 20 — Recall

**Q: What does "antibiotic stewardship" mean?**

A. Prescribing the strongest antibiotic available for every infection to ensure it is eliminated  
B. Rotating antibiotics weekly to prevent bacteria from developing resistance  
C. Using antibiotics for both bacterial and viral infections to prevent complications  
D. Using the right antibiotic, for the right bacteria, for the right duration to prevent resistance

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: D**

**A:** Antibiotic stewardship means using antibiotics **wisely and responsibly**.

The key idea: **The right drug, for the right bug, for the right amount of time.**

This includes:
- Not using antibiotics for viral infections (colds, flu)
- Using narrow-spectrum antibiotics when possible (instead of broad-spectrum)
- Following evidence-based guidelines for antibiotic selection
- Finishing the full course (no early stopping)
- Getting cultures to target the right bacteria

**Why it matters:** Overuse and misuse of antibiotics leads to **antibiotic resistance** — "superbugs" that are very hard to treat.

Nurses play a big role in stewardship by educating patients and questioning inappropriate orders.

</details>

## Flashcard 22 — Application

**Q: You are about to give the first dose of IV ampicillin. The provider has also ordered blood cultures. Which do you do first?**

A. Give the ampicillin first, then draw blood cultures 30 minutes later  
B. Draw blood cultures and give ampicillin at the same time through different IV sites

> *Think of it this way:* "Culture first, then treat." Get the evidence before you fight the infection.

## Flashcard 26 — Safety

**Q: Before giving ceftriaxone (a cephalosporin), you ask the patient about penicillin allergies. Why?**

A. Cephalosporins are converted into penicillin inside the body  
B. Patients with penicillin allergies always need a higher dose of cephalosporins  
C. Cephalosporins and penicillins share a beta-lactam ring structure, creating a cross-sensitivity risk  
D. The penicillin allergy history determines whether the ceftriaxone should be given orally or IV

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: C**

**A:** Cephalosporins and penicillins are **chemical cousins** — they both have a structure called a beta-lactam ring.

Because of this, a patient who is allergic to penicillin may also react to a cephalosporin. This is called **cross-sensitivity** (about 1–2% risk).

Before giving ceftriaxone, always ask:
- "Have you ever had a reaction to penicillin or amoxicillin?"
- "What happened?" (rash? breathing problems?)

If the patient had **anaphylaxis** to penicillin, cephalosporins are generally **avoided**. If they had a mild rash, the provider may still order it with close monitoring.

> *Think of it this way:* Penicillins and cephalosporins are in the same family. Always check for family allergies before giving a family member!

</details>

C. Food poisoning; obtain a dietary history and hold all medications  
D. Lactose intolerance; switch to a lactose-free diet and continue the antibiotic

> *Think of it this way:* Antibiotics kill the good guys along with the bad guys. When the good guys are gone, fungus throws a party. That is thrush.

</details>

## Flashcard 50 — Compare/Contrast

**Q: Which of the following correctly describes the difference between bactericidal and bacteriostatic antibiotics?**

A. Bactericidal drugs slow bacterial growth; bacteriostatic drugs kill bacteria outright  
B. Bactericidal drugs work only on gram-positive bacteria; bacteriostatic drugs work only on gram-negative bacteria  
C. Bactericidal drugs kill bacteria directly; bacteriostatic drugs stop bacteria from multiplying and rely on the immune system to finish the job  
D. Both terms mean the same thing but are used for different drug classes

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: C**

**A:** This is simpler than it sounds:

| | Bactericidal | Bacteriostatic |
|---|---|---|
| **What it does** | **Kills** the bacteria | **Stops** bacteria from growing/multiplying |
| **Who finishes the job?** | The drug kills them | The **immune system** must kill them |
| **Examples** | Penicillins, cephalosporins, vancomycin, aminoglycosides | Tetracyclines, macrolides (like erythromycin) |

**Why it matters:**
- In a patient with a **weak immune system** (like chemotherapy, HIV, transplant), a **bactericidal** drug is usually preferred because their immune system may not be strong enough to finish the job.
- In a patient with a **healthy immune system**, a bacteriostatic drug may be enough — the drug slows the bacteria down, and the immune system cleans up.

> *Think of it this way:* Bactericidal = "cidal" = kills (like homicide). Bacteriostatic = "static" = stops (like a statue, standing still).

</details>

## Flashcard 52 — Application

**Q: A patient is allergic to penicillin (they had anaphylaxis). The provider orders imipenem (a carbapenem). Should you question this order?**

A. Yes, because imipenem is a beta-lactam like penicillin, and cross-reactivity is possible with an anaphylaxis history  
B. No, because carbapenems are a completely different drug class with no relation to penicillins  
C. Yes, but only because imipenem requires cilastatin to be given with it  
D. No, because cross-reactivity only applies to cephalosporins, not carbapenems

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: A**

**A:** **Yes — you should clarify this with the provider.**

Here is why: Imipenem is a **carbapenem**, and carbapenems are in the same **beta-lactam family** as penicillins and cephalosporins. They share a similar chemical structure.

In a patient with a history of **anaphylaxis** to penicillin, there is a risk of **cross-reactivity** with carbapenems (though the risk is low — about 1%).

Your role as the nurse:
1. **Do not give the drug yet**
2. **Call the provider** and say: "I want to verify this order — the patient has a history of anaphylaxis to penicillin, and imipenem is a beta-lactam."
3. The provider may choose to proceed with monitoring, do allergy testing, or pick a different drug class (like a fluoroquinolone or aztreonam)

> *Think of it this way:* Penicillins, cephalosporins, and carbapenems are all beta-lactam cousins. Anaphylaxis to one = question giving any of them.

</details>

## Flashcard 53 — Safety

**Q: Which of the following is correct about C. difficile infection, its relationship to antibiotics, and the required precautions?**

A. C. diff is a virus that thrives after antibiotic use; airborne precautions and N95 masks are required  
B. C. diff is a fungal overgrowth treated with antifungals; standard precautions are sufficient  
C. C. diff occurs when antibiotics kill bacteria in the lungs; droplet precautions with alcohol-based hand sanitizer are needed  
D. C. diff is a bacterial superinfection from antibiotic disruption of gut flora; contact precautions with soap and water hand hygiene are required

<details>
<summary>Flip for Answer ✨</summary>

**Correct answer: D**

**A:** Let's break this into three parts:

**What is C. diff?**
Clostridioides difficile (C. diff) is a bacterium that produces toxins in the colon. It causes severe watery diarrhea, cramping, fever, and in bad cases, **toxic megacolon** (life-threatening).

**How does it relate to antibiotics?**
Antibiotics kill the normal "good" bacteria in the gut. When the good bacteria are gone, C. diff takes over and multiplies. It is a **superinfection** — an infection caused by the treatment of another infection.

Common antibiotic culprits: clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, broad-spectrum penicillins, cephalosporins.

**What precautions are needed?**
- 🧤 **Contact precautions** — gown and gloves every time you enter the room
- 🧼 **Soap and water hand washing** — alcohol-based sanitizer does NOT kill C. diff spores
- 🧴 **Bleach-based cleaning** for all surfaces and equipment
- 🚪 **Private room** if possible
- 🩺 **Dedicated equipment** (stethoscope, thermometer) for that patient only

> *Think of it this way:* Antibiotics wipe out the good guys → C. diff moves in. Soap, water, and bleach are your best weapons. Alcohol gel does NOT work here!

</details>
