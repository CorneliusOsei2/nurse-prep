## Question 1 — Red Man Syndrome Management

**A nurse is administering IV vancomycin to a patient. Thirty minutes into the infusion, the patient develops flushing and redness across the face, neck, and chest. The patient says, "I feel itchy all over." What should the nurse do FIRST?**

A. Stop the vancomycin infusion immediately and document an allergy
B. Administer epinephrine intramuscularly
C. Slow the IV infusion rate
D. Notify the rapid response team

**Correct Answer: C**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

Let's go through each choice:

- **A. Stop the vancomycin infusion immediately and document an allergy** — This would be correct if the patient was having **anaphylaxis** (throat swelling, wheezing, severe hypotension). But Red Man Syndrome is NOT a true allergy. It is caused by the infusion being too fast. Stopping the drug and documenting an allergy could prevent the patient from getting vancomycin in the future when they really need it.

- **B. Administer epinephrine intramuscularly** — Epinephrine is for **anaphylaxis**. The patient has flushing and itching but no airway compromise or hemodynamic instability. Giving epinephrine is not appropriate here.

- **C. Slow the IV infusion rate** ✅ — This is Red Man Syndrome! The flushing and itching are caused by histamine release from a too-fast infusion. **Slowing the rate** is the first and most important action. An antihistamine (diphenhydramine) can also be given.

- **D. Notify the rapid response team** — This patient is uncomfortable but not in danger. The nurse can manage this independently by slowing the rate. A rapid response is for emergencies.

> **Test-taking tip:** When you see "vancomycin + flushing/redness + during infusion," think **Red Man Syndrome = slow the rate**. The NCLEX wants you to know this is NOT anaphylaxis.

</details>

## Question 2 — Penicillin Allergy Assessment Before Cephalosporin

**A provider orders ceftriaxone (a cephalosporin) for a patient with pneumonia. The nurse reviews the chart and sees the patient has a documented penicillin allergy. Which question is MOST important for the nurse to ask the patient?**

A. "When was the last time you took penicillin?"
B. "What happened when you took penicillin?"
C. "Do you take any other antibiotics at home?"
D. "Have you ever been hospitalized for pneumonia before?"

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. "When was the last time you took penicillin?"** — This is helpful background information, but it does not tell you the most critical thing — what TYPE of reaction the patient had.

- **B. "What happened when you took penicillin?"** ✅ — This is the MOST important question. You need to know if the reaction was **mild** (rash, hives) or **severe** (anaphylaxis — throat swelling, breathing difficulty). If the patient had anaphylaxis, cephalosporins should generally be **avoided** because of cross-allergy risk. If it was a mild rash, the cephalosporin may be given with careful monitoring.

- **C. "Do you take any other antibiotics at home?"** — Good to know for the overall medication history, but not the priority here.

- **D. "Have you ever been hospitalized for pneumonia before?"** — Not directly relevant to the allergy safety concern.

> **Test-taking tip:** With drug allergies, always find out the **type and severity** of the reaction. "What happened?" gives you the information you need to make a safe decision.

</details>

## Question 3 — Aminoglycoside Trough Timing

**A patient is receiving gentamicin IV every 8 hours. The next dose is scheduled for 0800. When should the nurse draw the trough level?**

A. 0600 (2 hours before the dose)
B. 0730 (30 minutes before the dose)
C. 0830 (30 minutes after the dose)
D. 0900 (1 hour after the infusion ends)

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. 0600 (2 hours before the dose)** — This is too early. The standard timing for a trough draw is within 30 minutes before the next dose.

- **B. 0730 (30 minutes before the dose)** ✅ — The trough level is drawn **right before the next dose** — typically within 30 minutes before. This captures the **lowest** drug level in the blood. If this level is too high, the patient is at risk for toxicity (nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity).

- **C. 0830 (30 minutes after the dose)** — This would be too soon after the dose begins. This is not a trough or a peak.

- **D. 0900 (1 hour after the infusion ends)** — This timing would be appropriate for a **peak** level (highest drug level), not a trough.

> **Test-taking tip:** Remember: **"Trough = BEFORE."** The trough is the lowest point, so you draw it right before the next dose. **"Peak = AFTER."** The peak is the highest point, drawn 30–60 minutes after the infusion ends.

</details>

## Question 4 — C. diff Recognition and Management

**A patient has been taking clindamycin for 5 days. The patient now reports frequent watery, foul-smelling diarrhea and abdominal cramping. The patient has a temperature of 101.2°F (38.4°C). What should the nurse do FIRST?**

A. Administer loperamide (Imodium) to stop the diarrhea
B. Notify the provider and request a stool specimen for C. diff testing
C. Increase the patient's fluid intake
D. Document the findings and continue monitoring

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Administer loperamide (Imodium) to stop the diarrhea** — ⚠️ **Do NOT give anti-diarrheal medications** when C. diff is suspected! Stopping the diarrhea traps the toxins inside the colon, which makes the infection WORSE and can lead to toxic megacolon.

- **B. Notify the provider and request a stool specimen for C. diff testing** ✅ — This patient has classic signs of **C. difficile infection** (watery diarrhea, foul smell, abdominal pain, fever after antibiotic use). The nurse should notify the provider so the offending antibiotic can be stopped and C. diff testing can be ordered. Contact precautions should also be started.

- **C. Increase the patient's fluid intake** — This is a good supportive measure (the patient is losing fluids), but it is NOT the first priority. You need to address the likely cause — C. diff.

- **D. Document the findings and continue monitoring** — This is too passive! These symptoms strongly suggest C. diff, which needs immediate action, not just watching.

> **Test-taking tip:** Antibiotic use + watery diarrhea + fever = think **C. diff** until proven otherwise. Never give anti-diarrheal drugs for suspected C. diff. Notify the provider!

</details>

## Question 5 — Fluoroquinolone Tendon Rupture Teaching

**A nurse is providing discharge teaching to a 68-year-old patient who is being sent home with levofloxacin. The patient also takes prednisone daily. Which statement by the patient indicates a NEED for more teaching?**

A. "I should avoid direct sunlight while taking this medicine."
B. "I will stop the medicine and call my doctor if my ankle becomes painful or swollen."
C. "I can continue my daily jogging routine while taking this antibiotic."
D. "I should take this medicine 2 hours before my calcium supplement."

**Correct Answer: C**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. "I should avoid direct sunlight while taking this medicine."** — Correct understanding! Fluoroquinolones cause photosensitivity. This does NOT need more teaching.

- **B. "I will stop the medicine and call my doctor if my ankle becomes painful or swollen."** — Correct understanding! Tendon pain could indicate impending tendon rupture. This does NOT need more teaching.

- **C. "I can continue my daily jogging routine while taking this antibiotic."** ✅ — This needs MORE teaching! This patient is at **high risk** for tendon rupture because they are: (1) over 60 years old, (2) taking a corticosteroid (prednisone), AND (3) taking a fluoroquinolone. **Vigorous exercise (especially running) should be avoided** during treatment.

- **D. "I should take this medicine 2 hours before my calcium supplement."** — Correct understanding! Calcium reduces fluoroquinolone absorption. This does NOT need more teaching.

> **Test-taking tip:** "Need for more teaching" questions = look for the WRONG statement. The wrong patient statement is the correct answer. This patient has THREE risk factors for tendon rupture — age, steroid use, and the fluoroquinolone itself. Jogging is dangerous.

</details>

## Question 6 — Culture Before Antibiotics

**A patient in the emergency department is diagnosed with sepsis. The provider orders blood cultures and IV antibiotics. Which action should the nurse take FIRST?**

A. Administer the IV antibiotics immediately
B. Draw the blood cultures, then administer the IV antibiotics right after
C. Wait for the culture results before giving the antibiotics
D. Check with the pharmacy if the antibiotic is available

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Administer the IV antibiotics immediately** — In sepsis, speed matters. However, the best practice is to draw cultures FIRST (quickly!) so you can identify the bacteria. If you give the antibiotic first, the culture may be falsely negative.

- **B. Draw the blood cultures, then administer the IV antibiotics right after** ✅ — This is the correct sequence. In sepsis, time is critical. Draw the cultures as quickly as possible (do not wait for results!) and then give the antibiotics **immediately** afterward. Do not delay antibiotics in sepsis.

- **C. Wait for the culture results before giving the antibiotics** — ⚠️ **Never delay antibiotics in sepsis** to wait for culture results. Culture results can take 24–72 hours. The patient could die waiting. Draw cultures → give antibiotics → review results later and adjust.

- **D. Check with the pharmacy if the antibiotic is available** — While practical, this is not the first nursing action. Drawing cultures is the priority.

> **Test-taking tip:** The order is: **Culture → Treat → Adjust later.** In sepsis, draw cultures quickly and give antibiotics within 1 hour. Every hour of delay increases mortality.

</details>

## Question 7 — Anaphylaxis During Antibiotic Infusion

**A patient receiving IV ampicillin suddenly develops facial swelling, stridor (noisy breathing), widespread hives, and a blood pressure of 78/42 mmHg. Which action should the nurse take FIRST?**

A. Slow the IV infusion rate
B. Stop the ampicillin infusion
C. Administer diphenhydramine (Benadryl) IV
D. Place the patient in high Fowler's position

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Slow the IV infusion rate** — This would be appropriate for **Red Man Syndrome** with vancomycin, but this patient is having **anaphylaxis** (facial swelling, stridor, hypotension). You need to STOP the drug, not just slow it.

- **B. Stop the ampicillin infusion** ✅ — This patient is in **anaphylactic shock**. The FIRST action is to **stop the offending drug**. You must stop the source of the allergic reaction immediately. Then call for help and give epinephrine.

- **C. Administer diphenhydramine (Benadryl) IV** — Antihistamines are part of anaphylaxis treatment but are NOT the first-line drug. **Epinephrine** is the first-line treatment. Also, you must stop the drug before giving other medications.

- **D. Place the patient in high Fowler's position** — With severe hypotension (78/42), you would NOT put the patient upright. This could worsen the low blood pressure. The patient should be positioned **flat with legs elevated** (to improve venous return).

> **Test-taking tip:** In anaphylaxis, the order is: **STOP the drug → Call for help → Epinephrine → Airway management → IV fluids.** Epinephrine is the hero drug, but stopping the cause comes first.

</details>

## Question 8 — Metronidazole + Alcohol Teaching

**A nurse is discharging a patient with a prescription for metronidazole (Flagyl). Which statement by the patient shows correct understanding of the discharge instructions?**

A. "I can have a glass of wine with dinner as long as I take the pill in the morning."
B. "I should avoid alcohol while taking this medicine and for 3 days after I finish."
C. "I only need to avoid hard liquor. Beer and wine are fine."
D. "Once I feel better, I can stop the medicine and resume drinking."

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. "I can have a glass of wine with dinner as long as I take the pill in the morning."** — Incorrect! **No alcohol at all** during metronidazole treatment, regardless of when the pill is taken. Even small amounts of alcohol can cause the disulfiram-like reaction (severe nausea, vomiting, cramping).

- **B. "I should avoid alcohol while taking this medicine and for 3 days after I finish."** ✅ — Correct! Patients must avoid ALL alcohol during treatment and for at least **48–72 hours (about 3 days)** after the last dose. This includes beer, wine, liquor, and products containing alcohol (some mouthwashes, cough syrups).

- **C. "I only need to avoid hard liquor. Beer and wine are fine."** — Incorrect! ALL forms of alcohol must be avoided. Beer and wine contain alcohol and will cause the same reaction.

- **D. "Once I feel better, I can stop the medicine and resume drinking."** — Incorrect on TWO counts! (1) Never stop antibiotics early, and (2) no alcohol until 48–72 hours after finishing ALL the medication.

> **Test-taking tip:** Metronidazole + ANY alcohol = severe reaction. The NCLEX wants you to know this includes all forms of alcohol and lasts 48–72 hours after the last dose.

</details>

## Question 9 — Hold-or-Give Decision

**A patient is scheduled to receive gentamicin at 1000. At 0930, the nurse reviews the morning labs: BUN 42 mg/dL (normal: 7–20), creatinine 3.2 mg/dL (normal: 0.6–1.2), and gentamicin trough level 4 mcg/mL (therapeutic: <2 mcg/mL). What should the nurse do?**

A. Administer the gentamicin as scheduled
B. Hold the gentamicin and notify the provider
C. Give the gentamicin and recheck the trough in 4 hours
D. Administer the gentamicin with extra IV fluids

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Administer the gentamicin as scheduled** — ⚠️ This would be **dangerous**! The trough level is MORE than double the safe limit, and the kidney function labs are very elevated. Giving more drug would increase the risk of serious nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity.

- **B. Hold the gentamicin and notify the provider** ✅ — The **trough is dangerously high** (4 mcg/mL; normal is <2 mcg/mL), meaning the drug is not being cleared. The **BUN and creatinine are very elevated**, indicating the kidneys are not working well. The nurse must **hold the dose** and **notify the provider** so the dose or frequency can be adjusted.

- **C. Give the gentamicin and recheck the trough in 4 hours** — Never give a drug when the level is already toxic. Rechecking later does not fix the current danger.

- **D. Administer the gentamicin with extra IV fluids** — Extra fluids will not bring the drug level down fast enough. The dose must be held.

> **Test-taking tip:** When labs say the drug level is too high or the kidneys are struggling, the answer is almost always: **HOLD the drug and NOTIFY the provider.** Don't give a potentially toxic dose.

</details>

## Question 10 — Select All That Apply (SATA)

**A nurse is caring for a patient receiving IV vancomycin. Which nursing actions are appropriate? (Select all that apply.)**

A. Infuse the medication over at least 60 minutes
B. Monitor the patient for flushing and rash during the infusion
C. Check vancomycin trough level 30 minutes after the infusion ends
D. Assess baseline hearing before treatment begins
E. Monitor BUN and creatinine regularly
F. Administer the drug by rapid IV push for faster effect

**Correct Answers: A, B, D, E**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Infuse the medication over at least 60 minutes** ✅ — Vancomycin must be infused **slowly** (usually over 60 minutes or longer, depending on the dose) to prevent Red Man Syndrome. This is a key nursing action.

- **B. Monitor the patient for flushing and rash during the infusion** ✅ — The nurse should watch for signs of Red Man Syndrome (flushing, redness, itching) during every infusion. If it occurs, slow the rate.

- **C. Check vancomycin trough level 30 minutes after the infusion ends** — Incorrect timing! The trough level is drawn **right BEFORE the next dose** (not after the infusion). Drawing it after the dose would give you a level that is too high and not useful for trough monitoring.

- **D. Assess baseline hearing before treatment begins** ✅ — Vancomycin can cause **ototoxicity** (hearing damage). Getting a baseline hearing assessment allows you to detect changes during treatment.

- **E. Monitor BUN and creatinine regularly** ✅ — Vancomycin can cause **nephrotoxicity** (kidney damage). Regular monitoring of kidney function labs is essential.

- **F. Administer the drug by rapid IV push for faster effect** — ⚠️ **NEVER** give vancomycin by rapid IV push! This can cause severe hypotension, cardiac arrest, and Red Man Syndrome. Vancomycin must always be infused slowly.

> **Test-taking tip:** For SATA questions, evaluate each option independently — ask "Is this correct on its own?" Don't let one correct answer influence your judgment of another. With vancomycin, remember: slow infusion, trough before dose, watch kidneys, watch ears.

</details>

## Question 11 — Priority/First Action

**A nurse is caring for four patients. Which patient should the nurse assess FIRST?**

A. A patient on ciprofloxacin who reports mild nausea after taking the pill
B. A patient on amoxicillin who developed a small, localized rash on the arm
C. A patient on vancomycin IV who reports sudden difficulty breathing and throat tightness
D. A patient on metronidazole who reports a metallic taste in the mouth

**Correct Answer: C**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. A patient on ciprofloxacin who reports mild nausea** — Nausea is a common and expected side effect. Not urgent.

- **B. A patient on amoxicillin who developed a small, localized rash** — This needs assessment and reporting, but a small, localized rash is not immediately life-threatening. Monitor and notify the provider.

- **C. A patient on vancomycin IV who reports difficulty breathing and throat tightness** ✅ — This is a potential **anaphylactic reaction** or severe allergic response. Airway compromise is the highest priority — this is a life-threatening emergency. Assess immediately, stop the infusion, and prepare for emergency interventions.

- **D. A patient on metronidazole who reports a metallic taste** — This is a common and expected side effect of metronidazole. Not urgent at all.

> **Test-taking tip:** In priority questions, always assess the patient with the **most life-threatening** condition first. Use the **ABCs** (Airway, Breathing, Circulation). Difficulty breathing + throat tightness = airway problem = see this patient FIRST.

</details>

## Question 12 — Lab Interpretation

**A patient is receiving vancomycin for a MRSA wound infection. The vancomycin trough level result is 8 mcg/mL. The provider's goal is a trough of 15–20 mcg/mL. What does this result mean?**

A. The drug level is in the therapeutic range — continue the current dose
B. The drug level is too high — hold the next dose and notify the provider
C. The drug level is too low — the dose may need to be increased
D. The drug level is toxic — stop vancomycin immediately

**Correct Answer: C**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. The drug level is in the therapeutic range** — Incorrect. The target is 15–20 mcg/mL, and the result is only 8 mcg/mL. The level is below the therapeutic range.

- **B. The drug level is too high** — Incorrect. 8 mcg/mL is well below the target of 15–20 mcg/mL.

- **C. The drug level is too low — the dose may need to be increased** ✅ — The trough is **8 mcg/mL**, which is below the goal of **15–20 mcg/mL**. This means the drug is being cleared too quickly and may not be effective against the MRSA infection. The provider will likely **increase the dose or frequency**.

- **D. The drug level is toxic** — Incorrect. A level of 8 mcg/mL is not toxic — it is subtherapeutic (too low).

> **Test-taking tip:** Always compare the lab result to the therapeutic (goal) range. Too low = not enough drug = may need more. Too high = too much drug = risk of toxicity. Notify the provider either way.

</details>

## Question 13 — Application Scenario

**A nurse is administering oral vancomycin to a patient with C. difficile colitis. The patient asks, "Why am I taking this by mouth instead of through my IV?" What is the nurse's best response?**

A. "Oral vancomycin is cheaper than the IV form."
B. "The oral form goes directly to your intestines where the infection is."
C. "The IV form has been discontinued by the pharmacy."
D. "All antibiotics work better when taken by mouth."

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. "Oral vancomycin is cheaper than the IV form."** — While cost may be a factor, this is not the clinical reason for choosing oral over IV.

- **B. "The oral form goes directly to your intestines where the infection is."** ✅ — C. diff lives in the **gut (intestines)**. Oral vancomycin is NOT absorbed into the blood — it stays in the GI tract, which is exactly where it needs to be to fight C. diff. IV vancomycin goes into the blood and does NOT reach the gut in therapeutic amounts.

- **C. "The IV form has been discontinued by the pharmacy."** — This is not accurate and does not address the clinical reasoning.

- **D. "All antibiotics work better when taken by mouth."** — This is false. Many antibiotics work better or are only available IV.

> **Test-taking tip:** Oral vancomycin for C. diff is a very common NCLEX topic. Remember: C. diff = gut infection = oral vancomycin (stays in the gut). IV vancomycin = bloodstream infections (like MRSA).

</details>

## Question 14 — Patient Teaching

**A patient with a urinary tract infection is prescribed a 7-day course of amoxicillin. On day 3, the patient calls the clinic and says, "I feel so much better! Do I still need to take the rest of my pills?" What is the nurse's best response?**

A. "If you feel better, you can stop taking the antibiotic."
B. "You should finish all 7 days of the antibiotic, even though you feel better."
C. "Take the medicine for 2 more days and then stop."
D. "Switch to taking it every other day for the remaining pills."

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. "If you feel better, you can stop taking the antibiotic."** — ⚠️ This is dangerous advice! Stopping early leaves surviving bacteria that can come back stronger and become resistant.

- **B. "You should finish all 7 days of the antibiotic, even though you feel better."** ✅ — Always complete the full course. The bacteria that are still alive on day 3 are the strongest ones. If you stop now, they can regrow and the infection can come back — possibly resistant to the antibiotic.

- **C. "Take the medicine for 2 more days and then stop."** — Only a provider can change the prescribed course. The nurse should not independently shorten the treatment.

- **D. "Switch to taking it every other day for the remaining pills."** — This would give subtherapeutic doses, which promotes antibiotic resistance. Never modify the dosing schedule without a provider order.

> **Test-taking tip:** "Finish all your antibiotics" is one of the most important patient teaching points. Feeling better ≠ infection gone. The NCLEX loves to test this concept.

</details>

## Question 15 — Select All That Apply (SATA)

**A nurse is teaching a patient who has been prescribed ciprofloxacin. Which instructions should the nurse include? (Select all that apply.)**

A. "Avoid taking this medicine with your iron supplement at the same time."
B. "Report any pain or swelling in your tendons, especially the ankle."
C. "Apply sunscreen and wear protective clothing when going outside."
D. "It is safe to take this medicine with a glass of milk."
E. "Report any numbness or tingling in your hands or feet."
F. "Drink plenty of fluids while taking this medication."

**Correct Answers: A, B, C, E, F**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. "Avoid taking this medicine with your iron supplement at the same time."** ✅ — Iron (and calcium, magnesium, zinc, antacids) reduces absorption of ciprofloxacin. Take ciprofloxacin 2 hours before or 6 hours after these products.

- **B. "Report any pain or swelling in your tendons, especially the ankle."** ✅ — Fluoroquinolones carry a BLACK BOX WARNING for tendon rupture, especially the Achilles tendon. Patients must report tendon symptoms immediately.

- **C. "Apply sunscreen and wear protective clothing when going outside."** ✅ — Fluoroquinolones cause photosensitivity. Sun protection is essential.

- **D. "It is safe to take this medicine with a glass of milk."** — Incorrect! Milk contains **calcium**, which reduces ciprofloxacin absorption. Avoid dairy products close to dosing time.

- **E. "Report any numbness or tingling in your hands or feet."** ✅ — Fluoroquinolones carry a BLACK BOX WARNING for peripheral neuropathy. Numbness and tingling are early warning signs.

- **F. "Drink plenty of fluids while taking this medication."** ✅ — Adequate hydration helps prevent crystalluria (drug crystals in the urine) and supports kidney function.

> **Test-taking tip:** Ciprofloxacin has THREE black box warnings: tendon rupture, peripheral neuropathy, and CNS effects. Plus photosensitivity and mineral interactions. That's a lot to teach — if you remember "tendons, nerves, sun, and minerals," you'll cover the big ones.

</details>

## Question 16 — Hold-or-Give Decision

**A patient with MRSA bacteremia is scheduled for their next dose of IV vancomycin at 1400. At 1330, the nurse checks the vancomycin trough level, which is 28 mcg/mL (therapeutic goal: 15–20 mcg/mL). What should the nurse do?**

A. Give the dose as scheduled since the patient has a serious infection
B. Give half the dose to bring the level down gradually
C. Hold the dose and notify the provider
D. Give the dose and recheck the level in the morning

**Correct Answer: C**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Give the dose as scheduled since the patient has a serious infection** — Even though the infection is serious, giving more drug when the level is already high increases the risk of **nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity**. The level must be addressed first.

- **B. Give half the dose to bring the level down gradually** — The nurse cannot independently change the dose. This requires a provider order. Also, the level is already too high — adding any more drug is risky.

- **C. Hold the dose and notify the provider** ✅ — The trough level of 28 mcg/mL is **above the therapeutic range** (goal: 15–20 mcg/mL). This means the drug is accumulating and the patient is at increased risk for toxicity. The nurse should **hold the dose** and **notify the provider** to adjust the dosing.

- **D. Give the dose and recheck the level in the morning** — This ignores the current dangerous level. Never continue giving a drug when levels are toxic.

> **Test-taking tip:** When a drug level is above the therapeutic range, the safe answer is almost always **hold and notify**. The nurse should not administer a drug when there is clear evidence of potential toxicity.

</details>

## Question 17 — Application Scenario

**A nurse is caring for a patient who has been on broad-spectrum antibiotics for 10 days. The patient develops white patches on the tongue and inner cheeks. What does the nurse suspect, and what action is appropriate?**

A. The patient has a viral infection — administer antiviral medication
B. The patient has oral thrush (superinfection) — notify the provider and expect an antifungal order
C. The patient has an allergic reaction — stop the antibiotic immediately
D. The patient has poor oral hygiene — provide oral care education

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. The patient has a viral infection** — White patches on the tongue and cheeks after prolonged antibiotic use are not typically viral. This presentation is classic for fungal overgrowth.

- **B. The patient has oral thrush (superinfection)** ✅ — Broad-spectrum antibiotics kill both bad AND good bacteria. When good bacteria are gone, **fungi (like Candida/yeast) can overgrow**. White patches in the mouth = **oral thrush**. The nurse should notify the provider, who will likely order an **antifungal** (such as nystatin or fluconazole).

- **C. The patient has an allergic reaction** — Oral thrush is not an allergic reaction. Allergic reactions present with hives, rash, swelling, or breathing difficulty — not white patches in the mouth.

- **D. The patient has poor oral hygiene** — While oral care is always important, white patches after prolonged antibiotics is a classic sign of a superinfection, not just poor hygiene.

> **Test-taking tip:** White patches in the mouth + antibiotics = think **oral thrush (Candida superinfection)**. Watery diarrhea + antibiotics = think **C. diff**. Both are superinfections caused by loss of normal flora.

</details>

## Question 18 — Priority/First Action

**A nurse receives the following new lab results for a patient on gentamicin:**
- **Creatinine: 2.8 mg/dL** (baseline was 0.9 mg/dL)
- **BUN: 38 mg/dL** (baseline was 14 mg/dL)

**The patient's next gentamicin dose is due in 1 hour. What is the nurse's priority action?**

A. Administer the gentamicin on time to maintain therapeutic levels
B. Encourage the patient to drink more fluids
C. Hold the gentamicin and notify the provider immediately
D. Request a hearing test to assess for ototoxicity

**Correct Answer: C**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Administer the gentamicin on time** — ⚠️ The creatinine has tripled from baseline! This is a sign of **nephrotoxicity** (kidney damage) — a major side effect of aminoglycosides. Giving more drug could worsen the kidney damage.

- **B. Encourage the patient to drink more fluids** — Hydration is helpful, but it is not the priority when the kidneys are showing signs of damage. The drug must be held first.

- **C. Hold the gentamicin and notify the provider immediately** ✅ — The rising creatinine and BUN indicate the kidneys are being damaged by the gentamicin. The nurse must **hold the dose** to prevent further damage and **notify the provider** so the treatment plan can be changed (dose adjustment, different antibiotic, etc.).

- **D. Request a hearing test** — While monitoring for ototoxicity is important, the immediate concern is the **kidney function**. Holding the drug and notifying the provider is the priority.

> **Test-taking tip:** Always compare current lab values to baseline. A creatinine that triples = the kidneys are in trouble. With nephrotoxic drugs like aminoglycosides: rising creatinine → hold the drug → notify the provider.

</details>

## Question 19 — Application Scenario

**A nurse is preparing to administer ceftriaxone IV to a 5-day-old neonate. The neonate is currently receiving a calcium-containing IV solution (TPN with calcium). What should the nurse do?**

A. Mix the ceftriaxone in the same IV line as the TPN for convenience
B. Administer the ceftriaxone through a separate IV line that has been flushed, and ensure the calcium and ceftriaxone do not run at the same time
C. Add the ceftriaxone to the TPN bag for continuous infusion
D. Crush the ceftriaxone and give it orally instead

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Mix the ceftriaxone in the same IV line as the TPN** — ⚠️ **NEVER** mix ceftriaxone with calcium-containing solutions in neonates. This can form **deadly crystals** (precipitates) in the blood, lungs, and kidneys.

- **B. Administer the ceftriaxone through a separate IV line, and ensure they do not run at the same time** ✅ — In neonates, ceftriaxone and calcium must be given through **separate lines** and should NOT run simultaneously. The lines should be thoroughly flushed between administrations.

- **C. Add the ceftriaxone to the TPN bag** — Same danger as option A. Never combine ceftriaxone with calcium in any way in neonates.

- **D. Crush the ceftriaxone and give it orally** — Ceftriaxone is an injectable antibiotic. It is not available in an oral form, and you cannot crush an IV medication for oral use.

> **Test-taking tip:** Ceftriaxone + calcium + neonate = **fatal crystal formation**. This is a critical safety concept. Use separate lines, and do not run them at the same time in neonates.

</details>

## Question 20 — Select All That Apply (SATA)

**A patient is prescribed azithromycin (Z-pack) for community-acquired pneumonia. Which of the following are important for the nurse to monitor or teach? (Select all that apply.)**

A. Complete the full 5-day course, even if symptoms improve
B. Report any irregular heartbeat, dizziness, or fainting
C. GI upset (nausea, diarrhea) is common with this medication
D. Monitor liver function tests during treatment
E. The patient must avoid all sunlight exposure
F. Report any signs of watery diarrhea that could indicate C. diff

**Correct Answers: A, B, C, D, F**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Complete the full 5-day course** ✅ — Even though the Z-pack is only 5 days (shorter than many antibiotics), the patient must finish ALL doses. Stopping early can lead to resistance and recurrence.

- **B. Report any irregular heartbeat, dizziness, or fainting** ✅ — Azithromycin can cause **QT prolongation**, which can lead to dangerous heart arrhythmias. Patients should report cardiac symptoms.

- **C. GI upset is common** ✅ — Macrolides (especially erythromycin, but also azithromycin) commonly cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain.

- **D. Monitor liver function tests** ✅ — Macrolides can cause **hepatotoxicity** (liver damage). Liver function should be monitored, especially in patients with pre-existing liver conditions.

- **E. The patient must avoid all sunlight exposure** — While photosensitivity is a concern with **fluoroquinolones** and some other drugs, it is **not a major concern** with azithromycin. This would be an incorrect teaching point for this drug.

- **F. Report signs of watery diarrhea that could indicate C. diff** ✅ — ANY antibiotic can disrupt normal gut flora and lead to C. diff. Patients should report watery, foul-smelling diarrhea.

> **Test-taking tip:** Don't confuse drug side effects between classes! Photosensitivity = fluoroquinolones. QT prolongation = macrolides AND fluoroquinolones. Tendon rupture = fluoroquinolones ONLY. The NCLEX may try to trick you by mixing up side effects between drug classes.

</details>

## Question 21 — Lab Interpretation

**A patient on tobramycin has a peak level drawn 30 minutes after the IV infusion ends. The result is 14 mcg/mL (therapeutic peak: 5–10 mcg/mL). Which potential complication should the nurse be MOST concerned about?**

A. Allergic reaction
B. Nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity
C. Tendon rupture
D. QT prolongation

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Allergic reaction** — High drug levels do not specifically indicate an allergic reaction. Allergies are immune-mediated, not dose-dependent.

- **B. Nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity** ✅ — The peak level of **14 mcg/mL** is above the therapeutic range of **5–10 mcg/mL**. With aminoglycosides, high drug levels increase the risk of **nephrotoxicity** (kidney damage) and **ototoxicity** (hearing/balance damage). These are the two signature toxicities of aminoglycosides.

- **C. Tendon rupture** — This is associated with **fluoroquinolones**, not aminoglycosides. Don't mix up drug classes!

- **D. QT prolongation** — This is associated with **macrolides** and **fluoroquinolones**, not aminoglycosides.

> **Test-taking tip:** When you see an aminoglycoside with a high level, immediately think: **"nephro + oto"** (kidney + ear). The nurse should hold the next dose, notify the provider, monitor kidney function (creatinine), and assess hearing.

</details>

## Question 22 — Application Scenario

**A nurse is caring for a patient on contact precautions for C. difficile. After providing care, the nurse removes gloves and gown. Which hand hygiene method should the nurse use?**

A. Alcohol-based hand sanitizer for 15 seconds
B. Soap and water for at least 20 seconds
C. Antibacterial wipes
D. Alcohol-based hand sanitizer for at least 30 seconds

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Alcohol-based hand sanitizer for 15 seconds** — ⚠️ Alcohol-based sanitizers do **NOT kill C. diff spores**. This is a very important fact to know!

- **B. Soap and water for at least 20 seconds** ✅ — C. diff produces **spores** that are resistant to alcohol. Only **mechanical friction with soap and water** can physically remove C. diff spores from the hands. This is the required hand hygiene method for C. diff.

- **C. Antibacterial wipes** — These are not sufficient for removing C. diff spores. Soap and water is the standard.

- **D. Alcohol-based hand sanitizer for at least 30 seconds** — Even with longer application time, alcohol does NOT kill C. diff spores. The method matters, not just the duration.

> **Test-taking tip:** C. diff = soap and water ONLY. This is one of the few times that alcohol-based hand sanitizer is NOT acceptable. The NCLEX tests this frequently. Remember: "C. diff spores are tough — only soap and water can wash them away."

</details>

## Question 23 — Priority/First Action

**A nurse is reviewing morning medications for a patient who is NPO (nothing by mouth) for surgery scheduled at 1000. One of the ordered medications is oral metronidazole (Flagyl) for a C. diff infection, scheduled for 0800. What should the nurse do?**

A. Hold all oral medications since the patient is NPO
B. Give the metronidazole with a small sip of water after confirming with the provider and anesthesia
C. Crush the metronidazole and mix it into the IV fluids
D. Skip the dose and give it after surgery

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Hold all oral medications since the patient is NPO** — While many oral medications are held when NPO, some essential medications (like antibiotics for active infections) may still be given. The nurse should not automatically hold a critical medication without checking with the provider.

- **B. Give the metronidazole with a small sip of water after confirming with the provider and anesthesia** ✅ — C. diff is a serious, potentially life-threatening infection. Interrupting treatment could worsen the infection. The nurse should **contact the provider and/or anesthesia** to clarify whether this essential antibiotic can be given with a sip of water before surgery. In most cases, essential medications are allowed with a small amount of water even when NPO.

- **C. Crush the metronidazole and mix it into IV fluids** — You cannot add oral medications to IV fluids. This is an unsafe practice.

- **D. Skip the dose and give it after surgery** — Skipping an antibiotic dose for an active C. diff infection could allow the infection to worsen. Check with the provider first before omitting.

> **Test-taking tip:** When in doubt about giving a med to an NPO patient, the best action is to **contact the provider or anesthesia for clarification**. Don't automatically hold essential medications without checking.

</details>

## Question 24 — Application Scenario

**A nurse is about to administer the first dose of IV piperacillin-tazobactam (a penicillin-based antibiotic) to a new patient. Which assessment is the HIGHEST priority before starting the infusion?**

A. Check the patient's most recent white blood cell count
B. Verify the patient's IV site for signs of infiltration
C. Ask the patient about any history of drug allergies, specifically penicillin
D. Check that the medication was stored at the correct temperature

**Correct Answer: C**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. Check the WBC count** — While important for monitoring the infection, this is not the highest safety priority before the first dose.

- **B. Verify the IV site** — This is part of IV medication administration but is not the highest priority for a penicillin-based drug.

- **C. Ask about drug allergies, specifically penicillin** ✅ — Before giving ANY penicillin-based antibiotic, the **#1 safety check** is asking about **penicillin allergy**. An allergic reaction (especially anaphylaxis) can be **life-threatening**. This is the highest priority assessment.

- **D. Check medication storage temperature** — While medications should be stored properly, this is typically a pharmacy responsibility and not the highest bedside nursing priority.

> **Test-taking tip:** Before ANY antibiotic (but especially penicillins and cephalosporins), allergy assessment is the TOP priority. The NCLEX will always want you to check for allergies before the first dose.

</details>

## Question 25 — Patient Teaching

**A patient asks the nurse, "My doctor said I have MRSA. Why can't I just take regular antibiotics?" What is the nurse's best response?**

A. "MRSA is a virus, so antibiotics don't work on it."
B. "MRSA is a type of bacteria that has become resistant to many common antibiotics, so you need a special one like vancomycin."
C. "All antibiotics work the same. Your doctor just prefers vancomycin."
D. "You can take any antibiotic. MRSA is just a more severe infection."

**Correct Answer: B**

<details>
<summary>See Explanation 💡</summary>

- **A. "MRSA is a virus"** — Incorrect. MRSA is a **bacterium** (*Staphylococcus aureus*) that has become resistant to methicillin and many other antibiotics. It is not a virus.

- **B. "MRSA is a type of bacteria that has become resistant to many common antibiotics, so you need a special one like vancomycin."** ✅ — This is an accurate, patient-friendly explanation. MRSA stands for **Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus Aureus**. It does not respond to many common antibiotics (like penicillins and cephalosporins), so drugs like **vancomycin** are needed.

- **C. "All antibiotics work the same"** — Incorrect. Different antibiotics work in different ways against different bacteria. MRSA specifically requires antibiotics that it has not developed resistance to.

- **D. "You can take any antibiotic"** — Incorrect and dangerous. Using an antibiotic that MRSA is resistant to would not treat the infection and could worsen antibiotic resistance.

> **Test-taking tip:** MRSA = Methicillin-**Resistant**. The "R" is the key — this bacteria has learned to resist common antibiotics. Vancomycin is the go-to treatment for MRSA.

</details>
