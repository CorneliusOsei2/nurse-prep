### Flashcard 1 — Recall
**Q: What is the therapeutic drug level range for phenytoin (Dilantin)?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: 10–20 mcg/mL**

This is a narrow therapeutic range — a small increase above 20 can cause toxicity. Always check the level before and during treatment.

*Think of it this way:* "Phenytoin = 10 to 20" — just two simple numbers!
</details>

### Flashcard 2 — Recall
**Q: What is the normal potassium (K+) range?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: 3.5–5.0 mEq/L**

This is one of the most tested lab values on the NCLEX. Both high and low potassium can be dangerous to the heart.
</details>

---

### Flashcard 3 — Safety
**Q: What is the maximum IV rate for phenytoin, and why does it matter?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Never faster than 50 mg/min in adults.**

Giving phenytoin IV too fast causes:
- Cardiac arrhythmias (dangerous heart rhythms)
- Hypotension (low blood pressure)
- Cardiac arrest in severe cases

Always use an IV pump. Only mix in normal saline (0.9% NaCl) — never dextrose.
</details>

---

### Flashcard 4 — Safety (Critical!)
**Q: Can you give potassium chloride (KCl) by IV push?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: NEVER. Absolutely not.**

IV potassium must ALWAYS be diluted and given by slow IV infusion with a pump. Maximum rate is usually 10–20 mEq/hour (check facility policy).

Giving KCl by IV push = **fatal cardiac arrest**.

This is one of the most important safety rules in nursing!
</details>

---

### Flashcard 5 — Safety
**Q: What is the antidote for magnesium sulfate toxicity?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Calcium gluconate**

Keep calcium gluconate at the bedside whenever a patient is receiving a magnesium sulfate drip (especially in preeclampsia patients).

It reverses the dangerous effects of too much magnesium — respiratory depression, loss of reflexes, and hypotension.
</details>

### Flashcard 6 — Patient Teaching
**Q: Your patient is on phenytoin (Dilantin). What important oral hygiene teaching do you give and why?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Teach careful brushing, daily flossing, and regular dental visits.**

Phenytoin causes **gingival hyperplasia** — the gums become swollen and overgrown. Good oral hygiene helps prevent this.

*Think of it this way:* "Phenytoin grows the gums!" — classic NCLEX fact!
</details>

### Flashcard 7 — Compare/Contrast
**Q: How do the ECG changes differ between hypokalemia and hyperkalemia?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

| | Hypokalemia (Low K+) | Hyperkalemia (High K+) |
|---|---|---|
| **T waves** | Flattened T waves | **Peaked (tall, pointy) T waves** |
| **Other changes** | U waves appear, ST depression | Widened QRS, can progress to cardiac arrest |

*Think of it this way:*
- Low K+ = **flat** T waves (everything is weak and flat)
- High K+ = **peaked** T waves (the potassium is "spiking up" the T waves)
</details>

### Flashcard 8 — Compare/Contrast
**Q: What makes levetiracetam (Keppra) different from phenytoin when it comes to side effects and monitoring?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

| | Phenytoin (Dilantin) | Levetiracetam (Keppra) |
|---|---|---|

Keppra is simpler to use, but watch for mood and behavioral changes!
</details>

### Flashcard 9 — Safety
**Q: Before giving magnesium sulfate, what three things must you check?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**Check these three things EVERY TIME:**

1. **Deep tendon reflexes (DTRs)** — if absent, HOLD the dose
2. **Respiratory rate** — if below 12 breaths/min, HOLD the dose
3. **Urine output** — if below 30 mL/hour, HOLD the dose

*Think of it this way:* **"R-R-U"** — **R**eflexes, **R**espiratory rate, **U**rine output

If any of these are abnormal — HOLD and call the provider!
</details>

### Flashcard 10 — Recall
**Q: What are the two classic signs of hypocalcemia tested on the NCLEX?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Trousseau sign and Chvostek sign**

- **Trousseau sign:** Inflate a BP cuff above systolic for 3 minutes → hand spasms (carpopedal spasm)
- **Chvostek sign:** Tap on the facial nerve in front of the ear → face twitches

*Think of it this way:*
- **T**rousseau = **T**ourniquet (BP cuff) = hand **T**wists
- **Ch**vostek = **Ch**eek (tap cheek, face twitches)
</details>

### Flashcard 11 — Priority
**Q: A patient's potassium level comes back at 6.8 mEq/L. The provider orders multiple treatments. Which medication do you give FIRST?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Calcium gluconate IV — give this FIRST**

Calcium gluconate **protects the heart** by stabilizing the cardiac membrane. It does NOT lower the potassium level, but it prevents deadly arrhythmias while the other treatments work.

Then:
- Insulin + glucose → shifts K+ into cells
- Kayexalate → removes K+ from the body
- Dialysis if needed

*Think of it this way:* "Protect the heart FIRST, fix the potassium SECOND"
</details>

### Flashcard 12 — Patient Teaching
**Q: Your patient takes oral potassium chloride (KCl). What do you teach them about how to take it?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Take with food and a full glass of water.**

Why? Oral potassium is very irritating to the stomach and can cause nausea, vomiting, and even GI ulcers.

Also teach:

- Liquid form can be mixed with juice to improve taste
- Report stomach pain, nausea, or dark/tarry stools
</details>

### Flashcard 19 — Patient Teaching
**Q: What do you teach EVERY patient on seizure medications about stopping the medication?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: NEVER stop seizure medications suddenly.**

Stopping abruptly can trigger **status epilepticus** — a seizure that doesn't stop on its own. This is a life-threatening emergency.

If the medication needs to be changed or stopped, the provider will taper it slowly (reduce the dose gradually over time).

This applies to ALL seizure medications — phenytoin, valproic acid, levetiracetam, carbamazepine, all of them!

### Flashcard 21 — Compare/Contrast
**Q: Compare hyponatremia and hypernatremia — what are the key signs of each?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

| | Hyponatremia (Low Na+) | Hypernatremia (High Na+) |
|---|---|---|
| **Key signs** | Confusion, seizures, cerebral edema | Thirst, dry mucous membranes, altered LOC |
| **Think of it as** | "Watery brain" — too much water dilutes sodium | "Dry and thirsty" — not enough water |
| **Correction** | Correct SLOWLY (risk of osmotic demyelination) | Correct SLOWLY (risk of cerebral edema) |

Normal sodium: **135–145 mEq/L**

Both high and low sodium must be corrected slowly to prevent brain damage!
</details>

### Flashcard 22 — Priority
**Q: During a seizure, what is your FIRST nursing action?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Stay with the patient and protect them from injury.**

- Lower them to the ground if standing
- Move sharp or hard objects away
- Put padding under the head if possible
- Do NOT restrain them
- Do NOT put anything in their mouth
- Time the seizure
- Turn on their side AFTER the seizure stops

*Think of it this way:* **Protect → Time → Position → Document**

Call for help if the seizure lasts more than 5 minutes (status epilepticus).
</details>

### Flashcard 24 — Safety
**Q: A patient is on digoxin and needs IV calcium for hypocalcemia. What special concern does the nurse have?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Calcium increases the risk of digoxin toxicity.**

When calcium levels go up, the heart becomes more sensitive to digoxin. This can cause dangerous arrhythmias.

Nursing actions:

- Watch for signs of digoxin toxicity (nausea, visual changes, bradycardia)
- Notify the provider about the interaction

### Flashcard 25 — Application
**Q: Your patient's potassium is 2.9 mEq/L. You are infusing IV KCl and the patient says, "My arm really burns where the IV is." What do you do?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Slow the infusion rate and/or further dilute the potassium.**

IV potassium is irritating to veins and often causes burning at the IV site. This is common.

Actions:
- Slow the rate (if safe to do so per the order)
- Further dilute if possible
- Check the IV site for infiltration (swelling, redness, coolness)
- Apply a warm compress to the area (may help with discomfort)
- If the site is infiltrated, restart the IV in a new site
- NEVER increase the rate to "get it over with" — too fast = fatal arrhythmia
</details>

### Flashcard 27 — Safety
**Q: Why must phenytoin IV be given slowly? What can happen if it's pushed too fast?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Rapid IV phenytoin can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmias and severe hypotension.**

*Think of it this way:* "Phenytoin fast = heart stops" — always go slow!

### Flashcard 32 — Compare/Contrast
**Q: Levetiracetam vs phenytoin — which has fewer drug interactions and why does that matter?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Levetiracetam (Keppra) has far fewer drug interactions than phenytoin.**

| | Phenytoin (Dilantin) | Levetiracetam (Keppra) |

*Think of it this way:* "Keppra = Keep it simple!" 👍
</details>

### Flashcard 33 — Application
**Q: A patient on carbamazepine develops a new rash. Why is this concerning?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: A new rash on carbamazepine could be the start of Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS) — a life-threatening skin reaction.**

SJS warning signs:
- Rash that spreads quickly
- Blisters on the skin or mucous membranes
- Fever with the rash
- Peeling skin (looks like a burn)

**What to do:**

- Document and report right away

Not every rash is SJS, but every rash on carbamazepine must be taken seriously until proven otherwise.

*Think of it this way:* "Tegretol rash = Take it seriously!" 🚨
</details>

### Flashcard 36 — Safety (Critical!)
**Q: A nurse is about to IV push potassium chloride. Why must you STOP them?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: IV push potassium = INSTANT CARDIAC ARREST. You must stop them immediately.**

*Think of it this way:* "K+ push = Kill the patient" — NEVER do it! ⛔
</details>

### Flashcard 38 — Lab
**Q: ECG shows peaked T waves and a wide QRS. What electrolyte is probably too high?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Hyperkalemia (high potassium).**

The ECG changes of hyperkalemia happen in a predictable order:
1. **Peaked (tall, pointy) T waves** — earliest sign
2. **Widened QRS complex**
3. **Flattened or absent P waves**
4. **Sine wave pattern** → cardiac arrest if not treated

This is an emergency! Treatment priorities:
1. Calcium gluconate IV (protects the heart)
2. Insulin + glucose (shifts K+ into cells)
3. Kayexalate or patiromer (removes K+ from the body)
4. Dialysis if severe

*Think of it this way:* "Peaked T waves = Potassium is peaking!" 📈
</details>

### Flashcard 39 — Compare/Contrast
**Q: Hypokalemia vs hyperkalemia — list the key signs of each side by side.**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

| | Hypokalemia (K+ < 3.5) | Hyperkalemia (K+ > 5.0) |
|---|---|---|
| **Muscles** | Weakness, cramps, fatigue | Weakness, numbness, tingling |
| **Heart/ECG** | Flattened T waves, U waves, arrhythmias | Peaked T waves, wide QRS, arrhythmias |
| **GI** | Decreased bowel sounds, constipation, ileus | Nausea, diarrhea, cramping |
| **Reflexes** | Decreased (hyporeflexia) | May be decreased late |
| **Common causes** | Loop diuretics, vomiting, NG suction | Renal failure, potassium-sparing diuretics, crush injuries |

**The big picture:** Both extremes affect the heart — that's why potassium is so dangerous in either direction!

*Think of it this way:* Low K+ = everything is flat and weak. High K+ = everything is peaked and irritable.
</details>

### Flashcard 40 — Application
**Q: A patient with hyperkalemia (K+ 6.8) is given calcium gluconate. Does this lower the potassium?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: NO — calcium gluconate does NOT lower the potassium level.**

This is a very common NCLEX trick question! Here's what calcium gluconate actually does:
- It **stabilizes the heart membrane** — it protects the heart from the dangerous effects of high K+
- Think of it as a "shield" for the heart while you work on lowering the potassium

**What actually lowers potassium:**
- **Insulin + glucose** → shifts K+ into the cells (temporary fix)
- **Kayexalate / patiromer** → removes K+ from the body through the GI tract
- **Dialysis** → removes K+ from the blood directly

*Think of it this way:* "Calcium = cardiac protector, NOT a potassium reducer!" 🛡️
</details>

### Flashcard 42 — Hold Parameter
**Q: A preeclampsia patient on mag sulfate has RR 10 and absent DTRs. What do you do?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: STOP the magnesium infusion immediately and call the provider. This is magnesium toxicity.**

2. **Call the provider** — this is urgent
3. **Prepare calcium gluconate** — the antidote (it should already be at the bedside)
4. **Monitor** — respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, blood pressure, and level of consciousness
5. **Stay with the patient** — do not leave them alone

*Remember the hold parameters:* RR < 12, DTRs absent, or urine output < 30 mL/hr → HOLD and call! 🛑

### Flashcard 47 — Safety
**Q: What are Trousseau sign and Chvostek sign? How do you test for each?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Both are signs of hypocalcemia (low calcium). Here's how to tell them apart:**

**Trousseau sign:**
- Inflate a **blood pressure cuff** on the upper arm above systolic pressure
- Hold it for **3 minutes**
- Positive = the hand spasms into a claw shape (carpopedal spasm) ✋
- *Memory:* **T**rousseau = **T**ourniquet (BP cuff) = hand **T**wists

**Chvostek sign:**
- **Tap on the facial nerve** (just in front of the ear)
- Positive = the face twitches on that side 😬
- *Memory:* **Ch**vostek = **Ch**eek (tap cheek area, face twitches)

Both positive signs mean calcium is dangerously low and the nerves are hyperexcitable!
</details>

### Flashcard 48 — Application
**Q: A post-thyroidectomy patient has numbness around the mouth and tingling fingers. What do you suspect?**

<details>

*Think of it this way:* "Thyroidectomy → parathyroid damage → calcium drops → tingling!" 🔗

### Flashcard 49 — Compare/Contrast
**Q: Calcium gluconate vs calcium chloride — which is more concentrated and where should each be given?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Calcium chloride is 3x more concentrated than calcium gluconate.**

| | Calcium Gluconate | Calcium Chloride |
|---|---|---|
| **Concentration** | Less concentrated | **3 times more concentrated** |
| **IV access** | Can use a **peripheral IV** | Must use a **central line** |
| **Tissue damage** | Lower risk | High risk — causes severe tissue necrosis if it infiltrates |
| **Common use** | Mag toxicity antidote, mild–moderate hypocalcemia | Emergencies (cardiac arrest, severe hypocalcemia) |

**Key safety point:** If calcium chloride infiltrates a peripheral IV, it can destroy the surrounding tissue. That's why it must go through a central line.

*Think of it this way:* "Chloride = Concentrated = Central line" — all start with C! 💉
</details>

### Flashcard 50 — Lab
**Q: A patient with hypocalcemia also has low magnesium. Why must you fix the magnesium first?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: You cannot fix the calcium until you fix the magnesium. The body needs magnesium to regulate calcium.**

Here's why:
- Magnesium is needed for **parathyroid hormone (PTH)** to work properly
- PTH is what tells the body to raise calcium levels
- If magnesium is low → PTH can't do its job → calcium stays low no matter how much you give
- Giving calcium alone will NOT work until magnesium is corrected

**So the rule is:** Replace magnesium FIRST, then the calcium will start to correct on its own.

This is a favorite NCLEX trick — they love to test this connection!

*Think of it this way:* "Mag is the boss of calcium" — fix the boss first! 👑
</details>

### Flashcard 52 — Application
**Q: A patient is on furosemide, digoxin, and potassium supplements. They start vomiting and can't take oral meds. What is the biggest concern?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: The biggest concern is hypokalemia leading to digoxin toxicity.**

Here's the dangerous chain of events:
1. **Furosemide** causes potassium loss (loop diuretic)
2. The patient takes **oral potassium** to replace it — but now they're vomiting and can't take it
3. **Potassium drops** because it's still being lost but not replaced
4. **Low potassium + digoxin = digoxin toxicity** — the heart becomes extra sensitive to digoxin when K+ is low

**What to do:**
- Notify the provider IMMEDIATELY
- Expect an order for **IV potassium** (since they can't take oral)
- Check a STAT **potassium level** and **digoxin level**
- Monitor the **cardiac rhythm** closely
- Watch for signs of dig toxicity: nausea, visual changes (halos), bradycardia

*Think of it this way:* "Low K+ turns digoxin into a poison" — always protect the potassium! 💛
</details>

### Flashcard 53 — Compare/Contrast (Quick Reference!)
**Q: Quick reference card — What are the normal ranges for K+, Mg2+, Ca2+, and Na+?**

<details>
<summary>Flip the card ✨</summary>

**A: Here are the four key electrolyte ranges to memorize:**

| Electrolyte | Normal Range | Memory Trick |
|---|---|---|
| **K+ (Potassium)** | 3.5–5.0 mEq/L | "3.5 to 5" — small numbers for a small but mighty electrolyte |
| **Mg2+ (Magnesium)** | 1.5–2.5 mEq/L | "1.5 to 2.5" — the smallest range of all |

| **Na+ (Sodium)** | 135–145 mEq/L | "135 to 145" — the biggest numbers of all |

**Tips to remember:**
- K+ and Mg2+ are both in the single digits (mEq/L)
- Ca2+ is in the 8–10 range (mg/dL — different unit!)
- Na+ is way up in the 100s
- When in doubt, "the higher the normal range, the bigger the atom" (roughly!)
