## PART 1: SEIZURE MEDICATIONS

Seizure medications (also called **anticonvulsants** or **antiepileptic drugs**) help stop or prevent seizures. Some of these drugs have a **narrow therapeutic range** — that means the difference between "not enough" and "too much" is very small. So we watch lab levels very carefully.

---

### 🔹 Phenytoin (Dilantin)

**What it is used for:**
- Seizures (tonic-clonic and partial seizures)
- Sometimes used for certain heart arrhythmias

**The most important things to know:**

- **Therapeutic drug level: 10–20 mcg/mL**
- This drug has a **narrow therapeutic range** — a small change in the level can cause toxicity
- You MUST monitor drug levels regularly

**Giving phenytoin IV — be very careful:**
- Give IV **slowly** — **never faster than 50 mg/min** in adults
- Too fast = **cardiac arrhythmias** (dangerous heart rhythms) and **hypotension** (low blood pressure)
- Always use an IV pump
- Only mix with **normal saline (0.9% NaCl)** — it will crystallize in dextrose (D5W)
- Use a filter when giving IV
- Watch the IV site closely — if it leaks into the tissue, it can cause **purple glove syndrome** (painful swelling, discoloration, and tissue damage in the hand/arm)

**Side effects to watch for:**
- **Gingival hyperplasia** — the gums grow larger and become swollen
  - *Think of it this way:* "Phenytoin makes the gums grow" — this is a classic NCLEX fact!
  - **Teach the patient:** Brush and floss carefully every day. See the dentist regularly.
- Nystagmus (eyes moving back and forth — a sign of toxicity)
- Ataxia (trouble walking, unsteady — a sign of toxicity)
- Slurred speech (a sign of toxicity)
- Drowsiness, dizziness
- Rash — can be serious (Stevens-Johnson syndrome)
- Hirsutism (extra hair growth)

**Signs of phenytoin toxicity (level > 20 mcg/mL):**
1. Nystagmus (usually first sign)
2. Ataxia (unsteady walk)
3. Slurred speech
4. Confusion
5. At very high levels — seizures can actually get worse

> *Think of it this way:* "**N-A-S**" — **N**ystagmus, **A**taxia, **S**lurred speech = phenytoin toxicity

**Key labs to monitor:**
- Phenytoin drug level (10–20 mcg/mL)
- CBC (complete blood count) — can cause blood disorders
- Liver function tests (LFTs) — metabolized by the liver
- Albumin level — phenytoin binds to protein, so low albumin = more free drug = higher risk of toxicity even if the "total" level looks normal

**Drug interactions — there are MANY:**
- Phenytoin interacts with a LOT of other medications
- It is an **enzyme inducer** — it makes the liver break down other drugs faster
- This means other drugs (like oral contraceptives/birth control pills) may not work as well
- **Teach patients:** Tell every provider and pharmacist about phenytoin

**Important nursing points:**
- **NEVER stop phenytoin suddenly** — stopping abruptly can cause **status epilepticus** (a seizure that doesn't stop — this is a medical emergency)
- Give at the same time every day (consistent timing)
- Enteral feeding (tube feeding) can lower phenytoin absorption — hold tube feeding 1–2 hours before and after giving the drug
- Shake the oral suspension well before giving

**Patient teaching:**
- Good oral hygiene every day (gingival hyperplasia)
- Do not stop taking this medication without talking to the provider
- Wear a medical alert bracelet
- Avoid alcohol
- Birth control pills may not work as well — discuss other options
- Report rash, bruising, bleeding, sore throat, fever (blood disorder signs)
- Report unsteady walking, eye jerking, slurred speech (toxicity signs)

### 🔹 Valproic Acid (Depakote / Depakene)

**What it is used for:**
- Seizures (many types — broad spectrum)
- Bipolar disorder (mood stabilizer)
- Migraine headache prevention

**The most important things to know:**

- **Therapeutic drug level: 50–100 mcg/mL**
- Monitor drug levels regularly

**Serious risks:**
- **Liver toxicity (hepatotoxicity)** — this can be life-threatening
  - Monitor LFTs (liver function tests) regularly
  - **Teach patient:** Report right upper belly pain, nausea, vomiting, dark urine, jaundice (yellow skin/eyes)
  - Highest risk in first 6 months of treatment and in children under 2
- **Pancreatitis** — inflammation of the pancreas
  - **Teach patient:** Report severe abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting
- **Teratogenic** — can cause birth defects (especially neural tube defects like spina bifida)
  - **Contraindicated in pregnancy when possible**
  - *Think of it this way:* "Valproic acid is very bad for the baby" — always ask about pregnancy!
  - Women of childbearing age need reliable birth control while taking this drug
- **Thrombocytopenia** — low platelets (increased bleeding risk)
  - Monitor CBC
  - Watch for bruising, bleeding

**Side effects:**
- Nausea, vomiting, stomach upset (take with food to help)
- Weight gain
- Hair loss (usually temporary)
- Tremor
- Drowsiness

**Key labs to monitor:**
- Valproic acid level (50–100 mcg/mL)
- LFTs (liver function)
- CBC with platelets
- Ammonia level (can cause elevated ammonia — hyperammonemia)
- Coagulation studies if needed

**Important nursing points:**
- **NEVER stop abruptly** — taper slowly
- Give with food to reduce stomach upset
- Do not crush enteric-coated tablets
- Monitor for signs of liver problems and pancreatitis
- Ask about pregnancy status before starting

**Patient teaching:**
- Take with food
- Report belly pain, nausea, yellow skin, unusual bruising/bleeding
- Use reliable birth control — this drug can harm an unborn baby
- Do not stop suddenly
- Avoid alcohol

---

### 🔹 Levetiracetam (Keppra)

**What it is used for:**
- Seizures (partial onset, tonic-clonic, myoclonic)
- This has become one of the most commonly used seizure drugs

**Why providers like Keppra:**
- **Fewer drug interactions** than phenytoin or valproic acid
- Does NOT require routine drug level monitoring (no therapeutic range to memorize!)
- Can be given IV or oral

**Side effects to watch for:**
- Drowsiness, dizziness
- **Behavioral changes** — this is the big one to know!
  - Irritability, mood swings, agitation, aggression
  - Sometimes depression or anxiety
  - *Think of it this way:* "Keppra can make people cranky" — watch the mood!
  - **Teach patient and family:** Report mood changes, irritability, thoughts of self-harm

**Key labs:**
- **Kidney function (BUN, creatinine)** — Keppra is cleared by the kidneys
- Dose must be **adjusted for kidney function** — if kidneys aren't working well, the drug builds up
- No routine drug level monitoring needed

**Important nursing points:**
- **NEVER stop abruptly** (same as all seizure meds)
- Dose adjustment needed for renal impairment
- Can give IV if patient can't take oral
- Relatively safe — that's why it's used so often

**Patient teaching:**
- May cause drowsiness — be careful driving until you know how it affects you
- Report mood changes, irritability, depression
- Do not stop suddenly
- Take as prescribed at the same times each day

---

### 🔹 Carbamazepine (Tegretol) — Brief Overview

**What it is used for:**
- Seizures
- **Trigeminal neuralgia** (severe facial nerve pain) — this is a classic NCLEX connection!
- Sometimes bipolar disorder

**Important points:**
- **Therapeutic drug level: 4–12 mcg/mL**
- Monitor drug levels
- **Serious blood disorders possible:**
  - **Aplastic anemia** (bone marrow stops making enough blood cells)
  - **Agranulocytosis** (dangerously low white blood cells)
  - Monitor **CBC regularly**
  - **Teach patient:** Report fever, sore throat, mouth sores, unusual bruising/bleeding (signs of blood problems)
- **Enzyme inducer** — like phenytoin, it makes the liver process other drugs faster
  - Many drug interactions (birth control pills, warfarin, and others may be less effective)
- **Rash** — can be very serious (Stevens-Johnson syndrome)
  - Stop the drug and notify the provider immediately if a rash develops
- **NEVER stop abruptly**
- Monitor LFTs, CBC, drug levels

> *Think of it this way:* "Tegretol = Trigeminal neuralgia" — both start with "T"!

---

### ⚡ General Seizure Safety Points — KNOW THESE!

These come up on the NCLEX a lot:

**Medication safety:**
- ❌ **NEVER stop seizure medications suddenly** — this can trigger status epilepticus
- ⏰ Take medications at the **same time every day** — consistency matters
- 📊 Know the therapeutic ranges for drugs that are monitored:
  - Phenytoin: 10–20 mcg/mL
  - Valproic acid: 50–100 mcg/mL
  - Carbamazepine: 4–12 mcg/mL
- 🚗 Teach patients about **driving restrictions** — many states require being seizure-free for a certain period before driving
- 🏊 Safety around water, heights, and machinery

**During a seizure — what the nurse does:**
1. **Stay with the patient** — do not leave them
2. **Protect from injury** — lower to the ground if standing, move sharp objects away, pad side rails
3. ❌ **Do NOT put anything in the mouth** — no tongue blades, no fingers, nothing!
4. ❌ **Do NOT restrain** the patient
5. ⏱️ **Time the seizure** — this is very important
6. **Turn on side** (recovery position) **after the seizure stops** — protects airway
7. **Suction** at bedside if needed
8. **Document:** What happened before the seizure (aura?), what it looked like, how long it lasted, what happened after (postictal state)
9. Call for help / notify provider — especially if seizure lasts > 5 minutes (status epilepticus)

> *Think of it this way:* During a seizure — **protect, time, position, document**. That's your job!

## PART 2: ELECTROLYTE REPLACEMENTS

Electrolytes are minerals in your blood that carry an electric charge. They help your muscles work, your heart beat, and your nerves send signals. When they get too high or too low, it can be very dangerous — even life-threatening.

> **You need to know normal values, danger signs, and nursing actions. The NCLEX loves electrolyte questions!**

---

### 🔹 Potassium (KCl — Potassium Chloride)

**Normal range: 3.5–5.0 mEq/L**

*Think of it this way:* "**3.5 to 5**" — memorize this. It shows up everywhere on the NCLEX.

---

#### ⬇️ HYPOKALEMIA (Low Potassium — below 3.5 mEq/L)

**What causes it:**
- Diuretics — especially loop diuretics like **furosemide (Lasix)** — they make you lose potassium in the urine
- Vomiting, diarrhea (losing body fluids)
- NG suction
- Not eating enough potassium-rich foods
- Alkalosis (high pH pushes potassium into the cells)

**Signs and symptoms:**
- Muscle weakness, leg cramps
- Fatigue, lethargy
- **Irregular heartbeat (arrhythmias)** — this is the dangerous part
- Decreased bowel sounds, constipation, ileus
- Shallow respirations (if severe)
- **ECG changes:** Flattened T waves, U waves (extra wave after T wave), ST depression

> *Think of it this way:* Low potassium = everything gets **weak and flat** (weak muscles, flat T waves)

**Treatment — Potassium Replacement:**

**Oral KCl (potassium chloride):**
- Take with **food and a full glass of water** — it is very irritating to the stomach
- Do NOT crush extended-release tablets
- Liquid form tastes bad — mix with juice or water
- Can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea

**IV KCl — THIS IS CRITICAL SAFETY:**

⚠️ **NEVER GIVE IV POTASSIUM BY IV PUSH** ⚠️

This is one of the most important safety rules in nursing:
- IV KCl must **ALWAYS be diluted** and given by **slow IV infusion** (using a pump)
- **Maximum rate: usually 10–20 mEq/hour** (check your facility policy — some allow up to 20 mEq/hour on a monitored unit)
- Giving potassium too fast = **fatal cardiac arrhythmia (the heart stops)**
- The patient needs to be on a **cardiac monitor (ECG)** during IV potassium infusion
- The IV site may burn — if the patient says it hurts, slow the rate or further dilute
- **NEVER add KCl to a hanging IV bag** — it won't mix evenly and can give a dangerous bolus
- Check kidney function before giving — if the kidneys aren't working, potassium can build up

**What to check BEFORE giving potassium:**
- Current potassium level
- Kidney function (BUN, creatinine) — kidneys get rid of potassium
- Urine output — **if the patient isn't making urine, hold potassium and call the provider**
- ECG/cardiac monitor in place for IV administration
- IV site is good (no infiltration)

**What to monitor AFTER giving:**
- Potassium level (recheck)
- ECG for changes
- Signs of hyperkalemia if too much was given
- IV site (irritation, phlebitis)

---

#### ⬆️ HYPERKALEMIA (High Potassium — above 5.0 mEq/L)

**This is a medical emergency when potassium is very high (> 6.0 mEq/L)**

**What causes it:**
- Kidney failure (kidneys can't get rid of potassium)
- Potassium-sparing diuretics (spironolactone)
- ACE inhibitors, ARBs
- Tissue damage, burns, crush injuries (potassium leaks out of damaged cells)
- Acidosis (low pH pushes potassium out of cells)
- Too much potassium replacement

**Signs and symptoms:**
- Muscle weakness (yes — both high AND low potassium cause weakness!)
- **ECG changes:** **Peaked (tall, pointy) T waves** — this is the classic sign!
  - Also: widened QRS complex, and eventually the heart can stop
- Nausea, diarrhea, cramping
- Numbness, tingling
- **Cardiac arrest** if not treated

> *Think of it this way:* High potassium = **peaked T waves** = the heart is in danger

**Emergency treatment of hyperkalemia (know this order!):**

| Treatment | What It Does |
|---|---|
| **Calcium gluconate IV** | **Protects the heart** — stabilizes the cardiac membrane (does NOT lower K+) — give this FIRST |
| **Insulin + glucose (D50) IV** | **Shifts potassium INTO the cells** — temporary fix. Give glucose to prevent hypoglycemia |
| **Sodium bicarbonate IV** | Shifts potassium into cells (especially if acidotic) |
| **Albuterol nebulizer** | Shifts potassium into cells |
| **Kayexalate (sodium polystyrene sulfonate)** | Binds potassium in the gut and removes it from the body (takes hours) |
| **Dialysis** | Removes potassium from the blood — used if kidneys aren't working |

> *Think of it this way:* **"C-I-D"** — **C**alcium first (protect the heart), **I**nsulin + glucose (shift K+ into cells), **D**ialysis if needed (remove K+ from body)

**Patient teaching for potassium:**
- Eat potassium-rich foods if taking a potassium-wasting diuretic: bananas, oranges, potatoes, spinach, avocados
- Avoid potassium-rich foods if you have hyperkalemia or take potassium-sparing diuretics
- Report muscle weakness, irregular heartbeat, numbness/tingling
- Take oral KCl with food and water
- Keep all lab appointments

---

### 🔹 Magnesium (MgSO4 — Magnesium Sulfate)

**Normal range: 1.5–2.5 mEq/L**

**What it is used for:**
- **Hypomagnesemia** (low magnesium replacement)
- **Preeclampsia / eclampsia** — to **prevent seizures** in pregnant patients with high blood pressure
- **Torsades de pointes** (a specific type of dangerous heart rhythm)
- Severe asthma (sometimes)

> *Think of it this way:* Magnesium is the "calming" electrolyte — it calms the heart, calms the muscles, calms seizures.

---

#### ⬇️ HYPOMAGNESEMIA (Low Magnesium — below 1.5 mEq/L)

**What causes it:**
- Alcoholism (very common cause)
- Malnutrition
- Diuretics
- Diarrhea
- Certain medications

**Signs and symptoms:**
- Tremors, muscle twitching, cramps
- Hyperactive reflexes
- Seizures
- Cardiac arrhythmias (especially torsades de pointes)
- Often happens with low potassium and low calcium — they travel together!

> *Think of it this way:* Low magnesium = everything is **twitchy and excitable**

**Important fact:** If potassium is low and you can't get it to come up with replacement, **check the magnesium!** You often need to fix the magnesium first before the potassium will correct.

---

#### ⬆️ MAGNESIUM TOXICITY — very important for NCLEX!

This is especially important in **obstetric (OB) nursing** when magnesium sulfate is given to prevent seizures in preeclampsia.

**Signs of magnesium toxicity (in order as level rises):**

| Magnesium Level | What Happens |
|---|---|
| 4–7 mEq/L | **Loss of deep tendon reflexes (DTRs)** — this is the first sign! |
| 7–10 mEq/L | **Respiratory depression** (breathing slows down) |
| 10–15 mEq/L | **Hypotension** (blood pressure drops dangerously) |
| > 15 mEq/L | **Cardiac arrest** |

**Before EACH dose of magnesium sulfate, check THREE things:**

1. ✅ **Deep tendon reflexes (DTRs)** — if reflexes are absent, **HOLD the dose and notify the provider**
2. ✅ **Respiratory rate** — if RR is **less than 12 breaths/min**, **HOLD the dose and notify the provider**
3. ✅ **Urine output** — if less than 30 mL/hour, HOLD and notify (kidneys excrete magnesium — if they aren't working, it builds up)

> *Think of it this way:* Before giving mag — check **"R-R-U"**: **R**eflexes, **R**espiratory rate, **U**rine output

**Antidote for magnesium toxicity:**

⚠️ **CALCIUM GLUCONATE** — keep it at the bedside whenever a patient is on a magnesium sulfate drip!

- Calcium gluconate reverses the effects of magnesium toxicity
- This is a critical NCLEX fact — they will test you on this!

**What to monitor during magnesium infusion:**
- DTRs (before each dose and regularly)
- Respiratory rate and depth
- Blood pressure
- Urine output (I&O)
- Magnesium levels
- Fetal heart rate (in OB patients)
- Level of consciousness
- Have calcium gluconate at the bedside

---

### 🔹 Calcium (Calcium Gluconate, Calcium Chloride)

**Normal range: 8.5–10.5 mg/dL (total calcium)**

**What it is used for:**
- **Hypocalcemia** (low calcium replacement)
- **Hyperkalemia emergency** (protects the heart)
- **Magnesium toxicity antidote**
- Osteoporosis prevention (oral forms)

---

#### ⬇️ HYPOCALCEMIA (Low Calcium — below 8.5 mg/dL)

**What causes it:**
- Hypoparathyroidism (parathyroid glands removed or not working — often after thyroid surgery)
- Kidney disease
- Vitamin D deficiency
- Pancreatitis
- Blood transfusions (citrate in stored blood binds calcium)

**Signs and symptoms — the NCLEX loves these!**

- **Numbness and tingling** — around the mouth (perioral) and in the fingers and toes
- Muscle spasms, cramps
- **Seizures** (if severe)
- **Laryngospasm** — the airway can spasm (this is dangerous!)
- Cardiac arrhythmias

**Two classic signs to know:**

| Sign | How to Test It | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| **Trousseau sign** | Inflate a blood pressure cuff on the arm above systolic pressure for 3 minutes | The hand spasms (carpopedal spasm — the hand curls in) |
| **Chvostek sign** | Tap on the facial nerve (in front of the ear) | The facial muscles twitch on that side |

> *Think of it this way:*
> - "**Trousseau** = **T**ournament arm cuff = **T**wisting hand" (blood pressure cuff makes hand spasm)
> - "**Chvostek** = **Ch**eek" (tap the cheek, face twitches)

---

#### ⬆️ HYPERCALCEMIA (High Calcium — above 10.5 mg/dL)

**What causes it:**
- Hyperparathyroidism (too much parathyroid hormone)
- Cancer (especially bone cancers, lung cancer)
- Too much vitamin D
- Immobility (calcium leaves the bones)

**Signs and symptoms:**
- "**Bones, stones, groans, and moans**"
  - **Bones:** Bone pain, fractures
  - **Stones:** Kidney stones
  - **Groans:** Abdominal pain, constipation, nausea
  - **Moans:** Confusion, fatigue, depression

> *Think of it this way:* High calcium = everything slows down and hurts

---

#### Giving IV Calcium — Safety Points:

- Give IV calcium **SLOWLY** — too fast can cause **cardiac arrest**
- **Calcium chloride** is much more concentrated and irritating to veins — prefer a **central line**
- **Calcium gluconate** is gentler — can be given through a peripheral IV (but still give slowly)
- Monitor **cardiac rhythm** during administration
- **Do NOT mix calcium with certain IV fluids** — check compatibility
- Watch the IV site — calcium is a vesicant (causes tissue damage if it leaks)

**What to check BEFORE giving calcium:**
- Current calcium level
- Cardiac monitor in place
- IV site is good (especially for calcium chloride)
- Check for interactions — calcium and digoxin together increase the risk of digoxin toxicity

**What to monitor AFTER giving:**
- Calcium levels
- ECG
- Signs of hypercalcemia if too much was given
- IV site

**Important drug interaction:**
- **Calcium + digoxin** = increased risk of **digoxin toxicity** — if a patient is on digoxin, give calcium very carefully and monitor closely!

### 🔹 Sodium — Brief Overview

**Normal range: 135–145 mEq/L**

---

#### ⬇️ HYPONATREMIA (Low Sodium — below 135 mEq/L)

**Causes:** Too much water (dilutional — like SIADH), certain medications, kidney problems, excessive sweating with only water replacement

**Signs and symptoms:**
- Confusion, headache
- **Seizures** (if severe)
- Nausea, vomiting
- **Cerebral edema** (brain swelling) — this is the dangerous part
- Muscle cramps, weakness

**CRITICAL nursing point:**
- **Correct hyponatremia SLOWLY** — if you raise sodium too fast, it can cause **osmotic demyelination syndrome** (brain damage)
- Usually no faster than 8–12 mEq/L in 24 hours
- Monitor sodium levels frequently during correction
- Fluid restriction for dilutional hyponatremia

> *Think of it this way:* Low sodium = **watery brain** (too much water dilutes the sodium → brain swells)

---

#### ⬆️ HYPERNATREMIA (High Sodium — above 145 mEq/L)

**Causes:** Dehydration, not drinking enough water, diabetes insipidus, excessive sodium intake

**Signs and symptoms:**
- **Thirst** (intense)
- **Dry, sticky mucous membranes**
- Flushed, dry skin
- Altered level of consciousness (LOC) — restless, agitated, confused
- Elevated temperature
- Decreased urine output (concentrated urine)

**Treatment:**
- Replace fluids gradually (usually hypotonic solutions like 0.45% NaCl)
- **Correct slowly** — too fast can cause cerebral edema
- Monitor sodium levels and I&O

> *Think of it this way:* High sodium = **dry and thirsty** (not enough water)

## 🚨 Critical Safety Rules — Do NOT Forget!

1. **Never stop seizure medications abruptly** → status epilepticus
2. **Never IV push potassium** → fatal cardiac arrest
3. **Always check DTRs, RR, and urine output before giving magnesium** → toxicity prevention
4. **Keep calcium gluconate at bedside** for any patient on a magnesium drip → it's the antidote
5. **Give IV phenytoin slowly** (≤ 50 mg/min) in normal saline only → arrhythmia prevention
6. **Give IV calcium slowly** → cardiac arrest risk
7. **Correct sodium slowly** → prevent brain damage
8. **Valproic acid + pregnancy = NO** → teratogenic (birth defects)
