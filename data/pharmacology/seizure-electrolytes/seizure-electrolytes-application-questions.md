## Question 1 — Phenytoin Toxicity

A nurse is caring for a patient taking phenytoin (Dilantin) for seizures. The patient reports dizziness and the nurse notices the patient's eyes are moving rapidly back and forth. The patient's gait is unsteady when walking to the bathroom. Which action should the nurse take FIRST?

A. Give the next scheduled dose of phenytoin and recheck in 1 hour
B. Hold the phenytoin dose and notify the provider
C. Teach the patient about good oral hygiene
D. Administer a loading dose of phenytoin

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — Hold the phenytoin dose and notify the provider**

Let's break this down:

The patient is showing classic signs of **phenytoin toxicity**:
- **Nystagmus** (eyes moving back and forth) — usually the first sign
- **Ataxia** (unsteady gait)
- Dizziness

Remember **N-A-S**: **N**ystagmus → **A**taxia → **S**lurred speech = phenytoin toxicity

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — Giving MORE phenytoin to a patient with toxicity signs is dangerous. This would make things worse!
- **B is correct** — When you see toxicity signs, hold the medication and notify the provider. A STAT drug level will be ordered.
- **C is wrong** — Oral hygiene teaching is important for phenytoin (gingival hyperplasia), but it's not the priority right now. The patient has toxicity signs — safety first!
- **D is wrong** — A loading dose would give even MORE of the drug to an already toxic patient. Very dangerous.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** When a question shows you symptoms that look like toxicity or an adverse effect, the answer is almost always "hold the med and notify the provider." Don't give more of the drug that's causing the problem!
</details>

## Question 2 — IV Potassium Safety

A nurse receives an order to administer potassium chloride (KCl) 40 mEq IV to a patient with a potassium level of 2.8 mEq/L. Which action by the nurse is MOST important for patient safety?

A. Administer the KCl by slow IV push over 5 minutes
B. Add the KCl to a premixed IV bag and infuse using an IV pump at no more than 10–20 mEq/hour
C. Infuse the KCl as fast as possible because the level is critically low
D. Give the potassium orally instead since IV is never used

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — Add the KCl to a premixed IV bag and infuse using an IV pump at no more than 10–20 mEq/hour**

This is a **critical safety question**!

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — **NEVER give KCl by IV push!** This is one of the most dangerous medication errors. IV push potassium can cause **fatal cardiac arrest** within minutes.
- **B is correct** — IV potassium must ALWAYS be diluted in an IV bag and given slowly using a pump. The maximum rate is usually 10–20 mEq/hour. The patient should be on a cardiac monitor.
- **C is wrong** — Even though the potassium is low, giving it too fast is deadly. "Fast IV potassium" = "fatal arrhythmia." There is no situation where you push or rapidly infuse KCl.
- **D is wrong** — IV potassium is absolutely used when levels are critically low (< 3.0 mEq/L) or the patient can't take oral. Oral is preferred when possible, but IV is necessary here.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** If you see any answer choice that says "IV push KCl" — it is ALWAYS wrong. This is a never-do in nursing. The NCLEX will test you on this!
</details>

## Question 3 — Magnesium Toxicity in Preeclampsia

A nurse is caring for a patient with preeclampsia receiving magnesium sulfate IV. On assessment, the nurse finds: respiratory rate 10 breaths/min, absent deep tendon reflexes, and blood pressure 88/50 mmHg. What should the nurse do FIRST?

A. Continue the infusion and recheck vitals in 30 minutes
B. Increase the magnesium infusion rate as ordered
C. Stop the magnesium sulfate infusion and notify the provider
D. Give a dose of furosemide (Lasix) to increase urine output

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: C — Stop the magnesium sulfate infusion and notify the provider**

This patient is showing **magnesium toxicity**:
- RR of 10 (below 12 = respiratory depression)
- Absent DTRs (reflexes gone = first sign of toxicity)
- Hypotension (BP 88/50)

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — This patient is in danger NOW. Waiting 30 minutes with an RR of 10 and no reflexes could lead to respiratory arrest. Never wait when you see toxicity!
- **B is wrong** — Increasing the magnesium would make toxicity worse and could cause cardiac arrest. This is the opposite of what you should do.
- **C is correct** — Stop the magnesium immediately! Then notify the provider. Have **calcium gluconate** ready — it's the antidote for magnesium toxicity.
- **D is wrong** — Furosemide doesn't treat magnesium toxicity. While good urine output helps clear magnesium, the immediate action is to STOP the infusion.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** Remember the three checks before each magnesium dose — **R-R-U** (Reflexes, Respiratory rate, Urine output). If any are abnormal, HOLD and notify!
</details>

## Question 4 — Hyperkalemia ECG & Emergency Treatment

A nurse reviews a patient's lab results: potassium 7.1 mEq/L. The ECG shows tall, peaked T waves. The provider orders multiple medications. Which should the nurse administer FIRST?

A. Kayexalate (sodium polystyrene sulfonate) orally
B. Regular insulin with dextrose 50% IV
C. Calcium gluconate IV
D. Albuterol nebulizer treatment

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: C — Calcium gluconate IV**

This is a **hyperkalemia emergency** (K+ 7.1 with peaked T waves on ECG). The heart is at risk for fatal arrhythmia.

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — Kayexalate removes potassium from the body through the GI tract, but it takes hours to work. This patient needs something RIGHT NOW.
- **B is wrong** — Insulin + glucose shifts potassium into the cells and works faster than kayexalate, but the FIRST priority is to protect the heart.
- **C is correct** — Calcium gluconate is given FIRST because it **stabilizes the heart muscle** immediately. It does NOT lower the potassium level — it protects the heart while other treatments work to lower K+.
- **D is wrong** — Albuterol can shift potassium into cells, but it's not the first priority. Protect the heart first!

*Think of it this way:* **"C-I-D"** = **C**alcium first (protect heart) → **I**nsulin + glucose (shift K+ into cells) → **D**ialysis if needed (remove K+ from body)

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** In hyperkalemia, calcium gluconate is always first. It doesn't fix the potassium — it buys time to protect the heart.
</details>

## Question 5 — Hypocalcemia Signs

A nurse is assessing a patient 24 hours after a thyroidectomy. The patient reports tingling around the mouth and in the fingertips. The nurse taps on the patient's facial nerve and observes facial twitching on that side. What does the nurse suspect?

A. Hyperkalemia
B. Hypomagnesemia
C. Hypocalcemia
D. Hypernatremia

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: C — Hypocalcemia**

Let's connect the dots:

- **Thyroidectomy** → the parathyroid glands are near the thyroid and may have been accidentally damaged or removed → parathyroid hormone (PTH) drops → calcium drops → **hypocalcemia**
- **Tingling around the mouth and fingertips** = perioral and peripheral numbness/tingling = classic hypocalcemia
- **Tapping the facial nerve and getting twitching** = **Chvostek sign** = classic sign of hypocalcemia

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — Hyperkalemia causes muscle weakness and peaked T waves, not facial twitching or tingling.
- **B is wrong** — Hypomagnesemia can cause similar neurological signs, but after thyroidectomy, hypocalcemia is the expected complication. Always think about the clinical context!
- **C is correct** — Post-thyroidectomy + tingling + positive Chvostek sign = hypocalcemia until proven otherwise.
- **D is wrong** — Hypernatremia causes thirst, dry mucous membranes, and altered LOC — not tingling and facial twitching.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** After thyroidectomy, ALWAYS monitor for hypocalcemia. Keep IV calcium at the bedside. Check for Trousseau and Chvostek signs.
</details>

## Question 6 — Seizure Medication Teaching

A nurse is teaching a patient newly prescribed phenytoin (Dilantin) for seizures. Which statement by the patient shows they understood the teaching?

A. "I can stop taking this medicine once I haven't had a seizure for 2 weeks."
B. "I should brush and floss my teeth carefully every day."
C. "I can take my birth control pills the same way as before — they'll work fine."
D. "If I miss a dose, I should take a double dose to catch up."

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — "I should brush and floss my teeth carefully every day."**

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — You should NEVER stop seizure medications abruptly, even if you haven't had a seizure. Stopping suddenly can cause status epilepticus. The provider will decide when and how to stop.
- **B is correct** — Phenytoin causes gingival hyperplasia (overgrown gums). Good oral hygiene helps prevent this. This shows the patient understood!
- **C is wrong** — Phenytoin is an enzyme inducer — it makes the liver break down other drugs faster, including birth control pills. Oral contraceptives may NOT work as well. The patient needs to discuss alternative birth control methods.
- **D is wrong** — Taking a double dose of phenytoin is dangerous because of its narrow therapeutic range. A double dose could cause toxicity. If a dose is missed, follow the provider's instructions.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** For "which statement shows understanding" questions, look for the answer that matches correct teaching. Wrong answers usually contain common patient misconceptions.
</details>

## Question 7 — Valproic Acid & Pregnancy

A nurse is reviewing medications with a 28-year-old female patient who just found out she is pregnant. The patient takes valproic acid (Depakote) for seizures. Which action by the nurse is MOST appropriate?

A. Tell the patient to immediately stop taking valproic acid
B. Reassure the patient that valproic acid is safe during pregnancy
C. Notify the provider immediately about the pregnancy
D. Double the dose of valproic acid to better protect against seizures during pregnancy

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: C — Notify the provider immediately about the pregnancy**

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — Even though valproic acid is teratogenic, you should NEVER tell a patient to stop a seizure medication abruptly. This can cause status epilepticus, which is also dangerous to the mother and baby. The provider needs to make this decision.
- **B is wrong** — Valproic acid is **NOT safe in pregnancy**. It is teratogenic and can cause neural tube defects (like spina bifida) and other birth defects. This would be giving false reassurance.
- **C is correct** — The provider needs to know right away so they can evaluate the risks and potentially switch to a safer seizure medication. This is a time-sensitive situation.
- **D is wrong** — Increasing the dose would increase the risk to the baby. There is no reason to double the dose.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** When you're not sure what to do with a medication situation, "notify the provider" is often correct — especially when there's a new finding (like pregnancy) that changes the risk/benefit of a drug. But make sure it truly applies to the scenario!
</details>

## Question 8 — Lab Interpretation & Hold-or-Give

The nurse is about to give the 0800 dose of phenytoin (Dilantin) to a patient. The morning lab results show a phenytoin level of 24 mcg/mL. What should the nurse do?

A. Give the dose as ordered — the level is within normal range
B. Hold the dose and notify the provider
C. Give half the dose and recheck the level in 4 hours
D. Crush the tablet and mix it in applesauce to help it absorb better

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — Hold the dose and notify the provider**

The **therapeutic range for phenytoin is 10–20 mcg/mL**. A level of **24 mcg/mL is ABOVE the therapeutic range** — this is a toxic level!

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — 24 mcg/mL is NOT within normal range. The therapeutic range is 10–20. This patient is already at risk for toxicity symptoms (nystagmus, ataxia, slurred speech).
- **B is correct** — The level is toxic. Hold the dose and notify the provider. They may adjust the dose or hold additional doses until the level comes down.
- **C is wrong** — Nurses cannot independently modify a medication dose without a provider order. Also, giving any amount of the drug when the level is already toxic is inappropriate.
- **D is wrong** — The absorption method isn't the issue here. The drug level is already too high. Also, not all phenytoin formulations should be crushed.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** Always check the drug level before giving a drug with a narrow therapeutic range. If the level is above the therapeutic range, HOLD and notify!
</details>

## Question 9 — SATA (Select All That Apply)

A nurse is monitoring a patient receiving IV potassium chloride (KCl). Which nursing actions are appropriate? **Select all that apply.**

A. Infuse using an IV pump
B. Monitor the patient's ECG/cardiac rhythm
C. Give by IV push if the patient's potassium is critically low
D. Check urine output before and during infusion
E. Assess the IV site for signs of infiltration
F. Verify kidney function (BUN, creatinine)

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: A, B, D, E, F**

Let's go through each one:

- **A — Correct ✅** Always use an IV pump for KCl infusion to control the rate carefully. Too fast = fatal arrhythmia.
- **B — Correct ✅** The patient needs cardiac monitoring during IV potassium administration. ECG changes can indicate the potassium is rising too fast.
- **C — WRONG ❌** NEVER give KCl by IV push — no matter how low the potassium is. There is NO situation where IV push potassium is acceptable. IV push KCl = cardiac arrest.
- **D — Correct ✅** Kidneys excrete potassium. If the kidneys aren't working (low urine output), potassium can build up to dangerous levels. Always check kidney function.
- **E — Correct ✅** IV potassium is very irritating to veins. If the IV infiltrates, it can damage surrounding tissue. Always assess the IV site.
- **F — Correct ✅** BUN and creatinine tell you how well the kidneys are working. Poor kidney function = potassium accumulates = hyperkalemia risk.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** For SATA questions, evaluate EACH option independently. Ask yourself "Is this true?" for each one. Don't let one wrong answer make you doubt the right ones!
</details>

## Question 10 — Seizure Safety

A nurse enters a patient's room and finds the patient having a tonic-clonic seizure. The patient is in bed. Which actions should the nurse take? **Select all that apply.**

A. Insert a padded tongue blade into the patient's mouth to prevent tongue biting
B. Restrain the patient's arms and legs to prevent injury
C. Lower the head of the bed and remove the pillow
D. Time the duration of the seizure
E. Place the patient on their side after the seizure stops
F. Stay with the patient and call for help

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: C, D, E, F**

- **A — WRONG ❌** NEVER put anything in a patient's mouth during a seizure. Not a tongue blade, not your fingers, nothing. This can cause broken teeth, aspiration, or injury to the nurse.
- **B — WRONG ❌** NEVER restrain a patient during a seizure. Restraining can cause fractures or musculoskeletal injuries. You can gently guide, but do NOT hold them down.
- **C — Correct ✅** Lowering the bed and removing the pillow helps protect the airway and reduces fall risk.
- **D — Correct ✅** Timing the seizure is very important. If it lasts more than 5 minutes, it may be status epilepticus — a medical emergency requiring immediate treatment.
- **E — Correct ✅** After the seizure stops (postictal period), turn the patient on their side (recovery position) to keep the airway clear and prevent aspiration.
- **F — Correct ✅** Never leave a seizing patient alone. Stay with them and call for help (use the call light or yell for assistance).

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** During a seizure: **Protect, Time, Position (after), Document.** Don't put anything in the mouth. Don't restrain. These are common wrong answer choices the NCLEX uses as distractors!
</details>

## Question 11 — Provider Notification

A nurse is caring for a patient receiving a magnesium sulfate drip for preeclampsia. Which finding requires the nurse to IMMEDIATELY notify the provider?

A. The patient reports feeling warm and flushed
B. Deep tendon reflexes are 2+ (normal)
C. Urine output is 15 mL over the past hour
D. The patient's blood pressure has come down from 170/110 to 148/92

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: C — Urine output is 15 mL over the past hour**

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — Feeling warm and flushed is a common, expected side effect of magnesium sulfate. It causes vasodilation (blood vessels open up). This is not dangerous.
- **B is wrong** — 2+ DTRs are normal. You WANT to see reflexes present. Absent reflexes (0) would be a problem = magnesium toxicity.
- **C is correct** — Urine output below 30 mL/hour is dangerous in a patient on magnesium. The kidneys excrete magnesium. If urine output drops, magnesium can build up to toxic levels. This needs to be reported immediately.
- **D is wrong** — The blood pressure coming down is the GOAL of treatment in preeclampsia. Going from 170/110 to 148/92 is a good sign.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** For "notify the provider" questions, look for the finding that is unexpected, abnormal, or dangerous — not the expected effects of the treatment.
</details>

## Question 12 — Priority/First Action

A nurse receives morning lab results for four patients. Which patient should the nurse assess FIRST?

A. Patient A: Potassium 3.8 mEq/L
B. Patient B: Sodium 133 mEq/L
C. Patient C: Potassium 6.9 mEq/L
D. Patient D: Calcium 9.2 mg/dL

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: C — Patient C with potassium of 6.9 mEq/L**

Let's check each value:
- **Patient A: K+ 3.8** — Normal (3.5–5.0). No immediate concern.
- **Patient B: Na+ 133** — Slightly low (normal 135–145), but only mildly. Monitor but not an emergency.
- **Patient C: K+ 6.9** — **Critically high!** This is severe hyperkalemia. The heart is at risk for fatal arrhythmias. This patient needs immediate assessment, ECG, and treatment.
- **Patient D: Ca2+ 9.2** — Normal (8.5–10.5). No concern.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** In priority questions, look for the value that is most life-threatening. High potassium affects the HEART and can cause cardiac arrest. Whenever you see a potassium above 6.0, think "EMERGENCY!"
</details>

## Question 13 — Application

A nurse is about to administer the next dose of magnesium sulfate to a patient with preeclampsia. Upon assessment, the nurse finds the patient's patellar (knee-jerk) reflex is absent. Respiratory rate is 14 breaths/min. What should the nurse do?

A. Give the dose — the respiratory rate is within normal limits
B. Hold the dose and notify the provider
C. Give half the ordered dose
D. Increase the infusion rate because the patient may be having breakthrough seizures

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — Hold the dose and notify the provider**

Even though the respiratory rate is 14 (which is normal), **absent deep tendon reflexes** is a sign of magnesium toxicity.

Remember the three checks: **R-R-U**
- ✅ Reflexes — ABSENT (⚠️ ABNORMAL — this is the first sign of toxicity!)
- ✅ Respiratory rate — 14 (normal, but toxicity has already started)
- ✅ Urine output — not mentioned, but reflexes alone are enough to hold

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — Yes, the RR is okay, but absent reflexes means magnesium is building up. If you give more, respiratory depression could be next.
- **B is correct** — Absent DTRs = hold the magnesium and call the provider. Period.
- **C is wrong** — Nurses cannot independently reduce a dose. Also, any magnesium when reflexes are already absent could push the patient into deeper toxicity.
- **D is wrong** — Increasing the magnesium when there are toxicity signs could cause respiratory arrest or cardiac arrest.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** If EITHER reflexes are absent OR respiratory rate is < 12 OR urine output is < 30 mL/hr — HOLD the magnesium. You don't need all three to be abnormal. Just one is enough!
</details>

## Question 14 — Patient Teaching

A patient asks the nurse, "Why do I need to take my potassium pill with food? Can't I just swallow it with water?" What is the nurse's BEST response?

A. "Taking it with food helps the potassium absorb into your blood faster."
B. "Potassium can be very irritating to your stomach, so food helps protect your stomach lining."
C. "It doesn't really matter — you can take it however you prefer."
D. "You should actually take potassium on an empty stomach for best results."

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — "Potassium can be very irritating to your stomach, so food helps protect your stomach lining."**

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — The reason for taking KCl with food is NOT about absorption. It's about protecting the stomach from irritation.
- **B is correct** — Oral potassium chloride is a strong irritant to the GI tract. Taking it with food and a full glass of water helps cushion the stomach and reduces nausea, vomiting, and the risk of GI ulcers.
- **C is wrong** — It DOES matter. Taking KCl on an empty stomach can cause significant GI irritation, nausea, and even ulceration.
- **D is wrong** — The opposite is true. Never take potassium on an empty stomach.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** When a patient asks "why," give a clear, honest, simple explanation. The NCLEX wants nurses who can educate patients — not just follow orders without understanding the reason.
</details>

## Question 15 — Hold-or-Give Scenario

The nurse is about to administer phenytoin (Dilantin) IV to a patient. The current IV is running dextrose 5% in water (D5W). What should the nurse do?

A. Piggyback the phenytoin into the D5W line
B. Flush the line with normal saline, then administer the phenytoin with normal saline
C. Administer the phenytoin as an IV push as fast as possible
D. Mix the phenytoin directly into the D5W bag

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — Flush the line with normal saline, then administer the phenytoin with normal saline**

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — Phenytoin is NOT compatible with dextrose solutions. It will **crystallize** (form tiny particles) if mixed with D5W, which can block the IV line and harm the patient.
- **B is correct** — Phenytoin should only be mixed with **normal saline (0.9% NaCl)**. If the patient has a dextrose line, flush it with NS first, give the phenytoin in NS, then flush again before restarting the dextrose.
- **C is wrong** — Phenytoin IV must be given SLOWLY — never faster than 50 mg/min. Rapid IV push can cause cardiac arrhythmias and hypotension.
- **D is wrong** — Same as A. Never mix phenytoin with dextrose. It precipitates.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** "Phenytoin + dextrose = crystals." Remember this! If you see D5W and phenytoin in the same question, the answer involves switching to normal saline.
</details>

## Question 16 — Application

A nurse is caring for a patient with chronic kidney disease. The patient's most recent potassium level is 5.8 mEq/L. The patient asks for a snack. Which snack choice should the nurse recommend?

A. A banana
B. An orange
C. A baked potato with the skin
D. Plain crackers with a small amount of jelly

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: D — Plain crackers with a small amount of jelly**

This patient has **hyperkalemia** (K+ 5.8, above 5.0). They need to **avoid high-potassium foods**.

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — Bananas are VERY high in potassium. This is probably the most classic example. Not appropriate for a patient with elevated K+.
- **B is wrong** — Oranges and orange juice are high in potassium. Avoid in hyperkalemia.
- **C is wrong** — Potatoes (especially with skin) are very high in potassium. Not appropriate.
- **D is correct** — Crackers and jelly are low in potassium. This is a safe snack for a patient who needs to limit potassium intake.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** High-potassium foods to remember: **bananas, oranges, potatoes, spinach, avocados, tomatoes, dried fruits.** On the NCLEX, if a patient has high K+, steer them away from these!
</details>

## Question 17 — Application

A nurse is administering IV calcium gluconate to a patient with severe hypocalcemia. The patient is also taking digoxin (Lanoxin). Which action is MOST important?

A. Give the calcium as quickly as possible to correct the level
B. Monitor the cardiac rhythm closely during and after administration
C. Hold the calcium because it cannot be given with digoxin
D. Switch to calcium chloride because it works faster

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — Monitor the cardiac rhythm closely during and after administration**

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — IV calcium must always be given SLOWLY. Rapid administration can cause cardiac arrest. Speed is never the answer with IV calcium.
- **B is correct** — Calcium increases the effect of digoxin on the heart, raising the risk of **digoxin toxicity** (dangerous arrhythmias). Close cardiac monitoring is essential. Give the calcium slowly and watch the ECG carefully.
- **C is wrong** — Calcium CAN be given with digoxin when medically necessary (like severe hypocalcemia). It just requires extra caution and close monitoring. You don't automatically hold it.
- **D is wrong** — Calcium chloride is more concentrated and more irritating to veins. Switching to a stronger form is not safer — it's riskier. Also, it doesn't solve the digoxin interaction concern.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** When the NCLEX gives you a drug interaction scenario, the answer is usually about monitoring more closely — not about refusing to give the drug entirely (unless it's truly contraindicated).
</details>

## Question 18 — SATA

A nurse is providing discharge teaching to a patient prescribed levetiracetam (Keppra) for seizures. Which instructions should the nurse include? **Select all that apply.**

A. "Do not stop this medication suddenly — it must be tapered."
B. "Report any changes in mood, irritability, or feelings of depression."
C. "You need monthly blood draws to check your Keppra level."
D. "Be careful with activities like driving until you know how this medication affects you."
E. "Notify the provider if you develop a severe rash."
F. "Take the medication at the same time each day."

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: A, B, D, E, F**

- **A — Correct ✅** ALL seizure medications should never be stopped abruptly. Always taper under provider guidance.
- **B — Correct ✅** Behavioral changes (irritability, aggression, mood swings, depression) are the key side effects of levetiracetam. Patients and families should watch for these.
- **C — WRONG ❌** Levetiracetam does NOT require routine drug level monitoring. This is one of its advantages over phenytoin and valproic acid!
- **D — Correct ✅** Keppra can cause drowsiness and dizziness. Patients should be cautious with driving and operating machinery.
- **E — Correct ✅** Any severe rash with any anticonvulsant should be reported. Although rash is more associated with phenytoin and carbamazepine, it's still good practice to report.
- **F — Correct ✅** Consistent timing helps maintain steady drug levels and better seizure control.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** One of the biggest differences between Keppra and other seizure meds is that Keppra does NOT need routine drug level monitoring. If you see this in a question, it's probably a distractor!

## Question 19 — Priority/First Action

A nurse is caring for a postoperative thyroidectomy patient who suddenly develops stridor (harsh, high-pitched breathing sound), severe muscle spasms, and a positive Trousseau sign. What is the nurse's FIRST action?

A. Check the most recent calcium level
B. Call the provider and prepare to administer IV calcium gluconate
C. Position the patient supine with the head flat
D. Administer oral calcium carbonate tablets

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — Call the provider and prepare to administer IV calcium gluconate**

This patient has **severe, symptomatic hypocalcemia** — likely from accidental damage to the parathyroid glands during thyroidectomy.

The signs are classic:
- **Stridor** = laryngospasm (the airway is narrowing — THIS IS AN EMERGENCY)
- **Severe muscle spasms**
- **Positive Trousseau sign** (hand spasm with BP cuff)

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — While checking labs is important, this patient has an AIRWAY emergency (stridor). You don't wait for lab results when the patient can't breathe properly. Act NOW.
- **B is correct** — This is an emergency. The patient needs IV calcium gluconate immediately to reverse the hypocalcemia and stop the laryngospasm. Call the provider and have the calcium ready. Airway is always the priority!
- **C is wrong** — Positioning won't fix hypocalcemia or laryngospasm. The patient needs calcium replacement urgently.
- **D is wrong** — Oral calcium is too slow for an emergency like this. With stridor and laryngospasm, the patient may not even be able to swallow safely. IV calcium is needed.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** When you see stridor (airway compromise) + muscle spasms + post-thyroidectomy — think hypocalcemia emergency. The answer involves IV calcium, not oral, and not "check labs first."
</details>

## Question 20 — Application

A nurse is reviewing the medication list for a female patient of childbearing age with epilepsy. The patient is currently taking valproic acid (Depakote) and tells the nurse she and her partner are thinking about trying to get pregnant. Which response by the nurse is MOST appropriate?

A. "That's wonderful! Valproic acid is safe to continue during pregnancy."
B. "You should stop your valproic acid now so it's out of your system before you get pregnant."
C. "It's important that you talk to your provider before getting pregnant. Valproic acid carries risks for an unborn baby, and your provider may want to switch you to a safer medication first."
D. "Pregnancy is not possible while taking seizure medications."

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: C — "It's important that you talk to your provider before getting pregnant..."**

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — Valproic acid is NOT safe in pregnancy. It is one of the most teratogenic seizure medications and carries a high risk of neural tube defects (like spina bifida). This is false reassurance.
- **B is wrong** — A nurse should NEVER tell a patient to stop a seizure medication on their own. Stopping abruptly can cause status epilepticus. The provider needs to make the plan.
- **C is correct** — This answer is honest, educational, and directs the patient to the provider for preconception planning. The provider may switch to a less teratogenic seizure medication (like levetiracetam or lamotrigine) before the patient tries to conceive.
- **D is wrong** — This is false. People on seizure medications can become pregnant. The issue is the RISK of certain medications to the fetus, not that pregnancy is impossible.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** When a question involves pregnancy + a teratogenic drug, the best answer usually involves provider notification and preconception planning — not abrupt stopping, not false reassurance, and not telling the patient they can't get pregnant.
</details>

## Question 21 — Lab Interpretation & Action

A nurse reviews the following lab results for a patient on carbamazepine (Tegretol):
- Carbamazepine level: 6 mcg/mL (therapeutic range: 4–12 mcg/mL)
- WBC: 1,800 cells/mm³ (normal: 5,000–10,000)
- Platelets: 180,000/mm³ (normal: 150,000–400,000)

Which finding should the nurse report to the provider IMMEDIATELY?

A. The carbamazepine level of 6 mcg/mL
B. The WBC of 1,800 cells/mm³
C. The platelet count of 180,000/mm³
D. All values are within normal limits — no action needed

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — The WBC of 1,800 cells/mm³**

Let's interpret each lab:
- **Carbamazepine 6 mcg/mL** — This is within the therapeutic range (4–12). ✅ Normal.
- **WBC 1,800** — This is **critically low** (normal is 5,000–10,000). This could indicate **agranulocytosis** — a rare but serious side effect of carbamazepine where the bone marrow stops making enough white blood cells. The patient is at high risk for life-threatening infection.
- **Platelets 180,000** — This is within normal range (150,000–400,000). ✅ Normal.

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — The drug level is therapeutic. No action needed for this value.
- **B is correct** — A WBC of 1,800 is dangerously low. Carbamazepine can cause aplastic anemia and agranulocytosis. This must be reported immediately.
- **C is wrong** — Platelets are normal. No concern here.
- **D is wrong** — The WBC is NOT normal. Not recognizing this could put the patient in danger.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** Always look at ALL the lab values in the question. The NCLEX sometimes puts a normal drug level alongside an abnormal CBC to see if you catch the blood disorder. With carbamazepine, always check the CBC!
</details>

## Question 22 — Hold-or-Give Scenario

A nurse is about to give an IV potassium infusion to a patient. The patient's most recent labs show: K+ 3.1 mEq/L, BUN 45 mg/dL (high), creatinine 3.2 mg/dL (high). Urine output for the last 2 hours has been 10 mL total. What should the nurse do?

A. Give the potassium — the level is low and needs to be replaced
B. Hold the potassium, contact the provider, and report the kidney function and urine output
C. Give the potassium by IV push to correct the level quickly
D. Increase the potassium infusion rate to 40 mEq/hour

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — Hold the potassium, contact the provider, and report the kidney function and urine output**

This is tricky — yes, the potassium is low (3.1 mEq/L). But look at the whole picture:
- **BUN and creatinine are HIGH** = kidneys are not working well
- **Urine output is very low** (10 mL in 2 hours = 5 mL/hour, way below the minimum of 30 mL/hour)

The kidneys are responsible for excreting potassium. If the kidneys aren't working and you give potassium, it could rapidly build up and swing from hypokalemia to **fatal hyperkalemia**.

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — Even though the K+ is low, poor kidney function and low urine output make potassium administration dangerous. The provider needs to decide.
- **B is correct** — Hold the potassium and let the provider know about the kidney function and poor urine output. The provider may need to address the kidney problem first or adjust the dose/route.
- **C is wrong** — NEVER give potassium by IV push. Ever. This is always wrong.
- **D is wrong** — 40 mEq/hour is way above the safe maximum rate (10–20 mEq/hour). This would be dangerous even in a patient with good kidneys.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** Before giving potassium, ALWAYS check kidney function and urine output. The NCLEX loves this scenario — a low potassium with bad kidneys. The answer is always "hold and notify" because the risk of overcorrection is too high.
</details>

## Question 23 — Application with Prioritization

A nurse on a medical-surgical unit is caring for four patients. Which patient should the nurse see FIRST?

A. A patient on phenytoin who is due for their routine drug level to be drawn
B. A patient with a calcium level of 7.2 mg/dL who reports facial tingling and numbness in the fingertips
C. A patient on levetiracetam who reports feeling drowsy after taking the morning dose
D. A patient with a sodium of 134 mEq/L who is alert and asking for lunch

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — The patient with calcium of 7.2 mg/dL with facial tingling and numbness**

Let's evaluate each patient:
- **Patient A** — Routine drug level draw is important but not urgent. This can wait.
- **Patient B** — Calcium of 7.2 mg/dL is LOW (normal 8.5–10.5). Plus the patient has **symptoms**: facial tingling and fingertip numbness are early signs of hypocalcemia. If not treated, this can progress to muscle spasms, laryngospasm, seizures, and cardiac problems. This patient needs assessment and treatment NOW.
- **Patient C** — Drowsiness is a common, expected side effect of levetiracetam. Not urgent.
- **Patient D** — Sodium of 134 is only slightly low (normal 135–145). The patient is alert and eating. This is not an emergency.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** In priority questions, look for the patient with an abnormal lab value AND active symptoms. An abnormal value alone might be stable, but when combined with symptoms, it means the problem is getting worse and needs immediate attention.
</details>

## Question 24 — Provider Notification

A nurse caring for a patient taking valproic acid (Depakote) reviews the morning labs: AST 180 U/L (normal: 10–40), ALT 210 U/L (normal: 7–56). The patient complains of right upper abdominal pain and nausea. Which action is MOST important?

A. Give the scheduled dose of valproic acid and monitor
B. Hold the valproic acid and notify the provider immediately
C. Administer an antacid for the nausea and abdominal pain
D. Encourage the patient to eat a high-fat meal to help with medication absorption

<details>
<summary>Answer & Explanation 💡</summary>

**✅ Correct: B — Hold the valproic acid and notify the provider immediately**

This patient is showing signs of **hepatotoxicity (liver damage)** — a serious and potentially life-threatening side effect of valproic acid:
- **AST and ALT are significantly elevated** (both above normal) = liver enzymes are high = liver cells are being damaged
- **Right upper abdominal pain** = where the liver is located
- **Nausea** = common with liver problems

**Why each answer is right or wrong:**
- **A is wrong** — Giving more valproic acid to a patient with signs of liver damage could make the hepatotoxicity worse. This is dangerous.
- **B is correct** — When you see elevated liver enzymes + symptoms of liver problems in a patient on valproic acid, HOLD the drug and notify the provider immediately. The provider may discontinue the medication or order further testing.
- **C is wrong** — The nausea and pain are not simple stomach upset. They are signs of liver damage. An antacid won't fix this, and it delays recognizing the real problem.
- **D is wrong** — A high-fat meal has nothing to do with this situation. The liver is the issue, not absorption.

🎯 **Test-taking tip:** With valproic acid, always watch for signs of liver damage: elevated LFTs, right upper quadrant pain, nausea, jaundice, dark urine. If you see these — hold the drug and call the provider!
</details>
