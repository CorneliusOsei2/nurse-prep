# Anticoagulants & Antiplatelets — Application Questions

> **How to use these questions:** Read each question carefully. Cover the answer and try to pick the best choice on your own first. Then read every explanation — even for the choices you didn't pick. Learning WHY wrong answers are wrong is just as important as knowing the right answer. You are going to do great! 💪

---

### Question 1 — Priority / First Action

A nurse is caring for a patient receiving a continuous heparin infusion. The patient suddenly develops dark, tarry stools and has a blood pressure of 86/54 mmHg. Which action should the nurse take **first**?

A. Administer protamine sulfate as ordered
B. Stop the heparin infusion
C. Notify the healthcare provider
D. Obtain a stat aPTT level

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

Great question to start with! Here is the key idea: **when a patient is actively bleeding and unstable while on a blood thinner, the FIRST action is to stop the drug that is causing the problem.**

- **B is correct** — Stopping the heparin infusion is the immediate priority. Dark tarry stools (melena) plus low blood pressure tells us the patient is likely having a significant GI bleed. The heparin is making it worse. Stop it first.
- **A is wrong** — Protamine sulfate is the antidote for heparin, and the patient may need it. But you need a provider order to give it, and stopping the infusion is faster and does not require an order. You can turn off the pump right now.
- **C is wrong** — You absolutely should notify the provider, but stopping the heparin comes first. Think: remove the harm, then call for help.
- **D is wrong** — An aPTT is useful information, but getting a lab result takes time. The patient is unstable NOW. Stop the bleeding source first.

> **Test-taking tip:** When a question asks "what FIRST," look for the action that directly addresses the immediate danger. Stopping a harmful infusion is almost always the first step when there is active bleeding.

### Question 2 — Lab Interpretation

A nurse is reviewing lab results for a patient on warfarin. The INR result is **4.8** and the patient denies any bleeding. Which action should the nurse take?

A. Administer the next scheduled dose of warfarin
B. Hold the warfarin and notify the provider
C. Administer IV vitamin K immediately
D. Prepare to administer fresh frozen plasma

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

Think about it this way: the therapeutic INR for warfarin is usually 2.0–3.0. An INR of 4.8 is too high — the patient's blood is too "thin" and they are at increased risk for bleeding.

- **B is correct** — Hold the medication (do not give it) and notify the provider. The INR is significantly above the therapeutic range. The provider needs to know so they can decide the next steps (dose adjustment, possible vitamin K, closer monitoring).
- **A is wrong** — NEVER give warfarin when the INR is above the therapeutic range. This would make the situation worse and put the patient at serious bleeding risk.
- **C is wrong** — IV vitamin K is used for serious or life-threatening bleeding or very high INR levels (usually above 10). The patient has no bleeding and the INR, while elevated, does not typically require emergency IV vitamin K. The provider may order oral vitamin K at a low dose.
- **D is wrong** — Fresh frozen plasma is reserved for life-threatening bleeding with warfarin. There is no active bleeding here. This is too aggressive for this situation.

> **Test-taking tip:** For elevated lab values, the safe nursing response is usually "hold the medication and notify." You do not need a provider order to hold — it is within your nursing judgment to not give a drug when labs show it is unsafe.

### Question 3 — Patient Teaching

A nurse is teaching a patient who is being discharged on warfarin. Which statement by the patient shows they **understand** the teaching?

A. "I will stop eating green vegetables to keep my blood thin."
B. "I will eat about the same amount of green vegetables each week."
C. "I will take ibuprofen for my headaches instead of acetaminophen."
D. "I will double my dose if I miss a day."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

Here is the key idea: patients on warfarin do NOT need to avoid vitamin K foods — they need to keep their intake **consistent**.

- **B is correct** — Eating about the same amount of vitamin K foods each week keeps the INR stable. Consistency is the goal, not avoidance.
- **A is wrong** — Stopping all green vegetables is not necessary and could actually cause problems (nutritional deficits and unpredictable INR changes). The key word is consistency, not elimination.
- **C is wrong** — This is actually backwards! Ibuprofen is an NSAID that increases bleeding risk when combined with warfarin. Acetaminophen (Tylenol) is the safer choice for pain (though even Tylenol should be used in moderate amounts).
- **D is wrong** — NEVER double a dose of warfarin. If you miss a dose, take it as soon as you remember the same day. If it is the next day, skip the missed dose. Doubling could cause dangerous over-anticoagulation.

> **Test-taking tip:** For "which statement shows understanding" questions, look for the answer that matches correct teaching. Read each choice carefully — often one word makes it wrong.

### Question 4 — Safety / Hold-or-Give

A patient with a mechanical heart valve has been on warfarin for 2 years. Today's INR is 2.7. The morning warfarin dose is due. What should the nurse do?

A. Hold the warfarin because the INR is too high
B. Administer the warfarin as ordered
C. Notify the provider before giving the medication
D. Administer half the ordered dose

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

Think about it this way: for patients with mechanical heart valves, the therapeutic INR range is often **2.5 to 3.5**. An INR of 2.7 is right in the middle of that range — this is perfect!

- **B is correct** — The INR is therapeutic (within the goal range for a mechanical heart valve). Give the warfarin as ordered.
- **A is wrong** — 2.7 would be too high for a standard warfarin patient (goal 2.0–3.0, but 2.7 is still within range). For a mechanical heart valve patient, 2.7 is absolutely within range. No need to hold.
- **C is wrong** — There is no reason to call the provider. The lab is within the expected range, and the order is clear. Calling would be unnecessary.
- **D is wrong** — Never change a dose without a provider order. The nurse cannot decide to give half a dose on their own. The INR is within range, so the full dose should be given.

> **Test-taking tip:** Always think about the SPECIFIC patient. Mechanical heart valve = higher INR goal (2.5–3.5). Knowing the target range for specific conditions helps you decide whether to give or hold.

### Question 5 — SATA (Select All That Apply)

A nurse is providing discharge teaching to a patient starting warfarin therapy. Which instructions should the nurse include? **Select all that apply.**

A. Take the medication at the same time every day
B. Use a soft-bristled toothbrush
C. Increase intake of green leafy vegetables for better nutrition
D. Wear a medical alert bracelet
E. Use an electric razor for shaving
F. Avoid all over-the-counter medications without checking with your pharmacist first

**Correct Answers: A, B, D, E, F**

**Explanation:**

Let's go through each one:

- **A is correct** ✓ — Taking warfarin at the same time every day helps maintain consistent drug levels. This is standard teaching for warfarin.
- **B is correct** ✓ — A soft toothbrush prevents bleeding gums. All patients on blood thinners should use one.
- **C is wrong** ✗ — The instruction is to keep vitamin K intake **consistent**, not to increase it. Suddenly increasing green leafy vegetables would increase vitamin K, which works against warfarin and could lower the INR.
- **D is correct** ✓ — A medical alert bracelet informs emergency providers that the patient is on a blood thinner. This is very important.
- **E is correct** ✓ — An electric razor prevents cuts. Regular razors can cause nicks that bleed longer when on anticoagulants.
- **F is correct** ✓ — Many over-the-counter medications (like aspirin, NSAIDs, cold medicines) can interact with warfarin. Always check first.

> **Test-taking tip:** For SATA questions, evaluate EACH option independently. Ask yourself: "Is this one TRUE?" Do not let other choices influence your decision on a single option.

### Question 6 — Report to Provider

A patient has been receiving heparin for 7 days. The nurse reviews the following lab results: Platelet count today: 95,000/mm³ (baseline on admission: 240,000/mm³). Which action is most appropriate?

A. Document the results and continue monitoring
B. Administer the next dose of heparin as scheduled
C. Hold the heparin and notify the provider immediately
D. Increase IV fluid rate to prevent dehydration

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:**

Here is the key idea: this patient's platelet count dropped by more than 50% (from 240,000 to 95,000) while on heparin. This is a classic sign of **HIT (Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia)**.

- **C is correct** — Stop the heparin and call the provider right away. HIT is a dangerous condition where the body makes antibodies against heparin-platelet complexes. The platelet count drops, and paradoxically, the patient is at risk for CLOTS (not just bleeding). This is an emergency.
- **A is wrong** — A drop of more than 50% is not something you just "document and watch." This requires action now.
- **B is wrong** — Giving MORE heparin to a patient with suspected HIT would make the situation worse. All heparin must be stopped — including heparin flushes in IV lines.
- **D is wrong** — IV fluids have nothing to do with this problem. This is not a dehydration issue — it is a heparin-related platelet destruction problem.

> **Test-taking tip:** HIT = platelet drop of 50% or more + on heparin = STOP all heparin. This is one of those "always stop the drug" situations.

### Question 7 — Compare/Contrast

A nurse is caring for two patients: Patient A is on heparin, and Patient B is on warfarin. Both need their anticoagulant reversed for emergency surgery. Which reversal agents should the nurse anticipate?

A. Vitamin K for Patient A, protamine sulfate for Patient B
B. Protamine sulfate for Patient A, vitamin K for Patient B
C. Vitamin K for both patients
D. Protamine sulfate for both patients

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

The reason this matters is that each anticoagulant has its own specific reversal agent. Mixing them up could be dangerous.

- **B is correct** — Protamine sulfate reverses heparin. Vitamin K reverses warfarin. Each drug has its own antidote.
- **A is wrong** — This is exactly backwards! A common trick on exams is to reverse the pairings. Vitamin K does NOT reverse heparin. Protamine does NOT reverse warfarin.
- **C is wrong** — Vitamin K only works for warfarin. It has no effect on heparin.
- **D is wrong** — Protamine only works for heparin (and partially for enoxaparin). It has no effect on warfarin.

> **Memory helper:** **P**rotamine for he**P**arin (both have a P). **V**itamin K for war**f**arin (warfarin blocks **V**itamin K — they are "enemies").

### Question 8 — Application / Bedside Scenario

A patient on warfarin therapy is found on the floor in their room after a fall. The patient states, "I just tripped. I feel fine." There is no visible bleeding. What is the nurse's **priority concern**?

A. Skin tears on the patient's arms
B. Internal bleeding, especially intracranial hemorrhage
C. The patient's INR level is probably too high
D. The patient may need a higher bed rail

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

Think about it this way: a patient on warfarin has blood that clots much slower. A fall, even without visible injuries, can cause dangerous internal bleeding — especially bleeding inside the skull (intracranial hemorrhage).

- **B is correct** — Internal bleeding is the biggest concern. Intracranial hemorrhage from a fall can be life-threatening. The patient may feel "fine" initially, but symptoms can develop later. Perform a neurological assessment, monitor closely, and notify the provider.
- **A is wrong** — Skin tears are a valid concern, but they are not the PRIORITY. Internal bleeding is more life-threatening than external skin tears.
- **C is wrong** — The INR level may or may not be too high. The patient is on warfarin at any therapeutic level and is at risk from a fall. This is a reasonable thought but not the priority concern right now.
- **D is wrong** — Bed safety is important for prevention, but it does not address the immediate danger. We need to assess for internal bleeding first.

> **Test-taking tip:** On the NCLEX, falls in anticoagulated patients are treated as potential emergencies. Always think "hidden bleeding" first.

### Question 9 — Patient Teaching

A patient is being discharged with a prescription for enoxaparin (Lovenox) self-injections. Which statement by the patient indicates a need for **further teaching**?

A. "I will inject into the fatty area on my belly."
B. "I will push out the air bubble before injecting."
C. "I will not rub the spot after I inject."
D. "I will change sides on my belly each time."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

Here is the key idea: the air bubble in a prefilled enoxaparin syringe is there **on purpose**. It helps push all the medication out of the syringe.

- **B is correct (needs more teaching)** — The patient should NOT expel the air bubble. Pushing out the air bubble can cause medication loss and inaccurate dosing. This statement shows the patient misunderstands the technique.
- **A is wrong (correct technique)** — The abdomen (fatty tissue) is the correct injection site for enoxaparin.
- **C is wrong (correct technique)** — NOT rubbing the site is correct. Rubbing can cause bruising and hematoma.
- **D is wrong (correct technique)** — Rotating sides of the abdomen is correct. This prevents tissue damage in one spot.

> **Test-taking tip:** "Needs further teaching" questions are asking you to find the WRONG statement. All correct statements are the wrong answer choices.

### Question 10 — Safety

A nurse is caring for a patient on a heparin drip. The aPTT result comes back at 120 seconds (therapeutic range: 46–70 seconds). What should the nurse do?

A. Continue the heparin at the current rate since the patient is anticoagulated
B. Increase the heparin rate to ensure adequate anticoagulation
C. Hold the heparin infusion and notify the provider
D. Administer a heparin bolus to bring the levels up further

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:**

Think about it this way: an aPTT of 120 seconds is WAY above the therapeutic range (46–70 seconds). This patient is dangerously over-anticoagulated and at very high risk for bleeding.

- **C is correct** — Hold (stop) the heparin and call the provider. The aPTT is far too high. The provider will decide when to restart and at what rate.
- **A is wrong** — Continuing at the same rate would keep the patient in a dangerously over-anticoagulated state. Just because the patient "is anticoagulated" does not mean the level is safe.
- **B is wrong** — Increasing the rate would make the situation even more dangerous. The aPTT is already too high.
- **D is wrong** — A bolus would add even more heparin to a patient who already has too much. This could cause life-threatening bleeding.

> **Test-taking tip:** When a lab value is critically above the therapeutic range, the answer is almost always "hold the drug and notify."

### Question 11 — Medication Error Scenario

A nurse accidentally administered warfarin 5 mg to a patient who was supposed to receive warfarin 2 mg. The patient's INR this morning was 2.8. What should the nurse do **first**?

A. Administer vitamin K to reverse the extra warfarin
B. Notify the healthcare provider
C. Document the error in the patient's chart
D. Check the INR again in 2 hours

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

Here is the key idea: after a medication error, the priority is patient safety. The provider needs to know about the error so they can determine if any intervention is needed.

- **B is correct** — Notify the provider first. The provider can assess the risk and decide the plan of care (monitoring, lab checks, possible vitamin K, dose adjustments).
- **A is wrong** — Giving vitamin K is a medical decision that the provider needs to make. The nurse cannot independently decide to administer vitamin K without an order. Also, the patient may not need it — the extra dose may not cause a critically high INR.
- **C is wrong** — Documentation is important and an incident report should be filed, but notifying the provider comes FIRST. Patient safety before paperwork.
- **D is wrong** — Rechecking the INR is a good idea, but it takes time for warfarin to affect the INR (it works slowly). More importantly, the provider needs to know about the error first.

> **Test-taking tip:** For medication error questions, always notify the provider first, then assess the patient, then document. Safety first, always.

### Question 12 — SATA (Select All That Apply)

A patient is receiving a heparin infusion. Which assessment findings should the nurse report to the provider? **Select all that apply.**

A. aPTT of 55 seconds (therapeutic range: 46–70 seconds)
B. Blood in the patient's urine
C. Platelet count of 100,000/mm³ (baseline: 220,000/mm³)
D. Small bruise at the IV insertion site
E. Black, tarry stools
F. Patient reports bleeding gums when brushing teeth

**Correct Answers: B, C, E, F**

**Explanation:**

Let's look at each one:

- **A — Do NOT report** ✗ — An aPTT of 55 seconds is within the therapeutic range (46–70). This is exactly where we want it. No need to call the provider.
- **B — Report** ✓ — Blood in urine (hematuria) is a sign of bleeding, which is the main complication of heparin. Always report this.
- **C — Report** ✓ — The platelet count dropped from 220,000 to 100,000 — that is more than a 50% drop. This suggests possible HIT. This is urgent.
- **D — Do NOT report** ✗ — A small bruise at an IV site is common and expected, especially on anticoagulants. This alone is not concerning unless it is large or expanding.
- **E — Report** ✓ — Black, tarry stools (melena) indicate GI bleeding. This is a serious sign that must be reported.
- **F — Report** ✓ — Bleeding gums are a sign of excessive anticoagulation or bleeding tendency. This should be reported.

> **Test-taking tip:** For SATA questions about what to report, ask yourself: "Could this finding mean the patient is in danger?" If yes, report it.

### Question 13 — Compare/Contrast

A patient asks the nurse, "Why do I need both heparin and warfarin? Isn't that too much?" What is the best response?

A. "Your doctor ordered both, so it must be correct. You should trust the plan."
B. "Warfarin takes several days to work fully, so heparin protects you until then."
C. "One prevents clots in your veins and the other prevents clots in your arteries."
D. "We always give both medications together for better clot prevention."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

The reason this matters is that patients deserve clear, accurate explanations. Understanding their treatment helps with compliance and reduces anxiety.

- **B is correct** — This is the accurate explanation. Warfarin has a slow onset (3–5 days). Heparin works immediately and "bridges" the gap until warfarin reaches therapeutic levels. Once the INR is 2.0–3.0 for at least 24 hours, heparin is usually discontinued.
- **A is wrong** — While trusting the care team is good, this response dismisses the patient's valid question. Patients have the right to understand their medications. This is not therapeutic communication.
- **C is wrong** — This is not accurate. Both heparin and warfarin can prevent clots in veins and arteries. They are not separated by location. The real reason is the timing of onset.
- **D is wrong** — "We always give both" is not true and does not explain why. They are not always given together permanently — the heparin is temporary.

> **Test-taking tip:** When a patient asks "why," give a simple, honest, accurate explanation. Avoid dismissive or vague answers.

### Question 14 — Priority

A nurse is caring for four patients. Which patient should the nurse assess **first**?

A. A patient on warfarin with an INR of 2.5 who is due for discharge teaching
B. A patient on heparin whose aPTT was 58 seconds one hour ago
C. A patient on enoxaparin who has a large, new bruise on the abdomen and reports feeling dizzy
D. A patient on aspirin who is complaining of a mild headache

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:**

Think about it this way: you are looking for the patient who is in the most danger RIGHT NOW.

- **C is correct** — A large new bruise plus dizziness in a patient on an anticoagulant suggests significant bleeding (possibly internal). Dizziness could mean blood loss causing low blood pressure. This patient needs immediate assessment.
- **A is wrong** — INR of 2.5 is within the therapeutic range (2.0–3.0). This patient is stable and needs teaching, which can wait.
- **B is wrong** — aPTT of 58 is within the therapeutic range (46–70 seconds). This patient is stable with expected lab values.
- **D is wrong** — A mild headache in a patient on aspirin is not necessarily alarming. While headaches on blood thinners deserve attention, this presentation is less urgent than active signs of bleeding with hemodynamic changes.

> **Test-taking tip:** For "who to see first" questions, use the ABCs (airway, breathing, circulation) and look for the patient with unexpected or worsening findings. Dizziness + bruising = possible blood loss = circulation problem.

### Question 15 — Drug Interaction

A patient on clopidogrel (Plavix) tells the nurse, "My doctor started me on omeprazole for my heartburn." What should the nurse do?

A. Tell the patient this is a safe combination
B. Teach the patient to take the medications 12 hours apart
C. Notify the provider about the potential drug interaction
D. Administer both medications as ordered without concern

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:**

Here is the key idea: omeprazole (Prilosec) reduces the effectiveness of clopidogrel. This is a well-known, important drug interaction.

- **C is correct** — Notify the provider. Omeprazole interferes with the enzyme (CYP2C19) that converts clopidogrel into its active form. This means clopidogrel may not work well enough to prevent clots. The provider may switch to pantoprazole (Protonix), which does not have this interaction.
- **A is wrong** — This is NOT a safe combination. Telling the patient it is safe could lead to inadequate antiplatelet therapy and increased clot risk.
- **B is wrong** — Timing does not fix this interaction. The problem is that omeprazole blocks the enzyme that activates clopidogrel in the liver, regardless of when they are taken.
- **D is wrong** — Nurses should always be aware of drug interactions. Giving both without concern ignores a significant safety issue.

> **Test-taking tip:** Know your key drug interactions. Clopidogrel + omeprazole is a classic board question. The safe PPI alternative is pantoprazole.

### Question 16 — Patient Teaching

A nurse is teaching a patient who is starting apixaban (Eliquis). Which statement by the nurse is **most important** to include?

A. "You will need weekly INR blood tests."
B. "Do not stop this medication suddenly without talking to your doctor."
C. "You must eat consistent amounts of green leafy vegetables."
D. "You need to take this medication with your evening meal."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

Think about it this way: suddenly stopping an anticoagulant like apixaban can cause a rebound increase in clotting risk, which could lead to a stroke or blood clot.

- **B is correct** — This is the most important teaching point. Stopping apixaban suddenly (without medical guidance) can be very dangerous, especially for patients taking it to prevent stroke in atrial fibrillation.
- **A is wrong** — DOACs like apixaban do NOT require routine INR monitoring. This is one of their advantages over warfarin. Weekly INR is a warfarin thing.
- **C is wrong** — Consistent vitamin K intake is a warfarin teaching point, not a DOAC teaching point. DOACs have far fewer food interactions.
- **D is wrong** — Taking with the evening meal is a teaching point for **rivaroxaban (Xarelto)**, not apixaban. Apixaban can be taken with or without food.

> **Test-taking tip:** Watch out for answer choices that are correct for a DIFFERENT drug. The exam tries to trick you by mixing up drug-specific instructions.

### Question 17 — Lab Interpretation

A patient on warfarin has the following labs: INR 3.2, hemoglobin 8.1 g/dL (down from 12.4 g/dL two days ago), and the patient reports feeling very tired and lightheaded. What should the nurse suspect?

A. The patient is dehydrated
B. The patient is experiencing active bleeding
C. The warfarin dose is sub-therapeutic
D. The patient has a normal response to warfarin therapy

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

The reason this matters is that a significant drop in hemoglobin combined with an elevated INR and symptoms strongly suggests bleeding.

- **B is correct** — The clues are: INR above goal (3.2), hemoglobin dropped significantly (from 12.4 to 8.1 = a drop of 4.3 g/dL in 2 days), and the patient has symptoms of blood loss (fatigue, lightheadedness). This patient is likely bleeding somewhere — possibly internally.
- **A is wrong** — While dehydration can cause fatigue and lightheadedness, it would not explain the dramatic hemoglobin drop. The hemoglobin drop is the biggest clue here.
- **C is wrong** — Sub-therapeutic means the INR is too LOW (below 2.0). An INR of 3.2 is actually above the typical range, meaning the patient is over-anticoagulated.
- **D is wrong** — A hemoglobin drop of 4.3 g/dL in two days is NOT normal. Normal warfarin therapy does not cause this. Something is wrong.

> **Test-taking tip:** Always look at ALL the data together. One abnormal finding might not be concerning alone, but combined findings paint a picture. INR elevated + hemoglobin dropping + symptoms = bleeding.

### Question 18 — Safety / Medication Administration

A nurse is preparing to administer a subcutaneous heparin injection. Which actions are appropriate? **Select all that apply.**

A. Select an injection site on the abdomen
B. Aspirate before injecting to check for blood return
C. Use a 90-degree angle for the injection
D. Massage the site gently after injection
E. Rotate injection sites
F. Apply an ice pack to the site before injection if bruising is a concern

**Correct Answers: A, C, E, F**

**Explanation:**

Let's look at each one:

- **A is correct** ✓ — The abdomen is the preferred site for subcutaneous heparin injection.
- **B is wrong** ✗ — Do NOT aspirate before subcutaneous heparin injections. Aspiration is no longer recommended for SubQ injections and can increase tissue trauma and bruising.
- **C is correct** ✓ — A 90-degree angle is used for subcutaneous injections when there is adequate fatty tissue (which the abdomen usually has).
- **D is wrong** ✗ — Do NOT massage or rub the injection site. This increases the risk of bruising and hematoma formation with heparin.
- **E is correct** ✓ — Rotating injection sites prevents tissue damage and promotes better drug absorption.
- **F is correct** ✓ — Applying an ice pack for a brief period before injection can help reduce bruising. This is an acceptable nursing intervention.

> **Test-taking tip:** For injection technique questions, remember the "do NOT" rules: no aspiration and no rubbing for SubQ heparin.

### Question 19 — Report to Provider

A patient is 6 days post-op from a knee replacement and is taking enoxaparin (Lovenox) for DVT prevention. The nurse notes the patient's creatinine has increased from 1.0 mg/dL on admission to 2.4 mg/dL today. What should the nurse do?

A. Continue the enoxaparin as ordered since the dose is for prevention
B. Increase the patient's oral fluid intake
C. Notify the provider about the elevated creatinine
D. Switch to warfarin independently

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:**

Here is the key idea: enoxaparin is cleared by the **kidneys**. When kidney function declines (shown by rising creatinine), the drug builds up in the body and bleeding risk increases significantly.

- **C is correct** — The provider must be notified because the enoxaparin dose may need to be adjusted or the drug may need to be changed. A creatinine jump from 1.0 to 2.4 is significant and indicates worsening kidney function.
- **A is wrong** — Even though it is a prevention dose, worsening kidney function changes how the body handles the drug. Continuing without alerting the provider could lead to dangerous drug accumulation and bleeding.
- **B is wrong** — While hydration can be helpful for kidneys, the immediate concern is that the enoxaparin dose may no longer be safe. Increasing fluids alone does not fix this problem.
- **D is wrong** — Nurses cannot independently switch medications. Only the provider can make that decision. Also, warfarin has its own concerns and is not automatically the right alternative.

> **Test-taking tip:** For any drug that is cleared by the kidneys, a rising creatinine is always a red flag that requires provider notification.

### Question 20 — Application / Critical Thinking

A patient on heparin therapy is scheduled for a lumbar puncture. The most recent aPTT is 68 seconds (therapeutic range: 46–70 seconds). What should the nurse do?

A. Proceed with preparing the patient since the aPTT is therapeutic
B. Notify the provider — the heparin may need to be held before the procedure
C. Administer protamine sulfate before the procedure
D. Increase the heparin rate to prevent clot formation during the procedure

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

Think about it this way: a lumbar puncture is an invasive procedure involving the spinal area. Performing it on a patient who is anticoagulated creates a risk for **spinal hematoma** (bleeding around the spinal cord), which can cause paralysis.

- **B is correct** — Notify the provider. Even though the aPTT is therapeutic, heparin typically needs to be held before invasive procedures like lumbar punctures. The provider needs to weigh the risks and decide when to hold heparin and when the procedure can safely be done.
- **A is wrong** — A therapeutic aPTT does not mean it is safe to do an invasive procedure. Therapeutic means the heparin is working as intended — which means the blood is NOT clotting normally. That is exactly the problem for a procedure.
- **C is wrong** — Administering protamine sulfate is a medical decision. The nurse should not independently give a reversal agent without an order. Also, full reversal may not be the plan.
- **D is wrong** — Increasing heparin before a procedure that could cause bleeding is dangerous and illogical.

> **Test-taking tip:** Any invasive procedure + anticoagulation = notify the provider. Do not assume "therapeutic levels" mean it is safe to proceed.

### Question 21 — Patient Teaching / Safety

A patient being discharged on warfarin asks, "Can I take this cold medicine I have at home?" The patient shows the nurse a box of an over-the-counter cold medication that contains aspirin. What should the nurse say?

A. "That should be fine as long as you only take it for a few days."
B. "No, that cold medicine has aspirin in it, which can increase your bleeding risk. Talk to your pharmacist about a safe alternative."
C. "You can take it, but just take half the recommended dose."
D. "Stop your warfarin while you are taking the cold medicine."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

The reason this matters is that aspirin combined with warfarin significantly increases bleeding risk. Many over-the-counter medications contain hidden aspirin or NSAIDs.

- **B is correct** — This is accurate, clear, and gives the patient a safe next step (talk to the pharmacist). Aspirin + warfarin = doubled bleeding risk. The patient needs a cold medication that does NOT contain aspirin or other NSAIDs.
- **A is wrong** — Even a few days of aspirin with warfarin can increase bleeding risk. There is no safe short-term combination without provider approval.
- **C is wrong** — Reducing the dose does not eliminate the interaction. Even low-dose aspirin affects platelets and clotting. The nurse cannot modify medication doses independently.
- **D is wrong** — Stopping warfarin puts the patient at risk for blood clots and stroke. A patient should NEVER stop warfarin without provider guidance.

> **Test-taking tip:** Always teach patients on warfarin to CHECK LABELS on all over-the-counter medications. Many contain aspirin or NSAIDs.

### Question 22 — Priority / Critical Thinking

A nurse is caring for a patient on a heparin drip who develops sudden shortness of breath, chest pain, and one leg that is swollen, warm, and tender. What should the nurse suspect?

A. The heparin is causing an allergic reaction
B. The patient is developing fluid overload
C. The patient may have developed a new blood clot despite heparin therapy
D. The heparin dose is too high and causing bleeding into the lungs

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:**

Think about it this way: the symptoms describe two things — a possible pulmonary embolism (PE) with chest pain and shortness of breath, AND a possible deep vein thrombosis (DVT) with the swollen, warm, tender leg. These can happen even while on heparin if the dose is not therapeutic or if HIT is developing.

- **C is correct** — Despite being on heparin, the patient can still develop clots. The aPTT may be sub-therapeutic (dose too low), or the patient may be developing HIT (where clots form despite low platelets). This needs immediate investigation.
- **A is wrong** — Allergic reactions to heparin typically present with rash, hives, or anaphylaxis — not with a swollen leg. The combination of symptoms here points to blood clots.
- **B is wrong** — Fluid overload can cause shortness of breath, but it does not cause one leg to be swollen, warm, and tender. That leg symptom is classic for DVT.
- **D is wrong** — If the heparin dose were too high, you would see signs of bleeding (blood in sputum, dropping hemoglobin), not a swollen leg. A swollen, warm leg is a clot sign, not a bleeding sign.

> **Test-taking tip:** When you see leg swelling (one-sided, warm, tender) + chest pain + shortness of breath, think DVT → PE until proven otherwise, even if the patient is on anticoagulants.

### Question 23 — Patient Teaching

A nurse is providing education to a patient who was just prescribed low-dose aspirin (81 mg daily) after a cardiac stent placement. Which teaching point is **most critical**?

A. "Take the aspirin with food to protect your stomach."
B. "Do not stop taking the aspirin without talking to your cardiologist."
C. "Avoid eating grapefruit while taking aspirin."
D. "You should check your INR monthly."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

Here is the key idea: after stent placement, aspirin (often combined with clopidogrel) keeps the stent open. Stopping aspirin suddenly can cause the stent to clot shut, leading to a **heart attack**.

- **B is correct** — This is the MOST critical teaching. Stent thrombosis (clotting of the stent) is life-threatening and can happen quickly if antiplatelet medications are stopped. The patient must never stop without cardiologist guidance.
- **A is wrong** — Taking aspirin with food is a good general tip, but it is not the MOST critical teaching. Missing this point will not kill the patient. Stopping the aspirin could.
- **C is wrong** — Grapefruit interactions are more of a concern with certain statins and calcium channel blockers, not aspirin. This is not a significant aspirin interaction.
- **D is wrong** — INR is used to monitor warfarin, NOT aspirin. Aspirin is an antiplatelet and does not affect the INR in a way that requires monitoring.

> **Test-taking tip:** For stent patients, the most dangerous thing is stopping antiplatelet therapy. This is always the highest priority teaching point.

### Question 24 — Medication Error / Safety

A nurse discovers that a patient on warfarin also received a dose of enoxaparin that was NOT ordered. The patient now has two anticoagulants in their system. What is the nurse's **first** action?

A. Administer vitamin K to counteract both drugs
B. Assess the patient for signs of bleeding
C. File an incident report
D. Check the patient's aPTT and INR

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**

Think about it this way: the patient now has double anticoagulation, which greatly increases bleeding risk. The first priority is to check if the patient is already bleeding.

- **B is correct** — Assess the patient first. Check for visible bleeding, check vital signs (hypotension, tachycardia), ask about symptoms (headache, abdominal pain, dizziness). You need to know the patient's current status before deciding next steps.
- **A is wrong** — Vitamin K only reverses warfarin, not enoxaparin. Also, you need to assess the patient and notify the provider before administering any reversal agent. The provider will decide the plan.
- **C is wrong** — An incident report is necessary, but patient safety comes first. Assess, notify, intervene — then document.
- **D is wrong** — Labs are helpful but take time to result. The immediate action is a bedside assessment. You can assess the patient in seconds; labs take minutes to hours.

> **Test-taking tip:** After a medication error, the first step is ALWAYS assessing the patient. Think: "Is the patient safe right now?"

### Question 25 — Application

A patient with atrial fibrillation has been on warfarin for 3 days. The current INR is 1.4. The patient is also receiving a heparin infusion. The patient asks, "When can I go home without the IV?" What is the best response?

A. "Once your INR reaches 2.0 to 3.0 for at least 24 hours, the heparin can usually be stopped."
B. "The heparin will be stopped tomorrow regardless of your lab results."
C. "You will need to stay on both medications permanently."
D. "Once you stop having any symptoms, the heparin can be discontinued."

**Correct Answer: A**

**Explanation:**

The reason this matters is that the patient needs to understand why they are on both medications and what the milestones are for transitioning off heparin.

- **A is correct** — Warfarin takes 3–5 days to become fully effective. The heparin "bridges" until the INR is in the therapeutic range (2.0–3.0) and stays there for at least 24 hours. Once that happens, heparin can be safely discontinued. An INR of 1.4 on day 3 means warfarin has not reached its full effect yet.
- **B is wrong** — Heparin discontinuation is based on the INR reaching therapeutic levels, not on an arbitrary timeline. Stopping it too early leaves the patient unprotected.
- **C is wrong** — The patient will NOT need both permanently. Heparin is temporary. Once warfarin is working (shown by therapeutic INR), heparin stops.
- **D is wrong** — Atrial fibrillation may not have symptoms, and symptom resolution is not the criterion for stopping heparin. The decision is based on the INR level.

> **Test-taking tip:** Bridging therapy = heparin is the bridge, warfarin is the destination. The bridge comes down (heparin stops) when you safely arrive at the destination (therapeutic INR for 24+ hours).
