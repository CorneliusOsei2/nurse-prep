# Anticoagulants & Antiplatelets — Study Guide

> **Welcome!** This guide covers the most important blood-thinning medications you will see on the NCLEX and at the bedside. Take your time. Read slowly. You can do this! 💪

## Quick Concept: Anticoagulant vs. Antiplatelet

Before we look at each drug, let's understand the big picture.

**Anticoagulants** = slow down the clotting *process* (the clotting cascade).
- They make the blood take longer to form a clot.
- Examples: heparin, enoxaparin, warfarin, rivaroxaban, apixaban.

**Antiplatelets** = stop *platelets* from sticking together.
- Platelets are the tiny cell pieces that start a clot.
- Examples: aspirin, clopidogrel (Plavix).

> **Think of it this way:** Imagine building a wall. Anticoagulants slow down the workers who mix the cement. Antiplatelets stop the bricks from sticking together. Both make it harder to build the wall (the clot).

**Key point:** Neither type *dissolves* a clot that already exists. They *prevent new clots* or *stop a clot from getting bigger*.

## 1. Heparin (Unfractionated Heparin — UFH)

### What It Does (In Simple Words)
Heparin works fast to slow down blood clotting. It blocks certain clotting factors (especially thrombin and factor Xa) by helping a natural substance in your body called antithrombin.

> **In simple words:** Heparin is like a "fast brake" for clotting.

### Common Uses
- Treating blood clots (deep vein thrombosis — DVT, pulmonary embolism — PE)
- Preventing clots during surgery or bed rest
- During heart attacks or unstable angina
- Used as a "bridge" while waiting for warfarin to start working
- Keeping IV lines and dialysis machines from clotting

### Routes
- **IV (intravenous)** — continuous drip or bolus (a single quick dose)
- **SubQ (subcutaneous)** — injection under the skin (for prevention doses)

> **Important:** Heparin is NEVER given IM (intramuscular). This can cause serious bleeding into the muscle.

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- **aPTT** (activated partial thromboplastin time) — this is the key lab for heparin
  - Normal aPTT: about 25–35 seconds
  - Therapeutic goal on heparin: usually **1.5 to 2.5 times normal** (about 46–70 seconds, depending on facility)
- **Platelet count** — baseline and then regularly (because of HIT risk — see below)
- **Hemoglobin and hematocrit** — to watch for hidden bleeding
- **Signs of active bleeding** — check stool, urine, gums, skin
- **Vital signs** — especially blood pressure and heart rate
- **Allergies** — some heparin comes from pork; ask about pork allergy
- **Recent surgeries or procedures** — higher bleeding risk
- **Current medications** — other blood thinners, NSAIDs, aspirin increase bleeding risk

### Weight-Based Dosing
- Heparin IV doses are calculated based on the patient's weight.
- The doctor orders a **bolus** (loading dose) and then a **continuous drip** (units per hour).
- The aPTT is checked regularly (usually every 6 hours) and the drip rate is adjusted using a **heparin protocol** (a chart that tells you how to adjust the dose).

> **Nursing tip:** Always double-check heparin doses with another nurse. Heparin is a **high-alert medication**.

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **Bleeding** — this is the #1 concern!
  - Check for: blood in urine (hematuria), blood in stool (melena or bright red blood), bleeding gums, nosebleeds, bruising (ecchymosis), petechiae (tiny red/purple dots on skin)
  - Watch injection sites for bleeding or hematoma
  - Check for signs of internal bleeding: sudden drop in blood pressure, rising heart rate, abdominal pain or distension, back pain, confusion
- **aPTT results** — report if too high (over-anticoagulated) or too low (under-anticoagulated)
- **Platelet count drops** — could be HIT (see below)

### When to HOLD and Notify the Provider
- aPTT is above the therapeutic range
- Platelet count drops significantly (especially >50% drop from baseline)
- Any sign of active bleeding
- Patient is going for surgery or invasive procedure
- Patient falls

### Key Labs
| Lab | Why | How Often |
|-----|-----|-----------|
| aPTT | Monitors heparin effectiveness | Every 6 hours until stable, then daily |
| Platelets | Watches for HIT | Baseline, then every 2–3 days |
| Hemoglobin/Hematocrit | Detects hidden bleeding | As ordered |
| Stool guaiac | Checks for blood in stool | As ordered |

### HIT — Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia

This is a very dangerous side effect. Here is what happens:

1. The body makes antibodies against heparin + platelets.
2. Platelets get destroyed → platelet count drops.
3. But paradoxically (this is the tricky part!), the patient gets MORE clots, not less.

> **Think of it this way:** HIT is the opposite of what you'd expect. Low platelets usually mean bleeding — but in HIT, the patient gets *clots*.

**When does it happen?** Usually 5–10 days after starting heparin.

**What to watch for:**
- Platelet count drops by more than 50% from baseline
- New blood clots despite being on heparin
- Skin changes at injection sites

**What to do:**
- **STOP all heparin immediately** — including heparin flushes!
- Notify provider right away
- A different type of blood thinner will be started (like argatroban or bivalirudin)

### Antidote
**Protamine sulfate** — completely reverses heparin.
- Given IV, slowly
- 1 mg protamine reverses approximately 100 units of heparin
- Watch for: hypotension, bradycardia, allergic reaction (higher risk in patients with fish allergy or previous protamine exposure)

### Key Patient Teaching
- Report any unusual bleeding or bruising right away
- Use a soft toothbrush and electric razor
- Avoid activities that could cause injury
- Tell all healthcare providers you are on a blood thinner
- Do not rub the SubQ injection site after injection (press gently — do not massage)

### Key Drug Interactions
- **Aspirin, NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen)** — increase bleeding risk
- **Other anticoagulants** — increase bleeding risk
- **Some antibiotics and herbal supplements** — can affect clotting

## 2. Enoxaparin (Lovenox) — Low-Molecular-Weight Heparin (LMWH)

### What It Does (In Simple Words)
Enoxaparin is a "cousin" of heparin. It also slows down clotting, but it is more predictable and easier to use. It mainly blocks factor Xa.

> **In simple words:** Enoxaparin is like a more reliable version of heparin that patients can even give to themselves at home.

### Common Uses
- Preventing blood clots after surgery (hip/knee replacement)
- Treating DVT and PE
- Preventing clots in patients who are on bed rest
- Used as a "bridge" with warfarin

### Route
- **SubQ only** — injected into the fatty tissue of the abdomen (belly)
- **NEVER give IM** — can cause bleeding into the muscle

### SubQ Injection Technique (Important for Teaching!)
1. Wash hands.
2. The medication comes in a prefilled syringe — do NOT expel the air bubble. The air bubble helps push all the medication out.
3. Pinch a fold of skin on the abdomen (at least 2 inches from the belly button).
4. Insert the needle at a **90-degree angle** into the skin fold.
5. Inject slowly.
6. **Do NOT rub** the injection site afterward — just press gently.
7. Rotate injection sites (alternate left and right sides of the abdomen).

> **Why no rubbing?** Rubbing can cause bruising and bleeding under the skin.

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- **No routine aPTT needed!** (This is different from unfractionated heparin.)
- **Anti-Xa level** — only checked in special situations:
  - Very obese patients
  - Very thin patients
  - Patients with kidney problems
  - Pregnant patients
- **Platelet count** — baseline (HIT can still happen, though less common)
- **Kidney function (creatinine)** — enoxaparin is cleared by the kidneys. If kidneys are not working well, the drug builds up and bleeding risk goes up.
- **Signs of bleeding**
- **Vital signs**

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **Bleeding** — same signs as heparin (see above)
- **Injection site** — watch for bruising, hematoma, or hard lumps
- **Kidney function** — especially in elderly patients
- **Platelet count** — watch for drops

### When to HOLD and Notify the Provider
- Active bleeding
- Significant drop in platelet count
- Worsening kidney function (creatinine going up)
- Patient is going for surgery or spinal procedure

> **Spinal/epidural warning:** Enoxaparin increases the risk of spinal hematoma (bleeding around the spinal cord) in patients who have epidural catheters or spinal procedures. This can cause paralysis. Follow your facility's timing guidelines carefully.

### Key Labs
| Lab | Why | How Often |
|-----|-----|-----------|
| Anti-Xa level | Monitors drug level (only in special cases) | As ordered |
| Platelets | Watches for HIT | Baseline, then periodically |
| Creatinine/BUN | Kidney function | As ordered |
| Hemoglobin/Hematocrit | Detects hidden bleeding | As ordered |

### Antidote
**Protamine sulfate** — but it only **partially reverses** enoxaparin (about 60%).
- This is different from unfractionated heparin, where protamine fully reverses it.

### Key Patient Teaching
- How to self-inject (see technique above)
- Do NOT rub the injection site
- Rotate injection sites on the abdomen
- Report any unusual bleeding, bruising, or dark/tarry stools
- Keep all lab appointments
- Tell your dentist and all doctors you are taking this medication
- Store prefilled syringes at room temperature

### Key Drug Interactions
- **Aspirin, NSAIDs** — increase bleeding risk
- **Other anticoagulants or antiplatelets** — increase bleeding risk
- **Some herbal supplements** (garlic, ginger, ginkgo) — can increase bleeding risk

## 3. Warfarin (Coumadin)

### What It Does (In Simple Words)
Warfarin blocks vitamin K in the liver. The liver needs vitamin K to make certain clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X). Without enough vitamin K, the liver cannot make these clotting factors, so the blood takes longer to clot.

> **Think of it this way:** Vitamin K is like an ingredient the body needs to make clotting "glue." Warfarin takes that ingredient away, so less "glue" gets made.

### Common Uses
- Preventing stroke in patients with atrial fibrillation (A-fib)
- Treating and preventing DVT and PE
- Patients with mechanical heart valves
- After certain heart surgeries

### Route
- **Oral (by mouth)** — tablet form
- Taken once daily, usually in the evening (same time every day)

### VERY Important: Warfarin Takes Days to Work!
- Warfarin does NOT work right away. It takes about **3 to 5 days** (sometimes up to 7 days) for the full effect.
- During this waiting time, the patient needs another fast-acting anticoagulant (usually heparin or enoxaparin) as a "bridge."
- The bridge drug is stopped once the INR is in the therapeutic range for at least 24 hours.

> **In simple words:** Warfarin is slow to start. We use heparin like a "helper" until warfarin kicks in.

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- **INR (International Normalized Ratio)** — this is THE key lab for warfarin
  - Normal INR (for someone NOT on warfarin): about 0.8–1.2
  - Therapeutic INR goal on warfarin: usually **2.0 to 3.0**
  - For mechanical heart valves: goal may be **2.5 to 3.5**
- **Signs of bleeding**
- **Current diet** — how much vitamin K is the patient eating?
- **Current medications** — warfarin interacts with MANY drugs
- **Liver function** — warfarin is processed by the liver
- **Vital signs**
- **Pregnancy status** — warfarin is a **teratogen** (causes birth defects). It is Category X — NEVER give to pregnant patients.

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **Bleeding** — #1 concern (same signs as above)
- **INR results** — too high means too much anticoagulation (more bleeding risk)
- **Skin necrosis** — rare but serious complication, usually in the first few days. Look for painful purple or dark patches on the skin (buttocks, thighs, breasts).
- **Purple toe syndrome** — rare, look for painful blue/purple toes

### When to HOLD and Notify the Provider
- **INR above 3.0** (or above the ordered therapeutic range)
- Any sign of active bleeding
- Patient is going for surgery or invasive procedure
- Patient is pregnant or may be pregnant
- New medications that interact with warfarin

### INR Too High — What Happens?

| INR Level | Typical Action |
|-----------|---------------|
| 3.1 – 4.5 (mildly elevated) | Hold warfarin, recheck INR, may lower dose |
| 4.5 – 10 (no bleeding) | Hold warfarin, may give oral vitamin K |
| Above 10 (no bleeding) | Hold warfarin, give vitamin K (may be IV) |
| Any INR with serious bleeding | Hold warfarin, give IV vitamin K + fresh frozen plasma (FFP) or prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) |

### Key Labs
| Lab | Why | How Often |
|-----|-----|-----------|
| INR / PT | Monitors warfarin effectiveness | Frequently at first (every few days), then monthly when stable |
| CBC | Watches for anemia from bleeding | As ordered |
| Liver function tests | Warfarin needs a working liver | Baseline and as ordered |

### Antidote
**Vitamin K (phytonadione)** — reverses warfarin.
- Can be given orally, SubQ, or IV
- IV vitamin K works fastest but must be given **slowly** — risk of anaphylaxis (severe allergic reaction)
- Takes about **24 hours** to fully reverse warfarin
- For life-threatening bleeding: vitamin K + FFP or PCC for immediate reversal

### Key Patient Teaching (There Is a Lot!)

**Diet:**
- Do NOT suddenly change the amount of vitamin K foods you eat.
- You do NOT have to avoid vitamin K foods completely — just keep the amount **consistent** (about the same each week).
- High vitamin K foods include:
  - Green leafy vegetables: spinach, kale, broccoli, Brussels sprouts, collard greens, lettuce, Swiss chard
  - Green tea
  - Liver
  - Certain oils (soybean oil, canola oil)

> **Think of it this way:** If you eat a big salad every day, keep eating a big salad every day. If you never eat salad, don't suddenly start eating one every day. The key word is *consistent*.

**Medications to Avoid:**
- **Do NOT take aspirin or NSAIDs** (ibuprofen, naproxen) unless your doctor says so — they increase bleeding risk
- Use **acetaminophen (Tylenol)** for pain instead (but even Tylenol in high doses can increase INR)
- Tell EVERY doctor, dentist, and pharmacist that you take warfarin
- Many drugs interact with warfarin — always check before starting anything new, including:
  - Antibiotics (especially metronidazole, sulfamethoxazole/trimethoprim)
  - Antifungals
  - Some heart medications (amiodarone)
  - Herbal supplements (St. John's wort, ginkgo, garlic, ginger)

**General Teaching:**
- Take warfarin at the **same time every day**
- If you miss a dose, take it as soon as you remember on the same day. If it is the next day, skip the missed dose — do NOT double up.
- Keep all lab appointments (INR checks)
- Wear a **medical alert bracelet**
- Report any unusual bleeding or bruising
- Avoid contact sports and high-risk activities
- Use a soft toothbrush and electric razor
- Avoid alcohol or drink very little — alcohol affects how warfarin works

### Key Drug Interactions (Partial List)
| Increases Warfarin Effect (↑ bleeding risk) | Decreases Warfarin Effect (↓ clot protection) |
|---------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------|
| Aspirin, NSAIDs | Vitamin K (large amounts) |
| Many antibiotics | St. John's wort |
| Amiodarone | Rifampin |
| Acetaminophen (high doses) | Phenytoin, carbamazepine |
| Alcohol (acute/binge) | Oral contraceptives |
| Ginkgo, garlic, ginger | Chronic heavy alcohol use |

## 4. Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)

### Overview
DOACs are newer blood thinners that are easier to manage than warfarin.

The two most common ones you need to know:
- **Rivaroxaban (Xarelto)** — blocks factor Xa
- **Apixaban (Eliquis)** — blocks factor Xa

> **In simple words:** DOACs are the "newer, easier" blood thinners. They work in a more predictable way, so patients do not need as many blood tests.

### Common Uses
- Preventing stroke in atrial fibrillation (A-fib)
- Treating and preventing DVT and PE
- Preventing clots after hip or knee replacement surgery

### Route
- **Oral (by mouth)**
- Rivaroxaban: take with the **evening meal** (food helps absorption)
- Apixaban: can be taken with or without food

### Big Advantages Over Warfarin
| Feature | Warfarin | DOACs |
|---------|----------|-------|
| Routine INR monitoring? | Yes (frequent) | **No** |
| Many food interactions? | Yes (vitamin K foods) | **Fewer** |
| Dose adjustments frequent? | Yes | Less often |
| Bridge with heparin needed? | Yes (slow onset) | **No** (works quickly) |
| Drug interactions? | Very many | Fewer (but still some) |

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- **Kidney function (creatinine, GFR)** — DOACs are cleared by the kidneys. Poor kidney function means the drug builds up.
- **Liver function** — especially for rivaroxaban
- **Signs of bleeding**
- **Hemoglobin / hematocrit**
- **Current medications** — some drugs still interact
- **No routine INR or aPTT needed** — but these labs may be checked in emergencies

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **Bleeding** — still the #1 concern!
  - Same bleeding signs as all other anticoagulants
- **Kidney function** — especially in elderly patients
- **GI side effects** — some patients get stomach upset

### When to HOLD and Notify the Provider
- Active bleeding
- Worsening kidney function
- Patient going for surgery (each DOAC has specific timing for when to stop before surgery)
- Significant drop in hemoglobin

### Key Labs
| Lab | Why | How Often |
|-----|-----|-----------|
| Creatinine / GFR | Kidney function — drug clearance | Baseline, then at least yearly (more often if kidney problems) |
| CBC | Detects bleeding | As ordered |
| Liver function | Especially for rivaroxaban | As ordered |
| No routine INR or aPTT | Not used for monitoring DOACs | — |

### Reversal Agents
- **Andexanet alfa (Andexxa)** — reversal agent for rivaroxaban and apixaban (factor Xa inhibitors)
- This is expensive and not available everywhere
- For life-threatening bleeding, activated charcoal may be given if the drug was taken recently, and other supportive measures are used

> **Important for exams:** Know that DOACs now have some reversal options. Previously, a major disadvantage of DOACs was that they had NO antidote.

### Key Patient Teaching
- Take the medication exactly as prescribed — do NOT skip doses
- Rivaroxaban: take with your **evening meal**
- Do NOT stop taking without talking to your doctor — stopping suddenly can increase clot risk
- Report any unusual bleeding
- Tell all healthcare providers you take this medication
- Keep lab appointments for kidney function checks
- Avoid taking with other blood thinners unless prescribed
- **Do NOT crush** rivaroxaban tablets (affects absorption)
- Apixaban can be crushed if needed and mixed with applesauce

### Key Drug Interactions
- **Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors or inducers** (ketoconazole, rifampin) — can increase or decrease drug levels
- **Other anticoagulants or antiplatelets** — increase bleeding risk
- **NSAIDs** — increase bleeding risk
- **Some HIV medications** — can affect drug levels

## 5. Aspirin (Acetylsalicylic Acid — ASA)

### What It Does (In Simple Words)
Low-dose aspirin stops platelets from sticking together. Platelets are the first step in forming a clot. Aspirin blocks an enzyme called COX-1 in platelets, and this effect is **irreversible** — it lasts for the entire life of that platelet (about 7–10 days).

> **Think of it this way:** Once aspirin touches a platelet, that platelet can never stick to other platelets again. The body has to make brand new platelets to replace them.

### Common Uses (Low-Dose: 81 mg — "Baby Aspirin")
- Preventing heart attacks in patients with coronary artery disease
- Preventing stroke in certain patients
- After stent placement (usually combined with clopidogrel = "dual antiplatelet therapy")
- Acute heart attack treatment (the patient chews an aspirin in the ER)

### Route
- **Oral** — tablet, chewable, or enteric-coated
- During an acute MI (heart attack): patient should **chew** the aspirin (works faster)

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- **Allergy history** — aspirin allergy can cause severe reactions (bronchospasm, especially in patients with asthma or nasal polyps)
- **Bleeding history and current bleeding signs**
- **Current medications** — especially other blood thinners or NSAIDs
- **GI history** — history of ulcers or GI bleeding
- **Age** — NEVER give aspirin to children or teenagers with viral illness (risk of Reye syndrome)
- **Platelet count**
- **Hemoglobin / hematocrit**

### Reye Syndrome Warning
- Reye syndrome is a rare but **life-threatening** condition that causes liver failure and brain swelling.
- It is linked to aspirin use in **children and teenagers** who have viral illnesses (flu, chickenpox).
- **NEVER give aspirin to children under 19 with fever or viral illness.**
- Use acetaminophen (Tylenol) or ibuprofen instead for children.

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **GI bleeding** — aspirin irritates the stomach lining
  - Watch for: dark/tarry stools (melena), stomach pain, nausea, vomiting blood (hematemesis)
- **Bruising and prolonged bleeding** from cuts
- **Tinnitus** (ringing in the ears) — sign of aspirin toxicity (more relevant with high doses)
- **Allergic reactions** — especially in patients with asthma

### When to HOLD and Notify the Provider
- Active GI bleeding
- Allergy signs (rash, difficulty breathing, swelling)
- Before surgery (usually held 7–10 days before)
- Tinnitus or signs of toxicity
- Significant drop in hemoglobin

### Key Labs
| Lab | Why | How Often |
|-----|-----|-----------|
| Platelet count | Monitors platelet levels | As ordered |
| Hemoglobin / Hematocrit | Detects GI or other bleeding | As ordered |
| Stool guaiac | Checks for hidden GI blood | As ordered |
| Bleeding time | Rarely checked but may be prolonged | As ordered |

### Antidote
- There is **no specific antidote** for aspirin's antiplatelet effect.
- Since the effect is irreversible, you have to wait for the body to make new platelets (about 7–10 days).
- For severe bleeding: platelet transfusion may be given.

### Key Patient Teaching
- Take with food or a full glass of water to protect the stomach
- Do NOT take on an empty stomach
- Report any stomach pain, dark stools, or unusual bleeding
- Tell your surgeon or dentist you take aspirin (they may want you to stop before procedures)
- Do NOT give aspirin to children with fever or flu-like illness
- Do NOT stop taking without your doctor's approval (especially if you have a stent)
- Avoid alcohol — increases GI bleeding risk
- Check labels on over-the-counter medications — many contain aspirin (cold medicines, Alka-Seltzer, etc.)

### Key Drug Interactions
- **Other NSAIDs (ibuprofen)** — ibuprofen can reduce aspirin's antiplatelet effect if taken at the same time. Take aspirin first, then wait 30 minutes before ibuprofen.
- **Warfarin and other anticoagulants** — greatly increases bleeding risk
- **Alcohol** — increases GI bleeding risk
- **ACE inhibitors** — aspirin may reduce their blood pressure effect (at high doses)
- **Methotrexate** — aspirin can increase methotrexate toxicity

## 6. Clopidogrel (Plavix)

### What It Does (In Simple Words)
Clopidogrel stops platelets from sticking together by blocking a receptor called ADP (P2Y12 receptor) on the platelet surface. Like aspirin, its effect is **irreversible**.

> **In simple words:** Clopidogrel blocks a different "sticky spot" on platelets than aspirin does. That is why they are sometimes used together — they block two different pathways.

### Common Uses
- After heart stent placement (usually with aspirin = "dual antiplatelet therapy")
- Preventing stroke in patients who cannot take aspirin
- Acute coronary syndrome (ACS)
- After heart attack
- Peripheral arterial disease

### Route
- **Oral** — tablet, once daily

### What to Check BEFORE Giving
- **Bleeding history and current bleeding signs**
- **Upcoming surgeries** — must be stopped **5 to 7 days before** surgery
- **Current medications** — watch for drug interactions
- **Platelet count**
- **Hemoglobin / hematocrit**
- **Allergy to thienopyridines** (the drug class)

### What to Watch AFTER Giving
- **Bleeding** — #1 concern
  - Same bleeding signs as all blood thinners
- **Bruising**
- **GI symptoms** — stomach upset, diarrhea
- A rare but serious side effect: **TTP (thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura)** — very low platelets, fever, confusion, kidney problems, bruising. Report immediately.

### When to HOLD and Notify the Provider
- Active bleeding
- Surgery is planned within the next 5–7 days
- Significant platelet count drop
- Signs of TTP (fever, confusion, bruising, kidney changes)

### Key Labs
| Lab | Why | How Often |
|-----|-----|-----------|
| Platelet count | Monitors platelets | As ordered |
| Hemoglobin / Hematocrit | Detects bleeding | As ordered |
| CBC | Overall blood monitoring | As ordered |

### Antidote
- There is **no specific antidote** for clopidogrel.
- Since the effect is irreversible, you wait for new platelets (about 5–7 days for the effect to wear off).
- For severe bleeding: platelet transfusion may help.

### Key Patient Teaching
- Take at the same time every day
- **Do NOT stop taking** without talking to your doctor — especially if you have a stent (stopping suddenly can cause the stent to clot → heart attack)
- Report any unusual bleeding, bruising, or dark stools
- Tell your surgeon and dentist you take this medication — it needs to be held before surgery
- Use a soft toothbrush and electric razor
- Avoid activities with high injury risk

### Key Drug Interactions
- **Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)** — especially **omeprazole (Prilosec)** — can reduce clopidogrel's effectiveness. If a PPI is needed, **pantoprazole (Protonix)** is preferred.
- **Aspirin** — used together on purpose after stents, but increases bleeding risk
- **Other anticoagulants** — increase bleeding risk
- **NSAIDs** — increase bleeding risk

> **NCLEX favorite:** The interaction between clopidogrel and omeprazole is a commonly tested point. Remember: omeprazole reduces clopidogrel's effectiveness. Pantoprazole is the safer PPI choice.

## Bleeding Precautions — For ALL Patients on Anticoagulants or Antiplatelets

This section applies to EVERY drug in this guide. Any patient taking a blood thinner needs these safety measures.

### Personal Safety
- **Use a soft-bristled toothbrush** — hard bristles can make gums bleed
- **Use an electric razor** — regular razors cause nicks and cuts
- **Avoid contact sports** and activities with high risk of injury (football, boxing, skiing)
- **Be careful with sharp objects** — knives, scissors, etc.
- **Wear shoes or slippers** — even at home, to protect feet from cuts

### Fall Prevention
- Keep walkways clear of clutter
- Use non-slip mats in the bathroom
- Use handrails on stairs
- Get up slowly from bed or chair (to prevent dizziness and falls)
- Use a nightlight at night
- Report any dizziness to your nurse or doctor

> **Why falls matter so much:** When you are on a blood thinner, even a small fall can cause dangerous internal bleeding — especially in the brain (intracranial hemorrhage).

### What to Report IMMEDIATELY
Tell your nurse or doctor right away if you notice:
- **Bleeding gums** when brushing teeth
- **Nosebleeds** that will not stop
- **Blood in urine** (pink, red, or dark brown color)
- **Blood in stool** (bright red blood or dark, tarry stools)
- **Coughing up blood**
- **Vomiting blood** (may look like coffee grounds)
- **Unusual bruising** — bruises that appear without a reason
- **Prolonged bleeding** from a small cut that will not stop
- **Severe headache** (could mean bleeding in the brain)
- **Dizziness, weakness, or feeling faint**
- **Vision changes**
- **Abdominal or back pain** that is new or getting worse

### Medical Alert
- **Wear a medical alert bracelet or necklace** that says you take a blood thinner
- **Carry a card** in your wallet listing your medications
- Tell every healthcare provider (doctors, dentists, pharmacists, physical therapists) that you take a blood thinner

### Medication Safety
- **Never double a dose** if you miss one
- **Do not start or stop** any new medication (including vitamins, supplements, or herbal products) without asking your doctor or pharmacist
- **Do not drink alcohol** or limit it — alcohol increases bleeding risk and can interact with blood thinners
- **Check labels** on over-the-counter medications for aspirin or blood-thinning ingredients

## Memory Helpers

Here are some tips to help you remember:

- **Heparin → aPTT** (both start with a vowel-like sound — "H" and "a")
- **Warfarin → INR** (Warfarin = W = "Watch the INR")
- **Warfarin + Vitamin K** — they are "enemies." Warfarin blocks vitamin K. Vitamin K reverses warfarin.
- **HIT** = **H**eparin-**I**nduced **T**hrombocytopenia. Low platelets + MORE clots (the paradox!)
- **Protamine** reverses heparin. Think: **Pro**tamine **pro**tects from heparin.
- **DOACs = D**irect **O**ral **A**nti**C**oagulants — the "easy" ones (fewer labs, fewer food interactions)
- **Clopidogrel + Omeprazole = Bad combo.** Use pantoprazole instead.
- **Aspirin + Children + Virus = Reye syndrome.** Never give aspirin to kids with viral illness.
