# Exam 2 Flashcards

These flashcards cover ANS drugs and endocrine drugs for Pharmacology Exam 2. They focus on the safety decisions and bedside thinking that nursing exams love to test.

## How to use this deck

- **Front** = the question you try to answer from memory.
- **Back** = the answer — keep it short and clear.
- **Why it matters** = connects the card to exam thinking or real patient safety.
- Each card has a **(Tag)** so you know what skill it practices.

---

## Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) Drugs

### Card 1 (Recall)

**Front:** What is the difference between the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems?

**Back:**
- **Sympathetic** = fight or flight → raises heart rate and blood pressure, opens airways.
- **Parasympathetic** = rest and digest → slows heart, increases secretions, increases GI and bladder activity.

**Why it matters:** Once you know which side a drug works on, you can predict most of its effects and side effects.

### Card 2 (Recall)

**Front:** What do alpha-1, beta-1, and beta-2 receptors mainly do?

**Back:**
- **Alpha-1:** tightens blood vessels → raises blood pressure.
- **Beta-1:** speeds up and strengthens the heart.
- **Beta-2:** relaxes airway smooth muscle → opens the lungs.

Think of it this way: **"1 heart, 2 lungs"** — beta-**1** = heart, beta-**2** = lungs.

**Why it matters:** These three receptors explain most sympathetic drug effects in one sentence each.

### Card 3 (Emergency)

**Front:** A patient is in anaphylaxis with throat swelling and a blood pressure of 70/40. What drug do you expect first, and what does it do?

**Back:**
- **Epinephrine** (IM, usually the thigh).
- It hits alpha-1 (raises BP), beta-1 (supports heart), and beta-2 (opens airways) all at once.
- Watch for tachycardia, tremor, and hypertension afterward.

**Why it matters:** Epinephrine is the #1 drug for anaphylaxis. The exam may test that you give it fast, before steroids or antihistamines.

### Card 4 (Safety)

**Front:** A patient takes midodrine for orthostatic hypotension. They ask if they can take the evening dose right before bed. What do you say?

**Back:**
- No — midodrine is an alpha-1 agonist that raises blood pressure.
- Taking it before lying flat can cause dangerous **supine hypertension**.
- The last dose should be taken at least 3–4 hours before bedtime.

**Why it matters:** Exams test that the same drug that helps standing BP can hurt lying-down BP.

### Card 5 (Application)

**Front:** A COPD patient is short of breath right now. The nurse has albuterol and ipratropium available. Which one is the rescue drug?

**Back:**
- **Albuterol** — it is the fast-acting beta-2 rescue bronchodilator.
- **Ipratropium** is an inhaled anticholinergic used more for maintenance in COPD. It works slower.
- Albuterol side effects: tremor, tachycardia, nervousness.

**Why it matters:** Do not confuse these two. Exams love to ask which inhaler you grab first for acute distress.

### Card 6 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** What do you teach a patient who just started prazosin about safety?

**Back:**
- Prazosin is an alpha-1 blocker — it lowers blood pressure.
- The biggest risk is **first-dose orthostatic hypotension** (dizziness or fainting).
- Teach: take the first dose at bedtime, rise slowly from sitting or lying, and hold onto something steady.

**Why it matters:** First-dose syncope with prazosin is a classic exam question. Teaching prevents falls.

### Card 7 (Compare)

**Front:** How is tamsulosin different from prazosin?

**Back:**
- Both are alpha-1 blockers, but **tamsulosin** is more selective for alpha-1A receptors in the prostate and bladder neck.
- Tamsulosin is mainly used for BPH (benign prostatic hyperplasia) to improve urine flow.
- It causes less blood pressure drop than prazosin, but dizziness can still happen.

**Why it matters:** Exams ask you to pick the right alpha blocker for the right situation — blood pressure vs. urine flow.

### Card 8 (Hold Parameter)

**Front:** Metoprolol is due, but the patient's heart rate is 56 bpm. What do you do and why?

**Back:**
- **Hold the medication** and notify the provider.
- Metoprolol is a beta-1 blocker — it lowers heart rate further.
- General hold parameter: hold if HR < 60 or systolic BP < 90–100 (follow your facility's policy).
- Do not stop beta blockers abruptly on your own — just hold the dose and report.

**Why it matters:** "When do you hold a beta blocker?" is one of the most common exam questions. Know the numbers.

### Card 9 (Hold Parameter)

**Front:** Metoprolol is due, but the patient's blood pressure is 86/52. What do you do?

**Back:**
- **Hold the dose** and notify the provider.
- Metoprolol lowers both heart rate and blood pressure.
- Giving it at this BP could cause dangerous hypotension, dizziness, or fainting.

**Why it matters:** The exam may give you normal HR but low BP — you still hold. Check both numbers every time.

### Card 10 (Safety)

**Front:** Why should a nurse never tell a patient to just stop their beta blocker (like metoprolol)?

**Back:**
- Stopping suddenly can cause **rebound tachycardia** and even a hypertensive crisis or angina.
- Beta blockers must be tapered slowly under provider guidance.

Think of it this way: The heart has been "held back" by the drug. If you remove the drug all at once, the heart speeds up dangerously.

**Why it matters:** Abrupt discontinuation of beta blockers is a common exam safety question.

### Card 11 (Application)

**Front:** A patient on bethanechol says they have not urinated yet. Is this expected or a problem?

**Back:**
- It is a problem — bethanechol is a muscarinic agonist given to **help the bladder empty**.
- If the patient still cannot void, suspect a possible urinary obstruction and notify the provider immediately.
- Never give bethanechol if obstruction is suspected — the bladder could rupture.

**Why it matters:** Exams test that bethanechol helps the bladder contract, and that obstruction is a contraindication.

### Card 12 (Recall)

**Front:** What is pilocarpine used for, and what side effects match its class?

**Back:**
- Pilocarpine is a direct muscarinic agonist.
- Used for **glaucoma** (lowers eye pressure) and **dry mouth** (increases saliva).
- Side effects are classic parasympathetic: sweating, salivation, blurred vision, trouble seeing in dim light.

**Why it matters:** If you see sweating + salivation + blurred vision, think cholinergic drug.

### Card 13 (Priority)

**Front:** A patient on pyridostigmine for myasthenia gravis says they feel very weak. How do you figure out if this is too much or too little drug?

**Back:**
- **Myasthenic crisis** (too little drug) = weakness, trouble breathing, but no excess secretions.
- **Cholinergic crisis** (too much drug) = weakness PLUS sweating, drooling, diarrhea, small pupils, bradycardia.
- A provider may give a test dose of edrophonium (Tensilon) to tell the difference. If symptoms improve → myasthenic crisis. If symptoms get worse → cholinergic crisis.

**Why it matters:** Both crises cause weakness, but only cholinergic crisis adds "too wet" symptoms. This distinction saves lives.

### Card 14 (Emergency)

**Front:** A patient on pyridostigmine suddenly develops wheezing, excess drooling, diarrhea, and bradycardia. What is happening and what do you do?

**Back:**
- This is a **cholinergic crisis** — too much acetylcholine activity.
- **Stop the pyridostigmine** and call the provider immediately.
- The antidote is **atropine** (it blocks the excess cholinergic effects).
- Be ready to support the airway — secretions can block breathing.

**Why it matters:** Recognizing cholinergic crisis and knowing atropine is the antidote are high-priority exam topics.

### Card 15 (Application)

**Front:** A patient has symptomatic bradycardia with a heart rate of 38 and dizziness. Why is atropine the right drug, and what effect do you expect?

**Back:**
- Atropine is an **anticholinergic** — it blocks the parasympathetic nervous system.
- Since parasympathetic activity slows the heart, blocking it speeds the heart up.
- You expect heart rate to rise toward a safer range (usually above 60).
- Watch for overcorrection: dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention, tachycardia.

**Why it matters:** Atropine for symptomatic bradycardia is one of the most tested emergency drugs.

### Card 16 (Safety)

**Front:** An elderly patient on oxybutynin for overactive bladder is confused and has not had a bowel movement in 3 days. Should you be concerned?

**Back:**
- Yes — these are **anticholinergic side effects**: confusion (especially in elderly) and constipation.
- Other anticholinergic effects to watch: dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention, heat intolerance.
- Report to the provider — the medication may need to be changed.

Think of it this way: Anticholinergics make everything **dry and slow** — dry mouth, slow gut, slow urine, blurry eyes.

**Why it matters:** Elderly patients are extra sensitive to anticholinergic effects. Exams love this scenario.

### Card 17 (Compare)

**Front:** How do cholinergic effects compare with anticholinergic effects?

**Back:**
- **Cholinergic (parasympathetic ON):** more secretions, more urination, more GI activity, slower heart, smaller pupils.
- **Anticholinergic (parasympathetic OFF):** dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation, blurred vision, faster heart, dilated pupils.

Think of it this way: **Cholinergic = "too wet and too slow."** Anticholinergic = **"too dry and too fast."**

**Why it matters:** If you memorize one side, you automatically know the other — they are perfect opposites.

### Card 18 (Memory Helper)

**Front:** What does the phrase "too wet and too slow" help you remember?

**Back:**
- It describes **cholinergic side effects**: sweating, salivation, tears, diarrhea, urination, bronchospasm (all "wet") plus bradycardia ("slow").
- If a patient has these signs after a cholinergic drug, think **cholinergic excess**.
- The antidote for cholinergic excess is **atropine** (an anticholinergic that dries things up and speeds the heart).

**Why it matters:** This one phrase helps you answer questions about bethanechol, pyridostigmine, and organophosphate poisoning.

### Card 19 (Memory Helper)

**Front:** What is a simple way to remember anticholinergic side effects?

**Back:**
- **"Can't see, can't pee, can't spit, can't ——"** (a common nursing rhyme).
- Can't see = blurred vision / dilated pupils.
- Can't pee = urinary retention.
- Can't spit = dry mouth.
- Can't go (bowels) = constipation.
- Also: tachycardia, confusion (especially in elderly), heat intolerance.

**Why it matters:** This rhyme covers the side effects of atropine, oxybutynin, ipratropium, and any anticholinergic drug.

### Card 20 (Compare)

**Front:** Why are bethanechol and oxybutynin considered opposite bladder drugs?

**Back:**
- **Bethanechol** (cholinergic agonist) → makes the bladder contract → helps a patient who **cannot empty**.
- **Oxybutynin** (anticholinergic) → relaxes the bladder → helps a patient who **goes too often**.
- They work on the same organ but do opposite things.

**Why it matters:** Exams ask "which drug for urinary retention vs. overactive bladder?" This card answers both at once.

### Card 21 (Application)

**Front:** A patient on prazosin stands up and feels very dizzy. Their blood pressure is 78/48. What happened and what do you do?

**Back:**
- This is **orthostatic hypotension** — prazosin is an alpha-1 blocker that lowers blood pressure, especially when changing positions.
- Lay the patient down flat right away to help blood return to the brain.
- Check vital signs and notify the provider.
- This is most common with the **first dose** or after a dose increase.

**Why it matters:** Prazosin + position change + low BP = orthostatic hypotension. Know the intervention: lay them down, check vitals, report.

### Card 22 (Hold Parameter)

**Front:** A patient on albuterol nebulizer has a heart rate of 128. Should you give the treatment?

**Back:**
- **Hold the treatment** and notify the provider.
- Albuterol is a beta-2 agonist, but it can also stimulate beta-1 receptors → tachycardia.
- A heart rate this high means the drug could push it even higher, which is dangerous.
- General hold parameter: hold albuterol if HR is above 120 (follow your facility's policy).

**Why it matters:** Albuterol helps breathing but can speed up the heart. Always check HR before giving it.

### Card 23 (Compare)

**Front:** Why is epinephrine given IM in anaphylaxis but IV in cardiac arrest?

**Back:**
- In **anaphylaxis**, IM injection (usually the outer thigh) works fast enough and is safer. IV epi can cause fatal arrhythmias if pushed too fast.
- In **cardiac arrest**, the heart is not pumping, so an IM injection cannot circulate. You need the IV route to get the drug to the heart directly.
- Different situations → different routes → same drug.

**Why it matters:** Exams test that you know the right route for the right emergency. IM for anaphylaxis, IV for cardiac arrest.

### Card 24 (Safety)

**Front:** A patient accidentally gets both bethanechol and atropine. Which effects cancel out?

**Back:**
- Bethanechol is a **cholinergic agonist** (turns parasympathetic ON).
- Atropine is an **anticholinergic** (turns parasympathetic OFF).
- They are opposites — many effects cancel out: bladder contraction, GI activity, heart rate changes, secretions.
- But this does not mean it is safe! The timing and doses may not match perfectly, so the patient could swing between cholinergic and anticholinergic effects.

**Why it matters:** Understanding that these drugs are opposites helps you predict interactions and know the antidote for cholinergic excess.

### Card 25 (Application)

**Front:** An elderly patient on oxybutynin becomes confused and falls. What is the connection?

**Back:**
- Oxybutynin is an **anticholinergic** drug. In elderly patients, anticholinergics can cause confusion, dizziness, and sedation.
- Confusion led to the fall — this is a medication-related fall risk.
- The nurse should report this, document the fall, and the provider may need to switch to a safer medication.

Think of it this way: Elderly brains are more sensitive to anticholinergic effects. Confusion → unsteady → fall.

**Why it matters:** Anticholinergic drugs in the elderly causing falls is a major patient safety topic on exams.

### Card 26 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** What do you teach a patient with myasthenia gravis about timing pyridostigmine with meals?

**Back:**
- Take pyridostigmine **30–45 minutes before meals**.
- The drug strengthens muscle contractions — including the muscles used for chewing and swallowing.
- If taken too late, the patient may not have enough muscle strength to eat safely.
- Taking it too early means the effect could wear off before the meal ends.

**Why it matters:** Timing pyridostigmine with meals prevents choking and aspiration. This is a common teaching point on exams.

### Card 27 (Lab)

**Front:** What labs should you check for a patient on long-term beta blockers who also has diabetes?

**Back:**
- **Blood glucose** — beta blockers can mask the signs of hypoglycemia (like tachycardia and tremor).
- The patient may not feel their blood sugar dropping, which is dangerous.
- Also check **heart rate and blood pressure** at every visit.
- Some providers also monitor **lipid panels** since some beta blockers can affect cholesterol.

**Why it matters:** Beta blockers hiding hypoglycemia symptoms in diabetic patients is a classic exam trap. The patient feels fine, but their sugar is dangerously low.

### Card 28 (Priority)

**Front:** A patient has sudden severe bronchospasm. They have both albuterol and ipratropium available. Which do you give first?

**Back:**
- Give **albuterol first** — it is the fast-acting rescue bronchodilator (works in minutes).
- Ipratropium is an anticholinergic bronchodilator that works more slowly (15–30 minutes for full effect).
- In an acute emergency, speed matters most.
- They can be used together, but albuterol goes first.

**Why it matters:** Knowing which inhaler is rescue (albuterol) vs. maintenance (ipratropium) can show up on priority questions.

### Card 29 (Compare)

**Front:** How do you tell cholinergic crisis from myasthenic crisis apart?

**Back:**
- Both cause **muscle weakness** — that is the confusing part.
- **Myasthenic crisis** (too little drug): weakness + dry secretions, no excess GI symptoms.
- **Cholinergic crisis** (too much drug): weakness PLUS **excess secretions** — drooling, sweating, diarrhea, small pupils, bradycardia.
- Key question: "Is the patient wet or dry?" Wet = cholinergic crisis. Dry = myasthenic crisis.

**Why it matters:** This is one of the hardest ANS questions on exams. "Wet vs. dry" is the fastest way to answer it.

### Card 30 (Safety)

**Front:** What is the danger of combining two anticholinergic drugs in an elderly patient?

**Back:**
- The anticholinergic effects **add up** — this is called **anticholinergic burden**.
- Combined effects: severe confusion, delirium, urinary retention, constipation (even bowel obstruction), overheating, falls.
- Many common drugs have hidden anticholinergic effects (diphenhydramine, certain antidepressants, oxybutynin).
- Always check the full medication list before adding another anticholinergic.

**Why it matters:** The exam may list two seemingly unrelated drugs that both have anticholinergic effects. You need to spot the combined risk.

### Card 31 (Application)

**Front:** A patient on metoprolol needs emergency surgery. The surgeon says to stop the beta blocker today. What is the concern?

**Back:**
- Stopping metoprolol suddenly can cause **rebound tachycardia and hypertension** — the heart speeds up dangerously.
- This is especially risky during surgery when the heart is already under stress.
- The nurse should clarify with the surgeon and possibly consult the prescribing provider.
- Beta blockers should be **tapered**, not stopped abruptly, whenever possible.

**Why it matters:** Even in an emergency, abrupt beta blocker discontinuation is dangerous. The exam tests that you advocate for the patient.

### Card 32 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** A patient on atenolol says "I feel tired all the time." Is this expected?

**Back:**
- Yes — **fatigue is a common side effect** of beta blockers like atenolol.
- Beta blockers slow the heart and lower blood pressure, which can make patients feel tired, sluggish, or even a little down.
- Do not stop the medication — talk to the provider about adjusting the dose or switching drugs.
- Also report: dizziness, depression, or sexual dysfunction (other common beta blocker side effects).

**Why it matters:** Patients often stop beta blockers because of fatigue. Teaching them it is expected helps them stay on the medication safely.

### Card 33 (Compare)

**Front:** Prazosin vs. tamsulosin — when would you use each one?

**Back:**
- Both are **alpha-1 blockers**, but they are used for different things.
- **Prazosin** blocks alpha-1 broadly → used mainly for **high blood pressure** (and sometimes PTSD nightmares).
- **Tamsulosin** is selective for alpha-1A in the prostate → used for **BPH** (trouble urinating due to enlarged prostate).
- Tamsulosin causes less blood pressure drop, but dizziness can still happen.

**Why it matters:** Exams may ask you to pick the right alpha blocker for the situation. Blood pressure = prazosin. Prostate = tamsulosin.

### Card 34 (Safety)

**Front:** A patient on ipratropium accidentally sprays it in their eyes. What can happen?

**Back:**
- Ipratropium is an anticholinergic — in the eyes, it can cause **pupil dilation (mydriasis)** and **increased eye pressure**.
- This is especially dangerous in patients with **narrow-angle glaucoma** — it can trigger an acute glaucoma attack.
- Teach patients to use a mouthpiece (not a face mask if possible) or close their eyes during nebulizer treatments.
- If it happens: flush the eyes with water and report to the provider.

**Why it matters:** Ipratropium + eyes + glaucoma is a safety question that shows up on exams. Prevention is key.

## Endocrine Drugs

### Card 35 (Hold Parameter)

**Front:** A patient on metformin has a new lab: creatinine is 2.8 mg/dL. What is the concern?

**Back:**
- Metformin is cleared by the kidneys. High creatinine means the kidneys are not working well.
- If metformin builds up, the patient is at risk for **lactic acidosis** — a rare but life-threatening complication.
- Notify the provider — metformin may need to be held or stopped.

**Why it matters:** "Hold metformin when kidneys are failing" is a must-know safety rule for the exam.

### Card 36 (Lab)

**Front:** What labs should you watch for a patient taking metformin, and why?

**Back:**
- **Serum creatinine / GFR** — metformin must be held if kidney function drops (risk of lactic acidosis).
- **Blood glucose** — to see if the drug is working (metformin rarely causes hypoglycemia alone, but check anyway).
- **Lactic acid** — if the patient develops nausea, muscle pain, or fatigue, lactic acidosis must be ruled out.

**Why it matters:** Kidney function is the #1 lab to connect with metformin on the exam.

### Card 37 (Safety)

**Front:** A patient on a sulfonylurea (like glipizide) skipped lunch and now feels shaky, sweaty, and confused. What is happening and what do you do?

**Back:**
- This is **hypoglycemia** — sulfonylureas push the pancreas to release insulin even when glucose is low.
- Give a fast-acting sugar source (juice, glucose tablets) if the patient can swallow safely.
- Check blood glucose and notify the provider.
- Teach the patient: never skip meals while on this drug.

**Why it matters:** Sulfonylurea + missed meal = hypoglycemia is a classic exam scenario.

### Card 38 (Application)

**Front:** A patient asks why empagliflozin makes them urinate so much. How do you explain it simply?

**Back:**
- Empagliflozin is an SGLT2 inhibitor — it works in the kidneys to push extra glucose out through the urine.
- More glucose in the urine pulls more water with it → increased urination and thirst.
- This also means more sugar near the genital area → higher risk of **yeast infections and UTIs**.
- Rare risk: **ketoacidosis** (even with normal blood glucose sometimes).

**Why it matters:** Patients need to understand the "why" behind their symptoms so they stay on the drug and report problems early.

### Card 39 (Compare)

**Front:** How do metformin, sulfonylureas, and SGLT2 inhibitors (like empagliflozin) compare?

**Back:**
| Feature | Metformin | Sulfonylureas | SGLT2 inhibitors |
|---|---|---|---|
| How it works | Lowers liver glucose output | Makes pancreas release more insulin | Pushes glucose out in urine |
| Hypoglycemia risk | Low alone | **Yes** | Low alone |
| Key side effect | GI upset | Weight gain | Yeast infections, UTIs, dehydration |
| Key safety concern | Lactic acidosis if kidneys fail | Missed meals → low sugar | Rare ketoacidosis |

**Why it matters:** The exam loves comparison questions about oral diabetes drugs. One table answers many of them.

### Card 40 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** What do you teach a patient starting prednisone about stopping the medication?

**Back:**
- **Never stop prednisone suddenly** after taking it for more than a few days.
- Long-term use suppresses the adrenal glands — they need time to "wake up" again.
- Stopping suddenly can cause **adrenal crisis**: severe weakness, low blood pressure, nausea, shock.
- The provider will give a slow taper schedule — follow it exactly.

Think of it this way: Prednisone replaces what the adrenal glands make. If you stop fast, the glands are still "asleep" and cannot keep up.

**Why it matters:** Abrupt steroid discontinuation is a top safety topic across many nursing exams.

### Card 41 (Hold Parameter)

**Front:** A patient on prednisone has a blood glucose of 340 mg/dL. What should you do?

**Back:**
- **Report to the provider** — prednisone is a glucocorticoid and commonly raises blood sugar (steroid-induced hyperglycemia).
- The patient may need sliding-scale insulin or a medication adjustment.
- This is especially dangerous if the patient already has diabetes.

**Why it matters:** Steroids + high glucose is a very common exam combination. Always connect prednisone to blood sugar monitoring.

### Card 42 (Safety)

**Front:** A patient has been on prednisone for 3 weeks and has a low-grade fever. Why is this extra concerning?

**Back:**
- Prednisone suppresses the immune system — it can **mask signs of infection**.
- A low-grade fever in an immunosuppressed patient may actually mean a serious infection is hiding.
- Report immediately and expect further workup (blood cultures, labs).
- Also watch for poor wound healing.

**Why it matters:** "Infection risk with steroids" is one of the most common prednisone questions on exams.

### Card 43 (Lab)

**Front:** A patient on levothyroxine has a TSH of 8.2 mU/L (normal is about 0.5–4.5). What does this tell you?

**Back:**
- TSH is **high** → the body is still asking for more thyroid hormone → the levothyroxine dose is probably **too low**.
- The provider may increase the dose.
- Remember: TSH and thyroid hormone work in **opposite directions** (high TSH = low thyroid function).

Think of it this way: TSH is the brain "yelling" at the thyroid. If TSH is high, the brain is yelling louder because it is not getting enough hormone.

**Why it matters:** Exams test whether you know that high TSH = hypothyroid = need more drug, not less.

### Card 44 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** What are the key things you teach a patient starting levothyroxine?

**Back:**
- Take it on an **empty stomach**, usually first thing in the morning.
- Wait at least 30–60 minutes before eating.
- Keep it **away from calcium and iron supplements** (they block absorption — separate by 4 hours).
- It takes weeks to feel the full effect — be patient and keep taking it.
- Report signs of too much: fast heartbeat, tremor, weight loss, trouble sleeping.

**Why it matters:** Timing and food/supplement separation are the #1 levothyroxine teaching points on exams.

### Card 45 (Lab)

**Front:** A patient on levothyroxine has a TSH of 0.1 mU/L and complains of palpitations, weight loss, and insomnia. What is going on?

**Back:**
- TSH is **very low** → the body is getting too much thyroid hormone → the dose may be **too high**.
- The symptoms (palpitations, weight loss, insomnia, tremor) are signs of **hyperthyroidism / overreplacement**.
- Notify the provider — the dose likely needs to be decreased.

**Why it matters:** Exams test both directions: high TSH = underdosed, low TSH with hyper symptoms = overdosed.

### Card 46 (Emergency)

**Front:** A patient on a sulfonylurea is found unresponsive with a blood glucose of 34 mg/dL. What do you do?

**Back:**
- This is **severe hypoglycemia** — the patient cannot swallow safely.
- Give **IV dextrose (D50)** or **IM glucagon** — do not try to give oral glucose to an unresponsive patient (aspiration risk).
- Monitor blood glucose closely after treatment — sulfonylureas are long-acting, so hypoglycemia can come back.
- Notify the provider.

**Why it matters:** Knowing when to use IV dextrose or glucagon instead of oral sugar is a critical safety distinction on the exam.

### Card 47 (Memory Helper)

**Front:** How can you quickly remember the main danger of each oral diabetes drug class?

**Back:**
- **Metformin** → think **"kidneys and lactic acid"** → check renal function.
- **Sulfonylureas** → think **"low sugar if you skip meals"** → hypoglycemia risk.
- **SGLT2 inhibitors** → think **"sugar in the urine, yeast below"** → infections and rare ketoacidosis.

**Why it matters:** One quick phrase per drug class helps you answer safety questions fast under exam pressure.

### Card 48 (Hold Parameter)

**Front:** A patient on glipizide has a blood glucose of 68 mg/dL before breakfast. What do you do?

**Back:**
- **Hold the glipizide** and notify the provider.
- A blood glucose of 68 is below the normal range (usually below 70 is considered hypoglycemia).
- Glipizide is a sulfonylurea — it makes the pancreas release more insulin. Giving it now could drop the sugar even lower.
- Give the patient a small snack or juice first, recheck the glucose, and wait for provider guidance.

**Why it matters:** Know your hold parameters: do not give a drug that lowers blood sugar when blood sugar is already low.

### Card 49 (Lab)

**Front:** A patient on metformin has a creatinine of 2.8 mg/dL. What does this mean for the medication?

**Back:**
- The kidneys are not filtering well. Metformin depends on healthy kidneys to leave the body.
- If it builds up, the patient is at risk for **lactic acidosis** — a dangerous buildup of acid in the blood.
- The provider will likely **hold or stop metformin** until kidney function improves.
- Think of it this way: bad kidneys = metformin gets stuck = acid builds up.

**Why it matters:** Connecting creatinine to metformin safety is one of the most tested lab-drug relationships on the NCLEX.

### Card 50 (Application)

**Front:** A patient on prednisone for 3 months says "I'm feeling great, I'm going to stop taking it." What do you say?

**Back:**
- "I'm so glad you're feeling better! But please do not stop this medication on your own — it can be very dangerous."
- After months of use, the adrenal glands have stopped making their own cortisol. They need time to wake back up.
- Stopping suddenly can cause **adrenal crisis**: severe weakness, dangerously low blood pressure, nausea, even shock.
- The provider will create a **slow taper plan** — follow it exactly.

**Why it matters:** Patients who feel good often want to stop. Teaching them WHY they must taper can prevent a life-threatening emergency.

### Card 51 (Safety)

**Front:** What is euglycemic DKA and why is it tricky with SGLT2 inhibitors?

**Back:**
- **Euglycemic DKA** means the patient has diabetic ketoacidosis but their blood glucose is normal or only slightly high.
- SGLT2 inhibitors (like empagliflozin) push glucose out through the urine, so the blood sugar stays low even while the body is making dangerous ketones.
- This is tricky because nurses usually look for very high blood sugar to suspect DKA — with SGLT2 drugs, the sugar may look fine.
- Watch for: nausea, vomiting, belly pain, fruity breath, deep fast breathing — even if glucose is under 250.

**Why it matters:** This is a newer concept that exams are starting to test. Normal sugar does NOT rule out DKA if the patient is on an SGLT2 inhibitor.

### Card 52 (Compare)

**Front:** Why does metformin rarely cause hypoglycemia alone, but sulfonylureas often do?

**Back:**
- **Metformin** works by reducing the amount of glucose the liver releases. It does not force the pancreas to make more insulin. So if blood sugar is already normal, metformin does not push it lower.
- **Sulfonylureas** (like glipizide) directly stimulate the pancreas to release insulin — even if the patient has not eaten. That extra insulin can drop blood sugar too far.

Think of it this way: Metformin turns down the sugar faucet. Sulfonylureas force the insulin pump to run, even when it should rest.

**Why it matters:** This comparison explains why metformin is first-line and why sulfonylureas need meal timing. Exams test this difference often.

### Card 53 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** What do you teach about separating levothyroxine from calcium and iron?

**Back:**
- Take levothyroxine on an **empty stomach**, first thing in the morning.
- Wait at least **4 hours** before taking calcium or iron supplements.
- Calcium and iron bind to levothyroxine in the gut and stop it from being absorbed — so the drug does not work as well.
- Also separate from antacids, soy products, and high-fiber foods for the same reason.

Think of it this way: Calcium and iron are like sticky magnets — they grab the thyroid pill before your body can use it.

**Why it matters:** Supplement separation is the #1 patient teaching point for levothyroxine on the NCLEX.

### Card 54 (Lab)

**Front:** TSH comes back 0.1 mU/L (very low) in a patient on levothyroxine. What does this mean?

**Back:**
- The patient is getting **too much thyroid hormone**. The brain sees plenty of hormone, so it stops sending TSH.
- Remember: TSH and thyroid hormone go in **opposite directions**. Low TSH = too much hormone.
- The provider will likely **lower the levothyroxine dose**.
- Watch for signs of hyperthyroidism: fast heart rate, weight loss, anxiety, tremor, trouble sleeping.

**Why it matters:** Low TSH on levothyroxine = overreplacement. Exams test that you read TSH "backwards" from what you might expect.

### Card 55 (Application)

**Front:** A diabetic patient on metformin is scheduled for a CT scan with IV contrast. What might need to happen?

**Back:**
- Metformin is usually **held for 48 hours** after receiving IV contrast dye.
- The contrast dye can temporarily stress the kidneys. If kidney function drops, metformin can build up and cause **lactic acidosis**.
- The provider will check kidney function (creatinine/GFR) before restarting metformin.
- Teach the patient: this is temporary — you will go back on the medication once your kidneys are cleared.

**Why it matters:** "Hold metformin around contrast dye" is a classic NCLEX safety question. Know the timing: hold after the scan, check labs before restarting.

### Card 56 (Safety)

**Front:** A patient on prednisone has a blood glucose of 280 mg/dL. Is this expected?

**Back:**
- Yes — **steroid-induced hyperglycemia** is a very common side effect of prednisone.
- Glucocorticoids tell the liver to release more glucose and make the body's cells resist insulin.
- It does not mean the patient suddenly has permanent diabetes, but the high sugar still needs to be treated.
- The provider may order sliding-scale insulin or adjust diabetic medications while the patient is on prednisone.

**Why it matters:** Every time you see a patient on steroids, think about blood sugar. This connection shows up again and again on exams.

### Card 57 (Patient Teaching)

**Front:** What are the signs of adrenal crisis if prednisone is stopped too fast?

**Back:**
- **Severe weakness and fatigue** — the patient may not be able to stand.
- **Dangerously low blood pressure** — dizziness, fainting, even shock.
- **Nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain.**
- **Confusion or altered mental status.**
- This happens because the adrenal glands were suppressed by prednisone and cannot make enough cortisol on their own yet.

Think of it this way: Cortisol keeps you alive during stress. No cortisol = the body cannot handle even small stressors.

**Why it matters:** If a patient on long-term steroids suddenly feels very sick after stopping, think adrenal crisis immediately. This is a medical emergency.

### Card 58 (Compare)

**Front:** Empagliflozin vs. metformin — which one causes more UTIs and why?

**Back:**
- **Empagliflozin** (SGLT2 inhibitor) causes far more UTIs and yeast infections.
- It works by pushing glucose into the urine. Sugar in the urinary tract feeds bacteria and yeast → more infections.
- **Metformin** does not put extra sugar in the urine, so it does not increase infection risk.
- Teach patients on SGLT2 inhibitors: drink plenty of water, practice good hygiene, and report burning or unusual discharge right away.

**Why it matters:** This comparison helps you answer "which diabetes drug increases infection risk?" — it is always the one that puts sugar where it should not be.
