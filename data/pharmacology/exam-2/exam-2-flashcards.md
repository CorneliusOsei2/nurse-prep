# Exam 2 Flashcards

These flashcards follow the Pharmacology Exam 2 guide and focus on ANS drugs, endocrine drugs, and the safety points most likely to matter on nursing exams.

## How to use this deck

- **Front** = the question side.
- **Back** = the answer side.
- **Why it matters** = the quick memory or safety reminder.

## Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) drugs

### Card 1

**Front:** What is the difference between the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems?

**Back:**
- **Sympathetic:** fight or flight
- **Parasympathetic:** rest and digest
- Sympathetic activity usually raises heart rate and blood pressure.
- Parasympathetic activity usually increases secretions, urination, and GI activity while slowing the heart.

**Why it matters:** If you know the body effect, the drug class makes more sense.

### Card 2

**Front:** What do alpha-1, beta-1, and beta-2 receptors mainly do?

**Back:**
- **Alpha-1:** tightens blood vessels and raises blood pressure
- **Beta-1:** raises heart rate and contractility
- **Beta-2:** opens the airways

**Why it matters:** These three receptor effects explain many ANS drugs quickly.

### Card 3

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for epinephrine?

**Back:**
- Stimulates alpha-1, beta-1, and beta-2 receptors
- Used for anaphylaxis, cardiac arrest, and severe bronchospasm
- Raises blood pressure and heart rate
- Opens the airways
- Watch for tachycardia, palpitations, tremor, and hypertension

**Why it matters:** Epinephrine is a life-saving emergency drug.

### Card 4

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for midodrine?

**Back:**
- Alpha-1 agonist
- Used for orthostatic hypotension
- Raises blood pressure by tightening blood vessels
- Watch for **supine hypertension**
- Avoid taking it too close to bedtime unless specifically instructed

**Why it matters:** A drug that helps standing blood pressure can cause problems when the patient lies down.

### Card 5

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for albuterol?

**Back:**
- Beta-2 agonist bronchodilator
- Used as a rescue inhaler for bronchospasm
- Opens the airways quickly
- Watch for tremor, tachycardia, and nervousness

**Why it matters:** This is a classic rescue-versus-maintenance medication question.

### Card 6

**Front:** What is the key teaching for prazosin?

**Back:**
- It is an alpha-1 blocker.
- It lowers blood pressure.
- The major safety issue is **orthostatic hypotension**, especially with the first dose.
- The patient should change positions slowly.

**Why it matters:** Exams often test the first-dose dizziness risk.

### Card 7

**Front:** How is tamsulosin different from prazosin?

**Back:**
- Tamsulosin is more selective for alpha-1A receptors in the prostate and bladder neck.
- It is mainly used for BPH to improve urine flow.
- It can still cause dizziness, but it is more targeted than prazosin.

**Why it matters:** This is a common compare card for alpha blockers.

### Card 8

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for metoprolol and atenolol?

**Back:**
- They are mostly beta-1 blockers.
- They lower heart rate, contractility, and blood pressure.
- Watch for bradycardia, hypotension, and fatigue.
- Do not stop them abruptly.
- They may mask some hypoglycemia signs.

**Why it matters:** Beta blockers are common and easy to mix up with bronchodilators.

### Card 9

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for bethanechol?

**Back:**
- Direct-acting muscarinic agonist
- Used for nonobstructive urinary retention
- Stimulates bladder emptying and can increase GI activity
- Watch for bradycardia, diarrhea, sweating, bronchospasm, and cramping
- Do not use if an obstruction is suspected

**Why it matters:** Bethanechol has the opposite effect of anticholinergic bladder drugs.

### Card 10

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for pilocarpine?

**Back:**
- Direct-acting muscarinic agonist
- Used for glaucoma or dry mouth
- Increases secretions and can lower eye pressure
- Watch for sweating, salivation, blurred vision, and trouble seeing in dim light

**Why it matters:** This drug helps you recognize parasympathetic effects.

### Card 11

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for pyridostigmine?

**Back:**
- Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor
- Used for myasthenia gravis
- Improves neuromuscular transmission
- Often timed before meals to improve swallowing
- Watch for diarrhea, sweating, excess secretions, and bradycardia

**Why it matters:** Timing and cholinergic side effects are the big exam points.

### Card 12

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for atropine?

**Back:**
- Anticholinergic
- Used for symptomatic bradycardia and to dry secretions
- Raises heart rate
- Watch for dry mouth, blurred vision, constipation, urinary retention, and tachycardia

**Why it matters:** Atropine is a classic emergency anticholinergic drug.

### Card 13

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for oxybutynin?

**Back:**
- Anticholinergic used for overactive bladder
- Lowers urgency and frequency
- Watch for dry mouth, constipation, blurred vision, urinary retention, and heat intolerance

**Why it matters:** Overactive bladder questions often test anticholinergic side effects.

### Card 14

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for ipratropium?

**Back:**
- Inhaled anticholinergic bronchodilator
- Commonly used in COPD
- Watch for dry mouth and throat irritation
- Avoid spraying it into the eyes
- It is not the main rescue drug for sudden severe bronchospasm

**Why it matters:** Students often confuse ipratropium with albuterol.

### Card 15 (Compare)

**Front:** How do cholinergic effects compare with anticholinergic effects?

**Back:**
- **Cholinergic:** more secretions, more urination, more GI activity, slower heart rate
- **Anticholinergic:** dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation, blurred vision, faster heart rate

**Why it matters:** This one compare card explains many opposite medication effects.

### Card 16 (Application)

**Front:** Why are bethanechol and oxybutynin considered opposite bladder drugs?

**Back:**
- **Bethanechol:** helps the bladder contract and empty
- **Oxybutynin:** relaxes the bladder to reduce urgency and spasms

**Why it matters:** This is a fast way to answer several application questions.

## Endocrine Drugs

### Card 1

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for metformin?

**Back:**
- Lowers liver glucose production and improves insulin sensitivity
- Usually taken with food
- GI upset is common
- Does not usually cause hypoglycemia by itself
- Watch kidney function

**Why it matters:** Metformin is one of the most common oral diabetes drugs.

### Card 2

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for sulfonylureas?

**Back:**
- They make the pancreas release more insulin.
- They can cause **hypoglycemia**.
- Weight gain can occur.
- Skipping meals increases danger.

**Why it matters:** Sulfonylurea questions often center on low blood sugar risk.

### Card 3

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for empagliflozin?

**Back:**
- SGLT2 inhibitor
- Causes glucose loss in the urine
- Watch for genital yeast infection, UTI, dehydration, hypotension, and increased urination
- Rare ketoacidosis can occur

**Why it matters:** This drug has a very different side-effect pattern from metformin or sulfonylureas.

### Card 4

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for prednisone?

**Back:**
- Glucocorticoid
- Lowers inflammation and immune response
- Take with food if needed
- Do not stop abruptly after longer use
- Watch for hyperglycemia, infection, mood changes, fluid retention, and poor wound healing

**Why it matters:** Steroids affect many body systems and show up across many specialties.

### Card 5

**Front:** What are the main nursing points for levothyroxine?

**Back:**
- Replaces thyroid hormone
- Take on an empty stomach, usually in the morning
- Separate from calcium and iron
- Improvement takes time
- Too much can cause palpitations, tremor, weight loss, and insomnia

**Why it matters:** The biggest test points are timing and signs of overreplacement.

### Card 6 (Compare)

**Front:** How do metformin, sulfonylureas, and empagliflozin differ?

**Back:**
- **Metformin:** lowers liver glucose output; GI upset common; little hypoglycemia alone
- **Sulfonylureas:** increase insulin release; hypoglycemia risk
- **Empagliflozin:** causes glucose loss in urine; infection and dehydration risks

**Why it matters:** This is the cleanest way to compare the common oral diabetes drugs in this unit.
