# Pharmacology Exam 2 — Application Questions

These NCLEX-style practice questions cover the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) drugs and Endocrine drugs from Exam 2. They focus on realistic bedside decisions, client teaching, contraindications, and urgent adverse effects.

## How to use this file

- Try to answer each question before you look at the correct answer.
- Read every explanation, even for the choices you already knew were wrong. Understanding *why* each option is right or wrong builds stronger test-taking skills.
- Pay special attention to ABCs (airway, breathing, circulation), hemodynamic safety, hypoglycemia, infection risk, hold parameters, and patient teaching.
- Notice what the question is really asking: give or hold? teach or report? which client first?

## Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) Drugs

### Question 1 — Priority / Emergency

**Question:** A client suddenly develops lip swelling, wheezing, and a blood pressure of 78/40 mm Hg after an antibiotic infusion is started. Which medication action is the priority?

A. Administer diphenhydramine IV to reduce the allergic response  
B. Administer epinephrine IM to support the airway and blood pressure  
C. Increase the infusion rate of the current IV normal saline  
D. Administer albuterol by nebulizer for the wheezing

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: This client is in anaphylaxis — the airway is closing (wheezing, lip swelling) and the blood pressure is dangerously low. Epinephrine is always the first drug because it does three things at once: it opens the airway (beta-2), raises blood pressure (alpha-1), and slows the allergic cascade (beta-2). No other single drug can do all of that.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because diphenhydramine helps with hives and itching, but it cannot open a closing airway or raise a crashing blood pressure. It is a support drug, not the priority.
- **B** is correct because epinephrine is the only drug that addresses all three life-threatening problems — airway, breathing, and circulation — at the same time.
- **C** is not the best choice because IV fluids can help support blood pressure, but they do nothing for the airway. The airway threat must be treated first.
- **D** is not the best choice because albuterol can help with bronchospasm, but it does not raise blood pressure or stop the allergic cascade. It treats only one part of the problem.

**Test-taking tip:** When a question describes anaphylaxis (swelling + wheezing + low BP), pick epinephrine first. Think "epi for everything" in anaphylaxis.

### Question 2 — Patient Teaching

**Question:** Which statement by a client taking midodrine shows correct understanding of the medication?

A. "I should take my last dose at bedtime so my blood pressure stays up while I sleep."  
B. "I should watch for high blood pressure when I am lying down."  
C. "I should expect my heart rate to increase a lot with this medication."  
D. "I do not need to check my blood pressure at home anymore."

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Midodrine is an alpha-1 agonist that tightens blood vessels to help with orthostatic hypotension (low BP when standing). But when the client lies down, that same vessel-tightening can push the blood pressure too high. This is called supine hypertension, and it is the biggest safety concern.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because taking midodrine at bedtime is actually dangerous. The client will be lying down for hours, which raises the risk of supine hypertension. The last dose should be taken well before bedtime.
- **B** is correct because supine hypertension is a known and important risk with midodrine. Watching for this shows the client understands the medication.
- **C** is not the best choice because midodrine mainly affects blood vessels (alpha-1), not heart rate. A significant heart rate increase is not a typical effect.
- **D** is not the best choice because home blood pressure monitoring is especially important with midodrine. The nurse needs to know if the blood pressure goes too high when lying down or too low when standing.

**Test-taking tip:** With midodrine, think "no bedtime dose" and "watch lying-down BP." Those two facts answer most midodrine questions.

### Question 3 — Expected Effect

**Question:** A client with asthma uses albuterol for sudden wheezing. Ten minutes later, the wheezing has improved, the client has a mild hand tremor, and the heart rate is 108/min. What is the nurse's best interpretation?

A. The tremor and faster heart rate are common beta-2 agonist effects that still need monitoring  
B. The tremor suggests a serious neurological side effect that requires stopping the drug immediately  
C. The tachycardia means the client is having an allergic reaction to albuterol  
D. The symptoms suggest the albuterol dose was too low and should be repeated right away

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Albuterol is a beta-2 agonist. It opens the airways (that is the goal), but beta stimulation can also cause tremor and a faster heartbeat. When the wheezing is better and these side effects are mild, this is a normal, expected pattern. The nurse should keep monitoring but does not need to panic.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because mild tremor and mild tachycardia are well-known side effects of beta-2 agonists. The wheezing improved, which means the drug is working. Monitoring is the right next step.
- **B** is not the best choice because a mild hand tremor from albuterol is caused by beta stimulation of skeletal muscle, not a neurological emergency. Stopping the drug abruptly is not needed.
- **C** is not the best choice because mild tachycardia alone does not mean an allergic reaction. An allergic reaction would include things like hives, swelling, or worsening breathing — none of which are described here.
- **D** is not the best choice because the wheezing already improved, so the dose is working. Giving another dose without waiting could make the tachycardia and tremor worse.

**Test-taking tip:** When albuterol works and the client has mild tremor and a slightly faster heart rate, think "expected side effects — keep watching."

### Question 4 — Vital-Sign Hold-or-Give

**Question:** A client who started prazosin yesterday has a blood pressure of 82/50 mm Hg this morning and reports feeling dizzy when sitting up. The next dose of prazosin is due now. What is the nurse's best action?

A. Give the prazosin as scheduled and recheck the blood pressure in one hour  
B. Hold the dose and notify the provider about the low blood pressure  
C. Give half the dose to reduce the risk of further hypotension  
D. Encourage the client to stand up quickly to see if the dizziness happens again

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Prazosin is an alpha-1 blocker that lowers blood pressure. A systolic BP of 82 is already too low, and the client is symptomatic (dizzy). Giving more of a drug that lowers blood pressure would make things worse. The safe action is to hold the dose and let the provider know.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because the blood pressure is already dangerously low and the client is symptomatic. Giving the full dose could drop it further and cause a fall or fainting.
- **B** is correct because the nurse should hold a blood-pressure-lowering drug when the BP is too low and the client has symptoms. The provider needs to know so the dose or timing can be adjusted.
- **C** is not the best choice because nurses do not adjust doses on their own without a provider order. Even half a dose could push the BP lower.
- **D** is not the best choice because asking a hypotensive, dizzy client to stand up quickly is a fall risk. This would be unsafe.

**Test-taking tip:** The general rule: if the vital sign the drug affects is already too low (or too high in the opposite direction), hold the drug and call the provider. Safety first.

### Question 5 — Application

**Question:** A client with benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) has been taking tamsulosin for two weeks. Which finding best shows the medication is working?

A. The client reports easier urine flow and less hesitancy  
B. The client's blood pressure has decreased significantly  
C. The client reports less frequent headaches  
D. The client's prostate-specific antigen (PSA) level has dropped

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Tamsulosin is a selective alpha-1A blocker that relaxes smooth muscle in the prostate and bladder neck. The whole point of the drug is to make it easier for urine to flow. If the client says urination is easier, the drug is doing its job.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because the therapeutic goal of tamsulosin in BPH is improved urine flow and less hesitancy. This directly shows the drug is working.
- **B** is not the best choice because tamsulosin is selective for the prostate, so large blood pressure drops are not the expected therapeutic effect. Some BP decrease can happen, but it is a side effect, not the goal.
- **C** is not the best choice because tamsulosin is not a headache medication. Fewer headaches would not show that the BPH is improving.
- **D** is not the best choice because tamsulosin does not shrink the prostate or lower PSA. Drugs like finasteride affect PSA, not tamsulosin.

**Test-taking tip:** Match the drug to the symptom it is supposed to fix. Tamsulosin = easier urine flow. That is the therapeutic outcome you are looking for.

### Question 6 — Patient Teaching

**Question:** Which teaching point is most important for a client with diabetes who also takes metoprolol?

A. "This drug may hide some signs of low blood sugar, such as a racing heart."  
B. "You should increase your insulin dose while taking this drug."  
C. "This drug will make your blood sugar run higher than usual."  
D. "You no longer need to check your blood sugar as often."

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Beta blockers like metoprolol block the adrenergic response. One of the body's main warnings for low blood sugar is a fast heart rate (tachycardia). Metoprolol can hide that warning sign, so the client may not realize their blood sugar is dropping until it gets very low.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because this is a classic and dangerous drug-disease interaction. The client needs to know that sweating and confusion may be the only hypoglycemia clues they notice while on a beta blocker.
- **B** is not the best choice because the nurse should never tell a client to change their insulin dose without a provider order. Metoprolol does not require an automatic insulin increase.
- **C** is not the best choice because metoprolol does not typically raise blood sugar. It masks the *symptoms* of low blood sugar — that is a different problem.
- **D** is not the best choice because the opposite is true. Since warning signs are hidden, the client should check blood sugar more carefully, not less.

**Test-taking tip:** Beta blocker + diabetes = "masked hypoglycemia." Teach the client to watch for sweating and confusion, not just a fast heart rate.

### Question 7 — Contraindication

**Question:** The nurse is reviewing orders and sees bethanechol prescribed. Which client should the nurse question before giving this medication?

A. A client with nonobstructive urinary retention after surgery  
B. A client with dry mouth and mild constipation  
C. A client with a suspected mechanical bowel obstruction  
D. A client with a history of seasonal allergies

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Bethanechol is a cholinergic (muscarinic agonist) that makes smooth muscle contract — including the bladder and the gut. If there is an obstruction, making the muscle squeeze harder against a blockage can cause a perforation or rupture. That is extremely dangerous.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice for questioning because nonobstructive urinary retention is actually the main reason bethanechol is prescribed. This is the intended use.
- **B** is not the best choice for questioning because dry mouth and constipation are anticholinergic symptoms. Bethanechol would actually counteract those, so there is no contraindication here.
- **C** is correct because a mechanical obstruction means something is physically blocking the bowel. Bethanechol would make the gut squeeze harder against the block, risking perforation. The nurse must question this order.
- **D** is not the best choice for questioning because seasonal allergies are not a contraindication for bethanechol. There is no dangerous interaction here.

**Test-taking tip:** Think about it this way — if a pipe is blocked and you push harder, it can burst. That is why cholinergic drugs and obstructions do not mix.

### Question 8 — Expected Effect

**Question:** A client using pilocarpine eye drops says, "My vision seems blurrier in dim light now." What is the nurse's best response?

A. "That can happen with this medication because it makes your pupil smaller. Keep reporting any changes or safety concerns."  
B. "That is unusual. I will hold your next dose and notify the provider right away."  
C. "You should switch to using the drops only at bedtime to avoid this problem."  
D. "That probably means your eye pressure is getting worse. We should stop the drops."

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Pilocarpine is a muscarinic agonist that constricts the pupil (miosis). A smaller pupil lets in less light, so dim-light vision can feel blurry. This is an expected side effect, not a sign that something is going wrong. The nurse should reassure the client but also encourage continued reporting.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because it validates the client's experience, explains why it happens, and keeps the door open for future concerns. This is safe, honest, and supportive.
- **B** is not the best choice because blurry dim-light vision is expected with pilocarpine. Holding the dose and calling the provider would be an overreaction for a known side effect.
- **C** is not the best choice because the dosing schedule should not be changed by the nurse without an order. Bedtime-only dosing could reduce the drug's effectiveness for glaucoma.
- **D** is not the best choice because blurry dim-light vision does not mean the eye pressure is worsening. It means the pupil is smaller, which is exactly what pilocarpine is supposed to do.

**Test-taking tip:** Pilocarpine = small pupil = dim-light trouble. This is expected. Do not confuse an expected side effect with a complication.

### Question 9 — Timing / Safety

**Question:** Why is pyridostigmine often scheduled 30 to 60 minutes before meals for a client with myasthenia gravis?

A. To coat the stomach lining and prevent nausea from food  
B. To improve muscle strength for chewing and swallowing during the meal  
C. To reduce the risk of high blood pressure after eating  
D. To prevent the medication from being absorbed too quickly with food

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Myasthenia gravis causes muscle weakness because acetylcholine is broken down too fast. Pyridostigmine slows that breakdown, so the muscles work better for a while after each dose. Timing it before meals means the chewing and swallowing muscles are at their strongest when the client needs them most. This is a safety issue — weak swallowing muscles can lead to choking or aspiration.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because pyridostigmine is not an antacid or a stomach-coating drug. It does not protect the stomach lining.
- **B** is correct because the whole reason for timing the drug before meals is to have peak muscle strength during eating. Chewing and swallowing safely is the priority.
- **C** is not the best choice because pyridostigmine does not lower blood pressure. Post-meal blood pressure is not the concern here.
- **D** is not the best choice because the timing is about muscle function, not absorption speed. Taking it with food versus before food is not the issue — the issue is having working muscles when the client eats.

**Test-taking tip:** With myasthenia gravis, medication timing is all about function. Ask yourself: "When does the client need muscle strength the most?" The answer is at mealtimes.

### Question 10 — SATA / Cholinergic Excess

**Question:** A client taking pyridostigmine begins to show signs of cholinergic excess (too much acetylcholine activity). Which findings should the nurse report promptly? **Select all that apply.**

A. Excessive salivation and drooling  
B. Dry, flushed skin  
C. Wheezing and increased bronchial secretions  
D. Watery diarrhea  
E. Profuse sweating

**Correct answer:** A, C, D, E

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Cholinergic excess means the body has too much acetylcholine activity. Everything gets "wet" and "overactive." Think of the memory trick SLUDGE — Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Diarrhea, GI cramping, Emesis. Add sweating and bronchial secretions to that picture. These findings together are a medical urgency.

Let's look at each option:
- **A (excessive salivation)** is correct — too much acetylcholine overstimulates the salivary glands. Drooling is a classic cholinergic sign.
- **B (dry, flushed skin)** is not correct — dry and flushed is the opposite. That is an *anti*cholinergic pattern (too little acetylcholine). Cholinergic excess makes things wet, not dry.
- **C (wheezing and bronchial secretions)** is correct — acetylcholine constricts the airways and increases secretions. This combination can threaten breathing.
- **D (watery diarrhea)** is correct — too much acetylcholine speeds up the gut and increases fluid in the bowel.
- **E (profuse sweating)** is correct — cholinergic stimulation activates sweat glands. Profuse sweating is one of the most noticeable early signs.

**Test-taking tip:** Here is a helpful way to remember: cholinergic excess = everything is WET. If the client is wet (sweating, drooling, diarrhea, secretions), think cholinergic excess. If the client is DRY (dry mouth, dry skin, constipation), think anticholinergic.

### Question 11 — Vital-Sign Hold-or-Give

**Question:** A client's heart rate is 48 beats/min and the scheduled dose of metoprolol is due. The client says, "I feel a little lightheaded." What should the nurse do?

A. Give the metoprolol and recheck the heart rate in 30 minutes  
B. Hold the metoprolol and notify the provider about the heart rate and symptoms  
C. Give the metoprolol with a glass of orange juice to raise the heart rate  
D. Hold the metoprolol but wait until the next shift to inform the provider

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Metoprolol is a beta blocker that slows the heart rate. The standard hold parameter is usually a heart rate below 60 (or whatever the provider sets). At 48 with symptoms (lightheadedness), the heart rate is already too low. Giving more metoprolol could drop it further and cause serious problems like syncope or hemodynamic instability.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because giving a heart-rate-lowering drug to a client whose heart rate is already dangerously slow would make the problem worse. This is unsafe.
- **B** is correct because the nurse should hold the drug when the vital sign it affects is out of a safe range, especially when the client is symptomatic. The provider needs to know right away so the dose or schedule can be adjusted.
- **C** is not the best choice because orange juice does not raise the heart rate. This mixes up hypoglycemia treatment with bradycardia management.
- **D** is not the best choice because waiting until the next shift to report a symptomatic low heart rate is a dangerous delay. The provider needs this information now.

**Test-taking tip:** The hold-or-give rule is simple: if the vital sign the drug affects is already too far in the direction the drug pushes it, hold the drug and call the provider. HR too low + beta blocker = hold.

### Question 12 — Application / Emergency

**Question:** A client with symptomatic bradycardia is pale, dizzy, and has a heart rate of 38/min. Which medication should the nurse anticipate first?

A. Atropine  
B. Metoprolol  
C. Prazosin  
D. Propranolol

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: This client has symptomatic bradycardia — the heart is beating too slowly and the body is not getting enough blood flow (pale, dizzy). Atropine blocks the vagus nerve (parasympathetic), which takes the brake off the heart and lets it speed up. It is the first-line drug for symptomatic bradycardia.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because atropine is the standard first drug for symptomatic bradycardia. It works quickly by blocking parasympathetic slowing of the heart.
- **B** is not the best choice because metoprolol is a beta blocker — it would slow the heart even more. This would be dangerous and is the opposite of what is needed.
- **C** is not the best choice because prazosin is an alpha-1 blocker used for blood pressure or BPH. It does not increase heart rate and could actually drop the blood pressure further.
- **D** is not the best choice because propranolol is also a beta blocker, like metoprolol. It would make the bradycardia worse, not better.

**Test-taking tip:** Symptomatic bradycardia = atropine first. If you see a slow heart rate with symptoms (dizzy, pale, confused), think "block the vagus" with atropine.

### Question 13 — Patient Teaching

**Question:** Which statement by a client taking oxybutynin shows correct understanding?

A. "I might notice dry mouth and constipation while taking this drug."  
B. "I should expect heavier sweating, especially in warm weather."  
C. "This drug will make my bladder squeeze harder to empty."  
D. "I can stop this drug any time I feel better without calling my doctor."

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Oxybutynin is an anticholinergic drug that relaxes the overactive bladder. Anticholinergic drugs cause the "dry" pattern — dry mouth, constipation, dry eyes, urinary retention, and reduced sweating. Recognizing this pattern shows the client understands what to expect.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because dry mouth and constipation are classic anticholinergic side effects. A client who understands this can manage these symptoms and knows when to report them.
- **B** is not the best choice because anticholinergics actually decrease sweating. Less sweating in warm weather is a concern because it increases the risk of overheating (hyperthermia), not more sweating.
- **C** is not the best choice because oxybutynin does the opposite — it relaxes the bladder muscle. Making the bladder squeeze harder would be a cholinergic action, not anticholinergic.
- **D** is not the best choice because a client should always talk to the provider before stopping a prescribed medication. Stopping on their own could lead to return of symptoms.

**Test-taking tip:** Anticholinergic = "dry as a bone, blind as a bat, hot as a hare, red as a beet, mad as a hatter." If the side effects are all about drying things out, think anticholinergic.

### Question 14 — Patient Teaching / Drug Clarification

**Question:** A client with COPD says, "I always reach for my ipratropium inhaler first when I suddenly cannot breathe." Which response by the nurse is best?

A. "That is the right approach. Ipratropium works fastest for sudden severe shortness of breath."  
B. "Ipratropium helps, but for sudden severe bronchospasm, a fast-acting beta-2 agonist like albuterol is usually the first rescue drug."  
C. "You should switch to a metered-dose inhaler form of ipratropium for faster results."  
D. "You should use your ipratropium and your albuterol at the same time, always in equal doses."

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Ipratropium is a helpful anticholinergic bronchodilator, but it works more slowly than albuterol. For sudden, severe shortness of breath, a fast-acting beta-2 agonist (like albuterol) should be the first rescue drug. The client needs to understand this so they do not lose precious time during a breathing emergency.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because ipratropium is not the fastest-acting rescue inhaler. Telling the client this is correct could delay proper rescue treatment in an emergency.
- **B** is correct because it gently corrects the misunderstanding, explains why albuterol is the better first rescue choice, and keeps the client safe.
- **C** is not the best choice because changing the delivery device does not change how fast ipratropium works. The drug itself has a slower onset compared to albuterol, regardless of the inhaler type.
- **D** is not the best choice because "always in equal doses" is not an accurate instruction. These drugs may be used together in some situations, but the dosing is determined by the provider, not matched one-to-one.

**Test-taking tip:** Sudden severe breathing trouble = reach for albuterol (beta-2 agonist) first. Ipratropium is a helper, not the main rescue drug.

### Question 15 — Drug Interaction

**Question:** A client already taking metoprolol is started on verapamil. Which assessment finding should the nurse monitor most closely?

A. Blood glucose level  
B. Heart rate and rhythm  
C. Urine output  
D. Respiratory rate

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Both metoprolol (a beta blocker) and verapamil (a calcium channel blocker) slow the heart rate and reduce the force of contraction. When you combine two drugs that both put the brakes on the heart, the risk of severe bradycardia, heart block, or even cardiac arrest goes up significantly. The nurse needs to watch the heart very closely.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because while metoprolol can mask hypoglycemia symptoms, blood glucose is not the primary concern when adding verapamil. The biggest risk is cardiac.
- **B** is correct because both drugs slow the heart through different mechanisms (beta blockade + calcium channel blockade). Together, they can dangerously slow the heart rate or cause conduction problems.
- **C** is not the best choice because urine output is important to monitor in many situations, but it is not the most direct concern with this specific drug combination.
- **D** is not the best choice because respiratory rate is not the primary system affected by this combination. The heart is the main target.

**Test-taking tip:** When two drugs both slow the heart (beta blocker + rate-slowing calcium channel blocker), always think "watch the heart rate." Double-braking = danger.

### Question 16 — Anticholinergic Toxicity in the Elderly

**Question:** An 82-year-old client who recently started diphenhydramine for allergies is now confused, has a dry mouth, cannot urinate, and has a temperature of 38.6°C (101.5°F). Which condition should the nurse suspect?

A. Cholinergic crisis from too much acetylcholine  
B. Anticholinergic toxicity  
C. Serotonin syndrome  
D. Neuroleptic malignant syndrome

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Diphenhydramine has strong anticholinergic effects, and older adults are much more sensitive to them. The classic anticholinergic toxicity picture is: confusion ("mad as a hatter"), dry mouth ("dry as a bone"), urinary retention, and fever ("hot as a hare"). This client has all of those signs.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because a cholinergic crisis would look "wet" — sweating, drooling, diarrhea, and small pupils. This client is "dry" — dry mouth, urinary retention, and fever. It is the opposite pattern.
- **B** is correct because every finding matches the classic anticholinergic toxicity pattern: confusion, dry mouth, urinary retention, and fever. Diphenhydramine is a well-known anticholinergic, and older adults are at high risk.
- **C** is not the best choice because serotonin syndrome typically presents with agitation, tremor, clonus (involuntary muscle jerks), diarrhea, and high temperature. The dry mouth and urinary retention point more toward anticholinergic toxicity.
- **D** is not the best choice because neuroleptic malignant syndrome is associated with antipsychotic drugs and presents with severe muscle rigidity, very high fever, and altered consciousness. Diphenhydramine is not an antipsychotic.

**Test-taking tip:** Remember the anticholinergic phrase: "Dry as a bone, blind as a bat, hot as a hare, red as a beet, mad as a hatter." When you see these signs in an older adult on an anticholinergic drug, suspect toxicity.

### Question 17 — Multi-Patient Priority

**Question:** The nurse has four clients. Which client needs assessment first?

A. A client on albuterol who reports mild hand tremor after a nebulizer treatment  
B. A client on prazosin whose blood pressure is 80/48 mm Hg and who reports feeling very dizzy  
C. A client on tamsulosin who says urination has become easier this week  
D. A client on oxybutynin who reports mild dry mouth

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: When you have multiple clients, the one with the most urgent safety threat goes first. The client on prazosin has dangerously low blood pressure and is symptomatic — this could lead to a fall, syncope, or shock if not addressed right away. The other clients have expected findings or therapeutic improvements.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the most urgent because mild hand tremor is an expected side effect of albuterol. It should be monitored but is not life-threatening.
- **B** is correct because a systolic BP of 80 with dizziness on a blood-pressure-lowering drug is a safety emergency. This client is at risk for falls, syncope, or cardiovascular collapse.
- **C** is not the most urgent because easier urination is the therapeutic goal of tamsulosin. This is good news, not a problem.
- **D** is not the most urgent because mild dry mouth is a common anticholinergic side effect of oxybutynin. It is worth monitoring but not urgent.

**Test-taking tip:** In priority questions, look for the client who is unstable or at immediate risk of harm. Expected side effects and therapeutic responses are lower priority than abnormal vital signs with symptoms.

### Question 18 — SATA / Anticholinergic Side Effects

**Question:** The nurse is reviewing medications for an older adult client. Which side effects are associated with anticholinergic drugs? **Select all that apply.**

A. Urinary retention  
B. Watery diarrhea  
C. Dry mouth  
D. Blurred vision  
E. Constipation  
F. Excessive sweating

**Correct answer:** A, C, D, E

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Anticholinergic drugs block acetylcholine, which means they dry things out and slow things down. The body makes less saliva (dry mouth), the bowels slow down (constipation), the bladder relaxes too much (urinary retention), and the pupil dilates (blurred vision, especially near vision).

Let's look at each option:
- **A (urinary retention)** is correct — anticholinergics relax the bladder, which can make it hard to empty.
- **B (watery diarrhea)** is not correct — diarrhea is a cholinergic (too much acetylcholine) sign. Anticholinergics cause the opposite: constipation.
- **C (dry mouth)** is correct — reduced saliva production is one of the most common anticholinergic effects.
- **D (blurred vision)** is correct — anticholinergics dilate the pupil and paralyze the muscle that focuses the lens, causing blurry near vision.
- **E (constipation)** is correct — slowed gut motility from reduced parasympathetic activity leads to constipation.
- **F (excessive sweating)** is not correct — anticholinergics decrease sweating. Excessive sweating is a cholinergic sign.

**Test-taking tip:** Remember: anticholinergic = everything is DRY and SLOW. Cholinergic = everything is WET and FAST. If a side effect involves drying, slowing, or retaining, it is anticholinergic.

## Endocrine Drugs

### Question 19 — Patient Teaching

**Question:** Which statement by a client taking metformin shows correct understanding?

A. "I should take it with meals to help with stomach upset."  
B. "This medicine is likely to cause severe low blood sugar even when I take it alone."  
C. "I should skip my doses if I feel fine for a few days."  
D. "I should avoid drinking any water while taking this pill."

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Metformin commonly causes GI side effects like nausea, bloating, and diarrhea, especially when first started. Taking it with food helps reduce these symptoms. An important safety point is that metformin by itself usually does not cause significant hypoglycemia — this is what separates it from drugs like sulfonylureas that directly stimulate insulin release.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because taking metformin with food is standard teaching to reduce GI upset. This shows the client understands proper administration.
- **B** is not the best choice because metformin alone rarely causes severe hypoglycemia. It works by reducing liver glucose production and improving insulin sensitivity, not by forcing more insulin release. Hypoglycemia risk increases mainly when combined with other diabetes drugs.
- **C** is not the best choice because diabetes medication should be taken consistently as prescribed, even when the client feels fine. Blood sugar can be high without symptoms.
- **D** is not the best choice because adequate hydration is important, especially with metformin. There is no reason to avoid water. In fact, dehydration can increase the risk of lactic acidosis.

**Test-taking tip:** Metformin + food = less stomach trouble. Metformin alone = low risk of hypoglycemia. These two facts cover most metformin questions.

### Question 20 — Safety / Hold-or-Give

**Question:** A client who takes glipizide (a sulfonylurea) skips breakfast but the morning dose is due. Which concern is the nurse's highest priority?

A. The client may develop hyperkalemia without food  
B. The client is at high risk for hypoglycemia if the drug is taken without eating  
C. The client may have an allergic reaction to glipizide on an empty stomach  
D. The client's blood pressure may drop too low without breakfast

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Sulfonylureas like glipizide work by telling the pancreas to release more insulin. Insulin lowers blood sugar. If the client takes the drug but does not eat, there is no incoming food glucose to balance the extra insulin, and blood sugar can drop dangerously low. This is a classic hypoglycemia setup.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because glipizide does not affect potassium levels. Hyperkalemia is not associated with sulfonylureas.
- **B** is correct because sulfonylureas cause insulin release regardless of whether the client has eaten. No food + extra insulin = hypoglycemia risk. The nurse should hold the dose and make sure the client eats first.
- **C** is not the best choice because taking glipizide on an empty stomach does not increase the risk of an allergic reaction. Allergic reactions are not related to food timing.
- **D** is not the best choice because glipizide is a blood sugar medication, not a blood pressure medication. Skipping breakfast does not create a specific blood pressure risk with this drug.

**Test-taking tip:** Sulfonylurea + no food = hypoglycemia danger. Always check that the client is eating before giving a drug that forces insulin release.

### Question 21 — Report to Provider

**Question:** Which finding in a client taking empagliflozin (an SGLT2 inhibitor) should the nurse report to the provider promptly?

A. The client urinates more frequently than before starting the drug  
B. The client has lost two pounds over the past month  
C. The client reports nausea, abdominal pain, and rapid breathing  
D. The client's fasting blood glucose is 132 mg/dL

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: SGLT2 inhibitors like empagliflozin work by making the kidneys excrete more glucose into the urine. A rare but serious complication is euglycemic ketoacidosis — the body starts burning too much fat for fuel and produces ketones, even when the blood sugar is not extremely high. The warning signs are nausea, abdominal pain, and Kussmaul breathing (fast, deep breathing).

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the most urgent because increased urination is the expected mechanism of SGLT2 inhibitors. The drug works by flushing glucose through the urine, so more trips to the bathroom are normal.
- **B** is not the most urgent because mild weight loss is actually a known benefit of SGLT2 inhibitors due to calorie loss in the urine. Two pounds in a month is not alarming.
- **C** is correct because nausea + abdominal pain + fast breathing is the classic warning cluster for ketoacidosis. This is a medical emergency and the provider must be notified immediately.
- **D** is not the most urgent because a fasting glucose of 132 is mildly elevated but not an emergency. It may indicate that the treatment plan needs adjusting, but it is not an urgent report.

**Test-taking tip:** With SGLT2 inhibitors, normal urination changes are expected. But nausea + belly pain + fast breathing = think ketoacidosis, even if the blood sugar looks "okay." Report it right away.

### Question 22 — SATA / Prednisone Teaching

**Question:** The nurse is teaching a client who is starting prednisone for a chronic inflammatory condition. Which instructions should the nurse include? **Select all that apply.**

A. Take the medication with food to reduce stomach irritation  
B. Stop the medication immediately once symptoms improve  
C. Monitor blood glucose more closely, especially if you have diabetes  
D. Report any fever, sore throat, or signs of infection to the provider  
E. If taking the drug once a day, morning dosing is usually preferred  
F. Do not stop this medication suddenly without talking to your provider

**Correct answer:** A, C, D, E, F

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Prednisone is a corticosteroid with many important teaching points. It can irritate the stomach, raise blood sugar, suppress the immune system, and shut down the body's own cortisol production over time. The client needs to understand all of these effects to stay safe.

Let's look at each option:
- **A (take with food)** is correct — prednisone can cause GI irritation and even ulcers. Food helps protect the stomach.
- **B (stop immediately when better)** is not correct — this is dangerous. After using prednisone for more than a short time, the adrenal glands slow down their own cortisol production. Stopping suddenly can cause adrenal crisis (weakness, low blood pressure, shock). The dose must be tapered slowly.
- **C (monitor blood glucose)** is correct — corticosteroids raise blood sugar by increasing glucose production and reducing insulin effectiveness. Clients with diabetes need extra monitoring.
- **D (report infection signs)** is correct — prednisone suppresses the immune system, so the client may not fight infections as well. Fever and sore throat could be early signs of a serious infection.
- **E (morning dosing)** is correct — the body naturally produces the most cortisol in the morning. Taking prednisone in the morning mimics this natural rhythm and may cause fewer sleep problems.
- **F (do not stop suddenly)** is correct — this is one of the most critical safety points. Abrupt discontinuation after prolonged use can cause life-threatening adrenal insufficiency.

**Test-taking tip:** Prednisone has five big teaching points: take with food, watch blood sugar, watch for infection, take in the morning, and never stop suddenly. If you remember those five, you can answer most prednisone questions.

### Question 23 — Patient Teaching / Drug Interaction

**Question:** Which client statement about levothyroxine requires correction?

A. "I take it first thing in the morning on an empty stomach."  
B. "It may take a few weeks before I feel the full benefit."  
C. "I take it at the same time as my calcium and iron supplements every morning."  
D. "I will report palpitations or feeling unusually anxious to my doctor."

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Levothyroxine absorption is significantly reduced by calcium, iron, and antacids. These substances bind to levothyroxine in the gut and prevent it from being absorbed properly. The standard recommendation is to separate levothyroxine from these supplements by at least four hours.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct and does not need correction — taking levothyroxine on an empty stomach (usually 30 to 60 minutes before breakfast) gives the best absorption.
- **B** is correct and does not need correction — levothyroxine has a long half-life and it takes weeks to reach a steady state. The client should not expect instant results.
- **C** requires correction because calcium and iron both interfere with levothyroxine absorption. Taking them together means the client may not be getting the full dose of thyroid hormone, even if the prescription is correct.
- **D** is correct and does not need correction — palpitations and anxiety can be signs of too much thyroid hormone (hyperthyroid symptoms) and should be reported so the dose can be adjusted.

**Test-taking tip:** Levothyroxine has a simple rule: take it alone, on an empty stomach, away from calcium and iron. If the question shows these being taken together, that is the problem.

### Question 24 — Lab-Based Hold-or-Give

**Question:** A client takes levothyroxine daily. The morning lab results show a TSH of 0.2 mIU/L (normal: 0.5–4.5 mIU/L). The next dose is due. What should the nurse do?

A. Give the dose as scheduled because the TSH is within a safe range  
B. Give a double dose to bring the TSH back to normal faster  
C. Hold the dose and notify the provider because the low TSH suggests too much thyroid hormone  
D. Hold the dose and encourage the client to increase dietary iodine intake instead

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: TSH works on a feedback loop. When there is too much thyroid hormone in the body, the brain produces less TSH. A TSH of 0.2 is below normal, which tells us the client likely has too much thyroid hormone — either from the levothyroxine dose being too high or from overreplacement. Giving more of the drug would make this worse.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because 0.2 is below the normal range (0.5–4.5). This is not safe — it suggests the client is getting too much thyroid hormone.
- **B** is not the best choice because the TSH is already too low, meaning there is already too much thyroid hormone. Doubling the dose would push the client further into a hyperthyroid state, risking tachycardia, atrial fibrillation, or other serious problems.
- **C** is correct because a low TSH with levothyroxine use suggests overreplacement. The nurse should hold the dose and let the provider decide whether to reduce it. This protects the client from hyperthyroid complications.
- **D** is not the best choice because iodine intake is not the issue here. The problem is that the prescribed levothyroxine dose appears to be too high. Dietary changes do not fix a dosing problem.

**Test-taking tip:** Think about it this way — TSH and thyroid hormone move in opposite directions. Low TSH = too much thyroid hormone = hold the levothyroxine. High TSH = not enough thyroid hormone = the client may need more.

### Question 25 — Lab-Based Priority

**Question:** A client with type 2 diabetes takes both metformin and glipizide. The fingerstick blood glucose is 58 mg/dL and the client is awake but shaky and sweaty. What is the nurse's priority action?

A. Hold both medications and give the client 15 grams of a fast-acting carbohydrate  
B. Give both medications as scheduled and recheck the blood sugar in one hour  
C. Hold the metformin only and give the glipizide with a full meal  
D. Call a rapid response because the blood sugar is critically low

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: A blood glucose of 58 is below normal (hypoglycemia is generally below 70 mg/dL). The client is symptomatic — shaky and sweaty. The priority is to raise the blood sugar quickly with a fast-acting carbohydrate (such as 4 oz of juice or glucose tablets) and hold the medications that could drop it further. Follow the "Rule of 15": give 15 g of fast carbs, wait 15 minutes, and recheck.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because treating the hypoglycemia comes first. A fast-acting carbohydrate (juice, glucose tablets) raises the blood sugar quickly. Both medications should be held until the provider is informed, since glipizide is the likely cause and metformin could contribute if combined.
- **B** is not the best choice because giving blood-sugar-lowering drugs when the blood sugar is already dangerously low would make the situation worse. The client needs treatment now, not more medication.
- **C** is not the best choice because the blood sugar is 58 — the priority is to raise it immediately. Holding only one drug and giving the other does not address the urgent hypoglycemia.
- **D** is not the best choice because while 58 mg/dL is low and needs treatment, the client is awake and able to swallow. The nurse can treat with oral glucose first. A rapid response would be appropriate if the client were unable to swallow, unresponsive, or not improving after treatment.

**Test-taking tip:** Low blood sugar + awake client = give fast-acting carbohydrate first (Rule of 15). Low blood sugar + unconscious client = do not give anything by mouth (use IV dextrose or glucagon).

### Question 26 — Prednisone Abrupt Discontinuation

**Question:** A client has been taking prednisone 20 mg daily for six weeks. The client tells the nurse, "I feel so much better, so I stopped taking my prednisone three days ago." What is the nurse's most important concern?

A. The client may develop rebound hyperglycemia from stopping the drug  
B. The client is at risk for adrenal insufficiency because the body's cortisol production has been suppressed  
C. The client will likely develop an immediate allergic reaction to food  
D. The client's white blood cell count will rise dangerously within hours

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: When a client takes prednisone for several weeks, the body gets used to having cortisol from the medication and stops making its own. If the drug is stopped suddenly, the body cannot produce enough cortisol on its own right away. This is called adrenal insufficiency, and it can cause weakness, dangerously low blood pressure, nausea, and even life-threatening adrenal crisis. This is why prednisone must always be tapered slowly, never stopped all at once.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because "rebound hyperglycemia" from stopping prednisone is not a standard concern. Prednisone raises blood sugar while you take it, so stopping it would more likely let blood sugar come back down, not go up.
- **B** is correct because after six weeks of daily prednisone, the adrenal glands are suppressed. They need time to wake back up. Stopping abruptly can cause a cortisol crisis — low blood pressure, severe weakness, and potentially shock.
- **C** is not the best choice because stopping prednisone does not cause food allergies. This is not a recognized effect of corticosteroid withdrawal.
- **D** is not the best choice because while prednisone does affect white blood cells (it raises them while you take it), a dangerous rise within hours of stopping is not the main concern. Adrenal insufficiency is the life-threatening priority.

**Test-taking tip:** Here is a helpful way to remember: prednisone puts the adrenal glands to sleep. If you stop the drug suddenly, the glands are still sleeping and cannot make cortisol. Always taper. This is one of the most commonly tested safety points for corticosteroids.
