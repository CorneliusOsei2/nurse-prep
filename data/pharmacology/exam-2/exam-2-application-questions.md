# Pharmacology Exam 2 Application Questions

These NCLEX-style practice questions follow the Pharmacology Exam 2 outline and focus on realistic bedside medication decisions, client teaching, contraindications, and urgent adverse effects.

## How to use this file

- Try to answer the question before checking the correct answer.
- Use the explanation to connect the drug action to the bedside finding.
- Focus on ABCs, hemodynamic safety, hypoglycemia, infection risk, and patient teaching.
- Notice when the stem is really asking what to give first, what to hold, what to teach, or what to report quickly.

## Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) drugs

### Question 1 (Priority)

**Question:** A client suddenly develops lip swelling, wheezing, and blood pressure 78/40 mm Hg after an antibiotic is started. Which medication action is the priority?

A. Give an alpha-1 blocker to lower vascular resistance  
B. Give epinephrine to support airway opening and blood pressure  
C. Give tamsulosin to relax smooth muscle  
D. Give oxybutynin to reduce secretions

**Correct answer:** B. Give epinephrine to support airway opening and blood pressure

**Explanation:** This client has anaphylaxis, so both airway and circulation are threatened. Epinephrine is the priority because it opens the airways through beta-2 effects and supports blood pressure through alpha-1 effects. On NCLEX, this is the kind of emergency where one drug treats several life-threatening problems at once.

### Question 2 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** Which statement by a client taking midodrine shows correct understanding of the medication?

A. "I should take it right before bed so my pressure stays high overnight."  
B. "I should watch for high blood pressure when I am lying down."  
C. "I should expect it to work by slowing my bowels."  
D. "I no longer need to check my blood pressure."

**Correct answer:** B. "I should watch for high blood pressure when I am lying down."

**Explanation:** Midodrine helps orthostatic hypotension by tightening blood vessels, but that same effect can cause supine hypertension. That makes lying-down blood pressure an important safety point. Bedtime dosing is a common trap because it can worsen the lying-down blood pressure problem.

### Question 3 (Expected Effect)

**Question:** A client with asthma uses albuterol for sudden wheezing. Ten minutes later, the wheezing is improved, the client has a mild hand tremor, and the heart rate is 108/min. What is the nurse's best interpretation?

A. These are common beta-2 agonist effects that should still be monitored  
B. The medication is definitely causing liver failure  
C. The drug is working as an anticholinergic  
D. This proves the dose was given incorrectly

**Correct answer:** A. These are common beta-2 agonist effects that should still be monitored

**Explanation:** Tremor and a faster heartbeat are common effects with albuterol, especially when the breathing is improving at the same time. The nurse should recognize this as an expected medication pattern that still needs monitoring. The other options do not fit the usual beta-2 agonist response.

### Question 4 (Safety)

**Question:** The nurse is teaching a client about the first dose of prazosin. Which instruction is best?

A. "Take the first dose and then do heavy exercise."  
B. "Change positions slowly because dizziness can happen, especially with the first dose."  
C. "Drink grapefruit juice with each dose."  
D. "Expect your urine stream to stop completely."

**Correct answer:** B. "Change positions slowly because dizziness can happen, especially with the first dose."

**Explanation:** Prazosin can cause marked orthostatic hypotension, especially with the first dose. Position-change teaching is one of the biggest safety points because dizziness or syncope can lead to falls. The other options either increase risk or describe the wrong drug effect.

### Question 5 (Application)

**Question:** A client with benign prostatic hyperplasia starts tamsulosin. Which outcome shows the medication is working?

A. The client reports easier urine flow and less hesitancy  
B. The client reports more constipation and dry mouth  
C. The client has a heart rate of 52/min  
D. The client no longer needs blood glucose checks

**Correct answer:** A. The client reports easier urine flow and less hesitancy

**Explanation:** Tamsulosin relaxes the prostate and bladder neck, so the expected benefit is easier urine flow and less hesitancy. The question is testing whether you match the drug to the symptom it is supposed to improve. The other findings do not show that the medication is working for BPH.

### Question 6 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** Which teaching point is most important for a client with diabetes who takes metoprolol?

A. "This drug may mask some signs of low blood sugar, such as a racing heart."  
B. "This drug will make your rescue inhaler stronger."  
C. "This drug is used to treat urinary retention."  
D. "This drug should always be stopped suddenly when you feel better."

**Correct answer:** A. "This drug may mask some signs of low blood sugar, such as a racing heart."

**Explanation:** Beta blockers can hide some adrenergic warning signs of hypoglycemia, especially tachycardia. That means the client needs to notice other clues such as sweating, confusion, or weakness. This is a classic NCLEX teaching point because the medication can make a dangerous problem harder to notice.

### Question 7 (Contraindication)

**Question:** Which client should the nurse question before giving bethanechol?

A. A client with nonobstructive urinary retention  
B. A client with bowel sounds present and mild abdominal fullness  
C. A client with suspected urinary obstruction and wheezing  
D. A client with dry mouth after radiation

**Correct answer:** C. A client with suspected urinary obstruction and wheezing

**Explanation:** Bethanechol stimulates muscarinic receptors, so it can worsen bronchospasm and should not be used when obstruction is suspected. This option contains two important warning clues at the same time. On the exam, the best answer often combines the most direct contraindications.

### Question 8 (Expected Effect)

**Question:** A client using pilocarpine eye drops says, "My vision is blurrier in dim light now." What is the nurse's best response?

A. "That can happen with this medication, but keep reporting any worsening or safety problems."  
B. "That means the medication is an alpha blocker."  
C. "That shows the drug is causing hyperglycemia."  
D. "Stop all fluids today."

**Correct answer:** A. "That can happen with this medication, but keep reporting any worsening or safety problems."

**Explanation:** Pilocarpine can cause blurred vision and make dim-light vision harder. The safest response is to recognize that expected effect while also reinforcing safety and continued reporting. The other answers do not fit the medication's action.

### Question 9 (Timing)

**Question:** Why is pyridostigmine often scheduled before meals for a client with myasthenia gravis?

A. To lower stomach acid before eating  
B. To improve muscle strength for chewing and swallowing  
C. To raise blood pressure before standing  
D. To prevent urinary retention

**Correct answer:** B. To improve muscle strength for chewing and swallowing

**Explanation:** The timing clue matters here. Pyridostigmine improves neuromuscular transmission, so giving it before meals can help the client chew and swallow more safely. On NCLEX, medication timing is often tied to function and safety, not just convenience.

### Question 10 (SATA)

**Question:** A client taking pyridostigmine begins to show cholinergic excess. Which findings should the nurse report promptly? Select all that apply.

A. Wheezing  
B. Dry mouth  
C. Diarrhea  
D. Sweating  
E. Increased oral secretions

**Correct answer:** A, C, D, E

**Explanation:** Cholinergic excess makes the client look too wet and too reactive. Wheezing, diarrhea, sweating, and increased secretions all fit that pattern and need prompt attention. Dry mouth points in the opposite direction and does not fit the same excess-acetylcholine picture.

### Question 11 (Application)

**Question:** A client with symptomatic bradycardia is pale, dizzy, and has a heart rate of 38/min. Which medication fits the situation best?

A. Atropine  
B. Oxybutynin  
C. Tamsulosin  
D. Metoprolol

**Correct answer:** A. Atropine

**Explanation:** The stem points to symptomatic bradycardia with poor perfusion. Atropine blocks parasympathetic effects and can raise the heart rate, so it fits the situation best. The other drugs would not correct the same urgent problem.

### Question 12 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** Which statement by a client taking oxybutynin shows correct understanding?

A. "I should expect more sweating in hot weather."  
B. "Dry mouth and constipation can happen with this drug."  
C. "This drug is meant to make my bladder contract harder."  
D. "I should use it to treat sudden anaphylaxis."

**Correct answer:** B. "Dry mouth and constipation can happen with this drug."

**Explanation:** Oxybutynin is an anticholinergic, so dry mouth and constipation are classic side effects. The other choices either describe the opposite body effect or a completely unrelated emergency drug use. NCLEX often tests whether you recognize the dry anticholinergic pattern quickly.

### Question 13 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** A client with COPD says, "When I suddenly get very tight and short of breath, I keep using my ipratropium because that is my rescue inhaler." Which response by the nurse is best?

A. "That is correct. Ipratropium is the main rescue medicine for sudden severe bronchospasm."  
B. "Ipratropium can help open the airways, but sudden severe bronchospasm is usually treated with a fast rescue bronchodilator such as albuterol."  
C. "Use grapefruit juice with it so it reaches the lungs faster."  
D. "If it gets in your eyes, it will work better."

**Correct answer:** B. "Ipratropium can help open the airways, but sudden severe bronchospasm is usually treated with a fast rescue bronchodilator such as albuterol."

**Explanation:** Ipratropium is an inhaled anticholinergic bronchodilator, but it is not the main rescue drug for sudden severe bronchospasm. The safest teaching is to correct the misunderstanding before the client delays effective rescue treatment. The other options are inaccurate and unsafe.

## Endocrine Drugs

### Question 14 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** Which statement by a client taking metformin shows correct understanding?

A. "I should take it with food if it upsets my stomach."  
B. "This medicine usually causes severe low blood sugar all by itself."  
C. "I should stop drinking water while taking it."  
D. "I should double the dose if my sugar is high one morning."

**Correct answer:** A. "I should take it with food if it upsets my stomach."

**Explanation:** GI upset is common with metformin, so taking it with food is a standard teaching point. Metformin alone usually does not cause major hypoglycemia, which helps separate it from sulfonylureas. The other answers describe unsafe self-management.

### Question 15 (Safety)

**Question:** A client taking a sulfonylurea skips breakfast but still takes the morning dose. Which problem is the nurse most concerned about?

A. Hypernatremia  
B. Hypoglycemia  
C. Bradycardia  
D. Constipation

**Correct answer:** B. Hypoglycemia

**Explanation:** Sulfonylureas increase insulin release, so taking one without eating raises the risk of low blood sugar. This is why regular meal timing matters so much with this drug class. The other choices do not match the main danger in the stem.

### Question 16 (Report to Provider)

**Question:** Which finding in a client taking empagliflozin should the nurse report promptly?

A. Mild hunger before lunch  
B. Increased urination only  
C. Nausea, abdominal pain, and fast breathing  
D. Blood pressure 128/74 mm Hg

**Correct answer:** C. Nausea, abdominal pain, and fast breathing

**Explanation:** This cluster suggests possible ketoacidosis, which is a rare but serious risk with SGLT2 inhibitors such as empagliflozin. Increased urination alone can be expected, but nausea, abdominal pain, and fast breathing are very different. On NCLEX, a dangerous symptom cluster matters more than a mild expected side effect.

### Question 17 (SATA)

**Question:** The nurse is teaching a client who is starting prednisone for an inflammatory condition. Which instructions should the nurse include? Select all that apply.

A. Take the medication with food if it causes stomach upset.  
B. Stop the medication suddenly when symptoms improve.  
C. Monitor blood glucose more closely if diabetes is present.  
D. Report fever or other signs of infection.  
E. If taken once daily, morning dosing is often preferred.

**Correct answer:** A, C, D, E

**Explanation:** Prednisone can irritate the stomach, raise blood glucose, and suppress the immune response, so all of those teaching points matter. Morning dosing is also common when the drug is given once daily. Stopping the medication suddenly is unsafe after ongoing use and is the key trap in this question.

### Question 18 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** Which client statement about levothyroxine requires correction?

A. "I should take it on an empty stomach."  
B. "It may take time before I feel the full benefit."  
C. "I can take it at the same time as my calcium supplement every morning."  
D. "If I start having palpitations, I should report it."

**Correct answer:** C. "I can take it at the same time as my calcium supplement every morning."

**Explanation:** Calcium can reduce levothyroxine absorption, so the two should be separated instead of taken together. The other statements are correct and reflect safe teaching. This is a very common NCLEX-style medication-timing question because the drug can fail simply from poor administration habits.
