## Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) Drugs

### Intro and Adrenergic Drugs

(The autonomic nervous system controls the things your body does automatically — like heart rate, breathing, blood pressure, and digestion. You do not have to think about these; your body handles them on its own.)

- The **sympathetic nervous system** is the **fight-or-flight** side.
  - *Think of it this way:* Your body is getting ready to run from danger — heart beats faster, airways open wide, blood pressure goes up.
- The **parasympathetic nervous system** is the **rest-and-digest** side.
  - *Think of it this way:* Your body is calm and relaxed — heart slows down, digestion is active, you can rest.
- **Adrenergic** drugs work on the sympathetic (fight-or-flight) system.
- **Cholinergic** drugs work on the parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) system.
- A quick receptor review:
  - **Alpha-1:** tightens (squeezes) blood vessels → raises blood pressure.
  - **Beta-1:** speeds up the heart and makes it pump stronger.
  - **Beta-2:** relaxes the muscles around the airways → makes breathing easier.

#### Sympathetic Nervous System Agonists

(These drugs **turn on** the sympathetic system — they mimic fight-or-flight.)

##### Epinephrine

*In simple words:* Epinephrine is the "emergency power boost" drug. It hits almost every sympathetic receptor at once.

- Stimulates **alpha-1, beta-1, and beta-2** receptors.
- Common uses: **anaphylaxis** (severe allergic reaction), **cardiac arrest**, and **severe bronchospasm**.
- Major effects:
  - Raises blood pressure through vasoconstriction (squeezing blood vessels)
  - Raises heart rate and makes the heart pump stronger
  - Opens the airways so the patient can breathe
- For **anaphylaxis**, the key route is **IM injection** (into the muscle), usually given in the **mid-outer thigh**. This is the standard first-line route because IM gets the drug working quickly and reliably. IV epinephrine is used in some critical-care settings but requires very careful monitoring.
- Important adverse effects:
  - Tachycardia (fast heart rate)
  - Palpitations (the patient feels their heart pounding)
  - Tremor (shaking)
  - Anxiety
  - Hypertension (high blood pressure)
- **Before giving:**
  - In anaphylaxis, do not delay treatment — give it right away.
  - If time allows, note the baseline heart rate and blood pressure.
  - Check for known cardiac conditions if this is not an emergency use.
- **After giving:**
  - Monitor heart rate, blood pressure, and rhythm closely.
  - Watch the airway — is breathing getting better?
  - Watch for signs of too much stimulation: severe tachycardia, chest pain, very high blood pressure.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - The patient develops chest pain, severe hypertension, or dangerous arrhythmias after a dose.
  - In an emergency (anaphylaxis, cardiac arrest), you generally do **not** hold — give it and monitor closely.
- **Key labs:** None routinely needed in emergency use. In ongoing use, monitor potassium (epinephrine can lower it).
- **Patient teaching:**
  - If the patient has an epinephrine auto-injector at home, teach them to inject into the **mid-outer thigh** through clothing if needed.
  - After using it, always call emergency services — the effect wears off.
  - Store it at room temperature. Check the expiration date regularly.

##### Midodrine

*In simple words:* Midodrine squeezes the blood vessels to raise blood pressure in people whose pressure drops too low when they stand up.

- Mainly stimulates **alpha-1** receptors.
- Used for **symptomatic orthostatic hypotension** (blood pressure drops when standing up, causing dizziness or fainting).
- It tightens blood vessels and raises blood pressure.
- Important adverse effects:
  - **Supine hypertension** (blood pressure gets too high when lying flat)
  - Goosebumps (piloerection)
  - Tingling or itching of the scalp
  - Urinary retention
- **Before giving:**
  - Check blood pressure — both lying down and standing (orthostatic vitals).
  - Ask the patient when they plan to go to bed.
- **After giving:**
  - Recheck blood pressure, especially when the patient is lying down.
  - Watch for headache, blurred vision, or chest discomfort (signs of supine hypertension).
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - The patient's lying-down (supine) blood pressure is already high before the dose.
  - The patient reports severe headache or visual changes.
- **Key labs:** No routine labs specific to midodrine, but kidney and liver function may be monitored over time.
- **Patient teaching:**
  - **Avoid taking midodrine within 4–5 hours of bedtime.** Taking it too late can cause dangerously high blood pressure while lying in bed at night.
  - Do not lie flat right after taking it. Stay upright as much as possible.
  - Report headaches, pounding heartbeat, or vision changes.

##### Albuterol

*In simple words:* Albuterol is the "quick-relief" inhaler. It opens the airways fast when someone is having trouble breathing.

- A **beta-2 agonist** bronchodilator.
- Used as a **rescue inhaler** for bronchospasm (tight airways).
- It relaxes the smooth muscle around the airways and improves airflow.
- Important adverse effects:
  - Tremor (shaky hands)
  - Tachycardia (fast heart rate)
  - Nervousness or jitteriness
  - Possible low potassium (hypokalemia) with repeated high-dose use
- **Before giving:**
  - Check heart rate. If it is already very fast, notify the provider but do not usually withhold a rescue treatment if the patient is in distress.
  - Assess breathing: respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, breath sounds.
- **After giving:**
  - Reassess breathing within 15–20 minutes. Are breath sounds clearer? Is the respiratory rate better?
  - Check heart rate again. A mild increase is common, but very fast or irregular rates should be reported.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - The patient has severe tachycardia or arrhythmia and is not in acute respiratory distress.
  - The patient is not improving after repeated treatments — this may be an emergency.
- **Key labs:** Potassium may be checked if the patient is using high or frequent doses.
- **Patient teaching:**
  - Use the rescue inhaler only when you feel short of breath or wheezing — it is for quick relief, not daily prevention.
  - If you need your rescue inhaler more than 2 times per week (outside of exercise), tell your provider. This means your condition may not be well controlled.
  - Rinse your mouth after use if you notice a bad taste. Shake the inhaler before each use.

#### Sympathetic Nervous System Blockers

(These drugs **block** parts of the sympathetic system — they calm down the fight-or-flight response.)

##### Prazosin

*In simple words:* Prazosin relaxes blood vessels by blocking alpha-1 receptors, which lowers blood pressure. It is also used to help reduce nightmares in some trauma patients.

- An **alpha-1 blocker**.
- Used to lower blood pressure and sometimes to help with trauma-related nightmares (off-label for PTSD).
- It relaxes blood vessels and lowers peripheral resistance (makes it easier for blood to flow).
- Major safety point:
  - **Orthostatic hypotension**, especially with the very first dose ("first-dose syncope")
  - *Think of it this way:* The blood vessels relax so much that when the patient stands up, blood rushes to the legs and the brain does not get enough — they feel dizzy or faint.
- **Before giving:**
  - Check blood pressure (lying down and standing).
  - For the first dose, make sure the patient can be somewhere safe (sitting or lying down) in case they get very dizzy.
- **After giving:**
  - Monitor blood pressure and heart rate.
  - Ask the patient if they feel dizzy, lightheaded, or faint.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - SBP (systolic blood pressure — the top number) is below 90, or per facility guidelines.
  - The patient has significant dizziness or near-fainting.
- **Key labs:** No routine labs specific to prazosin, but blood pressure logs are important.
- **Patient teaching:**
  - Stand up slowly. Sit on the edge of the bed before getting up.
  - The first dose can make you very dizzy — take it at bedtime so you are already lying down.
  - Avoid alcohol; it can make the blood pressure drop even more.
  - Report dizziness, fainting, or a fast heartbeat.

---

##### Tamsulosin

*In simple words:* Tamsulosin is a more targeted version of prazosin. It mostly relaxes the muscles around the prostate and bladder opening, so urine flows better.

- A more selective **alpha-1A blocker** that mainly affects the prostate and bladder neck.
- Used for **benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)** — a non-cancerous enlarged prostate that makes it hard to urinate.
- It improves urine flow and lowers urinary hesitancy.
- It can still cause dizziness and orthostatic symptoms, even though it is more selective than prazosin.
- **Before giving:**
  - Check blood pressure.
  - Ask about urinary symptoms to have a baseline.
- **After giving:**
  - Monitor blood pressure and ask about dizziness.
  - Ask if urination is getting easier over time (improvement is gradual).
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - The patient has significant hypotension or dizziness.
- **Key labs:** No routine labs specific to tamsulosin. A PSA (prostate-specific antigen) may be monitored as part of BPH care but is not directly related to this drug.
- **Patient teaching:**
  - Take it about 30 minutes after the same meal each day to keep levels steady.
  - Stand up slowly to avoid dizziness.
  - Tell your eye doctor you take this drug — it can affect cataract surgery (a condition called "floppy iris").
  - Report dizziness, fainting, or difficulty urinating that gets worse.

##### Metoprolol and Atenolol (Beta Blockers)

*In simple words:* These drugs slow down the heart and lower blood pressure by blocking beta-1 receptors on the heart. Think of them as putting a gentle brake on the heart.

- These are mostly **beta-1 blockers** (cardioselective).
- They lower heart rate, contractility (how hard the heart squeezes), and blood pressure.
- Common uses include hypertension, angina (chest pain), heart rate control, and some heart failure plans.
- Major nursing concerns:
  - Bradycardia (heart rate too slow)
  - Hypotension (blood pressure too low)
  - Fatigue
  - Worsening heart block in patients who already have conduction problems
- They may **mask some signs of hypoglycemia**, especially tachycardia. *Think of it this way:* If a diabetic patient's blood sugar drops, their heart would normally speed up as a warning sign — but the beta blocker stops that from happening. Sweating is still present, so teach the patient to watch for sweating, shakiness, and confusion instead.
- Do **not** stop them suddenly. Stopping abruptly can cause **rebound tachycardia** (sudden fast heart rate) or rebound hypertension. Always taper under provider guidance.
- **Before giving:**
  - **Check the apical pulse for one full minute.** This is the gold standard — do not rely only on a finger pulse.
  - Check blood pressure.
  - **Hold if HR < 60 bpm or SBP < 90 mmHg** (or per your facility's specific hold parameters) and notify the provider.
- **After giving:**
  - Recheck heart rate and blood pressure.
  - Watch for dizziness, fatigue, or lightheadedness.
  - Monitor for signs of worsening heart failure: new or increasing shortness of breath, weight gain, or swelling.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - Heart rate is below 60 bpm.
  - Systolic blood pressure is below 90 mmHg.
  - The patient reports new dizziness, fainting, or severe fatigue.
  - Signs of heart block appear on the monitor (long PR interval, dropped beats).
- **Key labs:** Blood glucose in diabetic patients (remember the masking effect). Heart rhythm on telemetry if applicable.
- **Patient teaching:**
  - Take your pulse before taking each dose. If it is below 60 (or the number your provider gives you), do not take the pill — call your provider.
  - Never stop this medication on your own. Always talk to your provider first.
  - You may feel more tired than usual, especially at first.
  - If you have diabetes, watch for sweating, shakiness, and confusion as low-blood-sugar signs (your heart rate may not speed up as a warning).
  - Avoid alcohol — it can make the blood pressure drop more.
- ⚠️ **Drug interaction:** Metoprolol + **calcium channel blockers** (like verapamil or diltiazem) can cause **severe bradycardia or heart block**. If the patient takes both, monitor heart rate and rhythm very carefully.

### ANS Drugs: Cholinergic

(Cholinergic drugs work on the parasympathetic — the "rest-and-digest" — side. They either mimic or boost the effects of acetylcholine.)

- Parasympathetic effects include: more secretions, more GI activity, more urination, smaller pupils, and a slower heart rate.
- 🔑 **Big memory helper — "Too Wet and Too Slow":**
  - If a patient gets too much cholinergic effect, they will look **"too wet"** (sweating, drooling, tearing up, diarrhea, urinating a lot) and **"too slow"** (bradycardia, low blood pressure).
  - *Think of it this way:* Everything is leaking out and the heart is dragging. That is cholinergic overdose.
  - This pattern applies to bethanechol, pilocarpine, and pyridostigmine — all the cholinergic drugs in this guide.

---

#### Parasympathetic Nervous System Direct Acting

(These drugs directly turn on cholinergic receptors — like pushing the button yourself.)

##### Bethanechol

*In simple words:* Bethanechol tells the bladder to squeeze so urine can come out. It is used when the bladder is not emptying well, but only if there is no physical blockage.

- A direct-acting muscarinic agonist.
- Used for **nonobstructive urinary retention** and sometimes reduced GI motility.
- It stimulates bladder contraction and can increase GI activity.
- Important adverse effects (remember — "too wet and too slow"):
  - Bradycardia
  - Diarrhea
  - Sweating
  - Bronchospasm (airways tighten)
  - Abdominal cramping
- **Do not give it** when a mechanical urinary or GI obstruction is suspected. *Think of it this way:* If there is a blockage, making the muscle squeeze harder can cause serious harm.
- **Before giving:**
  - Check heart rate and blood pressure.
  - Make sure there is no suspected urinary or bowel obstruction.
  - Ask about asthma or breathing problems (bronchospasm risk).
- **After giving:**
  - Monitor for urination — is the bladder emptying?
  - Watch for "too wet and too slow" signs: excessive sweating, drooling, diarrhea, cramping, or a dropping heart rate.
  - Listen to lung sounds if the patient has a history of asthma.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - Heart rate drops significantly or the patient becomes symptomatic with bradycardia.
  - The patient develops wheezing or difficulty breathing.
  - There is any suspicion of a new obstruction.
- **Key labs:** No routine labs for bethanechol specifically. Monitor intake and output (I&O) to track bladder function.
- **Patient teaching:**
  - Take it on an empty stomach to reduce nausea.
  - Report any trouble breathing, severe cramping, or feeling faint.
  - Let your provider know if you are not urinating better.

---

##### Pilocarpine

*In simple words:* Pilocarpine turns on the "wet" switch — it boosts secretions (especially saliva and tears) and can lower eye pressure. It is used for glaucoma and severe dry mouth.

- A direct-acting muscarinic agonist.
- Used for **glaucoma** (lowers eye pressure by improving fluid drainage) and sometimes **severe dry mouth** (stimulates saliva).
- It increases secretions and can lower eye pressure.
- Important adverse effects (again — "too wet and too slow"):
  - Sweating
  - Excessive salivation
  - Blurred vision
  - Trouble seeing in dim light (miosis — pupils get very small)
- **Before giving:**
  - For eye drops: check baseline eye pressure if available. Ask about eye pain or vision changes.
  - For oral form: check heart rate and blood pressure.
- **After giving:**
  - Eye drops: ask about vision changes, eye comfort.
  - Oral form: watch for too much sweating, nausea, or bradycardia.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - The patient has significant bradycardia or hypotension.
  - Vision changes are severe or concerning.
- **Key labs:** No routine labs. Eye pressure (intraocular pressure) is monitored by the eye specialist.
- **Patient teaching:**
  - If using eye drops, your vision may blur for a little while after putting them in.
  - Be careful at night or in dim lighting — your pupils will be smaller and it is harder to see.
  - If using oral pilocarpine for dry mouth, drink water regularly and report heavy sweating or nausea.

#### Parasympathetic Nervous System Indirect Acting

(These drugs do not push the button directly. Instead, they stop the body from breaking down acetylcholine, so more of it stays active. *Think of it this way:* They turn down the "cleanup crew" so the natural chemical hangs around longer.)

##### Pyridostigmine

*In simple words:* Pyridostigmine helps patients with myasthenia gravis by keeping more acetylcholine available at the nerve-muscle connection. This helps them chew, swallow, and move better.

- An acetylcholinesterase inhibitor.
- Used mainly for **myasthenia gravis** (a condition where muscles get very weak because the nerve signals are not getting through properly).
- It improves neuromuscular transmission and can improve swallowing and muscle strength.
- **Timing matters.** It is often given 30–60 minutes before meals so chewing and swallowing are easier when the patient eats.
- Important adverse effects (too much = "too wet and too slow"):
  - Diarrhea
  - Sweating
  - Increased secretions (saliva, tears, mucus)
  - Bradycardia
  - Abdominal cramping
- Too much effect looks like **cholinergic crisis** — the patient becomes "too wet and too slow." This is different from a myasthenic crisis (too weak, too dry). Telling them apart is an important exam and clinical skill.
- **Before giving:**
  - Check heart rate and blood pressure.
  - Assess muscle strength (can the patient swallow? hold up their head? grip your hand?).
  - Give it on time — late doses can cause dangerous weakness.
- **After giving:**
  - Reassess muscle strength. Is swallowing better? Can the patient breathe more easily?
  - Watch for "too wet and too slow" — if the patient gets worse (more secretions, drooling, cramping, bradycardia), this may be cholinergic excess, not underdosing.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - There are signs of cholinergic crisis (severe "too wet and too slow").
  - Heart rate drops significantly.
  - Breathing gets worse instead of better — this is urgent.
- **Key labs:** No routine labs for pyridostigmine. Pulmonary function and muscle strength are the key bedside assessments.
- **Patient teaching:**
  - Take your medication at the exact times prescribed. Do not skip or delay doses.
  - Eat your meals when the drug is working best (usually 30–60 minutes after taking it).
  - Wear a medical alert bracelet that says you have myasthenia gravis.
  - Report increased drooling, diarrhea, cramping, or difficulty breathing right away.

#### Parasympathetic Nervous System: Anticholinergic

(These drugs **block** the parasympathetic system — they stop the "rest-and-digest" effects.)

🔑 **Big memory helper for anticholinergic side effects:**

> **"Hot as a hare, blind as a bat, dry as a bone, red as a beet, mad as a hatter."**

- **Hot as a hare** → Body cannot sweat properly → overheating, heat intolerance
- **Blind as a bat** → Pupils dilate → blurred vision, sensitivity to light
- **Dry as a bone** → No secretions → dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention
- **Red as a beet** → Skin flushing from vasodilation
- **Mad as a hatter** → Confusion, agitation, delirium (especially in older adults)

*Think of it this way:* Anticholinergics dry everything out, heat the body up, blur the vision, and can scramble thinking — especially in the elderly.

⚠️ **Anticholinergics in older adults:** These drugs are on the **Beers Criteria list** (a list of medications that are often risky for people 65 and older). In elderly patients, anticholinergics can increase the risk of **confusion, falls, urinary retention, and constipation**. Always use the lowest effective dose and monitor closely.

##### Atropine

*In simple words:* Atropine speeds up a slow heart and dries up secretions. It is the "antidote" to too much cholinergic activity.

- A muscarinic blocker.
- Used for **symptomatic bradycardia** and to reduce secretions before some procedures.
- Major effects:
  - Raises heart rate
  - Dries secretions
  - Relaxes some smooth muscle
- Important adverse effects (remember the rhyme above):
  - Dry mouth
  - Blurred vision
  - Urinary retention
  - Constipation
  - Tachycardia
  - Confusion (especially in older adults)
- **Before giving:**
  - Check heart rate and rhythm. Atropine is typically given when the heart rate is too slow and the patient has symptoms (dizziness, low blood pressure, altered consciousness).
  - Check blood pressure.
- **After giving:**
  - Monitor heart rate — it should come up.
  - Watch for over-correction: is the heart rate now too fast?
  - Monitor for urinary retention, especially in older male patients.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - The heart rate is already normal or fast — atropine would make it worse.
  - The patient develops tachyarrhythmia after the dose.
- **Key labs:** No routine labs specific to atropine. Cardiac monitoring (telemetry) is key.
- **Patient teaching:**
  - You may notice a very dry mouth — sip water, use sugar-free candy or gum.
  - You may have trouble urinating — report this.
  - Your vision may blur — avoid driving or activities that need sharp vision until it clears.
  - Stay cool. Avoid hot environments because your body cannot sweat well.

---

##### Oxybutynin

*In simple words:* Oxybutynin calms down an overactive bladder. It stops the bladder from squeezing when it should not, reducing the urgent "I have to go right now!" feeling.

- An anticholinergic used for **overactive bladder** (OAB).
- It reduces urgency, frequency, and bladder spasms.
- Important adverse effects (same anticholinergic pattern):
  - Dry mouth
  - Constipation
  - Blurred vision
  - Urinary retention (even though it is for the bladder, too much effect can make it hard to empty)
  - Heat intolerance from reduced sweating
  - Confusion in older adults
- **Before giving:**
  - Ask about current urinary symptoms (baseline).
  - Check for signs of urinary retention — is the patient already having trouble emptying their bladder?
  - Use caution in elderly patients (Beers list concern).
- **After giving:**
  - Monitor urine output. Is the patient urinating enough?
  - Ask about dry mouth, constipation, and vision changes.
  - In older patients, watch for new confusion or agitation.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - The patient develops urinary retention (cannot urinate).
  - There is new confusion or altered mental status, especially in an elderly patient.
- **Key labs:** No routine labs specific to oxybutynin. Postvoid residual (PVR) may be measured by bladder scan if retention is suspected.
- **Patient teaching:**
  - Sip water often for dry mouth. Use sugar-free gum or candy.
  - Eat fiber and drink fluids to prevent constipation.
  - Stay cool — avoid overheating because this drug reduces sweating.
  - Report difficulty urinating, confusion, or vision problems.

---

##### Ipratropium

*In simple words:* Ipratropium is an inhaled anticholinergic that helps open the airways. It is often used **together with** albuterol — not by itself as a quick rescue. Think of it as a "helper" bronchodilator.

- An inhaled anticholinergic bronchodilator.
- Often used in **COPD** and sometimes in asthma care — usually as an **adjunct** (add-on) **with albuterol**, not as a standalone rescue inhaler.
- *Think of it this way:* Albuterol is the main rescue player. Ipratropium is the teammate that helps albuterol work even better. In some combination nebulizer treatments, they are given together.
- It helps open the airways by a different pathway than albuterol (blocks the cholinergic bronchoconstriction).
- Important adverse effects:
  - Dry mouth
  - Throat irritation
  - Bitter taste
- Avoid spraying it into the eyes — it can worsen eye irritation and may raise eye-pressure concerns (especially with narrow-angle glaucoma).
- **Before giving:**
  - Assess respiratory status: respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, breath sounds.
  - Check if the patient also has albuterol ordered (they are often given together).
- **After giving:**
  - Reassess breathing. Is the patient breathing more easily?
  - Ask about dry mouth or throat irritation.
  - Watch for any eye discomfort if the patient is using a nebulizer mask (mist can get into the eyes).
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - The patient has known narrow-angle glaucoma and the medication is aerosolized near the eyes — discuss proper delivery method with the provider.
  - Breathing is not improving or is getting worse.
- **Key labs:** No routine labs for ipratropium. Respiratory assessments are the key monitoring tool.
- **Patient teaching:**
  - Rinse your mouth with water after each use to reduce dry mouth and bad taste.
  - If you use a nebulizer mask, close your eyes or use a mouthpiece instead to keep the mist out of your eyes.
  - This is **not** a fast rescue inhaler on its own — always keep your quick-relief inhaler (like albuterol) available.
  - Report any eye pain, vision changes, or worsening breathing.

### ANS Drugs: Application

(This section helps you compare opposite drug effects and think safely at the bedside. It is great for quick review before an exam!)

- **Stimulating the sympathetic system** usually: raises heart rate ↑, raises blood pressure ↑, opens airways ↑.
- **Blocking the sympathetic system** usually: lowers heart rate ↓, lowers blood pressure ↓, relaxes the prostate/bladder neck.
- **Stimulating the parasympathetic system** usually: increases secretions ↑, increases urination ↑, increases bowel activity ↑, slows the heart ↓.
- **Blocking the parasympathetic system** usually: dries everything out, causes constipation, causes urinary retention, speeds up the heart ↑.

**Opposite drug pairs** (common exam material — know these well!):

| Drug | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Bethanechol** | Helps the bladder **empty** (cholinergic) |
| **Oxybutynin** | Calms an **overactive** bladder (anticholinergic) |
| **Epinephrine** | Opens airways, raises BP and HR (sympathetic agonist) |
| **Metoprolol / Atenolol** | Slows heart, lowers BP (sympathetic blocker) |
| **Pilocarpine** | Constricts pupils, increases secretions (cholinergic) |
| **Atropine** | Dilates pupils, dries secretions (anticholinergic) |

**Nursing safety summary:**

- With **alpha blockers** (prazosin, tamsulosin): Watch for **orthostatic hypotension** — blood pressure drops when standing.
- With **adrenergic agonists** (epinephrine, albuterol): Watch airway status, heart rate, and blood pressure.
- With **cholinergic drugs** (bethanechol, pilocarpine, pyridostigmine): Watch for **"too wet and too slow"** — bradycardia, wheezing, diarrhea, excess secretions.
- With **anticholinergics** (atropine, oxybutynin, ipratropium): Watch for **"hot as a hare, blind as a bat, dry as a bone, red as a beet, mad as a hatter"** — dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision, heat intolerance, confusion.
- With **beta blockers** (metoprolol, atenolol): Always check **apical pulse for one full minute** and blood pressure before giving. Hold if HR < 60 or SBP < 90.

## Endocrine Drugs

### Endocrine Drugs: Antidiabetics

(These medications help lower blood sugar. Different drugs work in different ways. Let's go through each one carefully.)

#### Oral Meds for Hyperglycemia: Metformin, Sulfonylureas

##### Metformin

*In simple words:* Metformin helps your body use insulin better and tells the liver to stop making so much sugar. It is usually the very first pill tried for type 2 diabetes.

- Decreases liver glucose production and improves insulin sensitivity.
- Often used as a **first-line** oral drug for type 2 diabetes.
- Important points:
  - Usually taken **with food** to reduce GI upset (nausea, diarrhea, stomach cramps).
  - Does **not usually cause hypoglycemia when used alone** — this is a big advantage.
  - Monitor **kidney function** (creatinine, GFR) regularly.
  - **Lactic acidosis** is very rare, but it is the classic teaching point for metformin. It is more of a concern with severe kidney problems or conditions that reduce oxygen delivery to tissues. *In simple words:* If the kidneys cannot clear metformin, or the body is not getting enough oxygen, acid can build up in the blood. This is rare but serious.
  - With **long-term use**, metformin can reduce **vitamin B12 absorption**. Over time, this can lead to B12 deficiency, which may cause numbness/tingling in the hands and feet or anemia. Periodic B12 monitoring may be done.
- Some patients are told to hold metformin around iodinated IV contrast (the dye used in some CT scans), depending on kidney function and the provider's plan.
- **Before giving:**
  - Check kidney function — look at the **GFR** (glomerular filtration rate) or **creatinine** in the chart.
  - Check blood glucose.
  - Ask about nausea or GI symptoms.
- **After giving:**
  - Monitor blood glucose.
  - Watch for GI side effects (nausea, diarrhea, bloating) — these often improve over time.
  - Be alert for signs of lactic acidosis (rare): unusual muscle pain, trouble breathing, feeling very cold, dizziness, sudden weakness — this is an emergency.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - Kidney function has worsened (GFR dropping).
  - The patient is going for a procedure with IV contrast — ask the provider about holding metformin.
  - Signs of lactic acidosis appear.
  - The patient is very dehydrated, septic, or in any condition with poor oxygen delivery.
- **Key labs:**
  - **Blood glucose** and **A1C** (to track long-term sugar control)
  - **Creatinine and GFR** (kidney function)
  - **Vitamin B12 level** periodically with long-term use
  - **Lactic acid** if lactic acidosis is suspected
- **Patient teaching:**
  - Take with food to avoid stomach upset.
  - Do not drink a lot of alcohol — it raises the risk of lactic acidosis and low blood sugar.
  - Tell your provider if you have a procedure with contrast dye coming up.
  - Report unusual muscle pain, trouble breathing, or feeling very sick.
  - Long-term: your provider may check your B12 level. Report any new numbness or tingling in your hands or feet.

---

##### Sulfonylureas

*In simple words:* Sulfonylureas (like glipizide and glyburide) tell the pancreas to push out more insulin. More insulin means lower blood sugar — but that also means these drugs **can** cause low blood sugar.

- Examples include **glipizide** and **glyburide**.
- They make the pancreas release more insulin.
- Important adverse effects:
  - **Hypoglycemia** (low blood sugar) — this is the number one concern
  - Weight gain
- ⚠️ **Glyburide vs. glipizide in older adults:** **Glyburide** carries **extra hypoglycemia risk in elderly patients** compared to glipizide. Glyburide has active metabolites that hang around longer, especially when kidney function is reduced (common in older adults). Glipizide is often the preferred sulfonylurea in this population.
- Patients need regular food intake because skipping meals raises hypoglycemia risk.
- **Before giving:**
  - Check blood glucose. If it is already low, **do not give the sulfonylurea** — treat the hypoglycemia first.
  - Check that the patient is eating. If they are NPO (nothing by mouth), notify the provider.
  - Review kidney and liver function (affects drug clearance).
- **After giving:**
  - Monitor blood glucose — watch for hypoglycemia, especially before meals and at bedtime.
  - Watch for signs of low blood sugar: shakiness, sweating, confusion, irritability, fast heartbeat, hunger.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - Blood glucose is low before the dose.
  - The patient is not eating or is NPO.
  - The patient has a new decline in kidney or liver function.
- **Key labs:**
  - **Blood glucose** (frequent monitoring, especially early on)
  - **A1C** (long-term glucose control)
  - **Kidney and liver function** periodically
- **Patient teaching:**
  - Eat regular meals. Do not skip meals — this raises the risk of low blood sugar.
  - Always carry a quick sugar source (glucose tablets, juice, regular soda) in case of hypoglycemia.
  - Learn the signs of low blood sugar: sweating, shaking, confusion, hunger, fast heartbeat.
  - If you feel low, check your blood sugar and eat or drink something with sugar right away.
  - Take the medication as directed — usually before meals.

#### Oral Meds for Hyperglycemia: Empagliflozin

*In simple words:* Empagliflozin tells the kidneys to dump extra sugar into the urine. This lowers blood sugar and has bonus heart and kidney benefits, but all that extra sugar in the urine can cause infections.

- An **SGLT2 inhibitor**.
- It helps the kidneys remove extra glucose in the urine.
- Important benefits:
  - Helps glucose control
  - Can also help some **heart failure** and **kidney** outcomes — this is a big deal in current practice
- Important adverse effects:
  - **Genital yeast infections** (the sugar in the urine feeds yeast)
  - Urinary tract infections (UTIs)
  - Dehydration
  - Hypotension (low blood pressure)
  - Increased urination (polyuria)
- ⚠️ **Euglycemic DKA — a critical teaching point:**
  - A rare but serious problem is **diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)** that can happen **even when the blood sugar looks normal or only slightly elevated.** This is called **euglycemic DKA**.
  - *Think of it this way:* Normally, DKA comes with very high blood sugar, so it is easier to spot. With SGLT2 inhibitors, the kidneys are pulling sugar out of the blood, so the blood sugar number can be misleadingly normal — but the body is still making dangerous ketones.
  - **Red flags:** nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, fatigue, trouble breathing — even if the glucose reading is only mildly high or normal. If you see this pattern, **check for ketones** and notify the provider immediately.
- **Before giving:**
  - Check blood glucose.
  - Check blood pressure (risk of hypotension and dehydration).
  - Review kidney function (the drug may be less effective with very low GFR).
  - Ask about symptoms of UTI or yeast infection.
- **After giving:**
  - Monitor blood glucose.
  - Monitor blood pressure and hydration status.
  - Ask about any new genital itching, burning, or urinary symptoms.
  - Stay alert for signs of euglycemic DKA (nausea, vomiting, belly pain, trouble breathing) — even if glucose looks okay.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - Signs of DKA appear (even with normal-ish glucose).
  - The patient is significantly dehydrated or hypotensive.
  - Kidney function has worsened significantly.
  - The patient is about to have surgery (some providers hold SGLT2 inhibitors perioperatively to reduce DKA risk).
- **Key labs:**
  - **Blood glucose** and **A1C**
  - **Kidney function** (creatinine, GFR)
  - **Ketones** (blood or urine) if DKA is suspected
  - **Electrolytes** (dehydration can cause imbalances)
- **Patient teaching:**
  - You will urinate more often — this is how the drug works.
  - Drink plenty of water to stay hydrated.
  - Keep your genital area clean and dry. Report any itching, burning, or unusual discharge — yeast infections are more common with this drug.
  - **Very important:** Even if your blood sugar looks normal, go to the ER if you have nausea, vomiting, belly pain, or trouble breathing. This could be a type of ketoacidosis that happens without high blood sugar.
  - Report signs of UTI: burning when urinating, frequency, foul-smelling urine.

### Endocrine Drugs: Glucocorticoids

#### Prednisone

(Prednisone is a glucocorticoid — a powerful drug that lowers inflammation and turns down the immune system.)

*In simple words:* Prednisone is like a fire extinguisher for inflammation. It works very well, but it has a lot of side effects, especially with long-term use. The body also gets used to it, so you cannot just stop it suddenly.

- Common uses include asthma flares, autoimmune diseases, inflammatory conditions, and severe allergic reactions.
- Important teaching:
  - Take with food if it upsets the stomach.
  - If taken once daily, **morning dosing** is often preferred (this mimics the body's natural cortisol rhythm).
  - **Do not stop it abruptly** after longer use.
  - ⚠️ **Taper requirement:** When someone has been on prednisone for more than a short course, the body stops making its own cortisol. If you suddenly stop the drug, the body has no cortisol at all — this can cause **adrenal crisis**, a life-threatening emergency. The dose must be **gradually reduced (tapered)** under provider guidance so the adrenal glands can wake back up.
  - *Think of it this way:* The body's cortisol factory shut down because the drug was doing all the work. You have to slowly turn the factory back on before removing the drug completely.
- Important adverse effects:
  - **Hyperglycemia** (raises blood sugar — big concern in diabetic patients)
  - **Infection risk** (immune system is suppressed — the patient may not show typical signs of infection like fever)
  - Mood changes (irritability, insomnia, even psychosis in rare cases)
  - GI irritation and increased ulcer risk
  - Fluid retention and weight gain
  - **Osteoporosis** with long-term use (bones become weaker)
  - Poor wound healing
  - Moon face, buffalo hump, thin skin, easy bruising (Cushingoid features with prolonged use)
- **Before giving:**
  - Check blood glucose.
  - Check blood pressure.
  - Ask about current infections (the drug can mask them or make them worse).
  - Review the patient's medication list for interactions (see below).
- **After giving:**
  - Monitor blood glucose — it can spike, especially in diabetic patients.
  - Watch for signs of infection (but remember, typical signs like fever may be blunted).
  - Monitor mood and behavior changes.
  - Assess for GI discomfort.
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - The patient has a new serious infection.
  - Blood glucose is very high and difficult to manage.
  - The patient is showing signs of adrenal crisis (if they missed doses or the drug was stopped too fast): severe weakness, hypotension, confusion, nausea, vomiting — this is an emergency.
- **Key labs:**
  - **Blood glucose** (can be elevated significantly)
  - **Electrolytes** — especially potassium (prednisone can lower it) and sodium
  - **WBC** (may be elevated from the drug itself, not just infection)
  - **Bone density** with long-term use
- **Patient teaching:**
  - Take with food to protect your stomach.
  - Take in the morning if prescribed once daily.
  - **Never stop this drug on your own.** If you need to stop, your provider will slowly lower the dose over time (taper).
  - You may feel hungrier, have trouble sleeping, or feel moody — these are common side effects. Report anything severe.
  - Avoid people who are sick — your immune system is weaker on this drug.
  - If you have diabetes, check your blood sugar more often — it will likely run higher.
  - Report stomach pain, black or tarry stools, or signs of infection.
  - With long-term use, your provider may recommend calcium and vitamin D for bone health.
- ⚠️ **Drug interactions:**
  - **Prednisone + NSAIDs** (like ibuprofen or naproxen): Both irritate the stomach lining. Together, they significantly increase the risk of **GI bleeding and ulcers**. Avoid combining when possible; if both are needed, a stomach protector (like a PPI) may be used.
  - **Prednisone + diabetes medications:** Prednisone raises blood sugar. Patients on metformin, sulfonylureas, insulin, or other diabetes drugs may need **dose adjustments** because their glucose will be harder to control while on steroids. Monitor blood glucose closely.

### Levothyroxine

(Levothyroxine replaces the thyroid hormone that the body is not making enough of. It is the treatment for **hypothyroidism** — an underactive thyroid.)

*In simple words:* When your thyroid is sluggish and not making enough hormone, you feel tired, cold, and slow. Levothyroxine gives your body the hormone it is missing so everything speeds back up to normal.

- It raises the body's metabolic activity toward normal.
- Important teaching:
  - Take it on an **empty stomach**, usually first thing in the morning.
  - Wait at least **30–60 minutes** before eating.
  - Take it consistently the same way each day.
  - **Separate it from calcium, iron, and antacids** by several hours (these can block absorption).
- It is **not** a rescue medication. Symptom improvement often takes **days to weeks**. Be patient — it is working even if you do not feel different right away.
- **TSH monitoring — an important exam concept:**
  - **TSH** (thyroid-stimulating hormone) is the main lab used to check if the dose is right.
  - **High TSH** → The body is asking for more thyroid hormone → the dose may need to **increase**.
  - **Low TSH** → The body has too much thyroid hormone → possible **overreplacement** → the dose may need to **decrease**.
  - *Think of it this way:* TSH is like a thermostat. If the room (body) is cold (not enough thyroid hormone), the thermostat turns up (TSH goes high), calling for more heat (more hormone). If the room is too hot (too much thyroid hormone), the thermostat turns down (TSH goes low).
- Too much levothyroxine can cause signs of **hyperthyroidism**:
  - Tachycardia or palpitations
  - Weight loss
  - Tremor
  - Anxiety
  - Insomnia
  - Heat intolerance
  - Diarrhea
- **Before giving:**
  - Check the most recent **TSH** level. Is it in range?
  - Check heart rate — if the patient is already tachycardic, the dose may be too high.
  - Confirm the patient has an empty stomach.
- **After giving:**
  - Monitor for signs of too much thyroid hormone (tachycardia, nervousness, weight loss, tremor).
  - Monitor for improvement in hypothyroid symptoms over weeks (more energy, less constipation, feeling warmer).
- **Hold and notify the provider if:**
  - TSH is very low (suggests overreplacement).
  - The patient has new tachycardia, chest pain, or palpitations.
  - The patient reports significant anxiety, tremor, or weight loss.
- **Key labs:**
  - **TSH** — this is the main one. Checked about 6–8 weeks after any dose change, then periodically.
  - **Free T4** may also be checked.
- **Patient teaching:**
  - Take it first thing in the morning on an empty stomach. Wait 30–60 minutes before eating or drinking anything other than water.
  - Do not take it with calcium, iron, or antacids — these block absorption. Separate by at least 4 hours.
  - This is a lifelong medication for most people. Do not stop it because you feel better — you feel better because the medication is working.
  - Report fast heartbeat, chest pain, nervousness, or trouble sleeping — these could mean the dose is too high.
  - Be patient. It takes weeks to feel the full effect of a dose change.
  - Keep all follow-up appointments so your TSH can be checked.

You made it to the end — great job! 🎉 Here are some quick reminders for exam day:

1. **For every drug, always think:** What do I check before giving it? What do I watch after? When do I hold it?
2. **Cholinergic overdose = "Too wet and too slow."** Sweating, drooling, diarrhea, bradycardia.
3. **Anticholinergic overdose = "Hot as a hare, blind as a bat, dry as a bone, red as a beet, mad as a hatter."**
4. **Beta blockers:** Apical pulse × 1 full minute. Hold if HR < 60 or SBP < 90.
5. **Epinephrine for anaphylaxis:** IM into the mid-outer thigh. Do not delay.

7. **Sulfonylureas:** CAN cause hypoglycemia. Glyburide is riskier in elderly than glipizide.
8. **Empagliflozin:** Watch for **euglycemic DKA** — ketoacidosis with normal-looking blood sugar.
9. **Prednisone:** Must taper after long-term use. Raises blood sugar. Suppresses immune system.
10. **Levothyroxine:** Empty stomach, morning. High TSH = may need more. Low TSH = may be getting too much.
