# Exam 2 Guide

This guide follows your Pharmacology Exam 2 outline and focuses on autonomic nervous system drugs, antidiabetic medications, glucocorticoids, and other high-yield endocrine medications.

## Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) drugs

### Intro and Adrenergic

(The autonomic nervous system controls automatic body functions such as heart rate, breathing, blood pressure, and digestion.)

- The **sympathetic nervous system** is the **fight-or-flight** side.
- The **parasympathetic nervous system** is the **rest-and-digest** side.
- **Adrenergic** drugs affect the sympathetic system.
- **Cholinergic** drugs affect the parasympathetic system.
- A quick receptor review:
  - **Alpha-1:** tightens blood vessels and raises blood pressure.
  - **Beta-1:** increases heart rate and contractility.
  - **Beta-2:** relaxes bronchial smooth muscle and opens the airways.

#### Sympathetic Nervous System Agonists

(These drugs stimulate the sympathetic system.)

##### Epinephrine

- Stimulates **alpha-1, beta-1, and beta-2** receptors.
- Common uses: **anaphylaxis, cardiac arrest, and severe bronchospasm**.
- Major effects:
  - Raises blood pressure through vasoconstriction
  - Raises heart rate and contractility
  - Opens the airways
- Important adverse effects:
  - Tachycardia
  - Palpitations
  - Tremor
  - Anxiety
  - Hypertension
- In an emergency, airway and breathing are the main nursing priority.

##### Midodrine

- Mainly stimulates **alpha-1** receptors.
- Used for **symptomatic orthostatic hypotension**.
- It tightens blood vessels and raises blood pressure.
- Important adverse effects:
  - **Supine hypertension**
  - Goosebumps
  - Tingling or itching of the scalp
  - Urinary retention
- Teach the patient to avoid taking it too close to bedtime unless the prescriber specifically wants that plan.

##### Albuterol

- A **beta-2 agonist** bronchodilator.
- Used as a **rescue inhaler** for bronchospasm.
- It relaxes airway smooth muscle and improves airflow.
- Important adverse effects:
  - Tremor
  - Tachycardia
  - Nervousness
  - Possible low potassium with repeated high use
- If the patient needs it more often than expected, that may mean poor asthma or COPD control.

#### Sympathetic Nervous System Blockers

(These drugs block parts of the sympathetic system.)

##### Prazosin

- An **alpha-1 blocker**.
- Used to lower blood pressure and sometimes to help with trauma-related nightmares.
- It relaxes blood vessels and lowers peripheral resistance.
- Major safety point:
  - **Orthostatic hypotension**, especially with the first dose
- Dizziness and first-dose syncope are important teaching points.

##### Tamsulosin

- A more selective **alpha-1A blocker** that mainly affects the prostate and bladder neck.
- Used for **benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)**.
- It improves urine flow and lowers urinary hesitancy.
- It can still cause dizziness and orthostatic symptoms, even though it is more selective than prazosin.

##### Metoprolol and Atenolol

- These are mostly **beta-1 blockers**.
- They lower heart rate, contractility, and blood pressure.
- Common uses include hypertension, angina, rate control, and some heart failure plans.
- Major nursing concerns:
  - Bradycardia
  - Hypotension
  - Fatigue
  - Worsening heart block in susceptible patients
- They may **mask some signs of hypoglycemia**, especially tachycardia.
- Do **not** stop them abruptly because rebound tachycardia or hypertension can happen.

### ANS drugs: Cholinergic

(Cholinergic drugs mimic or increase parasympathetic effects.)

- Parasympathetic effects include more secretions, more GI activity, more urination, smaller pupils, and slower heart rate.
- A simple way to remember cholinergic excess is that the patient can look **too wet and too slow**.

#### Parasympathetic Nervous System Direct Acting

(These drugs directly stimulate cholinergic receptors.)

##### Bethanechol

- A direct-acting muscarinic agonist.
- Used for **nonobstructive urinary retention** and sometimes reduced GI motility.
- It stimulates bladder contraction and can increase GI activity.
- Important adverse effects:
  - Bradycardia
  - Diarrhea
  - Sweating
  - Bronchospasm
  - Abdominal cramping
- Do not give it when a mechanical urinary or GI obstruction is suspected.

##### Pilocarpine

- A direct-acting muscarinic agonist.
- Used for **glaucoma** and sometimes **dry mouth**.
- It increases secretions and can lower eye pressure.
- Important adverse effects:
  - Sweating
  - Salivation
  - Blurred vision
  - Trouble seeing in dim light

#### Parasympathetic Nervous System Indirect Acting

(These drugs increase acetylcholine by blocking its breakdown.)

##### Pyridostigmine

- An acetylcholinesterase inhibitor.
- Used mainly for **myasthenia gravis**.
- It improves neuromuscular transmission and can improve swallowing and muscle strength.
- Timing matters. It is often given before meals so chewing and swallowing are easier.
- Important adverse effects:
  - Diarrhea
  - Sweating
  - Increased secretions
  - Bradycardia
  - Abdominal cramping
- Too much effect can look like cholinergic excess.

#### Parasympathetic Nervous System: Anti-cholinergic

(These drugs block parasympathetic effects.)

##### Atropine

- A muscarinic blocker.
- Used for **symptomatic bradycardia** and to reduce secretions in some settings.
- Major effects:
  - Raises heart rate
  - Dries secretions
  - Relaxes some smooth muscle
- Important adverse effects:
  - Dry mouth
  - Blurred vision
  - Urinary retention
  - Constipation
  - Tachycardia

##### Oxybutynin

- An anticholinergic used for **overactive bladder**.
- It reduces urgency, frequency, and bladder spasms.
- Important adverse effects:
  - Dry mouth
  - Constipation
  - Blurred vision
  - Urinary retention
  - Heat intolerance from reduced sweating

##### Ipratropium

- An inhaled anticholinergic bronchodilator.
- Often used in **COPD** and sometimes with other bronchodilators in asthma care.
- It helps open the airways but is not the main rescue drug for sudden severe bronchospasm.
- Important adverse effects:
  - Dry mouth
  - Throat irritation
  - Bitter taste
- Avoid spraying it into the eyes because that can worsen eye irritation and may raise eye-pressure concerns.

### ANS drugs: Application

(This section helps you compare opposite drug effects and think safely at the bedside.)

- **Stimulating the sympathetic system** usually raises heart rate, blood pressure, and airway opening.
- **Blocking the sympathetic system** usually lowers heart rate or blood pressure, or relaxes the prostate and bladder neck.
- **Stimulating the parasympathetic system** usually increases secretions, urination, bowel activity, and can slow the heart.
- **Blocking the parasympathetic system** usually causes dryness, constipation, urinary retention, and a faster heart rate.
- Opposite drug pairs are common exam material:
  - **Bethanechol** helps the bladder empty.
  - **Oxybutynin** calms an overactive bladder.
  - **Epinephrine** opens the airways and supports blood pressure in anaphylaxis.
  - **Metoprolol or atenolol** slows the heart and lowers blood pressure.
- Nursing safety points:
  - Watch for **orthostatic hypotension** with alpha blockers.
  - Watch airway status and heart rate with adrenergic drugs.
  - Watch for **bradycardia, wheezing, diarrhea, or excess secretions** with cholinergic drugs.
  - Watch for **dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision, and heat intolerance** with anticholinergics.

## Endocrine Drugs

### Endocrine Drugs: antidiabetics

(These medications lower blood glucose in different ways.)

#### Oral Meds for Hyperglycemia: metformin, sulfonylureas

##### Metformin

- Decreases liver glucose production and improves insulin sensitivity.
- Often used as a first-line oral drug for type 2 diabetes.
- Important points:
  - Usually taken **with food** to reduce GI upset
  - Does **not usually cause hypoglycemia when used alone**
  - Monitor kidney function
  - Rare **lactic acidosis** risk is more concerning with severe renal impairment or major tissue hypoxia
- Some patients are told to hold metformin around iodinated IV contrast, depending on kidney function and the prescriber's plan.

##### Sulfonylureas

- Examples include **glipizide** and **glyburide**.
- They make the pancreas release more insulin.
- Important adverse effects:
  - **Hypoglycemia**
  - Weight gain
- Patients need regular food intake because skipping meals raises hypoglycemia risk.

#### Oral Meds for Hyperglycemia: empagliflozin

- An **SGLT2 inhibitor**.
- It helps the kidneys remove extra glucose in the urine.
- Important benefits:
  - Can help glucose control
  - Can also help some heart failure and kidney outcomes
- Important adverse effects:
  - Genital yeast infections
  - Urinary tract infections
  - Dehydration
  - Hypotension
  - Increased urination
- A rare but serious problem is **ketoacidosis**, which can happen even if the blood glucose is not extremely high.

### Endocrine Drugs: glucocorticoids

#### Prednisone

(Prednisone is a glucocorticoid that lowers inflammation and suppresses the immune response.)

- Common uses include asthma flares, autoimmune disease, inflammatory problems, and allergic conditions.
- Important teaching:
  - Take with food if it upsets the stomach.
  - If taken once daily, morning dosing is often preferred.
  - Do **not** stop it abruptly after longer use unless specifically instructed.
- Important adverse effects:
  - Hyperglycemia
  - Infection risk
  - Mood changes
  - GI irritation
  - Fluid retention
  - Osteoporosis with long-term use
  - Poor wound healing

### Levothyroxine

(Levothyroxine replaces thyroid hormone in hypothyroidism.)

- It raises the body's metabolic activity toward normal.
- Important teaching:
  - Take it on an **empty stomach**, usually in the morning.
  - Take it consistently the same way each day.
  - Separate it from calcium or iron supplements by several hours.
- It is **not** a rescue medication. Symptom improvement often takes days to weeks.
- Monitor **TSH** and clinical symptoms.
- Too much levothyroxine can cause:
  - Tachycardia or palpitations
  - Weight loss
  - Tremor
  - Anxiety
  - Insomnia
  - Heat intolerance
