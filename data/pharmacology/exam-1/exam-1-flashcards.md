# Exam 1 Flashcards

These flashcards follow the Pharmacology Exam 1 guide and focus on simple wording, strong medication safety habits, and exam-style nursing judgment.

## How to use this deck

- **Front** = the question side.
- **Back** = the answer side.
- **Why it matters** = the short exam or bedside reminder.

## Pharmacologic Principles

### Card 1

**Front:** What is pharmacodynamics?

**Back:**
- Pharmacodynamics means **what the drug does to the body**.
- It includes the drug's desired effect, side effects, adverse effects, and toxicity.
- It helps explain why the patient changes after getting a medication.

**Why it matters:** Many medication questions are really asking what body response the drug should cause.

### Card 2

**Front:** What is the difference between an agonist and an antagonist?

**Back:**
- An **agonist** turns a receptor on and creates a response.
- An **antagonist** blocks a receptor and prevents a response.

**Why it matters:** This simple difference explains many drug classes.

### Card 3

**Front:** What is the difference between a therapeutic effect, side effect, adverse effect, and toxicity?

**Back:**
- **Therapeutic effect:** the wanted helpful effect.
- **Side effect:** a secondary predictable effect.
- **Adverse effect:** an unwanted harmful effect.
- **Toxicity:** a dangerous drug effect or drug level.

**Why it matters:** Nursing questions often ask you to separate an expected effect from a dangerous one.

### Card 4

**Front:** What does absorption mean in pharmacokinetics?

**Back:**
- Absorption means how a drug gets from the route of administration into the bloodstream.
- IV drugs skip absorption because they enter the blood directly.

**Why it matters:** If a drug is not absorbed well, it may not work as expected.

### Card 5

**Front:** What factors commonly change drug absorption?

**Back:**
- Route of administration
- Food in the stomach
- Vomiting or diarrhea
- Gastric pH and GI motility
- Blood flow to IM or SubQ tissue
- Enteric-coated or extended-release formulation

**Why it matters:** A correct drug order can still work poorly if absorption changes.

### Card 6

**Front:** Why do sublingual drugs act faster than swallowed oral drugs?

**Back:**
- They absorb through the mucous membranes under the tongue.
- They avoid the stomach and first-pass liver metabolism.

**Why it matters:** This is a common compare question on exams.

### Card 7

**Front:** What does distribution mean in pharmacokinetics?

**Back:**
- Distribution means how the drug moves from the bloodstream into tissues and organs.
- Blood flow, protein binding, body fat, and body water all matter.

**Why it matters:** A drug must reach the right tissue before it can work there.

### Card 8

**Front:** Why does low albumin increase the risk of toxicity for some drugs?

**Back:**
- Many drugs bind to albumin in the blood.
- Only the **free** drug is active.
- Low albumin means more free drug is available, so the effect can become too strong.

**Why it matters:** This helps explain why some older or sicker clients are more medication-sensitive.

### Card 9

**Front:** What is the difference between metabolism and excretion?

**Back:**
- **Metabolism:** the body changes the drug, usually in the liver.
- **Excretion:** the body removes the drug, usually through the kidneys.

**Why it matters:** If either step slows down, the drug can build up.

### Card 10

**Front:** What is a prodrug?

**Back:**
- A prodrug is inactive or less active until the body changes it into the active form.
- This change often happens in the liver.

**Why it matters:** Liver disease can make some prodrugs work less well.

### Card 11

**Front:** How can liver failure affect drug metabolism?

**Back:**
- Many drugs are metabolized more slowly.
- Drug levels may rise and last longer.
- Toxicity risk can increase.
- Some prodrugs may not be activated well.

**Why it matters:** The same dose can become unsafe when the liver is failing.

### Card 12

**Front:** How can grapefruit affect some oral medications?

**Back:**
- Grapefruit can inhibit intestinal CYP3A4 enzymes for some drugs.
- Less drug is broken down before reaching the blood.
- Blood levels can rise and side effects or toxicity can increase.

**Why it matters:** This is a classic drug-interaction teaching point.

### Card 13

**Front:** What usually happens when drug metabolism is enhanced or inhibited?

**Back:**
- **Enhanced metabolism:** lower drug levels, weaker effect, shorter duration.
- **Inhibited metabolism:** higher drug levels, stronger effect, longer duration.
- Prodrugs can behave differently because activation may increase or decrease.

**Why it matters:** This helps you predict whether a drug may stop working or become toxic.

### Card 14

**Front:** What should you look up first in a drug guide when you are learning a new medication?

**Back:**
- Why the patient receives it
- How it works
- Usual dose and route
- Contraindications and precautions
- Nursing implications and patient teaching

**Why it matters:** A drug guide is most useful when it helps you make safe bedside decisions.

### Card 15

**Front:** What are the key rights of medication administration?

**Back:**
- Right patient
- Right drug
- Right dose
- Right route
- Right time
- Right reason
- Right documentation
- Right response
- Right education
- Right to refuse

**Why it matters:** These rights are the foundation of med safety.

### Card 16 (Priority)

**Front:** What bedside questions should a nurse ask before giving any medication?

**Back:**
- Why is this drug ordered?
- What assessment or lab matters first?
- Is it safe for this patient right now?
- What adverse effect am I watching for?
- How will I know it worked?

**Why it matters:** This is the practical way to connect pharmacology to nursing judgment.

## Exam #1

### Card 1

**Front:** What medication groups are commonly seen in clinical?

**Back:**
- Analgesics
- Antiemetics
- Acid-reducing drugs
- Anticoagulants
- Antihypertensives
- Diuretics
- Insulin

**Why it matters:** These are medication groups students often need to understand early in clinical.

### Card 2

**Front:** What does clinical judgment with medications mean?

**Back:**
- Notice the important patient data.
- Interpret whether the drug is safe and needed.
- Respond by giving, holding, clarifying, or reporting.
- Evaluate whether the drug helped or harmed.

**Why it matters:** Safe med administration is a thinking process, not only a task.

### Card 3

**Front:** What common situations raise the risk for medication errors?

**Back:**
- Interruptions
- Look-alike or sound-alike drugs
- Poor communication
- Wrong dose calculations
- Wrong patient identification
- Unclear orders

**Why it matters:** Exam questions often ask what makes a med pass unsafe.

### Card 4

**Front:** What should the nurse do first if a medication error occurs?

**Back:**
- Assess the patient first.
- Then notify the provider or follow policy.
- Complete the required report.
- Keep monitoring for harm.

**Why it matters:** The patient comes before paperwork.

### Card 5

**Front:** How do acetaminophen, NSAIDs, and opioids differ?

**Back:**
- **Acetaminophen:** pain and fever; watch total daily dose and liver risk.
- **NSAIDs:** pain, fever, and inflammation; watch bleeding, stomach, and kidneys.
- **Opioids:** stronger pain relief; watch breathing, sedation, constipation, and hypotension.

**Why it matters:** This is a high-yield compare card.

### Card 6

**Front:** What findings suggest opioid toxicity, and what reverses it?

**Back:**
- Very slow breathing
- Excessive sleepiness
- Low blood pressure
- Pinpoint pupils can occur
- **Naloxone** reverses serious opioid effects

**Why it matters:** Opioid safety is a frequent priority topic.

### Card 7

**Front:** What should the nurse reassess after giving pain medication?

**Back:**
- Pain level
- Respiratory rate
- Sedation level
- Blood pressure when relevant
- Functional comfort, such as ability to cough, move, or rest

**Why it matters:** Pain treatment is not finished when the drug is given.

### Card 8

**Front:** What are the main insulin categories?

**Back:**
- Rapid-acting
- Short-acting
- Intermediate-acting
- Long-acting

**Why it matters:** The insulin type helps predict when hypoglycemia risk is highest.

### Card 9

**Front:** What is the key meal-safety teaching for rapid-acting insulin?

**Back:**
- It works fast, so food should be available soon after the dose.
- Do not give it and then delay the meal without a clear reason and plan.

**Why it matters:** Timing errors with rapid insulin can cause hypoglycemia quickly.

### Card 10

**Front:** What does "clear before cloudy" mean with insulin?

**Back:**
- When mixing regular insulin with NPH, draw up the **clear** insulin first and the **cloudy** insulin second.

**Why it matters:** This prevents contaminating regular insulin with NPH.

### Card 11

**Front:** What are common signs of hypoglycemia?

**Back:**
- Sweating
- Shakiness
- Hunger
- Headache
- Weakness
- Confusion
- Irritability

**Why it matters:** Hypoglycemia can become an emergency if it is missed.

### Card 12 (Safety)

**Front:** Why is insulin considered a high-alert medication?

**Back:**
- A small dosing mistake can cause serious hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia.
- Doses must match the order, the blood glucose, and the patient's eating plan.

**Why it matters:** This is why insulin needs extra care every time.
