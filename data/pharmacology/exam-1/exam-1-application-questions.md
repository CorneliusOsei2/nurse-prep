# Pharmacology Exam 1 Application Questions

These NCLEX-style practice questions follow the Pharmacology Exam 1 outline and focus on bedside medication decisions, order-questioning, adverse-effect recognition, and safe nursing action.

## How to use this file

- Try to answer before looking at the correct answer.
- Use the explanation to understand the nursing logic, not just memorize a fact.
- Focus on safety words such as first, best, priority, hold, monitor, and report.
- Pay attention to airway, breathing, circulation, medication timing, and red-flag adverse effects.

## Pharmacologic Principles

### Question 1 (Compare/Contrast)

**Question:** A client with stable angina develops chest pressure while walking to the bathroom. The provider prescribes nitroglycerin sublingual. Why is the sublingual route helpful in this situation?

A. It absorbs slowly through the stomach for a longer effect  
B. It avoids first-pass liver metabolism and can act faster  
C. It is safer because it cannot affect blood pressure  
D. It works only after the medication reaches the large intestine

**Correct answer:** B. It avoids first-pass liver metabolism and can act faster

**Explanation:** The big clue is that the client needs fast relief. Sublingual medication absorbs through the oral mucosa and avoids the first pass through the liver, so the effect starts faster than with a swallowed tablet. On NCLEX-style questions, fast symptom relief often points to a route that bypasses the GI tract.

### Question 2 (Order to Question)

**Question:** A client with septic shock has blood pressure 84/48 mm Hg, cool mottled extremities, and delayed capillary refill. The provider changes an antiemetic from IV to IM. What is the nurse's best action?

A. Give the IM medication because absorption is faster in shock  
B. Question the IM route because poor perfusion can make absorption unreliable  
C. Change the medication to the oral route  
D. Hold all medications until the blood pressure is normal

**Correct answer:** B. Question the IM route because poor perfusion can make absorption unreliable

**Explanation:** IM drugs depend on blood flow to the muscle. In shock, peripheral perfusion is poor, so IM absorption can become slow and unpredictable. A strong NCLEX answer does not blindly follow the order when the route no longer fits the patient's condition.

### Question 3 (Application)

**Question:** A frail older adult has an albumin level of 2.2 g/dL and becomes more drowsy than expected after receiving a usual dose of a highly protein-bound medication. Which interpretation by the nurse is best?

A. The drug will always be excreted faster  
B. More free drug may be present, raising toxicity risk  
C. The drug will stay trapped in the stomach  
D. The medication can no longer reach target tissues

**Correct answer:** B. More free drug may be present, raising toxicity risk

**Explanation:** Albumin carries many drugs in the blood. When albumin is low, more of the medication stays free instead of bound, and the free portion is the active part. That means the same usual dose can act too strongly and cause toxicity in a client with low albumin.

### Question 4 (Application)

**Question:** A client with severe cirrhosis receives a medication that is a prodrug. Several doses are given, but the desired effect is weak. Which explanation is most accurate?

A. The drug may not be activated well enough to work  
B. The drug will always absorb faster  
C. The drug will definitely become more powerful  
D. The drug will have no side effects

**Correct answer:** A. The drug may not be activated well enough to work

**Explanation:** A prodrug needs the body to change it into an active form, and the liver often does that work. If the liver is badly failing, activation may be reduced, so the drug may seem weaker than expected. This is an important NCLEX idea because liver disease does not always make every drug stronger.

### Question 5 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** A client says, "I drink grapefruit juice every morning with my medications because it feels healthy." Which response by the nurse is best?

A. "That is always safe because juice helps absorption."  
B. "Grapefruit can raise the blood level of some oral medications, so it is important to check each drug."  
C. "Grapefruit only affects injectable medications."  
D. "Grapefruit always makes medications work less."

**Correct answer:** B. "Grapefruit can raise the blood level of some oral medications, so it is important to check each drug."

**Explanation:** This answer is best because it is both safe and accurate. Grapefruit can inhibit metabolism of some oral medications, which may raise the drug level and increase toxicity risk. On the exam, be careful with answers that say always or never when the real issue depends on the specific medication.

### Question 6 (Compare/Contrast)

**Question:** Two weeks after a new medication is added, a client's long-used oral medication seems much less effective. The chart notes that the new drug increases liver enzyme activity. What change should the nurse expect for most active drugs?

A. Higher drug level and stronger effect  
B. Lower drug level and weaker effect  
C. Faster absorption and slower excretion  
D. No change in duration

**Correct answer:** B. Lower drug level and weaker effect

**Explanation:** Increased metabolism usually means the body breaks the active drug down faster. That lowers the drug level, shortens the duration, and can make the medication seem weaker. On a test, a drug that suddenly stops working after an enzyme inducer is added often points to faster metabolism.

### Question 7 (Safety)

**Question:** During morning medication pass, a client looks at a tablet in the cup and says, "That pill is a different color from the one I took yesterday." What should the nurse do first?

A. Tell the client that the pharmacy sometimes changes the color  
B. Recheck the medication against the order and clarify any difference before giving it  
C. Ask the roommate whether the pill looks familiar  
D. Give the medication now so the dose is not late

**Correct answer:** B. Recheck the medication against the order and clarify any difference before giving it

**Explanation:** The client's concern is a safety clue, so the nurse should stop and verify the medication before giving it. NCLEX questions often reward the answer that pauses the med pass and checks the order when something does not fit. Staying on schedule never comes before medication safety.

### Question 8 (First/Best Action)

**Question:** A newly licensed nurse is preparing to give the first dose of an unfamiliar medication to a postoperative client. The client asks, "What is this for?" What should the nurse do first?

A. Ask another new nurse whether the medication is usually safe  
B. Review the medication in the drug guide, including the indication, nursing implications, and major adverse effects  
C. Give the medication on time and look it up after the med pass  
D. Tell the client the provider will explain it later

**Correct answer:** B. Review the medication in the drug guide, including the indication, nursing implications, and major adverse effects

**Explanation:** The safest first step is to understand the medication before giving it. A drug guide helps the nurse connect the order to why the client is receiving it, what to assess before administration, and what serious effects need monitoring. NCLEX usually rewards the answer that protects the client before the medication is given.

## Exam #1

### Question 9 (Priority)

**Question:** The nurse is preparing to give a scheduled dose of morphine to four postoperative clients. Which client should the nurse assess first?

A. A client reporting pain 8 out of 10 with a respiratory rate of 10/min and new drowsiness  
B. A client reporting pain 7 out of 10 with mild nausea after lunch  
C. A client reporting pain 6 out of 10 with dry mouth  
D. A client reporting pain 5 out of 10 with mild itching

**Correct answer:** A. A client reporting pain 8 out of 10 with a respiratory rate of 10/min and new drowsiness

**Explanation:** Pain matters, but breathing comes first. A low respiratory rate plus new drowsiness suggests opioid-related respiratory depression, so this client needs reassessment before another dose is given. The other findings are more expected opioid side effects and are less urgent.

### Question 10 (Application)

**Question:** Twenty minutes after IV hydromorphone, a client is hard to arouse, has a respiratory rate of 8/min, and an oxygen saturation of 88% on room air. What is the nurse's priority action?

A. Stay with the client, support airway and breathing, and prepare to give naloxone per protocol  
B. Give orange juice in case the client is hypoglycemic  
C. Reassess in 30 minutes because sedation is expected  
D. Document the findings after the next vital-sign check

**Correct answer:** A. Stay with the client, support airway and breathing, and prepare to give naloxone per protocol

**Explanation:** This client has clear opioid toxicity with respiratory compromise. The priority is airway and breathing support right away, not waiting for the next reassessment. On NCLEX, when a medication problem threatens oxygenation, the safest airway-focused action comes first.

### Question 11 (Medication Error)

**Question:** The nurse realizes insulin was given to the wrong client. What should the nurse do first?

A. Complete the incident report  
B. Notify risk management  
C. Assess the client who received the insulin  
D. Call the pharmacy

**Correct answer:** C. Assess the client who received the insulin

**Explanation:** When an error happens, the patient comes first. Insulin can cause dangerous hypoglycemia, so the immediate priority is to assess the client, check glucose, and begin monitoring. Reporting and documentation still matter, but they come after patient safety is addressed.

### Question 12 (SATA)

**Question:** A client received regular insulin 1 hour ago. Which findings suggest hypoglycemia? Select all that apply.

A. Sweating  
B. Shakiness  
C. Confusion  
D. Hunger  
E. Warm flushed skin

**Correct answer:** A, B, C, D

**Explanation:** Sweating, shakiness, confusion, and hunger are common hypoglycemia cues. The question is testing whether you recognize the pattern early before the client gets worse. Warm flushed skin does not fit the usual low-blood-sugar picture as well as the other options.

### Question 13 (Safety)

**Question:** The nurse is preparing rapid-acting insulin for a client whose lunch tray has not arrived. What is the best action?

A. Give the insulin now to stay on time  
B. Hold the insulin until food is available or the plan is clarified  
C. Give half the ordered dose without asking  
D. Switch the dose to long-acting insulin

**Correct answer:** B. Hold the insulin until food is available or the plan is clarified

**Explanation:** Rapid-acting insulin starts working quickly, so giving it when the meal is delayed can cause hypoglycemia. The safest choice is to stop and make sure food will be available or the plan is clarified. NCLEX often tests whether you choose patient safety over routine timing.

### Question 14 (Safety)

**Question:** The nurse reviews PRN analgesic orders for four clients. Which order should the nurse question?

A. Acetaminophen for a client with a temperature of 38.3 C (101 F)  
B. Ibuprofen for a client with creatinine 2.3 mg/dL and a history of peptic ulcer disease  
C. Morphine for a postoperative client with pain 8 out of 10 and respiratory rate 18/min  
D. Acetaminophen for a client with headache and no liver disease

**Correct answer:** B. Ibuprofen for a client with creatinine 2.3 mg/dL and a history of peptic ulcer disease

**Explanation:** Ibuprofen is an NSAID, so it can worsen kidney problems and increase GI bleeding risk. This client has both warning clues at the same time, which makes that order the one to question. Real-world NCLEX items often combine two safety red flags in the best answer.

### Question 15 (Reassessment)

**Question:** A client receives IV pain medication after surgery. Which nursing action is most important next?

A. Reassess pain relief and breathing within the expected time for the IV medication  
B. Wait until the end of the shift to see if the medication worked  
C. Ask the family if the client looks more comfortable  
D. Offer a bedtime snack first

**Correct answer:** A. Reassess pain relief and breathing within the expected time for the IV medication

**Explanation:** Pain treatment is not complete after the medication is given. IV pain medicine acts quickly, so the nurse needs to reassess both pain relief and respiratory safety in the expected time window. Real NCLEX questions often reward the answer that includes reassessment, not just administration.

### Question 16 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** Which statement by a client taking a new pain medicine shows the need for more teaching?

A. "I should tell the nurse if I feel too sleepy."  
B. "I should report trouble breathing right away."  
C. "If I get constipated, I should mention it."  
D. "If I still have pain, I should double my dose without asking."

**Correct answer:** D. "If I still have pain, I should double my dose without asking."

**Explanation:** The unsafe clue is deciding to change the dose alone. Opioids can quickly become dangerous if the dose is increased without guidance, especially because breathing and sedation can worsen before pain improves. The other statements show the client understands important side effects to report.
