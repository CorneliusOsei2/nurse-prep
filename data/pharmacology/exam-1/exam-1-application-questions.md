# Pharmacology Exam 1 Application Questions

These NCLEX-style practice questions follow the Pharmacology Exam 1 outline. They focus on bedside medication decisions, order-questioning, adverse-effect recognition, lab-based safety checks, and clinical judgment.

## How to use this file

- Cover the answer and try each question on your own first.
- Use the explanation to understand the nursing thinking, not just memorize a fact.
- Focus on safety words: **first, best, priority, hold, monitor, notify,** and **report**.
- Pay attention to **airway → breathing → circulation** order, medication timing, hold parameters, and red-flag adverse effects.
- For Select-All-That-Apply (SATA) questions, judge each option on its own. Do not look for a pattern.
- For ordering questions, think about what keeps the patient safest at each step.

## Pharmacologic Principles

### Question 1 — Sublingual Route (Compare/Contrast)

**Question:** A client with stable angina develops chest pressure while walking to the bathroom. The provider prescribes nitroglycerin sublingual. Why is the sublingual route helpful in this situation?

A. It allows slow absorption through the stomach lining so the effect lasts longer  
B. It avoids first-pass liver metabolism, so the drug reaches the bloodstream faster  
C. It prevents the medication from lowering blood pressure  
D. It lets the nurse delay cardiac monitoring because the route is gentler

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: The client needs fast pain relief. A sublingual tablet dissolves under the tongue and goes straight into the bloodstream. It skips the liver's first-pass metabolism, so it works much faster than a pill that has to travel through the stomach.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because the sublingual route does *not* go through the stomach. It absorbs through the tissue under the tongue.
- **B** is correct because bypassing the liver means more drug reaches the blood quickly, which is exactly what this client needs.
- **C** is not the best choice because nitroglycerin *does* lower blood pressure. That is actually part of how it works. The nurse should still monitor blood pressure.
- **D** is not the best choice because the route does not change the need for monitoring. A client with chest pain always needs cardiac monitoring.

**Test-taking tip:** When a question mentions a client in acute distress, look for the answer that explains the fastest way to get relief. Routes that skip the GI tract (sublingual, IV, inhalation) usually act faster.

### Question 2 — IM Route in Shock (Order to Question)

**Question:** A client with septic shock has a blood pressure of 84/48 mm Hg, cool mottled extremities, and delayed capillary refill. The provider changes an antiemetic from IV to IM. What is the nurse's best action?

A. Give the IM injection because intramuscular medications are always absorbed quickly  
B. Question the IM route because poor tissue perfusion can make absorption unreliable  
C. Switch the medication to the oral route to be gentler on the client  
D. Hold all medications until the blood pressure returns to normal

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: IM injections depend on blood flow to the muscle. In shock, blood is being sent to the vital organs, so the muscles get very little flow. That means the drug may sit in the tissue and absorb too slowly or unevenly.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because IM absorption is *not* always fast. It depends on perfusion. In shock, perfusion to the muscles is poor.
- **B** is correct because the nurse should question any order that does not fit the client's current condition. Advocacy for the patient is a key nursing role.
- **C** is not the best choice because a client in shock may not be able to swallow safely, and oral absorption is also unreliable when perfusion is low.
- **D** is not the best choice because holding *all* medications could be dangerous. The client may need life-saving drugs. The issue here is the route, not every medication.

**Test-taking tip:** When you see signs of shock (low BP, cool skin, mottled extremities), think about how poor blood flow changes the way the body handles drugs. The IV route is the most reliable in shock.

### Question 3 — Low Albumin and Drug Toxicity (Application)

**Question:** A frail older adult has an albumin level of 2.2 g/dL (normal is about 3.5–5.0 g/dL). After receiving a usual dose of a highly protein-bound medication, the client becomes more drowsy than expected. Which interpretation by the nurse is most accurate?

A. The low albumin means the drug is being excreted by the kidneys faster than normal  
B. More of the drug is free and unbound in the blood, which raises the risk of toxicity  
C. The drug cannot leave the bloodstream, so it will have no therapeutic effect  
D. The low albumin protects the client from side effects because less drug is active

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Think about it this way: Albumin is like a bus that carries drug molecules through the blood. Only the drug molecules that are *not* on the bus (the free ones) can actually work on the body. When albumin is low, there are fewer seats on the bus, so more drug molecules are free and active. That can make a normal dose feel like an overdose.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because low albumin does not speed up kidney excretion in this simple way. The problem here is too much free drug, not faster removal.
- **B** is correct because less albumin means less binding, so more free drug is active. That explains the extra drowsiness.
- **C** is not the best choice because the drug *can* leave the bloodstream. In fact, the free drug reaches tissues more easily, which is why toxicity is the concern.
- **D** is not the best choice because it is the opposite of what happens. More free drug means *more* risk, not less.

**Test-taking tip:** Low albumin + highly protein-bound drug = higher toxicity risk. This is a classic NCLEX combination. If you see low albumin in the stem, look for the answer about increased drug effect.

### Question 4 — Prodrug and Liver Disease (Application)

**Question:** A client with severe cirrhosis receives a medication that is a prodrug. After several doses, the desired therapeutic effect is weaker than expected. Which explanation is most accurate?

A. The damaged liver may not convert the prodrug into its active form effectively  
B. The cirrhosis is causing the drug to be absorbed too quickly from the GI tract  
C. The prodrug is being activated too strongly, so the body is clearing it faster  
D. The liver disease has no effect on prodrug metabolism because the kidneys take over completely

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is a helpful way to remember this: A prodrug is like a locked box. It only becomes useful medicine after the liver "unlocks" it. If the liver is badly damaged by cirrhosis, it cannot unlock the box properly. So the drug stays inactive, and the client does not get the full benefit.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because the liver is the main organ that converts most prodrugs into active forms. Severe liver damage means less conversion and a weaker effect.
- **B** is not the best choice because cirrhosis does not cause faster GI absorption. It affects metabolism, not absorption speed.
- **C** is not the best choice because the problem is *too little* activation, not too much. The drug is weaker, not stronger.
- **D** is not the best choice because while the kidneys help with excretion, they cannot fully replace the liver's role in activating prodrugs.

**Test-taking tip:** Not every liver problem makes a drug stronger. Prodrugs are the big exception — liver failure makes them *weaker* because they cannot be activated. If you see "prodrug + liver disease + weak effect," choose the answer about poor activation.

### Question 5 — Grapefruit Interaction (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** A client says, "I drink grapefruit juice every morning with my medications because it feels healthy." Which response by the nurse is best?

A. "Grapefruit juice is fine with all medications, so you can continue your routine."  
B. "Grapefruit juice can raise the blood level of certain oral medications, so let's check whether any of yours are affected."  
C. "Grapefruit only interacts with IV medications, so your oral pills are safe."  
D. "You should stop drinking all fruit juices while taking any medication."

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Grapefruit juice blocks certain enzymes in the gut and liver that normally break down some oral medications. When those enzymes are blocked, more drug gets into the blood than expected. That can raise the risk of side effects or toxicity.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because grapefruit is *not* safe with all medications. Some drugs are strongly affected and can reach dangerous levels.
- **B** is correct because it gives accurate, honest information and encourages the client to check each specific drug. It does not cause unnecessary fear.
- **C** is not the best choice because grapefruit affects *oral* medications (the ones you swallow), not IV medications. This answer has the interaction backwards.
- **D** is not the best choice because it is too extreme. Not all fruit juices cause this problem. Only grapefruit (and a few similar citrus fruits) block these specific enzymes.

**Test-taking tip:** Watch out for answer choices that use the words "all," "always," "never," or "none." Those absolute words are usually wrong on the NCLEX. The best nursing answer is often specific and balanced.

### Question 6 — Enzyme Induction (Compare/Contrast)

**Question:** Two weeks after a new medication is added, a client's long-used oral medication seems much less effective. The chart notes that the new drug is a known enzyme inducer. What change should the nurse expect for most active drugs?

A. The drug level will be higher because the liver is processing it more slowly  
B. The drug level will be lower because the liver is breaking it down faster  
C. The drug will be absorbed faster from the stomach and cause a stronger peak effect  
D. The drug's half-life will double, making it last much longer

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Think about it this way: An enzyme inducer is like adding extra workers to a factory line. The liver now breaks down the drug faster than before. That means the drug level in the blood drops, and the medication may seem like it stopped working, even though the client is still taking the same dose.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because an enzyme inducer speeds up metabolism, not slows it down. That means the drug level goes *down*, not up.
- **B** is correct because faster metabolism = lower drug level = weaker effect. The drug is being cleared before it can do its full job.
- **C** is not the best choice because enzyme induction affects how the liver breaks down the drug, not how fast the stomach absorbs it. These are different steps.
- **D** is not the best choice because faster breakdown actually *shortens* the half-life, not doubles it.

**Test-taking tip:** Enzyme inducer = faster metabolism = lower drug level = weaker effect. Enzyme inhibitor = slower metabolism = higher drug level = stronger effect (and higher toxicity risk). This pair shows up often on the NCLEX.

### Question 7 — Medication Verification (Safety)

**Question:** During the morning medication pass, a client looks at a tablet in the cup and says, "That pill is a different color from the one I took yesterday." What should the nurse do first?

A. Reassure the client that generic brands often look different and give the medication  
B. Hold the medication, recheck it against the order and the MAR, and clarify any discrepancy before giving it  
C. Administer the medication because the barcode scan matched the client's wristband  
D. Document that the client questioned the pill and continue with the scheduled dose

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: The client just gave you an important safety clue. Even if the barcode scanned correctly, something does not match what the client expected. The safest action is always to stop and verify before giving the medication.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because even though generic brands *can* look different, assuming that is the reason without checking is not safe. The pill could genuinely be the wrong medication.
- **B** is correct because it follows the principle "when in doubt, check it out." Rechecking the medication against the order protects the client.
- **C** is not the best choice because barcode scanning is a helpful safety tool, but it is not perfect. Technology can have errors. A client's concern still deserves investigation.
- **D** is not the best choice because documenting without verifying does not solve the safety problem. Documentation is important, but it comes after you make sure the medication is correct.

**Test-taking tip:** On the NCLEX, if a client or family member raises a concern about a medication, the correct answer is almost always to stop and verify. Never give the medication first and check later.

### Question 8 — Unfamiliar Medication (First/Best Action)

**Question:** A newly licensed nurse is preparing to give the first dose of an unfamiliar medication to a postoperative client. The client asks, "What is this for?" What should the nurse do first?

A. Ask a more experienced nurse on the unit to describe the medication quickly  
B. Look up the medication in the drug reference, including the indication, nursing implications, and major adverse effects  
C. Give the medication on time and plan to research it after the medication pass is finished  
D. Tell the client, "Your provider ordered it, so it should be fine"

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: You should never give a medication you do not understand. The safest first step is to look it up yourself. A drug reference will tell you why the client is getting it, what to check before giving it, and what serious effects to watch for after.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because relying on another nurse's memory is not as reliable as looking it up in an official reference. Colleagues can help, but the drug guide is the best first source.
- **B** is correct because it protects the client and builds the nurse's knowledge at the same time. You cannot teach a client about a drug you do not understand.
- **C** is not the best choice because giving a drug you do not know is unsafe. If something goes wrong, you would not recognize the adverse effect in time.
- **D** is not the best choice because the client deserves an informed answer. Saying "it should be fine" does not give the client useful information and does not show safe practice.

**Test-taking tip:** "Look it up before you give it" is one of the safest answers on the NCLEX. If the nurse does not know the drug, the right move is always to learn about it first.

## Exam 1 — Clinical Application

### Question 9 — Opioid Priority Assessment (Priority)

**Question:** The nurse is preparing to give a scheduled dose of morphine to four postoperative clients. Which client should the nurse assess first?

A. A client reporting pain 8/10 with a respiratory rate of 10 breaths/min and new drowsiness  
B. A client reporting pain 7/10 with mild nausea after lunch  
C. A client reporting pain 6/10 with a dry mouth and requesting water  
D. A client reporting pain 5/10 with mild itching on both arms

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: All four clients have pain, but only one client has a sign that breathing may be in danger. A respiratory rate of 10 with new drowsiness could mean the opioid is causing respiratory depression. That is a life-threatening change, so this client needs to be checked first.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because low respiratory rate + new drowsiness is the classic warning for opioid-induced respiratory depression. Breathing problems always come first.
- **B** is not the best choice because mild nausea is a common and expected side effect of opioids. It is uncomfortable but not life-threatening.
- **C** is not the best choice because dry mouth is a minor side effect. Offering water is kind, but it is not urgent.
- **D** is not the best choice because mild itching is a common opioid side effect related to histamine release. It needs monitoring but is not dangerous right now.

**Test-taking tip:** When NCLEX asks "which client first," always look for the one whose airway, breathing, or circulation is at risk. Pain is important, but breathing trumps pain every time.

### Question 10 — Opioid Toxicity (Application)

**Question:** Twenty minutes after IV hydromorphone, a client is hard to arouse, has a respiratory rate of 8 breaths/min, and an oxygen saturation of 88% on room air. What is the nurse's priority action?

A. Stay with the client, support the airway and breathing, and prepare to give naloxone per protocol  
B. Elevate the head of the bed and recheck the oxygen saturation in 15 minutes  
C. Increase the IV fluid rate to help flush the medication out faster  
D. Document the findings and notify the provider at the next scheduled rounding time

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: This client is showing textbook opioid toxicity — very sleepy, slow breathing, low oxygen. This is an emergency. The nurse must act *right now* to protect the airway and get ready to reverse the opioid with naloxone.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because airway and breathing come first in any emergency. Naloxone is the specific antidote for opioid overdose. Staying with the client ensures you can act if breathing stops.
- **B** is not the best choice because waiting 15 minutes with a respiratory rate of 8 and oxygen at 88% is dangerous. This client could stop breathing during that wait.
- **C** is not the best choice because IV fluids do not reverse opioid effects. The medication needs a specific antidote (naloxone), not extra fluid.
- **D** is not the best choice because waiting for scheduled rounds could cost the client their life. This is an urgent situation that requires immediate action.

**Test-taking tip:** A respiratory rate below 12 + hard to arouse + low oxygen after an opioid = act NOW. Think: Airway first, naloxone ready. Never wait and watch when breathing is this slow.

### Question 11 — Medication Error Response (Medication Safety)

**Question:** The nurse realizes that insulin was given to the wrong client five minutes ago. What should the nurse do first?

A. Assess the client who received the insulin and check the blood glucose immediately  
B. Complete an incident report so the event is documented before the end of the shift  
C. Notify the charge nurse and ask what to do next  
D. Call the pharmacy to ask whether the insulin dose could be harmful

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: When a medication error happens, the client always comes first. Insulin can cause dangerous hypoglycemia, so you need to check on the client right away. Everything else — reporting, documenting, notifying — is important, but it comes *after* you make sure the client is safe.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because patient safety is always the first priority. Checking glucose tells you whether the insulin is already causing a problem.
- **B** is not the best choice because the incident report is important for quality improvement, but it can wait until the client is assessed and stable.
- **C** is not the best choice because notifying the charge nurse is a good step, but it comes after you check on the client. Do not leave the client unassessed while you go find someone.
- **D** is not the best choice because calling the pharmacy takes time. Meanwhile, the insulin is already working in the client's body. Assess first, then gather more information.

**Test-taking tip:** After any medication error, the NCLEX answer is almost always "assess the client first." Think: Patient before paperwork. Safety before systems.

### Question 12 — Hypoglycemia Signs (SATA)

**Question:** A client received regular insulin 1 hour ago. Which findings suggest the client is becoming hypoglycemic? **Select all that apply.**

A. Sweating  
B. Shakiness and trembling hands  
C. Confusion or difficulty answering questions  
D. Hunger  
E. Warm, flushed, dry skin with fruity breath

**Correct answer:** A, B, C, D

**Explanation:**
Here is a helpful way to remember this: When blood sugar drops too low, the body releases stress hormones (like adrenaline) to try to raise it. That causes sweating, shaking, and hunger. The brain also needs glucose to work, so confusion is a sign that the brain is not getting enough fuel.

Let's look at each option:
- **A (Sweating)** is correct. This is one of the earliest signs of hypoglycemia. The body is trying to respond to the low sugar.
- **B (Shakiness)** is correct. Trembling hands and feeling jittery happen because of the adrenaline surge.
- **C (Confusion)** is correct. The brain runs on glucose. When glucose is low, thinking becomes foggy and slow.
- **D (Hunger)** is correct. The body is sending a signal: "I need fuel." Hunger is a classic early warning sign.
- **E (Warm, flushed, dry skin with fruity breath)** is not correct for hypoglycemia. This picture describes *hyperglycemia* (high blood sugar), especially diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). The fruity breath is a key difference.

**Test-taking tip:** Hypoglycemia = cold and clammy (sweaty, shaky, confused, hungry). Hyperglycemia = hot and dry (flushed, fruity breath, thirsty). This contrast appears on the NCLEX often. If you can remember "cold and clammy = need some candy," you will get these questions right.

### Question 13 — Rapid-Acting Insulin Timing (Safety)

**Question:** The nurse is preparing to give rapid-acting insulin (lispro) for a client whose lunch tray has not arrived yet. What is the nurse's best action?

A. Give the insulin now to stay on the medication schedule  
B. Hold the insulin until food is available and clarify the timing plan with the provider if needed  
C. Give half the ordered dose without a provider order to reduce the risk  
D. Substitute a long-acting insulin such as glargine at the same dose

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Rapid-acting insulin starts working in about 15 minutes. If there is no food coming, the blood sugar will drop fast and the client could become hypoglycemic. The safest choice is to wait for the meal and contact the provider if there is a delay.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because sticking to the schedule is normally good, but not when it puts the client at risk. No food + rapid insulin = hypoglycemia danger.
- **B** is correct because holding the dose and clarifying the plan protects the client from a preventable low blood sugar episode.
- **C** is not the best choice because the nurse cannot change a dose without a provider order. Even a smaller dose without food can cause hypoglycemia.
- **D** is not the best choice because you cannot switch insulin types without an order. Rapid-acting and long-acting insulins work very differently, and mixing them up is dangerous.

**Test-taking tip:** Rapid-acting insulin (lispro, aspart) is tied to meals. No meal = hold the dose and check with the provider. This is a safety question the NCLEX loves to ask.

### Question 14 — NSAID Contraindication (Safety)

**Question:** The nurse reviews PRN analgesic orders for four clients. Which order should the nurse question first?

A. Acetaminophen 650 mg PO for a client with a temperature of 38.3°C (101°F)  
B. Ibuprofen 600 mg PO for a client with a serum creatinine of 2.3 mg/dL and a history of peptic ulcer disease  
C. Morphine 2 mg IV for a postoperative client with pain 8/10 and a respiratory rate of 18 breaths/min  
D. Acetaminophen 500 mg PO for a client with a mild headache and no liver disease

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Ibuprofen is an NSAID (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug). NSAIDs can harm the kidneys and increase the risk of GI bleeding. This client has *two* red flags at the same time — a high creatinine (meaning the kidneys are already struggling) *and* a history of stomach ulcers. That makes this order the most dangerous one on the list.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice to question because acetaminophen is appropriate for fever and is not harmful to this client based on the information given.
- **B** is correct because this client has kidney impairment (high creatinine) *and* GI risk (peptic ulcer history). Giving an NSAID could make both problems worse.
- **C** is not the best choice to question because morphine is appropriate for severe postoperative pain, and the respiratory rate of 18 is normal and safe.
- **D** is not the best choice to question because acetaminophen at this dose is safe for a client without liver disease.

**Test-taking tip:** NSAIDs have two main danger zones: kidneys and stomach. When you see a high creatinine or a history of GI bleeding in the question, and the drug is an NSAID, that is usually the answer to question.

### Question 15 — Post-Administration Reassessment (Reassessment)

**Question:** A client receives IV morphine 2 mg for postoperative pain. Which nursing action is most important 15 minutes later?

A. Reassess the client's pain level and respiratory rate  
B. Ask the family whether the client looks more comfortable  
C. Wait until the next scheduled vital-sign check in 4 hours  
D. Document that the medication was given and move on to the next client

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Giving the medication is only half the job. The other half is going back to check: Did it work? Is the client breathing safely? IV morphine peaks quickly, so 15 minutes is the right time to reassess.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because reassessing pain and respiratory rate tells you whether the medication is working and whether it is safe. This completes the nursing process.
- **B** is not the best choice because family observations are helpful, but they do not replace a nursing assessment. The nurse needs to check vital signs and pain level directly.
- **C** is not the best choice because 4 hours is far too long to wait after IV morphine. The drug peaks in minutes, and respiratory depression can happen quickly.
- **D** is not the best choice because documentation is important, but it does not replace reassessment. You need to know the outcome before you can accurately document it.

**Test-taking tip:** The nursing process does not end at "give the med." It ends at evaluation. If an answer says "reassess" after a medication, it is usually the right choice.

### Question 16 — Patient Teaching Opioid Safety (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** Which statement by a client who was just prescribed an opioid pain medication shows the nurse that more teaching is needed?

A. "I will tell the nurse if I start to feel very sleepy."  
B. "I should report any trouble breathing right away."  
C. "If I get constipated, I will let my nurse know."  
D. "If my pain does not go away, I will take an extra dose on my own to catch up."

**Correct answer:** D

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Taking extra opioid doses without asking is dangerous. Opioids can slow down breathing, and doubling up could push the client into respiratory depression. The client needs to understand that dose changes must go through the provider.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the answer because this statement shows good understanding. Reporting excessive sleepiness helps catch early signs of opioid toxicity.
- **B** is not the answer because reporting trouble breathing is exactly right. Respiratory depression is the most dangerous opioid side effect.
- **C** is not the answer because constipation is a very common opioid side effect, and reporting it shows the client is paying attention.
- **D** is correct (needs more teaching) because taking extra doses without a provider's guidance can lead to overdose. This is the unsafe statement.

**Test-taking tip:** "Needs more teaching" questions ask you to find the *wrong* statement. Read each answer as if the client said it to you. Three of them should sound safe. The one that makes you think "wait, that could be dangerous" is the answer.

### Question 17 — Lab-Based Hold-or-Give: Potassium and Furosemide (Lab Safety)

**Question:** A client's morning potassium level is 3.0 mEq/L (normal: 3.5–5.0 mEq/L). Furosemide (Lasix) 40 mg PO is due at 0900. What is the nurse's best action?

A. Give the furosemide as scheduled because it is a routine morning medication  
B. Hold the furosemide, notify the provider of the low potassium, and request further orders  
C. Give the furosemide and encourage the client to eat a banana afterward  
D. Give half the furosemide dose to reduce potassium loss

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Furosemide is a loop diuretic. It makes the body lose potassium through the urine. This client's potassium is already below normal (3.0 is low). Giving furosemide now could drop the potassium even further, which can cause dangerous heart rhythm problems.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because "routine" does not mean safe. The lab value changes the situation. A low potassium + a potassium-wasting diuretic = a safety red flag.
- **B** is correct because holding the drug and notifying the provider protects the client. The provider may want to replace potassium first and then decide about the diuretic.
- **C** is not the best choice because eating a banana is a nice idea for mild potassium support, but it is not enough to fix a truly low level. You cannot give a potassium-wasting drug and hope food will keep up.
- **D** is not the best choice because the nurse cannot change the dose without a provider order. Giving half a dose is still practicing outside your scope.

**Test-taking tip:** Before giving a diuretic, always check the potassium. Before giving an ACE inhibitor or potassium-sparing diuretic, check the potassium too (but watch for it being too *high*). Know which drugs lower potassium and which raise it.

### Question 18 — Vital-Sign Hold-or-Give: Heart Rate and Beta-Blocker (Vital Sign Safety)

**Question:** A client's heart rate is 52 beats/min. Metoprolol (Lopressor) 25 mg PO is scheduled for 0800. What should the nurse do?

A. Give the metoprolol because the client has no complaints  
B. Hold the metoprolol, document the heart rate, and notify the provider  
C. Give the metoprolol and recheck the heart rate in 4 hours  
D. Crush the tablet and mix it with applesauce to help it absorb more slowly

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Metoprolol is a beta-blocker. Beta-blockers slow the heart rate. If the heart rate is already low (below 60 is the usual hold parameter), giving another dose could make it dangerously slow. The safe action is to hold the dose and let the provider know.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because even if the client feels fine, a heart rate of 52 is below the typical hold threshold of 60. How the client *feels* is important, but the vital sign tells you the drug effect is already strong.
- **B** is correct because holding the drug, documenting, and notifying the provider follows standard nursing safety practice for beta-blockers.
- **C** is not the best choice because giving the drug when the heart rate is already low could cause it to drop further. Rechecking later does not undo the dose you already gave.
- **D** is not the best choice because crushing the tablet and slowing absorption does not solve the problem. The issue is that the heart rate is too low for the drug, not the formulation.

**Test-taking tip:** Here is a helpful way to remember hold parameters: Hold beta-blockers (metoprolol, atenolol, propranolol) if HR < 60. Hold digoxin if HR < 60. Hold ACE inhibitors/ARBs if systolic BP < 90. These numbers show up on the NCLEX regularly.

### Question 19 — Vital-Sign Hold-or-Give: Blood Pressure and ACE Inhibitor (Vital Sign Safety)

**Question:** A client's blood pressure is 86/52 mm Hg. Lisinopril (Zestril) 10 mg PO is due this morning. The client says, "I feel a little dizzy when I sit up." What is the nurse's best action?

A. Give the lisinopril and tell the client to stay in bed for 30 minutes  
B. Hold the lisinopril, keep the client safe in bed, and notify the provider about the low blood pressure  
C. Give the lisinopril with extra fluids to keep the blood pressure up  
D. Reposition the client and give the medication once the dizziness passes

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Lisinopril is an ACE inhibitor. It lowers blood pressure. This client's blood pressure is already low (86/52) and the client is dizzy. Giving a blood-pressure-lowering drug now could cause the pressure to drop further, which might lead to fainting or falls.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because staying in bed does not prevent the blood pressure from dropping more. The medication will still lower it.
- **B** is correct because holding the drug and notifying the provider is the safest path. The provider may adjust the dose or address the cause of the low BP first.
- **C** is not the best choice because extra fluids may help temporarily, but they do not make it safe to give a BP-lowering drug when the pressure is already too low.
- **D** is not the best choice because the dizziness is a symptom of the low blood pressure, not just a position issue. Repositioning does not fix the underlying problem.

**Test-taking tip:** If systolic blood pressure is below 90–100, hold any medication that lowers blood pressure (ACE inhibitors, ARBs, beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, nitrates). Notify the provider. Always check BP before giving these drugs.

### Question 20 — Provider Notification: Heparin Adverse Effect (Provider Notification)

**Question:** A client started on a continuous IV heparin infusion 12 hours ago. Which finding should the nurse report to the provider immediately?

A. The aPTT is 55 seconds (therapeutic range for heparin is 46–70 seconds)  
B. The client has a small bruise at the old IV insertion site  
C. The client reports blood in the urine and has new-onset bleeding from the gums  
D. The client's heart rate is 78 beats/min

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Heparin is a blood thinner (anticoagulant). The most dangerous side effect is bleeding. Blood in the urine *plus* bleeding gums means the client may be over-anticoagulated or having a serious bleeding complication. This combination needs immediate provider notification.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the answer because an aPTT of 55 seconds is within the therapeutic range. This means the heparin is working as intended. No urgent action is needed.
- **B** is not the answer because a small bruise at a previous IV site can happen normally, especially on a blood thinner. Monitor it, but it does not require an emergency call.
- **C** is correct because active bleeding from two different sites (urinary tract and gums) is a serious sign. This goes beyond a small bruise and needs the provider's attention right away.
- **D** is not the answer because a heart rate of 78 is completely normal. Heparin does not typically affect heart rate.

**Test-taking tip:** For anticoagulants (heparin, warfarin, enoxaparin), always watch for signs of bleeding: blood in urine, blood in stool, bleeding gums, nosebleeds, large bruises, or changes in mental status. Multiple bleeding sites = notify provider immediately.

### Question 21 — Digoxin Lab Check (Lab Safety)

**Question:** A client takes digoxin 0.125 mg PO daily for heart failure. The morning lab results show: digoxin level 2.4 ng/mL (therapeutic range: 0.8–2.0 ng/mL), potassium 3.2 mEq/L (normal: 3.5–5.0 mEq/L). What is the nurse's priority action?

A. Give the digoxin because the client has no symptoms yet  
B. Hold the digoxin, check the heart rate, and notify the provider about both lab results  
C. Give the digoxin and schedule a repeat digoxin level for tomorrow  
D. Encourage the client to drink more water to dilute the digoxin level

**Correct answer:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: The digoxin level is above the safe range (2.4 is higher than the max of 2.0). On top of that, the potassium is low. Low potassium makes digoxin toxicity *worse* because the two compete for the same spot on heart cells. This is a double danger.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because you do not need to wait for symptoms when the lab clearly shows a toxic level. Symptoms of digoxin toxicity (nausea, visual changes, dangerous heart rhythms) may come on quickly.
- **B** is correct because holding the drug prevents more digoxin from building up, checking the heart rate looks for early signs of toxicity, and the provider needs to know about both the high digoxin *and* the low potassium.
- **C** is not the best choice because giving a drug that is already at a toxic level is unsafe. A repeat level tomorrow does not protect the client today.
- **D** is not the best choice because drinking water does not lower a digoxin level in any meaningful way. The drug needs to be held and the provider must decide on next steps.

**Test-taking tip:** Digoxin + low potassium = increased toxicity risk. Always check both the digoxin level AND the potassium before giving digoxin. Hold if HR < 60, digoxin level > 2.0, or potassium is abnormal. Notify the provider.

### Question 22 — Multi-Patient Priority (Priority/Delegation)

**Question:** The nurse has four clients with medications due at 0900. After checking lab values and vital signs, which client should the nurse assess first?

A. A client on warfarin whose INR is 2.5 (therapeutic range: 2.0–3.0) and who has no bleeding signs  
B. A client on metformin whose fasting blood glucose is 136 mg/dL and who reports mild thirst  
C. A client on gentamicin whose trough level is 3.8 mcg/mL (normal trough: < 2 mcg/mL) and who reports ringing in the ears  
D. A client on lisinopril whose blood pressure is 128/78 mm Hg and who reports no complaints

**Correct answer:** C

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Gentamicin is an aminoglycoside antibiotic. High trough levels mean the drug is building up in the body. Ringing in the ears (tinnitus) is an early warning sign of ototoxicity (ear damage), which can become permanent. This client has a toxic drug level *and* a symptom of toxicity. That combination is urgent.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the priority because the INR of 2.5 is within the therapeutic range and there is no bleeding. This client is stable.
- **B** is not the priority because a fasting glucose of 136 is mildly elevated but not dangerous. Mild thirst alone is not an emergency.
- **C** is correct because the trough level is nearly double the safe limit, and the client is already showing signs of toxicity. This needs assessment and provider notification now.
- **D** is not the priority because the blood pressure is normal and the client has no complaints. This client is stable.

**Test-taking tip:** When prioritizing, look for the client who has an abnormal lab *plus* a matching symptom. A bad lab alone is concerning, but a bad lab with a related symptom means the problem is already affecting the client. That client comes first.

### Question 23 — Digoxin Toxicity Signs (SATA)

**Question:** A client has been taking digoxin for heart failure. The nurse reviews the morning assessment. Which findings could indicate digoxin toxicity? **Select all that apply.**

A. Nausea and loss of appetite  
B. Visual changes such as seeing yellow-green halos around lights  
C. Heart rate of 48 beats/min  
D. Blood pressure of 130/82 mm Hg  
E. New irregular heart rhythm on the monitor

**Correct answer:** A, B, C, E

**Explanation:**
Here is a helpful way to remember this: Digoxin toxicity affects three main systems — the gut (nausea, vomiting, no appetite), the eyes (blurred vision, yellow-green halos), and the heart (bradycardia, irregular rhythms). If you remember "gut, eyes, heart," you can catch most of the signs.

Let's look at each option:
- **A (Nausea and loss of appetite)** is correct. GI symptoms are often the first sign of digoxin toxicity.
- **B (Yellow-green halos)** is correct. This is a classic and well-known sign of digoxin toxicity that the NCLEX loves to test.
- **C (Heart rate of 48)** is correct. Digoxin slows the heart, and a rate below 60 in a client on digoxin is a red flag for toxicity.
- **D (Blood pressure 130/82)** is not a sign of toxicity. This blood pressure is within a normal range and is not specifically related to digoxin effects.
- **E (New irregular rhythm)** is correct. Digoxin toxicity can cause dangerous dysrhythmias, including heart blocks and ventricular rhythms.

**Test-taking tip:** Remember the digoxin toxicity triad: **Gut** (nausea, vomiting, anorexia), **Eyes** (halos, blurred vision), **Heart** (bradycardia, dysrhythmias). If the question mentions any of these three in a client on digoxin, think toxicity.

### Question 24 — Right Patient Verification (SATA)

**Question:** The nurse is about to administer a medication. Which actions are part of correct patient identification? **Select all that apply.**

A. Ask the client to state their full name  
B. Check the client's identification wristband  
C. Verify the client's date of birth  
D. Ask the client's roommate to confirm the name  
E. Compare identifiers to the medication administration record (MAR)

**Correct answer:** A, B, C, E

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: Safe patient identification requires at least two unique identifiers — usually the client's name and date of birth. You check these against the wristband and the MAR. The client themselves (or their wristband) is the source, never another patient or visitor.

Let's look at each option:
- **A (Ask the client to state their name)** is correct. Having the client state their own name is an active identification step. It is better than just asking "Are you Mr. Smith?" because the client has to give the information, not just agree.
- **B (Check the wristband)** is correct. The wristband is a reliable, standardized identifier that stays with the client.
- **C (Verify date of birth)** is correct. This is the second identifier. Using two different pieces of information makes misidentification much less likely.
- **D (Ask the roommate)** is not correct. Another patient is not a reliable source. They may not know the person well, and this does not meet the standard for identification.
- **E (Compare to the MAR)** is correct. The MAR connects the client's identity to the medication order. Matching the identifiers to the MAR completes the verification loop.

**Test-taking tip:** Two patient identifiers. Every time. Name + date of birth is the most common combination. Never use the room number as an identifier — clients can be moved. This basic safety step appears on the NCLEX more than you might expect.

### Question 25 — Medication Error Sequence (Ordering)

**Question:** A nurse discovers that the wrong medication was given to a client 10 minutes ago. **Put the following nursing actions in the correct order from first to last.**

A. Assess the client for any adverse effects  
B. Notify the provider of the error and the client's current status  
C. Document the event and complete an incident report  
D. Continue to monitor the client as directed by the provider  
E. Check what medication was given and what was supposed to be given

**Correct order:** E, A, B, C, D

**Explanation:**
Let's walk through the thinking step by step:

1. **E — Check what was given vs. what was ordered.** First, you need to understand the error. What drug went in? What was the right drug? How different are they? This takes only a moment and tells you how worried to be.
2. **A — Assess the client.** Now that you know what happened, go to the client immediately. Check vital signs, level of consciousness, and any symptoms that match the wrong drug's side effects.
3. **B — Notify the provider.** Call the provider with the facts: what was given, what was ordered, and the client's current condition. The provider may order labs, monitoring, or an antidote.
4. **C — Document and file an incident report.** Once the client is safe and the provider is aware, write down exactly what happened. The incident report helps the hospital learn and prevent future errors.
5. **D — Continue monitoring.** Follow the provider's orders for ongoing observation. Some drug effects take time to appear, so continued monitoring keeps the client safe.

**Test-taking tip:** For ordering questions, always start with the step that gives you the most information or protects the patient first. Think: "What do I need to know?" → "Is the client safe?" → "Who do I tell?" → "What do I write down?" → "What comes next?"

### Question 26 — Unfolding Case Part 1: Warfarin Management (Clinical Judgment)

**Scenario:** Mrs. Chen, 72 years old, takes warfarin 5 mg daily for atrial fibrillation. She comes to the clinic for a routine visit. Her INR today is 4.8 (therapeutic range for her condition: 2.0–3.0). She denies any bleeding, bruising, or falls. Her vital signs are stable.

**Part 1 Question:** Based on these findings, what is the nurse's most appropriate first action?

A. Administer the warfarin as usual because the client has no symptoms  
B. Hold the warfarin dose and notify the provider of the elevated INR  
C. Tell the client to take half the dose today and come back next week  
D. Administer vitamin K immediately without contacting the provider

**Correct answer for Part 1:** B

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: An INR of 4.8 is well above the therapeutic range. Even though Mrs. Chen feels fine right now, her blood is much thinner than it should be. She is at high risk for bleeding, even from something small like bumping her arm. The nurse must hold the warfarin and notify the provider.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because giving more warfarin when the INR is already dangerously high would increase the bleeding risk further. "No symptoms yet" does not mean safe.
- **B** is correct because holding the drug stops the problem from getting worse, and the provider needs to decide the next steps (dose adjustment, vitamin K, more frequent monitoring).
- **C** is not the best choice because the nurse cannot change the dose independently. Adjusting warfarin requires a provider order because the dosing is very precise.
- **D** is not the best choice because vitamin K is the antidote for warfarin, but it is not given automatically for every high INR. The provider makes that decision based on the INR level and whether there is active bleeding.

**Test-taking tip:** INR above therapeutic range = hold warfarin + notify provider. INR below range = the drug is not working enough. Always check the INR before giving warfarin.

### Question 27 — Unfolding Case Part 2: Warfarin Management Continued (Clinical Judgment)

**Scenario continued:** The provider tells the nurse to hold the warfarin and recheck the INR tomorrow. Two hours later, the nurse calls Mrs. Chen at home to review safety precautions. Mrs. Chen says, "I just noticed some blood in my urine, and my gums started bleeding when I brushed my teeth."

**Part 2 Question:** What is the nurse's most appropriate action now?

A. Reassure Mrs. Chen that minor bleeding is expected and to recheck the INR tomorrow as planned  
B. Tell Mrs. Chen to drink cranberry juice to help with the urinary bleeding  
C. Instruct Mrs. Chen to come to the emergency department immediately and notify the provider  
D. Advise Mrs. Chen to take her warfarin tonight to help regulate the INR

**Correct answer for Part 2:** C

**Explanation:**
Here is the key idea: The situation just changed. Mrs. Chen now has active bleeding from two sites (urine and gums) with an INR of 4.8. This is no longer just an abnormal lab result — it is a clinical emergency. She needs to be evaluated right away.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is not the best choice because bleeding from two sites with an INR this high is not minor. This could get worse quickly. Waiting until tomorrow is not safe.
- **B** is not the best choice because cranberry juice does not treat bleeding. In fact, cranberry juice may interact with warfarin. This is not a helpful recommendation.
- **C** is correct because active multi-site bleeding with a supratherapeutic INR needs urgent medical evaluation. The provider may need to give vitamin K or other treatments to stop the bleeding.
- **D** is not the best choice because taking warfarin would thin the blood even more. That is the opposite of what this client needs right now.

**Test-taking tip:** In unfolding case questions, always re-read the new information carefully. The situation changes, and so should your answer. What was safe two hours ago (hold and recheck tomorrow) is no longer safe when active bleeding starts. Always respond to the *current* picture.

### Question 28 — Insulin Types and Timing (Application)

**Question:** A client with type 1 diabetes is on both glargine (Lantus) insulin at bedtime and lispro (Humalog) insulin before meals. The client asks, "Why do I need two kinds of insulin?" Which explanation by the nurse is best?

A. "The bedtime insulin covers your blood sugar all day and night like a background level. The mealtime insulin handles the blood sugar spike from eating."  
B. "Both insulins do the same thing, but we use two to make sure the dose is not too high at once."  
C. "The mealtime insulin lasts 24 hours, and the bedtime insulin works for only 15 minutes."  
D. "You only really need the bedtime one. The mealtime one is optional if you eat healthy."

**Correct answer:** A

**Explanation:**
Think about it this way: Your body needs insulin all the time, not just at meals. Glargine (Lantus) is a long-acting insulin that works slowly over 24 hours — like a steady background drip. Lispro (Humalog) is rapid-acting and covers the quick rise in blood sugar after eating. Together, they mimic what a healthy pancreas does naturally.

Let's look at each option:
- **A** is correct because it explains both roles simply and accurately. The "background + mealtime" concept is exactly how basal-bolus therapy works.
- **B** is not the best choice because the two insulins do different jobs. They are not interchangeable. Splitting a dose is not the reason for using two types.
- **C** is not the best choice because it has the durations backwards. Glargine (bedtime) is the long-acting one (24 hours). Lispro (mealtime) is the rapid-acting one (starts in 15 minutes, lasts a few hours).
- **D** is not the best choice because both insulins are necessary for type 1 diabetes. A client with type 1 does not make their own insulin, so both basal and bolus coverage are needed regardless of diet.

**Test-taking tip:** Know the insulin pairs: long-acting (glargine, detemir) = basal/background. Rapid-acting (lispro, aspart) = bolus/mealtime. The NCLEX often tests whether you know which is which and when each should be given.

## Quick Reference: Common Hold Parameters

Use this table to review before your exam:

| Medication Type | Check Before Giving | Hold and Notify If |
|---|---|---|
| Beta-blockers (metoprolol, atenolol) | Heart rate, blood pressure | HR < 60 or systolic BP < 90 |
| Digoxin | Heart rate, potassium, digoxin level | HR < 60, K+ < 3.5 or > 5.0, digoxin level > 2.0 |
| ACE inhibitors (lisinopril, enalapril) | Blood pressure, potassium, creatinine | Systolic BP < 90, K+ > 5.0 |
| Warfarin | INR, signs of bleeding | INR above therapeutic range or active bleeding |
| Heparin | aPTT, signs of bleeding | aPTT above therapeutic range or active bleeding |
| Loop diuretics (furosemide) | Potassium, blood pressure | K+ < 3.5 or systolic BP < 90 |
| Insulin (rapid-acting) | Blood glucose, meal availability | No food available or glucose already low |
| Aminoglycosides (gentamicin) | Trough level, creatinine | Trough above normal range |
