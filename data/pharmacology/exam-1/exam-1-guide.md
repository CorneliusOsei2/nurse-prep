## Pharmacologic Principles

### Pharmacodynamics

(Pharmacodynamics means what the drug does to the body.)

*In simple words: You put the drug in → the drug does something to the body. That "something" is pharmacodynamics.*

- Drugs work by attaching to receptors, enzymes, or other cell sites and changing body function.
- An **agonist** turns a receptor on and produces a response.
  - Think of it this way: An agonist is like pressing a light switch ON.
- An **antagonist** blocks a receptor and prevents a response.
  - Think of it this way: An antagonist covers the light switch so nobody can turn it on.
- The **therapeutic effect** is the desired helpful effect — the reason we give the drug.
- A **side effect** is a predictable extra effect that may or may not be harmful.
- An **adverse effect** is an unwanted harmful effect.
- **Toxicity** means the drug effect or drug level has become dangerous.
- Dose matters: too little may not work, and too much can cause harm.

### Pharmacokinetics: Absorption

(Absorption means how the drug gets from where you give it into the bloodstream.)

*In simple words: The drug has to travel into the blood before it can do its job. How it gets there is absorption.*

- **Intravenous (IV)** drugs are not absorbed. They go directly into the blood and act fastest.
- **Oral** drugs must move through the gastrointestinal tract, so they are slower and affected by food, stomach pH, vomiting, diarrhea, and gut motility.
- **Sublingual and buccal** drugs absorb through the mucous membranes under the tongue or in the cheek. They skip first-pass liver metabolism.
- **Intramuscular (IM) and subcutaneous (SubQ)** absorption depends on blood flow to the tissue.
- Shock, edema, poor perfusion, or very cold skin can slow IM and SubQ absorption.
- Enteric-coated and extended-release tablets should not be crushed unless the drug instructions say it is safe.
- Some drugs absorb better with food, while others need an empty stomach.
- Reduced absorption can make a drug work poorly.
- Increased absorption can make the effect happen faster or stronger and may raise toxicity risk.

### Medication Administration

(Safe medication administration means giving the right drug to the right patient in the right way, then watching the response.)

*In simple words: Before you give any medicine, you check everything. After you give it, you watch the patient.*

- Use at least **two patient identifiers** (for example, name and date of birth).
- Check **allergies** before giving the medication.
- Follow the **rights of medication administration**: right patient, drug, dose, route, time, reason, documentation, response, education, and the patient's right to refuse.
- Compare the medication label with the order during the usual safety checks.
- Know the **hold parameters** before giving a drug, such as heart rate, blood pressure, lab values, or level of consciousness.
  - Think of it this way: Hold parameters are the "stop signs" that tell you when it is NOT safe to give a drug.
- Ask whether the medication is safe **right now**. A correct order can still be unsafe if the patient's condition has changed.
- **High-alert medications** such as insulin, anticoagulants, and opioids need extra care and careful checking.
- Explain the medication in plain, simple language before giving it.
- Document administration and reassess whether the medication worked or caused a problem.

### Looking Up a Medication

(A drug guide helps you connect the drug name to safe bedside practice.)

When you look up a medication, focus on:

- **Generic and brand name**
- **Drug class**
- **Why the patient receives it** (the indication)
- **How it works** (mechanism of action)
- **Normal dose and route**
- **Onset, peak, and duration** when that matters clinically
- **Contraindications and precautions**
- **Common side effects**
- **Serious adverse effects**
- **Nursing implications** (what YOU need to do)
- **Patient teaching** (what the PATIENT needs to know)

A safe student question is not only "What is this drug?" but also:

- Why is my patient getting it?
- What should I assess before I give it?
- What problem am I watching for after I give it?

### Pharmacokinetics: Distribution

(Distribution means how the drug moves from the blood to tissues and target organs.)

*In simple words: Once the drug is in the blood, it needs to travel to the right place in the body to work.*

- Blood flow affects distribution. Well-perfused organs such as the brain, heart, liver, and kidneys receive drug quickly.
- Poor perfusion can delay how fast a drug reaches tissues.
- Many drugs bind to **albumin** (a protein) in the blood.
- Only the **free, unbound** part of the drug can leave the blood and act.
- Low albumin can increase the free drug level and raise toxicity risk.
  - Think of it this way: Albumin is like a bus seat for the drug. If there are fewer seats, more drug molecules are "standing free" and active — which can be too much.
- Age, body fat, body water, and pregnancy can change drug distribution.
- Some drugs cross the **blood-brain barrier** or **placenta**, and some do not.
- A drug can be in the bloodstream but still not have the expected effect yet if it has not distributed well to the target tissue.

### Pharmacokinetics: Metabolism and Excretion

(Metabolism changes the drug, and excretion removes the drug from the body.)

#### Metabolism

(Most drug metabolism happens in the liver.)

- Metabolism may inactivate a drug, activate a prodrug, or create active metabolites.
- A **prodrug** is inactive or less active until the body changes it into the active form.
  - Think of it this way: A prodrug is like a seed. The liver "waters" it and turns it into the working plant.

#### Impact of liver failure

(Liver failure changes how many drugs are handled.)

- Liver failure often slows metabolism.
- When metabolism is slower, many drugs stay in the body longer and can build up.
- This can cause stronger effects, longer effects, or toxicity.
- Prodrugs are a special case: if the liver cannot activate them well, the drug may work less than expected.

#### Impact of grapefruit

(Grapefruit matters because it can change the metabolism of some oral medications.)

- Grapefruit can **inhibit** intestinal CYP3A4 enzymes for some oral drugs.
- That means less drug is broken down before it reaches the blood.
- The result can be **higher drug levels** and a higher risk of side effects or toxicity.
- Grapefruit does not affect every medication, but it is a common drug-interaction warning.

#### What happens when metabolism is enhanced or inhibited?

(These changes affect both drug level and drug effect.)

- **Enhanced metabolism** means the body breaks a drug down faster.
  - Faster breakdown usually causes **lower drug levels, weaker effect, and shorter duration**.
  - For some prodrugs, enhanced metabolism can increase the active form and sometimes increase toxicity.
- **Inhibited metabolism** means the body breaks a drug down more slowly.
  - Slower breakdown usually causes **higher drug levels, stronger effect, and longer duration**.
  - For some prodrugs, inhibited metabolism may reduce activation and make the drug less effective.

#### Excretion

(Most excretion happens through the kidneys.)

- Reduced kidney function can cause drug accumulation and toxicity.
- Urine output, creatinine, and estimated kidney function (GFR) help guide safer dosing.
- Bile and stool also help remove some drugs.

### Pharmacotherapeutics

(Pharmacotherapeutics means using drugs to prevent disease, treat disease, relieve symptoms, or support body function.)

- Every medication should have a clear **indication** (the reason it is ordered).
- Nurses think about **benefit versus risk** before and after administration.
- **Contraindications** mean situations where the drug should not be used.
- **Precautions** mean extra caution and monitoring are needed.
- A **therapeutic range** is the blood level where a drug is expected to work safely.
- Drugs with a **narrow therapeutic index** have a small gap between a helpful dose and a harmful dose.
  - Think of it this way: A narrow therapeutic index is like a very thin path on a cliff. A little too much or too little, and you fall off.
- Some drugs need **peak and trough** levels or close lab monitoring.
- The nurse also watches for allergies, interactions, side effects, and whether the patient's symptoms improve.

### Putting it all together

(Bedside medication thinking connects the order, the patient, and the drug response.)

Before giving a medication, ask yourself:

1. What problem is this drug treating?
2. What assessment or lab matters first?
3. Is this the safest route and time for this patient right now?
4. What side effect or complication is most important to watch for?
5. How will I know whether the drug worked?

After giving it:

- Reassess the patient at the right time (sooner for IV, a bit later for oral).
- Compare the new findings with the reason the drug was ordered.
- Teach the patient what to report, especially red flags.

### Medications Commonly Given in Clinical — Safety Quick-Reference

(These are medication groups students often see in bedside care. For each one, you will find what to check before giving, what to watch after, when to hold and call the provider, key labs, and patient teaching.)

#### Acetaminophen (Tylenol)

**What it is for:** Pain and fever.

**⚠️ Maximum daily dose: 3–4 g/day for most adults. Use a LOWER max (2 g/day or per provider order) in patients with liver disease or heavy alcohol use.**

*In simple words: Acetaminophen is gentle on the stomach, but it is hard on the liver if you take too much.*

**Before giving — check:**
- Has the patient already received acetaminophen today (including combination products like Percocet or Norco that contain acetaminophen)? Add up the total.
- Any history of liver disease or heavy alcohol use?
- Check the liver function labs if available (AST, ALT).

**After giving — watch for:**
- Pain or fever improvement (reassess in 30–60 minutes for oral).
- Signs of liver problems (rare with normal doses): nausea, vomiting, right upper belly pain, dark urine, jaundice (yellowing of skin or eyes).

**When to hold and notify the provider:**
- Total daily dose would exceed 3–4 g (or the lower limit set for this patient).
- Patient has signs of liver failure.
- Liver labs (AST, ALT) are very elevated.

**Key labs:** AST, ALT (liver enzymes), acetaminophen level if overdose is suspected.

**Patient teaching:**
- "Many over-the-counter medicines also contain acetaminophen. Always check labels so you do not accidentally take too much."
- "Do not drink alcohol while taking this medicine — it raises the risk of liver damage."
- "Tell your nurse or doctor if you feel nausea, belly pain, or notice yellow skin."

---

#### NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Ketorolac, Naproxen)

**What they are for:** Pain, fever, and inflammation.

*In simple words: NSAIDs fight swelling and pain, but they can irritate the stomach and stress the kidneys.*

**Before giving — check:**
- Blood pressure (NSAIDs can raise it).
- Any history of stomach ulcers, GI bleeding, or kidney problems?
- Is the patient on blood thinners? NSAIDs increase bleeding risk.
- Check kidney labs (BUN, creatinine) if available.
- Allergies — some patients allergic to aspirin are also allergic to other NSAIDs.

**After giving — watch for:**
- Pain or inflammation improvement.
- Stomach upset, heartburn, nausea, or dark/tarry stools (sign of GI bleed).
- Decreased urine output or rising creatinine (kidney stress).
- Bruising or unusual bleeding.

**When to hold and notify the provider:**
- Signs of GI bleeding (black tarry stools, vomiting blood or "coffee ground" material).
- Kidney function worsening (creatinine rising, urine output dropping).
- Patient is about to have surgery (increased bleeding risk).

**Key labs:** BUN, creatinine (kidneys), CBC/hemoglobin (for bleeding).

**Patient teaching:**
- "Take with food or a full glass of water to protect your stomach."
- "Tell your nurse right away if you see blood in your stool or vomit, or if your stool looks black and tarry."
- "Do not take other NSAIDs or aspirin at the same time unless your doctor says so."
- "Drink plenty of water to help protect your kidneys."

---

#### Opioids (Morphine, Hydromorphone, Oxycodone, Fentanyl)

**What they are for:** Moderate to severe pain.

*In simple words: Opioids are strong pain medicines. The biggest danger is that they can slow breathing too much.*

**Before giving — check:**
- **Respiratory rate: Hold if less than 12 breaths per minute** (or per your facility policy).
- **Sedation level: Hold if the patient is too drowsy to stay awake during conversation** (use your facility's sedation scale).
- Blood pressure — opioids can cause hypotension.
- Pain level — assess using a pain scale.
- Is the patient opioid-naïve (never or rarely used opioids)? They are more sensitive to side effects.
- Allergies.
- Check if the patient has taken other sedating medicines (benzodiazepines, muscle relaxants, antihistamines) — these add up.

**After giving — watch for:**
- Pain relief (reassess in 15–30 min for IV, 30–60 min for oral).
- **Respiratory depression** — this is the most dangerous side effect. Count the breathing rate.
- Excessive sedation — the patient becomes harder and harder to wake up.
- Nausea, vomiting, constipation.
- Hypotension (low blood pressure), especially when the patient stands up.
- Urinary retention (patient cannot urinate).

**When to hold and notify the provider:**
- Respiratory rate below 12 (or per facility policy).
- Sedation score too high — patient is very difficult to arouse.
- Systolic blood pressure below 90 (or per facility policy).
- New confusion or change in level of consciousness.
- Signs of allergic reaction (rash, itching, swelling, trouble breathing).

**Naloxone (Narcan) — the rescue drug:**
- Naloxone is an **opioid antagonist**. It reverses opioid effects quickly.
- Given if the patient has severe respiratory depression from opioids.
- **⚠️ Important: Naloxone can wear off BEFORE the opioid does.** The opioid may still be active in the body after the naloxone stops working.
  - This means the patient can slip back into respiratory depression after naloxone wears off.
  - **Keep monitoring** respiratory rate, sedation, and oxygen levels closely — even after the patient looks better.
  - The patient may need a second dose or a continuous infusion of naloxone.
- After giving naloxone, expect the patient may suddenly feel pain and may be agitated or nauseated.

**Key labs:** No routine labs needed for single doses, but monitor oxygen saturation continuously.

**Patient teaching:**
- "This medicine may make you sleepy, dizzy, or nauseated. That is expected."
- "Do not get up without help — you could fall."
- "Tell your nurse right away if you feel very dizzy, have trouble breathing, or cannot urinate."
- "Do not drink alcohol or take sleeping pills with this medicine."
- "Constipation is very common with opioids — eat fiber, drink water, and ask about a stool softener."

#### Antiemetics (Ondansetron / Zofran)

**What they are for:** Nausea and vomiting.

*In simple words: Antiemetics stop the "vomiting signal" in the brain so the patient feels less sick.*

**Before giving — check:**
- Why is the patient nauseated? (Post-surgery? Medication side effect? Bowel problem?)
- Check the heart rhythm if the patient has a history of heart problems — ondansetron can affect the QT interval.
- Check for constipation — ondansetron can make it worse.
- Allergies.

**After giving — watch for:**
- Relief of nausea and vomiting.
- Headache or dizziness (common side effects).
- Constipation.
- In at-risk patients: monitor for irregular heartbeat.

**When to hold and notify the provider:**
- Patient has a known prolonged QT interval or is taking other QT-prolonging drugs — notify the provider before giving.
- Severe constipation or signs of bowel obstruction (no bowel sounds, distended abdomen).

**Key labs:** Electrolytes (low potassium or magnesium can worsen QT risk).

**Patient teaching:**
- "This medicine helps your nausea. It may cause a headache or constipation."
- "Tell your nurse if the nausea does not get better, or if you feel your heart beating strangely."

---

#### Acid-Reducing Drugs (Pantoprazole / Protonix)

**What they are for:** Reflux (GERD) or stress ulcer prevention.

*In simple words: These drugs lower the acid in the stomach so it does not damage the stomach lining.*

- Usually given once or twice daily.
- IV pantoprazole is common in hospital patients who cannot eat.
- Long-term use can lower magnesium and increase fracture risk, but this is less relevant for short hospital stays.
- Give IV push slowly (over at least 2 minutes) or as directed.

---

#### Anticoagulants (Heparin, Enoxaparin / Lovenox, Warfarin)

**What they are for:** Preventing or treating blood clots (deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, atrial fibrillation).

*In simple words: Anticoagulants make the blood take longer to clot. This helps prevent dangerous clots, but it also means the patient can bleed more easily.*

**Before giving — check:**
- **Key labs:**
  - **Heparin (IV drip):** aPTT (activated partial thromboplastin time). The target range depends on the protocol.
  - **Enoxaparin (SubQ):** anti-Xa level (not always checked, but may be ordered).
  - **Warfarin (oral):** INR (International Normalized Ratio). Therapeutic range is usually 2–3 for most conditions.
- Platelet count — watch for heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT). If platelets are dropping, notify the provider.
- Check for active bleeding, recent surgery, or fall risk.
- Blood pressure.
- Allergies.

**After giving — watch for:**
- **Signs of bleeding** — this is the most important thing to monitor:
  - Easy bruising or bruises getting bigger.
  - Blood in urine (pink or red urine) or blood in stool (dark tarry stool or bright red blood).
  - Bleeding gums when brushing teeth.
  - Prolonged bleeding from cuts, IV sites, or wounds.
  - Vomiting blood or "coffee ground" emesis.
  - Severe headache or changes in consciousness (could be bleeding in the brain).
- Proper injection site for SubQ heparin or enoxaparin (abdomen, rotate sites).

**When to hold and notify the provider:**
- Active bleeding or signs of internal bleeding.
- Lab values out of range (aPTT too high for heparin, INR above therapeutic range for warfarin).
- Platelet count dropping significantly (concern for HIT).
- Patient is about to go for a procedure or surgery.

**Key labs:** aPTT (for heparin), INR/PT (for warfarin), platelets, hemoglobin/hematocrit (for blood loss).

**Patient teaching:**
- "This medicine helps prevent blood clots, but it also makes you bleed more easily. Be careful with sharp objects."
- "Use a soft toothbrush and an electric razor."
- "Tell your nurse right away if you notice blood in your urine, stool, or vomit, or if you get bruises you cannot explain."
- "Do not take aspirin or ibuprofen unless your doctor says it is okay — these increase bleeding risk."
- For warfarin: "Keep your diet consistent with vitamin K foods (leafy greens). You do not have to avoid them, but do not suddenly eat a lot more or a lot less than usual."

#### Antihypertensives (Metoprolol — a beta-blocker)

**What they are for:** Lowering blood pressure and heart rate. Also used for heart failure, angina, and arrhythmias.

*In simple words: Metoprolol tells the heart to slow down and squeeze less hard, which lowers the blood pressure.*

**Before giving — check:**
- **Heart rate (apical pulse for a full minute): Hold if HR is less than 60 bpm** (or per provider order/facility policy).
- **Systolic blood pressure: Hold if SBP is less than 90–100 mmHg** (or per provider order/facility policy).
- Is the patient dizzy or lightheaded?
- Check for signs of heart failure worsening (new swelling, shortness of breath, weight gain).

**After giving — watch for:**
- Blood pressure and heart rate decrease (this is the expected, desired effect).
- Dizziness or lightheadedness, especially when standing up (orthostatic hypotension).
- Fatigue or weakness.
- Very slow heart rate (bradycardia).
- Signs of bronchospasm in patients with asthma or COPD (beta-blockers can tighten airways).

**When to hold and notify the provider:**
- HR below 60 (or per facility policy).
- SBP below 90–100 (or per facility policy).
- New shortness of breath or wheezing.
- Patient reports feeling faint or very dizzy.

**Key labs:** Not routine for a single dose, but the provider may monitor BMP for potassium and kidney function over time.

**Patient teaching:**
- "Take your pulse before taking this medicine at home. If it is below 60, do not take it and call your doctor."
- "Stand up slowly — this medicine can make you dizzy when you change positions."
- "Do not stop this medicine suddenly. Stopping suddenly can cause dangerous heart problems. Always talk to your doctor first."
- "Tell your doctor if you feel very tired, dizzy, or short of breath."

#### Diuretics (Furosemide / Lasix — a loop diuretic)

**What they are for:** Removing extra fluid from the body. Used for heart failure, edema, and high blood pressure.

*In simple words: Furosemide tells the kidneys to make more urine, which removes extra water and salt from the body. But it also removes potassium, and that can cause problems.*

**Before giving — check:**
- Blood pressure — hold if the patient is hypotensive.
- **Potassium level** — furosemide wastes potassium. A low potassium (hypokalemia) can cause dangerous heart rhythms.
  - Normal potassium: approximately 3.5–5.0 mEq/L.
  - If potassium is low, notify the provider before giving furosemide.
- Kidney function (BUN, creatinine).
- Daily weight and intake/output — these track how well the diuretic is working.
- Is the patient dehydrated? Check skin turgor, mucous membranes, urine color.

**After giving — watch for:**
- Increased urine output (expected and desired).
- Blood pressure changes — watch for drops.
- **Signs of hypokalemia (low potassium):**
  - Muscle weakness or cramps.
  - Fatigue.
  - Irregular heartbeat or palpitations.
  - Constipation.
  - In severe cases: dangerous heart rhythms.
- Dizziness or lightheadedness.
- Hearing changes (ototoxicity — rare but possible with high IV doses given too fast).

**When to hold and notify the provider:**
- Potassium below 3.5 mEq/L (or per facility policy).
- Blood pressure too low.
- Signs of dehydration (very concentrated urine, poor skin turgor, dry mouth, dizziness).
- Patient reports muscle cramps, weakness, or heart palpitations.
- Creatinine is rising (kidney function worsening).

**Key labs:** Potassium, sodium, BUN, creatinine, magnesium (furosemide can also waste magnesium).

**Patient teaching:**
- "This medicine makes you urinate more. That is how it removes extra fluid."
- "Take it in the morning so it does not wake you up at night."
- "Eat potassium-rich foods such as bananas, oranges, potatoes, and spinach (unless your doctor says otherwise)."
- "Weigh yourself every morning at the same time, wearing similar clothing. Report a gain of 2–3 pounds in one day or 5 pounds in one week."
- "Stand up slowly — this medicine can make you dizzy."
- "Tell your nurse right away if you feel muscle cramps, weakness, or your heart beating irregularly."

#### Insulin (High-Alert Medication ⚠️)

**What it is for:** Lowering blood glucose (blood sugar).

*In simple words: Insulin is the "key" that opens the door for sugar to leave the blood and enter the cells. Without enough insulin, sugar stays in the blood and the level goes too high.*

**Types of insulin — onset, peak, and duration:**

| Category | Examples | Onset | Peak | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Rapid-acting** | Lispro (Humalog), Aspart (NovoLog) | 10–15 min | 1–2 hours | 3–5 hours |
| **Short-acting** | Regular (Humulin R, Novolin R) | 30–60 min | 2–4 hours | 5–8 hours |
| **Intermediate-acting** | NPH (Humulin N, Novolin N) | 1–2 hours | 4–12 hours | 12–18 hours |
| **Long-acting** | Glargine (Lantus), Detemir (Levemir) | 1–2 hours | No sharp peak (relatively flat) | Up to 24 hours |

*Think of it this way: Rapid-acting insulin is a sprinter — fast start, quick finish. Long-acting insulin is a marathon runner — slow and steady all day.*

**Why peak time matters:** The peak is when the insulin is working the hardest. That is when the patient is at the **highest risk for hypoglycemia** (low blood sugar). Be extra watchful during peak times.

**Before giving — check:**
- **Blood glucose level.** Follow the sliding scale or provider order.
  - If blood glucose is below 70 mg/dL, **do NOT give insulin.** Treat the hypoglycemia first and notify the provider.
- What type of insulin is ordered? Match it to the correct timing (before meals for rapid, at bedtime for long-acting, etc.).
- When did the patient last eat or when will they eat? Rapid-acting insulin given without food can cause dangerous hypoglycemia.
- Check the insulin bottle: correct type, not expired, correct appearance (clear vs. cloudy).
  - Regular, lispro, aspart, glargine, and detemir should be **clear**.
  - NPH should be **cloudy** (gently roll the vial to mix — do not shake).
- Allergies.

**After giving — watch for:**
- Blood glucose going down (the desired effect).
- **Signs of hypoglycemia** (blood sugar dropping too low — below 70 mg/dL):
  - Sweating, shakiness, trembling.
  - Hunger.
  - Fast heartbeat.
  - Headache, dizziness.
  - Confusion, irritability, difficulty concentrating.
  - Weakness, pale skin.
  - In severe cases: seizures, loss of consciousness.

**When to hold and notify the provider:**
- Blood glucose below 70 mg/dL — treat the hypoglycemia, do NOT give insulin.
- Patient has not eaten and rapid-acting or short-acting insulin is ordered with meals.
- Blood glucose is dropping quickly even though it is still above 70.
- Patient is NPO (nothing by mouth) — confirm the insulin order with the provider.

**Treating hypoglycemia — the Rule of 15:**

1. Give **15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate** (examples: 4 oz juice, 4 oz regular soda, 3–4 glucose tablets, 1 tablespoon of honey).
2. Wait **15 minutes**.
3. **Recheck** the blood glucose.
4. If still below 70 mg/dL, repeat: give another 15 g of carbs and recheck in 15 min.
5. Once blood glucose is above 70, give a small snack with protein and carbs if the next meal is more than an hour away.
6. **Notify the provider** and document everything.

*Think of it this way: 15-15-15. Give 15 grams, wait 15 minutes, recheck. Repeat if needed.*

**Mixing insulin — if required:**
- The usual rule is **clear before cloudy** (draw up the clear insulin first, then the cloudy NPH).
- **Never mix long-acting insulin** (glargine, detemir) with any other insulin in the same syringe.

**Key labs:** Blood glucose (fingerstick and/or lab value), HbA1c (shows average blood sugar over the past 2–3 months — normal is below about 5.7%, target for diabetics is usually below 7%).

**Patient teaching:**
- "Check your blood sugar as your doctor ordered. Know your target range."
- "Always carry a fast-acting sugar with you (juice box, glucose tablets, candy) in case your blood sugar drops."
- "Signs of low blood sugar include shaking, sweating, hunger, dizziness, and confusion. If you feel these, check your sugar and eat something sweet right away."
- "Do not skip meals after taking your insulin."
- "Store unopened insulin in the refrigerator. The vial you are currently using can be at room temperature for up to 28 days (check the label)."
- "Rotate your injection sites to prevent lumps under the skin."
- "Wear a medical ID bracelet that says you have diabetes."

### Clinical Judgement with Medications

(Clinical judgment means using patient data to decide what is safest before, during, and after giving a drug.)

*In simple words: Every time you give a medicine, you are a detective. You gather clues, decide if it is safe, act, and then check what happened.*

Use this four-step thinking for every medication:

1. **Notice** the key data: vital signs, pain, labs, allergies, symptoms, and the reason the drug is ordered.
2. **Interpret** whether the drug is indicated, safe, and likely to help right now.
3. **Respond** by giving, holding, clarifying with the provider, or reporting a concern.
4. **Evaluate** whether the medication improved the problem or caused a new one.

**Common red flags after any medication:**
- New confusion or change in mental status.
- Low blood pressure or a sudden drop in blood pressure.
- Respiratory rate below 12 or labored breathing.
- New rash, hives, or swelling (possible allergic reaction).
- Bleeding that is new or worse than before.
- Very abnormal lab results.
- Patient says something feels "wrong" — always take this seriously.

### Medication Errors — Classroom Exercise

(Medication errors are preventable mistakes that can happen during prescribing, transcribing, dispensing, giving, or monitoring.)

- Common causes include interruptions, look-alike or sound-alike drug names, poor communication, unreadable orders, and dose calculation mistakes.
- **Decimal point errors are dangerous.** Use a **leading zero** for doses less than 1 (write 0.5, not .5). Do **not** use a trailing zero (write 5, not 5.0).
- Barcode scanning and a quiet med pass routine help reduce errors.
- If an error happens:
  1. First, assess the patient — are they okay right now?
  2. Then follow your facility's reporting and notification policy.
- Be honest and accurate. The goal is patient safety and prevention of future harm. Errors are learning opportunities, not reasons to hide.

### Analgesics

(Analgesics reduce pain, but the safest choice depends on the type and severity of pain.)

#### Nonopioid analgesics

- **Acetaminophen** helps pain and fever but does not reduce inflammation well.
  - ⚠️ Max dose: 3–4 g/day for most adults. Lower with liver disease.
  - Always add up ALL sources of acetaminophen (including combination drugs).
- **NSAIDs** reduce pain, fever, and inflammation.
  - NSAIDs can raise bleeding risk and can harm the stomach or kidneys.
  - See the NSAID safety section above for full details.

#### Opioid analgesics

- Opioids are used for more severe pain.
- They can cause **respiratory depression, sedation, constipation, nausea, urinary retention, and hypotension**.
- The nurse should assess pain, respiratory rate, sedation level, and blood pressure **before and after** giving them.
- **Hold if respiratory rate is below 12** or sedation level is too high.
- **Naloxone (Narcan)** reverses opioid effects if severe respiratory depression occurs.
  - ⚠️ **Naloxone can wear off before the opioid does.** Continue monitoring the patient closely even after giving naloxone. The patient could stop breathing again once the naloxone wears off. They may need repeat doses or a continuous naloxone drip.
- Nonpharmacologic comfort measures and multimodal pain control (using more than one type of pain relief) can lower total opioid need and reduce side effects.

### Pain Case Study

(Pain questions usually test assessment, safety, and reassessment.)

- Assess the pain: location, quality, intensity (use a scale), timing, and what makes it better or worse.
- Match the plan to the situation. Severe acute postoperative pain and chronic arthritis pain do not always need the same treatment.
- Reassess after treatment:
  - IV pain medicine: reassess in about **15–30 minutes**.
  - Oral pain medicine: reassess in about **30–60 minutes**.
- Watch for sedation, low breathing rate, or poor pain control.
- Combine medication with nonpharmacologic methods such as repositioning, splinting, ice or heat when appropriate, relaxation techniques, and patient education.

### Quick-Reference Summary: Before You Give ANY Medication

Use this checklist every single time:

- ✅ Right patient? (Check two identifiers.)
- ✅ Right drug, dose, route, time?
- ✅ Any allergies?
- ✅ Do I know why this patient is getting this drug?
- ✅ Have I checked the hold parameters? (HR, BP, labs, sedation, glucose, etc.)
- ✅ Is this safe RIGHT NOW based on how the patient looks and feels?
- ✅ Have I explained the medicine to the patient?
- ✅ After giving it — did I reassess and document?

*You've got this. Take it one drug at a time, one patient at a time. 💛*
