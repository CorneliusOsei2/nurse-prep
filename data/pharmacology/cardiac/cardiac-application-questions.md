## Question 1 — Digoxin Toxicity Recognition

A nurse is caring for a patient who takes digoxin daily. The patient says, "I've been feeling nauseous all morning and everything has a yellowish tint." The patient's heart rate is 52 bpm. Which action should the nurse take FIRST?

A. Give the scheduled dose of digoxin and monitor the patient closely
B. Hold the digoxin dose and notify the health care provider
C. Administer an antiemetic for the nausea
D. Encourage the patient to eat potassium-rich foods

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — This patient has THREE classic signs of digoxin toxicity: nausea (GI symptom), yellow-tinged vision (visual changes), and bradycardia (HR 52, which is below 60). The nurse should hold the drug and notify the provider right away. The provider will likely order a digoxin level and potassium level.
- **A is wrong** — You should NEVER give digoxin when the heart rate is below 60 bpm. Plus, this patient is showing signs of toxicity. Giving more drug would be dangerous!
- **C is wrong** — While the patient is nauseous, treating the nausea does not address the underlying problem (toxicity). We need to stop the drug and notify the provider first.
- **D is wrong** — Potassium-rich foods are appropriate general teaching for patients on digoxin, but this is not the priority right now. The patient needs the drug held and the provider notified immediately.

> **Test-taking tip:** When you see nausea + visual changes + slow heart rate in a patient on digoxin, think toxicity immediately. The first action is to hold the drug and notify the provider.

## Question 2 — ACE Inhibitor Angioedema Emergency

A patient who has been taking lisinopril for 2 years arrives at the emergency department with swelling of the lips, tongue, and difficulty breathing. What is the nurse's PRIORITY action?

A. Place the patient in a supine position
B. Administer the next scheduled dose of lisinopril
C. Discontinue the lisinopril and prepare to manage the airway
D. Apply ice packs to the swollen areas to reduce inflammation

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:**
- **C is correct** 🎯 — This is angioedema, a life-threatening side effect of ACE inhibitors. The swelling of the tongue and lips can close off the airway. The nurse must STOP the causative drug immediately and prepare to maintain the airway (have suction and intubation supplies ready, be prepared to give epinephrine). This is an emergency!
- **A is wrong** — Lying flat could actually make it harder to breathe if the tongue is swelling. An upright position helps keep the airway open.
- **B is wrong** — This is the drug CAUSING the problem! Giving more lisinopril could make the swelling worse and be fatal.
- **D is wrong** — Ice packs will not stop angioedema. This is not simple swelling from an injury — it is a serious drug reaction that needs immediate medical treatment. This wastes precious time.

> **Test-taking tip:** Angioedema can happen even after years of taking an ACE inhibitor. If you see swollen lips, tongue, or throat + an ACE inhibitor in the question, the answer always involves stopping the drug and managing the airway.

## Question 3 — Hold-or-Give Scenario

A patient is prescribed metoprolol (a beta-blocker) and diltiazem. Before the morning medication pass, the nurse assesses the patient and finds: heart rate 54 bpm, blood pressure 88/56 mmHg. Which action should the nurse take?

A. Give both medications as prescribed
B. Give the metoprolol but hold the diltiazem
C. Hold both medications and notify the health care provider
D. Give the diltiazem but hold the metoprolol

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:**
- **C is correct** 🎯 — Both metoprolol (beta-blocker) and diltiazem (non-DHP CCB) lower heart rate AND blood pressure. This patient's HR is 54 (below 60) and BP is 88/56 (systolic below 90). BOTH medications would make these dangerously low numbers even worse. Hold BOTH and notify the provider.
- **A is wrong** — Giving both drugs when the HR and BP are already dangerously low could cause severe bradycardia, hypotension, or cardiac arrest. This would harm the patient.
- **B is wrong** — You should not give either drug. The heart rate is below 60 (hold parameter for beta-blockers too) and the BP is below 90.
- **D is wrong** — Same reasoning — neither drug should be given with these vital signs. Both drugs lower HR and BP.

> **Test-taking tip:** When a patient is on TWO drugs that both lower heart rate or blood pressure, and the vital signs are already low — hold BOTH. Do not try to pick which one to give. Safety first!

## Question 4 — Nitroglycerin Patient Teaching

A nurse is teaching a patient about sublingual nitroglycerin for chest pain. Which statement by the patient shows they understand the teaching correctly?

A. "I should take all 3 tablets at once when I have chest pain."
B. "If my chest pain continues after 3 tablets taken 5 minutes apart, I should call 911."
C. "I should stand up when taking my nitroglycerin so it works faster."
D. "I can keep my nitroglycerin in my bathroom medicine cabinet for easy access."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — This is the correct protocol! Take one tablet, wait 5 minutes. If pain continues, take a second. Wait 5 more minutes. If still in pain, take a third. After 3 tablets (15 minutes total) with no relief → call 911. This patient understands!
- **A is wrong** — You do NOT take all 3 at once! One at a time, 5 minutes apart. Taking all 3 at once could cause a dangerous drop in blood pressure.
- **C is wrong** — The patient should SIT or LIE DOWN when taking nitroglycerin. Standing up increases the risk of dizziness, lightheadedness, and falling because nitroglycerin lowers blood pressure.
- **D is wrong** — The bathroom has too much heat and humidity, which makes nitroglycerin lose its potency. It should be stored in the original dark glass bottle, away from light, heat, and moisture.

## Question 5 — PDE5 Inhibitor Interaction

A 62-year-old male patient arrives at the emergency department with chest pain. The provider orders sublingual nitroglycerin. Before administering, the patient mentions he took sildenafil (Viagra) last night. What should the nurse do?

A. Give the nitroglycerin as ordered — the sildenafil was taken hours ago
B. Give half the nitroglycerin dose to be safe
C. Hold the nitroglycerin and notify the provider immediately
D. Give the nitroglycerin and monitor blood pressure every 2 minutes

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:**
- **C is correct** 🎯 — Sildenafil (Viagra) was taken within the last 24 hours. Nitroglycerin + PDE5 inhibitors = SEVERE, potentially life-threatening hypotension. The nurse must hold the nitroglycerin and notify the provider so an alternative pain management plan can be made.
- **A is wrong** — "Hours ago" is not long enough. Sildenafil's effects last up to 24 hours (tadalafil/Cialis lasts up to 48 hours). This combination is still dangerous.
- **B is wrong** — There is no safe reduced dose. The interaction is a contraindication — meaning do NOT give nitroglycerin at all when a PDE5 inhibitor has been used recently.
- **D is wrong** — Giving the drug and just monitoring more closely does not prevent the dangerous interaction. The blood pressure could crash, and monitoring alone cannot fix that.

> **Test-taking tip:** Always ask about PDE5 inhibitors (Viagra, Cialis) before giving nitroglycerin. This question comes up often. "Last night" or "yesterday" = still too recent. Hold the NTG!

## Question 6 — Statin Myopathy Recognition

A patient taking simvastatin calls the clinic and reports severe muscle pain in both legs and that their urine looks "dark like cola." What should the nurse advise?

A. "This is a normal side effect. Take ibuprofen for the pain."
B. "You need to come in right away. Stop taking the simvastatin and come to the clinic or ER."
C. "Increase your fluid intake and rest for a few days."
D. "Switch to taking your simvastatin in the morning instead of at night."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — Severe muscle pain + dark cola-colored urine in a patient on a statin = classic signs of **rhabdomyolysis** (muscle breakdown). This is a medical emergency. The muscle contents can flood the kidneys and cause kidney failure. The drug must be stopped and the patient needs immediate evaluation, CK levels, and kidney function tests.
- **A is wrong** — This is NOT normal. Also, NSAIDs like ibuprofen could worsen kidney damage. Severe muscle pain with dark urine on a statin is a red flag — never dismiss it.
- **C is wrong** — While hydration is helpful, this patient needs medical evaluation NOW. Rhabdomyolysis can cause acute kidney failure. Rest and fluids at home are not enough.
- **D is wrong** — Changing the time of day does not address rhabdomyolysis. This is not about timing — it is about a dangerous reaction that requires stopping the drug and getting medical help.

> **Test-taking tip:** Muscle pain + dark urine + statin = rhabdomyolysis. Stop the drug, get to the hospital. The key lab is CK (creatine kinase).

## Question 7 — Lab Interpretation: Potassium and Digoxin

A nurse reviews morning labs for a patient on digoxin and furosemide (Lasix). The results show: Digoxin level 1.8 ng/mL, Potassium 2.9 mEq/L. Which is the nurse's priority concern?

A. The digoxin level is too high
B. The low potassium puts the patient at risk for digoxin toxicity
C. The furosemide dose needs to be increased
D. The patient needs a higher dose of digoxin

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — The digoxin level of 1.8 is technically within the therapeutic range (0.5–2.0 ng/mL), but the potassium of 2.9 is dangerously low (normal: 3.5–5.0). Low potassium increases digoxin's effect on the heart, putting the patient at risk for toxicity EVEN with a "normal" digoxin level. This is the priority concern!
- **A is wrong** — The digoxin level of 1.8 is within the therapeutic range (0.5–2.0). It is on the higher end, but not technically "too high." However, combined with the low potassium, even this level becomes dangerous.
- **C is wrong** — Furosemide is a potassium-wasting diuretic — it is the reason the potassium is low! Increasing the dose would make the potassium even lower and increase toxicity risk.
- **D is wrong** — Increasing the digoxin dose when potassium is low would be very dangerous. The patient is already at increased toxicity risk.

> *Think of it this way:* The digoxin level looks "okay" on paper, but the low potassium changes the picture completely. Always look at potassium AND digoxin level together.

## Question 8 — Priority / First Action

A nurse is caring for four patients. Which patient should the nurse see FIRST?

A. A patient on amlodipine who reports ankle swelling
B. A patient on atorvastatin who reports severe muscle pain and dark urine
C. A patient on lisinopril who has a dry cough
D. A patient on simvastatin whose cholesterol has not decreased after 2 weeks

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — Severe muscle pain + dark urine in a patient on a statin = possible rhabdomyolysis. This can lead to kidney failure and is a medical emergency. This patient needs immediate attention!
- **A is wrong** — Ankle swelling (peripheral edema) is a common, expected side effect of amlodipine. It is not an emergency. The nurse should address it, but it is not the priority.
- **C is wrong** — A dry cough is the classic side effect of ACE inhibitors. It is annoying but not dangerous. It can be addressed at a routine time.
- **D is wrong** — Statins take time to work (usually 4–6 weeks to see full effect). Two weeks is too early to be concerned about lack of response. This is not urgent.

> **Test-taking tip:** For priority questions, look for the most life-threatening situation. Ankle swelling and dry cough are expected side effects. Not responding at 2 weeks is too early to worry. But muscle pain + dark urine = emergency!

## Question 9 — SATA (Select All That Apply)

A nurse is providing discharge teaching to a patient prescribed enalapril (an ACE inhibitor). Which instructions should the nurse include? **Select all that apply.**

A. "Rise slowly from a sitting or lying position."
B. "You may use salt substitutes to flavor your food."
C. "Report any swelling of your face, lips, or tongue immediately."
D. "A dry cough may occur — report it, but do not stop the medication on your own."
E. "Keep all scheduled lab appointments for blood work."
F. "This medication is safe to continue if you become pregnant."

**Correct Answers: A, C, D, E**

**Explanation:**
- **A is correct** ✅ — ACE inhibitors can cause orthostatic hypotension (blood pressure drops when standing up), especially after the first dose. Rising slowly prevents dizziness and falls.
- **B is wrong** ❌ — Salt substitutes contain potassium! ACE inhibitors already cause the body to retain potassium. Adding more potassium from salt substitutes can cause dangerously high potassium levels (hyperkalemia).
- **C is correct** ✅ — This describes angioedema, a rare but life-threatening emergency. Patients must know to report this immediately and go to the ER.
- **D is correct** ✅ — The dry cough is a known side effect that should be reported so the provider can decide if a medication change is needed. But the patient should not stop the drug on their own.
- **E is correct** ✅ — Patients on ACE inhibitors need regular monitoring of potassium and kidney function (creatinine, BUN).
- **F is wrong** ❌ — ACE inhibitors are CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy. They can cause birth defects and fetal kidney damage. A patient who becomes pregnant must stop the drug and contact their provider immediately.

> **Test-taking tip for SATA:** Evaluate each option independently — ask "Is this TRUE and would I teach this?" for each one. Do not let one answer influence another.

## Question 10 — Nitroglycerin Storage Teaching

A patient with chronic angina asks the nurse how to store their nitroglycerin tablets at home. Which response by the nurse is MOST appropriate?

A. "Keep them in any pill organizer so you remember to take them."
B. "Store them in the original dark glass bottle, away from light, heat, and moisture."
C. "Refrigerate them to keep them fresh longer."
D. "Transfer them to a clear plastic bag so you can easily see how many you have left."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — Nitroglycerin is very sensitive to light, heat, and moisture. It must stay in the original dark glass container to maintain its potency. Patients should replace their supply every 6 months.
- **A is wrong** — Pill organizers expose the tablets to light and air, which breaks down the medication. Nitroglycerin needs the protection of its dark glass bottle.
- **C is wrong** — Refrigeration is not necessary and the moisture inside a refrigerator could actually damage the tablets. Room temperature in the dark bottle is correct.
- **D is wrong** — A clear plastic bag exposes the tablets to light, and plastic can absorb some of the medication. Always use the original dark glass container.

## Question 11 — Calcium Channel Blocker and Heart Failure

A patient with heart failure is receiving verapamil. The nurse assesses the patient and finds: weight gain of 3 pounds overnight, crackles in the lungs, and increased shortness of breath. What should the nurse do?

A. Continue the medication and elevate the head of the bed
B. Hold the verapamil and notify the health care provider
C. Give an extra dose of verapamil to lower the blood pressure
D. Encourage the patient to limit fluid intake and reassess in 4 hours

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — These are signs of worsening heart failure: sudden weight gain (fluid retention), crackles (fluid in the lungs), and increased shortness of breath. Non-DHP calcium channel blockers like verapamil can WORSEN heart failure because they reduce the heart's ability to contract. The nurse should hold the drug and notify the provider immediately.
- **A is wrong** — Elevating the head of bed helps with breathing, but continuing the verapamil ignores the fact that it may be causing the worsening. The medication needs to be addressed.
- **C is wrong** — Giving MORE of a drug that is potentially worsening heart failure is dangerous. More verapamil = weaker heart contractions = worse fluid overload.
- **D is wrong** — Waiting 4 hours delays necessary treatment. Weight gain of 3 pounds overnight with respiratory symptoms needs immediate attention, not a "wait and see" approach.

## Question 12 — Amiodarone Side Effects

A patient who has been taking amiodarone for 6 months reports a new dry cough and shortness of breath when walking. Which action by the nurse is MOST important?

A. Reassure the patient that cough is a common side effect of many medications
B. Advise the patient to use cough drops and continue taking the medication
C. Report the symptoms to the provider immediately — this could indicate pulmonary toxicity
D. Suggest the patient increase physical activity gradually

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:**
- **C is correct** 🎯 — Pulmonary toxicity is the MOST DANGEROUS side effect of amiodarone. A new cough and shortness of breath with exertion could be early signs of pulmonary fibrosis (scarring of the lungs). This needs immediate evaluation with chest X-ray and pulmonary function tests. Delayed recognition can lead to permanent lung damage.
- **A is wrong** — While cough IS common with some drugs (like ACE inhibitors), a cough with amiodarone + shortness of breath is a red flag for lung toxicity. Do not dismiss it!
- **B is wrong** — Cough drops will not help if the lungs are being damaged. Continuing the medication without evaluation could lead to irreversible lung damage.
- **D is wrong** — If the patient is short of breath from pulmonary toxicity, increasing physical activity would make it worse and is not helpful. The patient needs medical evaluation, not an exercise plan.

> *Think of it this way:* Amiodarone + cough + shortness of breath = think LUNGS. Report it immediately.

## Question 13 — SATA: Digoxin Administration

Before administering digoxin, which nursing assessments should be performed? **Select all that apply.**

A. Check the apical pulse for one full minute
B. Review the most recent digoxin level
C. Check the serum potassium level
D. Assess the patient's pupil size
E. Check kidney function (creatinine)
F. Ask about recent grapefruit intake

**Correct Answers: A, B, C, E**

**Explanation:**
- **A is correct** ✅ — The apical pulse must be checked for a full 60 seconds. Hold if below 60 bpm in adults. This is the most important pre-administration check.
- **B is correct** ✅ — The digoxin level should be within the therapeutic range (0.5–2.0 ng/mL). Giving digoxin when the level is already high increases toxicity risk.
- **C is correct** ✅ — Low potassium increases digoxin toxicity risk. The nurse must know the potassium level.
- **D is wrong** ❌ — Pupil assessment is not part of routine digoxin administration checks. While digoxin toxicity can cause visual changes (halos), we do not check pupils before each dose.
- **E is correct** ✅ — Digoxin is excreted (removed) by the kidneys. If kidney function is poor, digoxin builds up in the body → toxicity risk. Creatinine and BUN help assess kidney function.
- **F is wrong** ❌ — Grapefruit interaction is associated with statins and some calcium channel blockers, not digoxin. This is a good distractor, but it does not apply here.

## Question 14 — Patient Teaching: ACE Inhibitor

A patient newly prescribed lisinopril asks, "Why do I need to take the first dose at bedtime?" Which is the BEST response by the nurse?

A. "The medication works better when you are sleeping."
B. "The first dose can cause a drop in blood pressure, and lying down reduces the risk of falling."
C. "Taking it at night prevents the dry cough side effect."
D. "All blood pressure medications must be taken at night."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — ACE inhibitors can cause **first-dose hypotension** — a significant drop in blood pressure after the very first dose. Taking it at bedtime means the patient is already lying down, which reduces the risk of dizziness, lightheadedness, and falls.
- **A is wrong** — The medication does not "work better" at night. The timing recommendation is about safety (preventing falls from hypotension), not effectiveness.
- **C is wrong** — Taking lisinopril at bedtime does not prevent the dry cough. The cough can happen regardless of when the drug is taken. It is caused by increased bradykinin, not timing.
- **D is wrong** — Not all blood pressure medications need to be taken at night. The bedtime recommendation is specific to the FIRST DOSE of ACE inhibitors because of first-dose hypotension.

## Question 15 — Potassium Level with ACE Inhibitor

A patient on enalapril has the following lab results: Potassium 5.6 mEq/L, Creatinine 1.0 mg/dL, BUN 15 mg/dL. Which finding requires immediate follow-up?

A. Creatinine 1.0 mg/dL
B. BUN 15 mg/dL
C. Potassium 5.6 mEq/L
D. All values are within normal limits

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:**
- **C is correct** 🎯 — Normal potassium is 3.5–5.0 mEq/L. A level of 5.6 is HIGH (hyperkalemia). ACE inhibitors cause potassium retention, so this is a known risk. Hyperkalemia can cause dangerous cardiac arrhythmias — potentially fatal! The nurse should hold the enalapril and notify the provider.
- **A is wrong** — Creatinine 1.0 mg/dL is within the normal range (0.6–1.2). This kidney function is fine.
- **B is wrong** — BUN 15 mg/dL is within the normal range (7–20). No concerns here.
- **D is wrong** — The potassium is NOT normal. It is above 5.0 mEq/L, which is elevated and requires follow-up.

> *Think of it this way:* ACE inhibitors + high potassium = danger. Always check potassium with these drugs.

## Question 16 — Digoxin and Amiodarone Interaction

A patient currently taking digoxin is now prescribed amiodarone. Which action by the nurse is MOST important?

A. Monitor for signs of amiodarone pulmonary toxicity
B. Verify whether the digoxin dose has been reduced

D. Schedule an eye exam for the patient

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — Amiodarone significantly increases digoxin levels (can nearly double them!). When amiodarone is added, the digoxin dose typically needs to be reduced by about 50%. If the dose was not adjusted, the patient is at high risk for digoxin toxicity. The nurse should verify this was done.
- **A is wrong** — While pulmonary toxicity monitoring IS important with amiodarone, the most immediate concern when ADDING amiodarone to digoxin is the drug interaction that increases digoxin levels.
- **C is wrong** — Grapefruit interaction is relevant for statins and some CCBs, not for this drug combination.
- **D is wrong** — Eye exams are important for long-term amiodarone use (corneal deposits), but this is not the most important action when the drug is first prescribed alongside digoxin. The immediate concern is the interaction.

## Question 17 — Nitrate-Free Period

A patient wearing a nitroglycerin transdermal patch asks why they need to remove it at night. What is the BEST explanation?

A. "Wearing it at night can cause nightmares."
B. "Your body needs a drug-free period so the medication keeps working effectively."
C. "The patch can cause skin burns if worn while sleeping."
D. "Nitroglycerin only works during the daytime."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — If nitroglycerin is given continuously without a break, the body develops **tolerance** (the drug stops working as well). A 10–12 hour "nitrate-free period" — usually overnight — prevents tolerance and keeps the medication effective.
- **A is wrong** — Nitroglycerin does not cause nightmares. This is not a known side effect.
- **C is wrong** — The patch does not cause burns during sleep. While skin irritation can occur at the application site, it is not related to nighttime use.
- **D is wrong** — Nitroglycerin works regardless of the time of day. The patch removal is about preventing tolerance, not about daytime effectiveness.

## Question 18 — Priority: Multiple Cardiac Medications

A nurse administers morning medications to a patient who takes digoxin 0.125 mg daily, lisinopril 10 mg daily, and furosemide 40 mg daily. Which assessment finding should the nurse report to the provider FIRST?

**Explanation:**

- **A is wrong** — BP of 138/82 is slightly elevated but not an emergency. The lisinopril is treating this.

## Question 19 — Patient Teaching: Statin Timing

A patient is prescribed simvastatin. Which instruction about timing is MOST appropriate?

A. "Take it first thing in the morning with breakfast."
B. "Take it at bedtime."
C. "Take it at noon with your largest meal."
D. "It does not matter when you take it."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — Simvastatin is a short-acting statin. The liver produces the most cholesterol at night, so taking simvastatin at bedtime matches the drug's action to the body's peak cholesterol production. This makes the drug most effective.
- **A is wrong** — Morning dosing is less effective for short-acting statins because the drug's peak effect would not line up with nighttime cholesterol production.
- **C is wrong** — Noon dosing is not recommended for the same reason. The drug should be working when cholesterol production is highest (nighttime).
- **D is wrong** — This would be true for long-acting statins like atorvastatin or rosuvastatin (which can be taken at any time), but NOT for simvastatin. Timing matters for short-acting statins.

## Question 20 — SATA: Nitroglycerin Contraindications

A nurse is about to give sublingual nitroglycerin to a patient with chest pain. Which findings would cause the nurse to HOLD the nitroglycerin? **Select all that apply.**

A. Blood pressure 86/52 mmHg
B. The patient reports taking tadalafil (Cialis) yesterday
C. Heart rate 88 bpm
D. The patient has a headache
E. The patient reports the chest pain started 10 minutes ago
F. The patient has a right ventricular infarction

**Correct Answers: A, B, F**

**Explanation:**
- **A is correct** ✅ — SBP is 86, which is below 90 mmHg. Nitroglycerin lowers blood pressure further. Giving it now could cause dangerous hypotension or shock. HOLD and notify.
- **B is correct** ✅ — Tadalafil (Cialis) is a PDE5 inhibitor. It was taken within the last 48 hours. Combining it with nitroglycerin can cause severe, life-threatening hypotension. HOLD and notify.
- **C is wrong** ❌ — A heart rate of 88 is normal and is NOT a reason to hold nitroglycerin. NTG is held for low blood pressure, not for heart rate (unless the provider specifies otherwise).
- **D is wrong** ❌ — A headache is actually an expected side effect of nitroglycerin (from blood vessel dilation). Having a headache before the dose is not a contraindication.
- **E is wrong** ❌ — Chest pain that started 10 minutes ago is exactly the reason TO give nitroglycerin. This is not a reason to hold it.
- **F is correct** ✅ — Right ventricular infarction (right-sided heart attack) depends on adequate preload (blood returning to the heart). Nitroglycerin reduces preload, which can cause a catastrophic drop in cardiac output and blood pressure. HOLD nitroglycerin in right ventricular infarctions.

> **Test-taking tip for SATA:** For "hold" questions, think about each contraindication separately. Low BP, PDE5 inhibitors, and right ventricular infarction are the big three for NTG.

## Question 21 — Amiodarone Patient Teaching

A nurse is providing discharge teaching to a patient starting amiodarone. Which statement by the patient indicates a NEED for further teaching?

A. "I will wear sunscreen and long sleeves when I go outside."
B. "I will report any new cough or difficulty breathing right away."
C. "I can stop this medication when my heart rhythm feels normal."
D. "I will keep all my follow-up appointments for blood tests and eye exams."

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:**
- **C is correct** 🎯 — This statement is WRONG and shows the patient needs more teaching. Amiodarone should NEVER be stopped on the patient's own without talking to the provider. The drug controls arrhythmias — stopping it suddenly could cause dangerous, life-threatening heart rhythms to return. Also, amiodarone has a very long half-life (stays in the body for weeks to months), so changes must be managed carefully by the provider.
- **A is wrong (this answer is correct teaching)** — Amiodarone causes severe photosensitivity. Wearing sunscreen and protective clothing is excellent self-care.
- **B is wrong (this answer is correct teaching)** — Cough and difficulty breathing could indicate pulmonary toxicity — the most dangerous side effect. Reporting these immediately is exactly right.
- **D is wrong (this answer is correct teaching)** — Amiodarone requires regular monitoring of thyroid, liver, lungs, and eyes. Keeping all appointments is essential.

> **Test-taking tip:** "Need for further teaching" means the WRONG statement. Look for the answer that shows a misunderstanding. All the other choices are things you WANT the patient to say.

## Question 23 — Warfarin + Amiodarone Interaction

A patient takes warfarin (a blood thinner) and is now starting amiodarone. Which lab value should the nurse monitor MORE closely?

A. Potassium level
B. INR (International Normalized Ratio)
C. Hemoglobin A1C
D. CK (creatine kinase)

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — Amiodarone increases the effect of warfarin, meaning the blood becomes "thinner" (takes longer to clot). The INR measures how long it takes blood to clot. With amiodarone added, the INR can rise dangerously high → increased bleeding risk. The warfarin dose usually needs to be reduced, and INR needs very close monitoring.
- **A is wrong** — While potassium should be monitored with amiodarone (low K+ worsens arrhythmias), the most important drug interaction concern when adding amiodarone to warfarin is the increased anticoagulation effect.
- **C is wrong** — Hemoglobin A1C measures long-term blood sugar control. It is not related to this drug combination.
- **D is wrong** — CK is used to monitor for rhabdomyolysis with statins, not for this amiodarone-warfarin interaction.

## Question 24 — Heart Failure and Medication Selection

A patient with heart failure and a persistent dry cough from lisinopril asks the nurse about alternatives. The provider switches the patient to losartan. Which explanation is BEST?

A. "Losartan is a stronger medication that works better for heart failure."
B. "Losartan works in a similar way but is much less likely to cause the cough."
C. "Losartan is a completely different type of drug with no similar effects."
D. "Losartan cures heart failure, while lisinopril only manages symptoms."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — ARBs (like losartan) work on the same blood pressure system (RAAS) as ACE inhibitors but at a different point. They achieve similar goals (lower BP, protect heart and kidneys) but rarely cause the dry cough because they do not increase bradykinin levels. This is the most common reason for switching from an ACE inhibitor to an ARB.
- **A is wrong** — Losartan is not necessarily "stronger." It is an alternative, not an upgrade. The switch is about tolerability (the cough), not potency.
- **C is wrong** — Losartan and lisinopril actually work on the SAME system (RAAS). They are related drug classes, not completely different.
- **D is wrong** — Neither drug cures heart failure. Both manage and treat it. No current medication "cures" heart failure.

## Question 25 — Comprehensive Safety Scenario

A nurse is caring for a patient who takes the following medications: digoxin 0.125 mg daily, furosemide 40 mg daily, and potassium chloride 20 mEq daily. The morning assessment reveals: heart rate 58 bpm, BP 118/72, potassium 3.4 mEq/L, digoxin level 2.2 ng/mL. The patient reports feeling "a bit nauseous." Which action should the nurse take FIRST?

A. Give all three medications as scheduled
B. Give the furosemide and potassium, but hold the digoxin and notify the provider
C. Hold all medications and call a rapid response
D. Give the digoxin and furosemide, but hold the potassium

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- **B is correct** 🎯 — Let's look at the data:
  - HR is 58 (below 60 — hold parameter for digoxin)
  - Digoxin level is 2.2 (above 2.0 — toxic range)
  - Potassium is 3.4 (slightly below normal — increases toxicity risk)
  - Nausea (early sign of digoxin toxicity)
  - The digoxin MUST be held (HR < 60, level > 2.0, nausea = likely toxicity)
  - The furosemide is still appropriate for its indication
  - The potassium should be given — the level is LOW, and we need to bring it up (especially since low K+ worsens digoxin toxicity)
  - Notify the provider about the findings
- **A is wrong** — Giving digoxin with all these red flags (HR < 60, toxic level, nausea, low K+) could be life-threatening.
- **C is wrong** — While holding digoxin is correct, there is no reason to hold the furosemide and potassium or call a rapid response at this point. The patient is alert and talking — holding the digoxin and notifying the provider is the appropriate first step.
- **D is wrong** — This is backwards! The digoxin should be HELD (it is toxic), and the potassium should be GIVEN (it is low and needs to come up). Giving digoxin and holding potassium would make things worse.

> **Test-taking tip:** When a question gives you multiple medications and multiple data points, match each piece of data to the right medication. Ask: "Is this drug safe to give based on these numbers?" Go through each one systematically.
