# 💊 Cardiac Medications — Flashcards

> **How to use these cards:** Cover the back and try to answer from memory. Then check!
> The "Why it matters" section tells you why this shows up on the NCLEX. 🌟

---

## Card 1

**Front:** What must you ALWAYS check before giving digoxin, and what is the hold parameter?

**Back:**
- Check the **apical pulse for one full minute** (not radial — apical!)
- **Hold if heart rate is below 60 bpm** in an adult
- Also check the digoxin level and potassium level before giving

**Why it matters:** This is one of the most tested safety questions. The NCLEX loves to ask: "What should the nurse do first before giving digoxin?" The answer is always check the apical pulse. Giving digoxin to a patient with a slow heart rate can make it dangerously slow.

---

## Card 2

**Front:** What is the therapeutic range for digoxin? What level means toxicity?

**Back:**
- Therapeutic range: **0.5–2.0 ng/mL**
- Above **2.0 ng/mL** = toxicity risk
- Digoxin has a **narrow therapeutic index** — meaning the helpful dose and the toxic dose are very close together

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX may give you a digoxin level and ask what to do. If it is above 2.0, hold the drug and notify the provider. You need to know this number by heart.

---

## Card 3

**Front:** Name FOUR signs of digoxin toxicity.

**Back:**
1. **Nausea and vomiting** (GI symptoms often appear first)
2. **Yellow-green halos** around lights (visual changes)
3. **Bradycardia** (slow heart rate)
4. **Dysrhythmias** (irregular heart rhythms)

Also: anorexia (loss of appetite), confusion, fatigue

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX will describe a patient on digoxin with these symptoms and ask you to identify the problem. The yellow-green halos are the classic clue — if you see that in a question, think digoxin toxicity immediately!

---

## Card 4

**Front:** Why does LOW potassium (hypokalemia) increase the risk of digoxin toxicity?

**Back:**
- Digoxin and potassium compete for the same binding sites on heart cells
- When potassium is low, digoxin fills MORE of those sites
- More digoxin effect = toxicity even if the digoxin level is "normal"
- *Think of it this way:* Potassium and digoxin are fighting for the same seats. When potassium leaves, digoxin sits in ALL the seats — too much effect!

**Why it matters:** This is a critical connection. The NCLEX loves to combine these two concepts — "A patient takes digoxin AND furosemide (Lasix). The potassium is 2.8 mEq/L. What is the nurse's priority?" Answer: the low potassium puts the patient at risk for digoxin toxicity!

---

## Card 5

**Front:** What is the antidote for severe, life-threatening digoxin toxicity?

**Back:**
- **Digoxin immune Fab (Digibind/DigiFab)**
- Given IV for life-threatening toxicity (dangerous arrhythmias, very high levels)

**Why it matters:** You may not see this antidote often in practice, but the NCLEX may ask about it. Know the name!

---

## Card 6

**Front:** What is the CLASSIC side effect of ACE inhibitors (-pril drugs)?

**Back:**
- **Dry, persistent cough**
- Caused by increased bradykinin in the lungs
- The cough is annoying but not dangerous
- If the patient cannot tolerate the cough, the provider may switch to an **ARB (-sartan)** instead

**Why it matters:** This is probably the most commonly tested ACE inhibitor fact. If a question describes a patient on lisinopril with a new dry cough, that is the ACE inhibitor causing it — not pneumonia, not a cold.

---

## Card 7

**Front:** What is angioedema? Why is it dangerous with ACE inhibitors? What do you do?

**Back:**
- **Angioedema** = sudden, severe swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat
- It is dangerous because it can **block the airway** — the patient cannot breathe!
- **Nursing actions:**
  1. **STOP the drug immediately**
  2. Maintain the airway
  3. Call for emergency help
  4. Be ready to give epinephrine
- Can happen at any time during treatment — even after taking the drug for years

**Why it matters:** This is a life-threatening emergency. The NCLEX tests whether you can recognize it and act quickly. If a patient on an ACE inhibitor has a swollen tongue and difficulty breathing — stop the drug and call for help!

---

## Card 8

**Front:** Compare ACE inhibitors (-pril) vs. ARBs (-sartan) — what are the main differences?

**Back:**
| Feature | ACE Inhibitor | ARB |
|---------|--------------|-----|
| Ending | -pril | -sartan |
| Dry cough | YES (common) | NO (rare) |
| Angioedema risk | Higher | Lower |
| Hyperkalemia risk | Yes | Yes |
| Pregnancy safe? | NO | NO |
| Main switch reason | Cough → switch to ARB | — |

Both protect the heart and kidneys. Both are contraindicated in pregnancy.

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX may ask why a provider switches a patient from lisinopril to losartan. The answer is usually the dry cough. Know that ARBs are the common alternative.

---

## Card 9

**Front:** Which electrolyte do you monitor closely with ACE inhibitors and ARBs? Why?

**Back:**
- **Potassium (K+)**
- ACE inhibitors and ARBs cause the body to **retain potassium** → risk of **hyperkalemia** (high potassium)
- Normal potassium: 3.5–5.0 mEq/L
- Also: Do NOT give potassium supplements or salt substitutes (they contain potassium!)

**Why it matters:** This is the opposite of digoxin! With digoxin, we worry about LOW potassium. With ACE inhibitors and ARBs, we worry about HIGH potassium. The NCLEX tests whether you can keep these straight.

---

## Card 10

**Front:** Compare DHP calcium channel blockers (amlodipine) vs. Non-DHP CCBs (diltiazem, verapamil).

**Back:**
| Feature | DHP (amlodipine) | Non-DHP (diltiazem, verapamil) |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Main target | Blood vessels | Heart AND blood vessels |
| Lowers heart rate? | No (may increase) | YES |
| Hold for HR < 60? | No | YES |
| Peripheral edema? | YES (very common) | Less common |
| Constipation? | No | YES (especially verapamil) |
| Worsens heart failure? | Less likely | YES |

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX expects you to know the difference. A question about holding for a low heart rate with a CCB is about the non-DHP type, not amlodipine.

---

## Card 11

**Front:** Your patient is on diltiazem. Heart rate is 56 bpm. What do you do?

**Back:**
- **Hold the dose and notify the provider**
- Diltiazem is a non-DHP calcium channel blocker that slows the heart rate
- Hold parameter: HR below 60 bpm
- Document your findings and the provider's response

**Why it matters:** Hold-or-give scenarios are a favorite on the NCLEX. When HR is below 60 with a rate-slowing drug (digoxin, diltiazem, verapamil, beta-blocker), the answer is almost always HOLD and notify.

## Card 12

**Front:** What is the nitroglycerin sublingual protocol for chest pain?

**Back:**
1. Sit or lie the patient down
2. Check blood pressure
3. Give **1 tablet under the tongue**
4. Wait **5 minutes**
5. Still in pain? BP okay? Give **2nd tablet**
6. Wait **5 more minutes**
7. Still in pain? BP okay? Give **3rd tablet**
8. **Not relieved after 3 doses (15 minutes)? → Call 911 / activate emergency response**

**Remember: 3 doses, 5 minutes apart, then call for help!**

**Why it matters:** This protocol is tested frequently. Know the number of doses (3), the time between doses (5 minutes), and the action if not relieved (call 911). Also know to check BP before each dose.

---

## Card 13

**Front:** Why should nitroglycerin NEVER be given with PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil/Viagra)?

**Back:**
- Both drugs dilate blood vessels and lower blood pressure
- Together they can cause **severe, life-threatening hypotension**
- The blood pressure can drop so low that the patient goes into shock
- **Always ask** if the patient has taken sildenafil (Viagra), tadalafil (Cialis), or vardenafil (Levitra) in the last 24–48 hours BEFORE giving nitroglycerin

**Why it matters:** This is a safety-critical interaction the NCLEX loves to test. A common scenario: a male patient comes in with chest pain. Before giving NTG, you MUST ask about erectile dysfunction medications.

---

## Card 14

**Front:** How should sublingual nitroglycerin tablets be stored?

**Back:**
- In the **original dark glass bottle**
- Away from **light, heat, and moisture**
- **Replace every 6 months** (they lose potency)
- Do NOT store in the bathroom (humidity)
- A fresh tablet should cause a slight **tingling or burning under the tongue**

**Why it matters:** Patient teaching questions may ask about correct storage. If a patient says their NTG tablets do not tingle anymore, they may be expired and need to be replaced.

---

## Card 15

**Front:** A patient on nitroglycerin complains of a headache. Is this a problem?

**Back:**
- **No — a headache is an EXPECTED side effect**
- It happens because nitroglycerin dilates blood vessels in the head
- Actually, the headache tells us the drug is working!
- Treat with **acetaminophen (Tylenol)**
- Reassure the patient

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX may try to trick you into thinking a headache after NTG is a bad sign. It is not. It is expected. Do not hold the drug or call the provider for a headache alone.

---

## Card 16

**Front:** What is the biggest safety concern with statins (-statin drugs)?

**Back:**
- **Myopathy (muscle pain/damage)** that can progress to **rhabdomyolysis**
- Rhabdomyolysis = severe muscle breakdown → muscle contents flood the blood → **kidney failure**
- Warning signs: unexplained muscle pain, weakness, dark (cola-colored) urine
- **If a patient on a statin reports muscle pain → check CK (creatine kinase) level**

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX will describe a patient on a statin with muscle pain and ask what to do or what lab to check. Answer: check CK level and notify the provider. Dark urine is the red flag for rhabdomyolysis.

---

## Card 17

**Front:** Why do some statins need to be taken at bedtime?

**Back:**
- The liver makes the most cholesterol **at night**
- Short-acting statins (simvastatin, lovastatin) work best when taken at bedtime to match this peak
- Long-acting statins (atorvastatin, rosuvastatin) can be taken at any time of day because they stay in the body longer

**Why it matters:** This is a patient teaching point. If the question asks about timing for simvastatin, the answer is bedtime. For atorvastatin, any time is fine.

---

## Card 18

**Front:** Which cardiac drugs are contraindicated in pregnancy? (Name at least 3 classes)

**Back:**
1. **ACE inhibitors** (-pril) — can cause birth defects and fetal kidney damage
2. **ARBs** (-sartan) — same risks as ACE inhibitors
3. **Statins** (-statin) — can cause birth defects

*Think of it this way:* If a question mentions a pregnant patient, these three classes are automatic "NO" answers.

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX frequently tests pregnancy contraindications. If you see a pregnant patient in a question stem, immediately think about which drugs are unsafe.

---

## Card 19

**Front:** What are FIVE things to monitor with amiodarone? Why is it called a "high-risk" drug?

**Back:**
Remember: **Lungs, Liver, Thyroid, Eyes, Skin**
1. **Lungs** — pulmonary toxicity (cough, shortness of breath, chest X-ray, PFTs)
2. **Liver** — liver toxicity (ALT, AST, jaundice)
3. **Thyroid** — hypo/hyperthyroidism (TSH, T3, T4) — contains iodine
4. **Eyes** — corneal microdeposits (eye exams)
5. **Skin** — photosensitivity, blue-gray discoloration (wear sunscreen)

It is "high-risk" because it affects almost every organ system and stays in the body for weeks to months.

**Why it matters:** Amiodarone monitoring is heavily tested. Remember the body systems it affects, and you can answer almost any amiodarone question. Pulmonary toxicity is the most dangerous.

---

## Card 20

**Front:** A patient takes digoxin AND furosemide (Lasix). Why is this combination risky? What do you monitor?

**Back:**
- **Furosemide is a potassium-wasting diuretic** — it causes the body to lose potassium
- **Low potassium (hypokalemia) increases digoxin toxicity risk**
- So the furosemide can lower potassium → which makes the digoxin more dangerous
- **Monitor:** potassium level, digoxin level, heart rate, signs of toxicity
- **Teach:** Eat potassium-rich foods (bananas, oranges, potatoes, spinach)

**Why it matters:** This drug combination comes up ALL the time on the NCLEX. They want to know that you understand the potassium connection between these two drugs.

---

## Card 21

**Front:** A patient on verapamil reports constipation. Is this expected?

**Back:**
- **YES — constipation is a well-known side effect of verapamil**
- Verapamil slows smooth muscle activity in the GI tract
- **Nursing interventions:**
  - Increase fiber in the diet
  - Increase fluid intake
  - Encourage physical activity
  - Stool softeners if needed

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX may list several side effects and ask which one is expected with verapamil. Constipation is the answer. This is specific to verapamil among the CCBs.

---

## Card 22

**Front:** Which lab do you check if a patient on a statin complains of muscle pain?

**Back:**
- **CK (creatine kinase)** — also called CPK
- An elevated CK indicates muscle damage
- This screens for **rhabdomyolysis** (severe muscle breakdown)
- Also notify the provider — the statin may need to be stopped or changed

**Why it matters:** Quick recall question that links the right lab to the right symptom. Muscle pain + statin = check CK. If you remember nothing else about statins, remember this!

---

## Card 23

**Front:** Your patient is on an ACE inhibitor and you notice their lips and tongue are swelling. What is your FIRST action?

**Back:**
- **STOP the drug immediately** (first priority — remove the cause)
- Then: maintain the airway, call for emergency help, prepare for possible epinephrine
- This is **angioedema** — a life-threatening allergic-type reaction
- The airway can close — act FAST

> **Test-taking tip:** When the question asks "what is the FIRST action?" — think about what removes the immediate danger. Stopping the drug removes the cause. Then you manage the airway.

**Why it matters:** This is a priority question. On the NCLEX, recognizing angioedema and knowing to stop the ACE inhibitor immediately can be the difference between a right and wrong answer.

---

## Card 24

**Front:** A patient says their nitroglycerin tablet did not tingle when they put it under their tongue. What does this suggest?

**Back:**
- The tablet may be **expired or improperly stored**
- Fresh nitroglycerin should cause a slight **tingling or burning** sensation under the tongue
- **Teach the patient:**
  - Replace nitroglycerin every 6 months
  - Store in original dark glass bottle, away from light/heat/moisture
  - If no tingling, get a new prescription

**Why it matters:** This is a patient teaching and safety question. A tablet that does not tingle may not work when they need it most — during a heart attack!

## Card 25

**Front:** What is the grapefruit interaction with statins, and which statins are affected?

**Back:**
- Grapefruit inhibits the enzyme (CYP3A4) that breaks down certain statins in the body
- This means MORE drug stays in the blood → higher drug levels → more risk of side effects (especially myopathy)
- **Affected statins:** simvastatin, atorvastatin, lovastatin
- **Not significantly affected:** pravastatin, rosuvastatin
- Teach patients to **avoid grapefruit and grapefruit juice**

**Why it matters:** Food-drug interactions are tested on the NCLEX. Grapefruit + statin is one of the most commonly asked about.

---

## Card 26

**Front:** Your patient takes lisinopril. Their potassium level is 5.8 mEq/L. What do you do?

**Back:**
- **Hold the lisinopril and notify the provider immediately**
- Normal potassium: 3.5–5.0 mEq/L
- 5.8 is dangerously high (**hyperkalemia**)
- ACE inhibitors cause potassium retention — this level is too high to give another dose
- **Hyperkalemia can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmias**
- The provider may order: IV calcium gluconate (to protect the heart), insulin + glucose (to shift K+ into cells), or kayexalate (to remove K+)

**Why it matters:** Lab interpretation + drug safety + knowing when to hold = classic NCLEX combination. High K+ with ACE inhibitors is a common scenario.

---

## Card 27

**Front:** The apical pulse is 54 and digoxin is due. What do you do?

**Back:**
- **Hold the digoxin. Do NOT give it.**
- 54 bpm is below the hold parameter of 60 bpm
- Notify the provider and document your findings
- Recheck the pulse later as ordered

*Think of it this way:* The heart is already beating too slowly. Digoxin would slow it even more — that is dangerous!

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX loves "hold or give?" scenarios. For digoxin, the magic number is 60. Below 60 = hold. Always check **apical** pulse (not radial), for one full minute.

## Card 28

**Front:** Potassium is 3.2 mEq/L and the patient is on digoxin + furosemide. Why is this dangerous?

**Back:**
- Normal potassium: **3.5–5.0 mEq/L** → 3.2 is LOW (hypokalemia)
- **Furosemide (Lasix) caused the low potassium** — it is a potassium-wasting diuretic
- **Low potassium increases digoxin toxicity** — even if the digoxin level looks "normal"
- This is a **double danger**: the furosemide drops the potassium, and the low potassium makes the digoxin toxic

**Priority:** Replace potassium first. Check the digoxin level. Monitor the heart rhythm. Notify the provider.

**Why it matters:** This three-way connection (furosemide → low K+ → digoxin toxicity) is one of the NCLEX's favorite pharmacology combos. You will almost certainly see it!

## Card 29

**Front:** A patient on digoxin says "Everything looks yellow-green." What is happening?

**Back:**
- This is a **classic sign of digoxin toxicity**
- Yellow-green halos or tinted vision = the digoxin level is too high
- **What to do:**
  1. Hold the digoxin
  2. Check the digoxin level and potassium level
  3. Notify the provider immediately
  4. Monitor heart rate and rhythm

*Memory tip:* Yellow-green vision + digoxin = toxicity. Every single time. 💛💚

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX may describe the visual change without saying "digoxin toxicity." If a patient on digoxin mentions ANY visual changes — especially yellow-green color changes — you should immediately think toxicity.

## Card 30

**Front:** Why does hypokalemia increase digoxin toxicity? Explain it simply.

**Back:**
- Picture a parking lot with 100 spaces 🅿️
- **Potassium and digoxin both want to park** in the same spots on heart cells
- When potassium is normal, they share the spots — everything is balanced
- When potassium is LOW, there are **empty spots everywhere**
- Digoxin rushes in and takes ALL the empty spots
- More digoxin sitting on heart cells = **stronger (toxic) effect on the heart**
- The digoxin blood level may look "normal," but the EFFECT is too strong

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX expects you to understand WHY, not just memorize the fact. This explanation helps you reason through tricky questions where the digoxin level is normal but the patient is still toxic.

---

## Card 31

**Front:** What do you teach a patient at home about checking their pulse before taking digoxin?

**Back:**
- **"Check your pulse EVERY time before you take your digoxin pill"**
- Teach them to count at the **wrist (radial pulse)** for **one full minute** (at home, radial is fine)
- If the pulse is **below 60**, do NOT take the pill
- Call the provider before the next dose
- Also teach them to watch for: nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite, visual changes
- Keep a log of daily pulse readings to share with the provider

*Helpful tip for your patient:* "Put your digoxin bottle next to a clock. Every morning, count your pulse for a full minute. If it is 60 or above, take your pill. If it is below 60, skip it and call us."

**Why it matters:** Patient teaching is a huge part of nursing — and the NCLEX! Teaching digoxin self-monitoring shows you understand both the drug and health literacy.

---

## Card 32

**Front:** A patient on digoxin has HR 48, nausea, and blurred vision. What is the priority action?

**Back:**
- **This is digoxin toxicity until proven otherwise!**
- All three signs point to it: bradycardia (48), GI symptoms (nausea), visual changes (blurred vision)
- **Priority actions (in order):**
  1. **Hold the digoxin** — do not give the next dose
  2. Check digoxin level and potassium level STAT
  3. Notify the provider immediately
  4. Place the patient on continuous cardiac monitoring
  5. Be prepared for digoxin immune Fab (DigiFab) if toxicity is severe

**Why it matters:** This is a classic NCLEX priority question. They give you multiple signs and ask what to do FIRST. Holding the drug and getting labs are your top priorities. Don't just "continue to monitor" — act!

## Card 33

**Front:** A patient on lisinopril develops lip and tongue swelling. What is this and what do you do FIRST?

**Back:**
- This is **angioedema** — a life-threatening reaction to the ACE inhibitor
- The swelling can close the airway — the patient could stop breathing!
- **FIRST action: STOP the lisinopril immediately**
- Then:
  1. Assess and maintain the **airway** (this is the danger)
  2. Call for emergency help / rapid response
  3. Be ready to give **epinephrine**
  4. The patient should **NEVER take an ACE inhibitor again**

> **Test tip:** "What do you do FIRST?" = stop the drug that is causing the problem. Then manage the airway.

**Why it matters:** Angioedema can happen at any time — even after years on the drug. Recognizing it fast and stopping the drug FIRST saves lives. This is a high-priority NCLEX question.

## Card 34

**Front:** Potassium is 5.8 mEq/L in a patient on lisinopril. What is the concern and what do you do?

**Back:**
- Normal K+: **3.5–5.0 mEq/L** → 5.8 is HIGH (**hyperkalemia**)
- ACE inhibitors cause the body to **hold onto potassium** → levels can get dangerously high
- **Hyperkalemia can cause fatal heart arrhythmias** — this is an emergency!
- **Actions:**
  1. **Hold the lisinopril**
  2. Notify the provider STAT
  3. Get a **12-lead ECG** (look for tall, peaked T waves)
  4. Expect orders for: **IV calcium gluconate** (protects the heart), insulin + glucose (shifts K+ into cells), kayexalate (removes K+)

**Why it matters:** This links lab values + drug knowledge + emergency action. The NCLEX wants to know you can connect the dots: ACE inhibitor → high K+ → danger → act NOW.

## Card 35

**Front:** A patient has a dry cough on lisinopril. The provider switches to losartan. Why?

**Back:**
- The **dry cough is a common side effect of ACE inhibitors** (-pril drugs)
- It is caused by a buildup of **bradykinin** in the lungs
- **ARBs (-sartan drugs) do NOT cause this cough** because they work differently
- Losartan gives the same heart and kidney protection as lisinopril — without the annoying cough
- Both still carry a risk of hyperkalemia and are both contraindicated in pregnancy

*Think of it this way:* Same benefits, no cough. That is why providers switch from -pril to -sartan.

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX loves this question! "Why was the patient switched from an ACE inhibitor to an ARB?" The answer is almost always the cough.

## Card 36

**Front:** What do you teach a patient on an ACE inhibitor about pregnancy?

**Back:**
- **ACE inhibitors are DANGEROUS in pregnancy — they can cause:**
  - Birth defects
  - Fetal kidney damage and failure
  - Low amniotic fluid
  - Fetal death (especially in 2nd and 3rd trimester)
- **Teaching points:**
  1. "If you are pregnant or planning to become pregnant, tell your provider RIGHT AWAY"
  2. "Use reliable birth control while taking this medication"
  3. "If you think you might be pregnant, STOP the drug and call us immediately"
  4. The provider will switch to a pregnancy-safe blood pressure medication

**Why it matters:** Pregnancy + ACE inhibitor = automatic NCLEX red flag. If you see a pregnant patient on a -pril drug in a question, the answer usually involves stopping the drug.

## Card 37

**Front:** A patient on enalapril has a creatinine increase from 1.0 to 1.8 mg/dL. Should the nurse be concerned?

**Back:**
- **YES — be concerned!**
- A creatinine jump of **more than 30% from baseline** is a red flag
- This went up 80% (1.0 → 1.8) — that is significant
- **Why it happens:** ACE inhibitors change blood flow inside the kidneys. In some patients, this reduces kidney function.
- **What to do:**
  1. Notify the provider
  2. The provider may reduce the dose or stop the drug
  3. Continue monitoring creatinine and BUN
- This is more likely in patients with existing kidney problems or renal artery stenosis

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX may give you "before and after" lab values and ask if you should be worried. You need to recognize that a big creatinine jump on an ACE inhibitor means the kidneys may be in trouble.

## Card 38

**Front:** Amlodipine vs. diltiazem — which one lowers heart rate and which one doesn't?

**Back:**
- **Diltiazem LOWERS heart rate** (non-DHP calcium channel blocker)
  - Acts on the heart AND blood vessels
  - Must check HR before giving — hold if HR < 60
- **Amlodipine does NOT lower heart rate** (DHP calcium channel blocker)
  - Acts mainly on blood vessels only
  - May actually slightly INCREASE heart rate (reflex tachycardia)
  - No need to hold for low HR

*Memory trick:* **D**iltiazem = **D**ecreases heart rate. **A**mlodipine = **A**ffects vessels only.

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX tests whether you know which CCB affects heart rate. If a question asks about holding a CCB for a low heart rate, it is asking about diltiazem or verapamil — NOT amlodipine.

---

## Card 39

**Front:** A patient on diltiazem has HR 52 and BP 88/56. What do you do?

**Back:**
- **Hold the diltiazem — do NOT give it!**
- BOTH the heart rate AND blood pressure are too low
  - HR 52 = below the hold parameter of 60
  - BP 88/56 = hypotension (systolic below 90)
- **Actions:**
  1. Hold the dose
  2. Keep the patient in bed with side rails up (fall risk!)
  3. Notify the provider immediately
  4. Monitor HR, BP, and rhythm closely
  5. Be prepared for IV fluids or atropine if ordered

**Why it matters:** When BOTH HR and BP are low, this is even more urgent than just one being low. The NCLEX wants to see that you recognize the double danger and act quickly.

---

## Card 40

**Front:** A patient is on both metoprolol and verapamil. Why is this combination risky?

**Back:**
- **Both drugs slow the heart rate and lower blood pressure**
  - Metoprolol = beta-blocker → slows HR, lowers BP
  - Verapamil = non-DHP CCB → slows HR, lowers BP
- Together, they can cause:
  - **Severe bradycardia** (dangerously slow heart)
  - **Heart block** (electrical signals get blocked)
  - **Dangerous hypotension** (very low blood pressure)
- *Think of it this way:* Two brakes on the heart at the same time = the heart can slow down too much or even stop conducting properly!

**Why it matters:** Drug-drug interactions are heavily tested. If you see a beta-blocker + non-DHP CCB together on the NCLEX, think: double slowing effect = danger. Always monitor HR and BP closely.

---

## Card 41

**Front:** A patient on amlodipine has ankle swelling. Is this the drug or heart failure?

**Back:**
- **Most likely the drug!** Ankle swelling (peripheral edema) is a very common side effect of amlodipine.
- **Why it happens:** Amlodipine relaxes blood vessels → blood pools in the legs → fluid leaks into tissues → swollen ankles
- **BUT always assess for heart failure too!** Look for:
  - Sudden weight gain (> 2 lbs/day or 5 lbs/week)
  - Crackles in the lungs
  - Jugular vein distention (JVD)
  - Shortness of breath, especially lying down
- If it is just the drug: elevate legs, may switch medications
- If heart failure signs are present: notify provider immediately

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX loves to test whether you can tell the difference between a drug side effect and a worsening condition. Assess first, then decide!

## Card 42

**Front:** Walk through the 3-dose nitroglycerin protocol for chest pain, step by step.

**Back:**
Here is the protocol — memorize these steps! 📋

1. **Patient has chest pain** → have them sit or lie down
2. **Check blood pressure** (do NOT give if systolic is below 90)
3. **Give 1 NTG tablet sublingual (under the tongue)**
4. **Wait 5 minutes** ⏱️
5. Still in pain and BP is okay? → **Give 2nd NTG tablet**
6. **Wait 5 more minutes** ⏱️
7. Still in pain and BP is okay? → **Give 3rd NTG tablet**
8. **Still in pain after 3 doses (15 minutes total)?** → **Call 911 / activate emergency response** 🚨

**The rule:** 3 doses, 5 minutes apart, check BP before each one, then call for help.

**Why it matters:** You WILL see this on the NCLEX. They may ask how many doses, how far apart, or what to do after 3 doses. Memorize: 3-5-911!

## Card 43

**Front:** A patient took sildenafil (Viagra) last night and now has chest pain. Can you give nitroglycerin?

**Back:**
- **NO! Absolutely not!** 🚫
- **Both drugs are vasodilators** (they relax blood vessels and lower blood pressure)
- Together they can cause **severe, life-threatening hypotension** — blood pressure can crash so low the patient goes into shock
- **Must wait at least 24–48 hours** after sildenafil before giving NTG
- This also applies to tadalafil (Cialis) and vardenafil (Levitra)
- **What to do instead:** Notify the provider. They will order an alternative for the chest pain (such as morphine or other treatments).

> **Always ask male patients with chest pain:** "Have you taken any medications for erectile dysfunction in the last 48 hours?"

**Why it matters:** This is a patient safety question the NCLEX tests frequently. If you see Viagra/sildenafil + nitroglycerin in a question, the answer is always NO.

## Card 44

**Front:** After sublingual nitroglycerin, the BP drops to 82/50. What do you do?

**Back:**
- **This is hypotension from the nitroglycerin** — the drug lowered the BP too much
- **Actions (in order):**
  1. **Do NOT give another nitroglycerin dose**
  2. Lay the patient **flat** and **elevate the legs** (brings blood back to the heart)
  3. **Start IV fluids** if ordered (normal saline bolus)
  4. Notify the provider
  5. Monitor BP, HR, and symptoms closely
  6. Keep the patient safe — bed rest, side rails up

*Remember:* Nitroglycerin dilates blood vessels → blood pools in the extremities → less blood returns to the heart → BP drops.

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX wants to know you can manage a drug side effect in real time. Low BP after NTG = stop the drug, position the patient, give fluids. Do not panic — you know what to do!

## Card 45

**Front:** How should nitroglycerin be stored and why?

**Back:**
- Store in the **original dark glass bottle** (protects from light)
- Keep away from **light, heat, and moisture** — all three break down NTG
- Do **NOT** store in the bathroom (too much humidity)
- Do **NOT** transfer to a plastic pill organizer (plastic absorbs the drug)
- **Replace every 6 months** — NTG loses potency over time
- A fresh tablet should cause a slight **tingling or burning under the tongue**
- No tingle? → The tablet may be expired. Get a new prescription!

**Why it matters:** Patient teaching about NTG storage is a common NCLEX question. The key words to remember: dark glass, no heat/light/moisture, replace every 6 months, tingling = fresh.

## Card 46

**Front:** A patient on atorvastatin reports severe muscle pain and dark (cola-colored) urine. What do you suspect?

**Back:**
- **Rhabdomyolysis** — a life-threatening breakdown of muscle tissue 🚨
- The muscles are breaking down → **myoglobin** floods the bloodstream → the kidneys try to filter it → **kidney failure**
- The dark urine IS the myoglobin coming out
- **Actions:**
  1. **STOP the statin immediately** — notify the provider
  2. Check **CK (creatine kinase)** — it will be very elevated
  3. Check **kidney function** (BUN, creatinine)
  4. Aggressive **IV fluids** to protect the kidneys
  5. Monitor urine output closely

**Why it matters:** Muscle pain on a statin = check CK. Muscle pain + dark urine on a statin = rhabdomyolysis emergency. The NCLEX expects you to recognize this dangerous progression.

## Card 47

**Front:** What labs do you monitor for a patient on statins and why?

**Back:**
- **Liver function tests (ALT, AST)**
  - Statins can cause liver damage (hepatotoxicity)
  - Checked at baseline and periodically
  - If elevated → notify provider, may need to stop the drug
- **CK (creatine kinase)**
  - Checked if the patient reports **muscle pain or weakness**
  - Elevated CK = muscle damage → watch for rhabdomyolysis
- **Lipid panel (cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides)**
  - To check if the statin is actually working!
  - Usually checked 4–12 weeks after starting, then periodically

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX may ask "what labs should the nurse monitor?" for a patient on a statin. Know all three: liver, CK (if symptomatic), and lipids. Liver and CK are the safety monitors; lipids check effectiveness.

## Card 48

**Front:** A patient asks "Can I drink grapefruit juice with my statin?" What do you say?

**Back:**
- **"It is best to avoid grapefruit juice while taking your statin."**
- **Why:** Grapefruit blocks the enzyme (CYP3A4) that breaks down certain statins
- This means **more drug stays in your blood** → higher levels → more risk of muscle damage and rhabdomyolysis
- **Most affected:** simvastatin, atorvastatin, lovastatin
- **Less affected:** pravastatin, rosuvastatin (but safer to just avoid it)
- Even small amounts of grapefruit can have an effect — it is not just large amounts!

*Simple way to explain it to the patient:* "Grapefruit juice keeps your body from getting rid of the medication properly. Too much medication builds up and can hurt your muscles."

**Why it matters:** Food-drug interactions are popular NCLEX questions. Grapefruit + statin is one of the most commonly tested. When in doubt, teach the patient to avoid grapefruit.

## Card 49

**Front:** What are the major organ systems amiodarone can damage? Name at least 4.

**Back:**
Remember this memory trick: **"Amiodarone affects Lungs, Liver, Thyroid, Eyes, and Skin"** 🫁🫀

1. **Lungs** — pulmonary toxicity/fibrosis (most dangerous!) → monitor with chest X-ray and PFTs
2. **Liver** — hepatotoxicity → monitor ALT, AST
3. **Thyroid** — can cause hypo- OR hyperthyroidism (amiodarone contains iodine) → monitor TSH
4. **Eyes** — corneal microdeposits → regular eye exams
5. **Skin** — photosensitivity + blue-gray skin discoloration with sun exposure

**Why it is "high-risk":** Amiodarone has a very long half-life (40–55 days!). It stays in the body for weeks to months even after you stop it. Side effects can appear long after starting the drug.

**Why it matters:** The NCLEX loves amiodarone questions because there is so much to monitor. If you remember those 5 body systems, you can answer almost anything they throw at you!

## Card 50

**Front:** A patient on amiodarone is going on vacation to the beach. What do you teach?

**Back:**
- Amiodarone causes **severe photosensitivity** — the skin burns much more easily in the sun!
- **Teaching points:**
  1. **Wear SPF 50+ sunscreen** — reapply every 2 hours and after swimming
  2. Wear **protective clothing**: long sleeves, wide-brimmed hat, sunglasses
  3. **Avoid direct sun** during peak hours (10 AM – 2 PM)
  4. Sit under an umbrella or in the shade as much as possible
  5. Even on cloudy days, UV rays can still cause a burn
  6. Long-term sun exposure can cause a **blue-gray skin discoloration** that may be permanent

*Kind way to say it:* "You can still enjoy your vacation! Just be extra careful about sun protection. Your skin is much more sensitive now because of this medication." ☀️🧴

**Why it matters:** Patient teaching about sun safety with amiodarone is a classic NCLEX question. If a question says "amiodarone + going outside," think sunscreen and protective clothing!

## Card 51

**Front:** A patient takes digoxin, furosemide, and a potassium supplement. They are vomiting and can't keep anything down. What is the biggest danger?

**Back:**
- **The biggest danger is digoxin toxicity!** Here is why:
  1. The patient is vomiting → they **cannot absorb** their oral potassium supplement
  2. But furosemide is still working → still **wasting potassium** through the kidneys
  3. Potassium level drops (hypokalemia)
  4. Low potassium → **digoxin becomes more toxic** (even if digoxin level looks normal)
  5. Vomiting itself can also be a **sign of digoxin toxicity** — so it may already be happening!

- **Priority actions:**
  1. Check potassium level and digoxin level **STAT**
  2. Notify the provider — may need **IV potassium** (since oral is not staying down)
  3. Hold the digoxin until levels are checked
  4. Treat the nausea/vomiting
  5. Monitor heart rhythm

**Why it matters:** This is a complex, multi-drug scenario — exactly the kind the NCLEX loves. The student who connects vomiting → can't absorb K+ → K+ drops → dig toxicity will get this right!

## Card 52

**Front:** A heart failure patient is on lisinopril, carvedilol, and furosemide. Which vital signs matter MOST before giving all three?

**Back:**
- **Blood pressure** 🩺 — CHECK BEFORE ALL THREE
  - Lisinopril (ACE inhibitor) lowers BP
  - Carvedilol (beta-blocker) lowers BP
  - Furosemide (diuretic) lowers BP through fluid loss
  - All three together can cause **significant hypotension**
  - Hold if systolic BP is below 90–100 (per facility policy)

- **Heart rate** 💓 — CHECK BEFORE CARVEDILOL
  - Carvedilol slows the heart rate
  - Hold if HR is below 60 bpm

- **Also check:**
  - **Potassium** — lisinopril raises K+, furosemide lowers K+ (they may balance out, but check!)
  - **Daily weight and I&O** — tracks fluid status
  - **Creatinine** — ACE inhibitors can affect kidneys

**Why it matters:** Real patients take multiple drugs. The NCLEX tests whether you can manage the whole picture, not just one drug at a time. For this combo: BP is king, HR matters for carvedilol, and labs tie it all together.

## Card 53

**Front:** Quick reference: Which cardiac drugs need HR checked before giving? Which need BP checked? Which need labs checked?

**Back:**

**✅ CHECK HEART RATE (hold if < 60 bpm):**
- Digoxin

- ACE inhibitors (-pril)

| Warfarin* | INR/PT |

*Warfarin is an anticoagulant, not a "cardiac drug" exactly, but you will often see it in cardiac patients!
