# 💊 Cardiac Medications — Your Complete Study Guide

> **Welcome!** This guide covers the heart medications you need to know for the NCLEX.
> Take your time with each section. These drugs save lives — and understanding them well will help you feel confident on test day and at the bedside. You've got this! 💪

---

## 📖 How to Use This Guide

Each drug section follows the same pattern so your brain can organize the information easily:

1. **What is it?** — The drug name and class
2. **Why do we give it?** — The indication (reason)
3. **How does it work?** — Simple explanation of the mechanism
4. **Before you give it** — What to check first
5. **After you give it** — What to watch for
6. **When to hold it** — Parameters that mean "do NOT give this dose"
7. **Key labs** — Numbers you need to monitor
8. **Teach your patient** — What the patient needs to know
9. **Interactions** — Other drugs or foods that cause problems
10. **Contraindications** — Situations where this drug should NOT be used

## 1. Cardiac Glycosides

### 💛 Digoxin (Lanoxin)

**What is it?**
- Digoxin is a cardiac glycoside — a drug that comes from the foxglove plant
- It works directly on the heart muscle

**Why do we give it?**
- **Heart failure** — makes the heart squeeze stronger (so it pumps blood better)
- **Atrial fibrillation** — slows a heart that is beating too fast and irregularly

**How does it work?**
- *Think of it this way:* Digoxin does TWO things to the heart — it makes each beat **stronger** (positive inotropic) and makes the heart beat **slower** (negative chronotropic)
- Stronger squeeze = more blood gets pumped out each beat
- Slower rate = the heart has more time to fill with blood between beats

**Before you give it — ALWAYS do this:**
- ✅ Check the **apical pulse for one full minute** (use your stethoscope on the chest, at the 5th intercostal space, midclavicular line)
- ✅ Check the latest **digoxin level** — therapeutic range is **0.5–2.0 ng/mL**
- ✅ Check the **potassium level** — low potassium makes digoxin toxicity MORE likely
- ✅ Check the **kidney function** (creatinine, BUN) — digoxin leaves the body through the kidneys

**🛑 When to HOLD digoxin:**
- **Adult heart rate below 60 bpm** — hold the dose and notify the provider
- **Child heart rate below the normal range for their age** — hold and notify
- Digoxin level above 2.0 ng/mL — hold and notify
- Any signs of toxicity (see below) — hold and notify

**After you give it — watch for these:**
- Monitor heart rate and rhythm
- Watch for signs of **digoxin toxicity** — this is a HIGH-PRIORITY safety topic!

**⚠️ Digoxin Toxicity — Know These Signs!**
- **GI symptoms** (usually appear first): nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite, diarrhea
- **Visual changes**: seeing yellow-green halos around lights, blurred vision, changes in color vision
- **Heart changes**: bradycardia (slow heart rate), any new dysrhythmia (irregular rhythm)
- **Neurological**: confusion, fatigue, weakness

> *Think of it this way:* If your patient on digoxin says "Everything looks yellow and I feel sick to my stomach" — think toxicity RIGHT AWAY!

**Key labs to monitor:**
| Lab | Normal/Therapeutic Range | Why It Matters |
|-----|------------------------|----------------|
| Digoxin level | 0.5–2.0 ng/mL | Above 2.0 = toxicity risk |
| Potassium (K+) | 3.5–5.0 mEq/L | Low K+ increases toxicity risk |
| Creatinine | 0.6–1.2 mg/dL | Kidneys remove digoxin from the body |
| BUN | 7–20 mg/dL | Another kidney function marker |

**The potassium connection — very important!**
- Digoxin and potassium compete for the same spot on heart cells
- When potassium is LOW (hypokalemia), digoxin takes over more spots → more drug effect → toxicity!
- When potassium is HIGH, digoxin has less effect
- *Think of it this way:* Potassium and digoxin are fighting for the same seat. If potassium leaves (low K+), digoxin sits in ALL the seats — too much effect!

**Antidote for severe digoxin toxicity:**
- **Digoxin immune Fab (Digibind)** — this is the antidote for life-threatening toxicity

**Teach your patient:**
- Take your pulse for one full minute before taking your pill each day
- If your heart rate is below 60, do NOT take the pill — call your doctor
- Report any nausea, vomiting, vision changes, or feeling your heart skip beats
- Keep all lab appointments — we need to check your drug level and potassium
- Take the dose at the same time every day
- Do NOT double up if you miss a dose
- Eat potassium-rich foods (bananas, oranges, potatoes) if you are also taking a diuretic

**Interactions:**
- **Potassium-wasting diuretics** (furosemide/Lasix, hydrochlorothiazide) — they lower potassium, which increases digoxin toxicity risk
- **Amiodarone** — raises digoxin levels (dose may need to be reduced)
- **Verapamil** — raises digoxin levels
- **Antacids** — can decrease digoxin absorption (separate doses by 2 hours)

**Contraindications:**
- Ventricular fibrillation
- Severe heart block (unless the patient has a pacemaker)
- Hypokalemia (must be corrected first)

## 2. ACE Inhibitors

### 💛 Lisinopril (Zestril), Enalapril (Vasotec)

> **Memory tip:** ACE inhibitors end in **-pril** — lisinopril, enalapril, captopril, ramipril. If you see "-pril," think ACE inhibitor!

**What is it?**
- ACE inhibitors — they block an enzyme called ACE (angiotensin-converting enzyme)
- ACE is part of a system that raises blood pressure, so blocking it lowers blood pressure

**Why do we give it?**
- **High blood pressure** (hypertension)
- **Heart failure** — protects and helps the heart work better over time
- **After a heart attack** — protects the heart from further damage
- **Kidney protection** — especially for patients with diabetes

**How does it work?**
- *Think of it this way:* Your body has a system called RAAS that raises blood pressure when it thinks it needs to. ACE is one step in this system. By blocking ACE, the drug stops the blood pressure from going up
- Blood vessels relax and open wider → blood pressure goes down
- Less stress on the heart → the heart can work easier
- Protects the kidneys by reducing pressure inside the tiny kidney blood vessels

**Before you give it:**
- ✅ Check **blood pressure** — hold if systolic blood pressure (SBP) is too low (usually below 90 mmHg, or per facility policy)
- ✅ Check **potassium level** — ACE inhibitors can raise potassium
- ✅ Check **kidney function** (creatinine, BUN)
- ✅ Ask about **pregnancy** — these drugs harm the unborn baby

**🛑 When to HOLD:**
- SBP below 90 mmHg (or per provider order)
- Potassium above 5.0 mEq/L — notify the provider
- Signs of angioedema (see below) — STOP the drug immediately

**After you give it — watch for:**
- **Dry cough** — this is the CLASSIC side effect of ACE inhibitors. It happens because ACE inhibitors increase a substance called bradykinin in the lungs. The cough is annoying but not dangerous. If the patient cannot tolerate it, the provider may switch to an ARB instead
- **Dizziness/lightheadedness** — from blood pressure dropping, especially after the first dose
- **Hyperkalemia** (high potassium) — because ACE inhibitors cause the body to keep more potassium
- **Changes in kidney function** — check creatinine

**🚨 ANGIOEDEMA — This is an Emergency!**
- Angioedema means sudden, severe swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat
- This can block the airway — the patient cannot breathe!
- It is rare, but it can happen at any time during treatment (even after years of taking the drug)
- **What to do:** STOP the drug immediately, maintain the airway, call for help, be ready to give epinephrine
- *Think of it this way:* If your patient on an ACE inhibitor suddenly has a swollen tongue and trouble breathing — this is angioedema until proven otherwise. Act FAST!

**First-dose hypotension:**
- The very first dose can cause a big drop in blood pressure
- Give the first dose at bedtime so the patient is lying down
- Have the patient get up slowly (sit on the edge of the bed first)

**Key labs to monitor:**
| Lab | Normal Range | Why It Matters |
|-----|-------------|----------------|
| Potassium (K+) | 3.5–5.0 mEq/L | ACE inhibitors can raise K+ dangerously |
| Creatinine | 0.6–1.2 mg/dL | Monitors kidney function |
| BUN | 7–20 mg/dL | Monitors kidney function |
| Blood pressure | Per patient baseline | Tracks drug effectiveness and safety |

**Teach your patient:**
- Rise slowly from sitting or lying down (to avoid dizziness from low blood pressure)
- Report any swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat immediately — go to the ER
- A dry cough is a known side effect — report it, but do not stop the drug on your own
- Do NOT use potassium supplements or salt substitutes (they contain potassium!)
- Do NOT take this drug if you are pregnant or planning to become pregnant
- Keep all lab appointments
- Do not stop taking this drug suddenly

**Interactions:**
- **Potassium supplements and potassium-sparing diuretics** (spironolactone) — risk of dangerously high potassium
- **NSAIDs** (ibuprofen, naproxen) — can reduce the effectiveness of ACE inhibitors and harm kidneys
- **Lithium** — ACE inhibitors can increase lithium levels

**Contraindications:**
- **Pregnancy** (causes birth defects — Category X in 2nd and 3rd trimesters)
- History of angioedema from ACE inhibitors
- Bilateral renal artery stenosis (narrowing of blood vessels to both kidneys)

## 3. ARBs (Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers)

### 💛 Losartan (Cozaar), Valsartan (Diovan)

> **Memory tip:** ARBs end in **-sartan** — losartan, valsartan, irbesartan, candesartan. See "-sartan," think ARB!

**What is it?**
- ARBs block the receptor where angiotensin II (a chemical that raises blood pressure) works
- They do a similar job as ACE inhibitors but in a slightly different way

**Why do we give it?**
- **High blood pressure**
- **Heart failure**
- **Kidney protection** (especially in diabetes)
- Often used when a patient **cannot tolerate ACE inhibitors** (usually because of the dry cough)

**How does it work?**
- *Think of it this way:* ACE inhibitors stop angiotensin II from being MADE. ARBs let it be made but BLOCK it from working. Same result — blood vessels relax, blood pressure goes down
- ARBs do NOT increase bradykinin → so they usually do NOT cause a dry cough

**Before you give it:**
- ✅ Check **blood pressure**
- ✅ Check **potassium level**
- ✅ Check **kidney function** (creatinine, BUN)
- ✅ Ask about **pregnancy**

**🛑 When to HOLD:**
- SBP below 90 mmHg (or per provider order)
- Potassium above 5.0 mEq/L — notify the provider

**After you give it — watch for:**
- Dizziness/lightheadedness (from lower blood pressure)
- Hyperkalemia (high potassium) — same risk as ACE inhibitors
- Changes in kidney function
- Angioedema is VERY rare with ARBs (much less common than with ACE inhibitors), but still possible

**Key labs to monitor:**
| Lab | Normal Range | Why It Matters |
|-----|-------------|----------------|
| Potassium (K+) | 3.5–5.0 mEq/L | ARBs can raise K+ |
| Creatinine | 0.6–1.2 mg/dL | Monitors kidney function |
| BUN | 7–20 mg/dL | Monitors kidney function |
| Blood pressure | Per patient baseline | Tracks effectiveness and safety |

**Teach your patient:**
- Rise slowly from sitting or lying down
- Do NOT use potassium supplements or salt substitutes
- Do NOT take this drug if you are pregnant or planning to become pregnant
- Report any facial swelling or difficulty breathing
- Keep all lab appointments

**Interactions:**
- **Potassium supplements and potassium-sparing diuretics** — risk of high potassium
- **NSAIDs** — can reduce effectiveness and harm kidneys
- **Lithium** — ARBs can increase lithium levels
- Do NOT combine with ACE inhibitors (dual blockade increases risks without clear benefit)

**Contraindications:**
- **Pregnancy** (same risk as ACE inhibitors)
- Bilateral renal artery stenosis

> **ACE Inhibitor vs. ARB — Quick Comparison:**
>
> | Feature | ACE Inhibitor (-pril) | ARB (-sartan) |
> |---------|----------------------|---------------|
> | Dry cough | YES (common) | NO (rare) |
> | Angioedema risk | Higher | Lower (but possible) |
> | Hyperkalemia risk | Yes | Yes |
> | Pregnancy safe? | NO | NO |
> | Kidney protection | Yes | Yes |
> | Common switch reason | Cough → switch to ARB | — |

## 4. Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)

> **Important:** There are TWO types of calcium channel blockers, and they work differently! This is a common test topic.

### 💛 Type 1: Dihydropyridines (DHP) — Mainly Blood Vessels

#### Amlodipine (Norvasc)

> **Memory tip:** Most dihydropyridines end in **-dipine** — amlodipine, nifedipine, felodipine.

**What is it?**
- A calcium channel blocker that mainly works on **blood vessels** (not so much on the heart itself)

**Why do we give it?**
- **High blood pressure**
- **Angina** (chest pain from reduced blood flow to the heart)

**How does it work?**
- *Think of it this way:* Calcium makes muscles squeeze tight. Blood vessel walls have muscle. By blocking calcium from entering these muscles, the blood vessel relaxes and opens wider → blood pressure goes down
- DHP calcium channel blockers focus on the blood vessel muscles, not the heart muscle

**Before you give it:**
- ✅ Check **blood pressure** — especially systolic (top number)

**🛑 When to HOLD:**
- SBP below 90 mmHg (or per provider order)

**After you give it — watch for:**
- **Peripheral edema** — swelling in the ankles and feet (this is very common with amlodipine). It happens because blood vessels in the legs dilate and fluid leaks out
- **Headache and flushing** — from blood vessel dilation
- **Reflex tachycardia** — sometimes when blood pressure drops, the heart speeds up to compensate. This is more common with shorter-acting DHPs like nifedipine
- **Dizziness** — from lower blood pressure

**Key labs to monitor:**
| Lab | Why It Matters |
|-----|----------------|
| Blood pressure | Track effectiveness and watch for hypotension |
| Heart rate | Watch for reflex tachycardia |

**Teach your patient:**
- Rise slowly from sitting or lying down
- Some ankle swelling is expected — elevate your feet when sitting
- Report swelling that gets much worse
- Do not stop the drug suddenly
- Avoid grapefruit juice with some DHPs (it can increase drug levels)

---

### 💛 Type 2: Non-Dihydropyridines (Non-DHP) — Heart AND Vessels

#### Diltiazem (Cardizem) and Verapamil (Calan)

**What is it?**
- Calcium channel blockers that work on BOTH the **heart** and the **blood vessels**
- They slow the heart rate AND lower blood pressure

**Why do we give it?**
- **High blood pressure**
- **Angina**
- **Atrial fibrillation and other fast heart rhythms** — to slow the heart rate (especially diltiazem)

**How does it work?**
- *Think of it this way:* These drugs block calcium in BOTH places — the heart and the blood vessels
- In the **heart**: slower heart rate, weaker contractions (the heart does not squeeze as hard)
- In the **blood vessels**: vessels relax and open → blood pressure goes down

**Before you give it:**
- ✅ Check **heart rate** (apical pulse)
- ✅ Check **blood pressure**
- ✅ Check for signs of **heart failure** (these drugs can make heart failure worse because they weaken heart contractions)

**🛑 When to HOLD:**
- **Heart rate below 60 bpm** — hold and notify the provider
- **SBP below 90 mmHg** — hold and notify the provider
- Signs of worsening heart failure (new or worsening shortness of breath, weight gain, edema)

**After you give it — watch for:**
- **Bradycardia** (slow heart rate) — especially if the patient is also taking beta-blockers or digoxin
- **Hypotension** (low blood pressure)
- **Constipation** — this is very common with **verapamil** specifically
- **Dizziness, fatigue**
- **Worsening heart failure** — watch for shortness of breath, swelling, weight gain

**Key labs to monitor:**
| Lab | Why It Matters |
|-----|----------------|
| Heart rate | Watch for bradycardia (below 60) |
| Blood pressure | Watch for hypotension |
| ECG/rhythm | Watch for heart blocks |
| Daily weight | Catches fluid retention early |

**Teach your patient:**
- Check your pulse daily — report if below 60
- Rise slowly from sitting or lying down
- If taking verapamil, eat high-fiber foods, drink plenty of water (for constipation)
- Report any shortness of breath, swelling, or weight gain of more than 2 pounds in a day
- Do not stop the drug suddenly
- Avoid grapefruit juice (can increase drug levels)

**Interactions:**
- **Beta-blockers** — BOTH slow the heart → together they can cause dangerously slow heart rate or heart block. Use with caution, especially with verapamil
- **Digoxin** — non-DHP CCBs can increase digoxin levels and add to heart rate slowing
- **Grapefruit juice** — increases blood levels of some CCBs
- **Simvastatin** — verapamil increases simvastatin levels (higher risk of muscle damage)

**Contraindications:**
- Severe bradycardia or heart block (2nd/3rd degree, without pacemaker)
- Severe heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (non-DHPs)
- Sick sinus syndrome (without pacemaker)

> **DHP vs. Non-DHP — Quick Comparison:**
>
> | Feature | DHP (amlodipine) | Non-DHP (diltiazem, verapamil) |
> |---------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
> | Main effect | Blood vessels | Heart AND blood vessels |
> | Lowers heart rate? | No (may increase it) | YES |
> | Hold for HR < 60? | No | YES |
> | Peripheral edema? | YES (common) | Less common |
> | Constipation? | No | YES (verapamil) |
> | Used for arrhythmias? | No | YES |
> | Worsens heart failure? | Less likely | YES (can worsen) |

## 5. Nitrates

### 💛 Nitroglycerin (NTG)

**What is it?**
- A nitrate medication that opens blood vessels very quickly
- Available in many forms: sublingual tablet, sublingual spray, IV drip, topical paste, transdermal patch

**Why do we give it?**
- **Acute angina** (chest pain) — quick relief with sublingual form
- **Chronic angina prevention** — with patches or long-acting forms
- **Acute heart failure** — IV form reduces the heart's workload

**How does it work?**
- *Think of it this way:* Nitroglycerin relaxes blood vessels, especially the **veins**
- When veins relax and get wider, less blood comes BACK to the heart (this is called reducing **preload**)
- Less blood coming in = the heart does not have to work as hard = less chest pain
- At higher doses, it also relaxes arteries, lowering blood pressure

**Before you give it:**
- ✅ Check **blood pressure** — hold if SBP below 90 mmHg
- ✅ Check **heart rate**
- ✅ Ask: "Have you taken any erectile dysfunction medications in the last 24–48 hours?" (sildenafil/Viagra, tadalafil/Cialis)

**🛑 When to HOLD:**
- **SBP below 90 mmHg** — hold and notify
- Patient has taken a **PDE5 inhibitor** (sildenafil, tadalafil, vardenafil) in the last 24–48 hours — SEVERE hypotension risk!
- Right ventricular infarction (these patients depend on preload)
- Severe anemia or increased intracranial pressure

**The Sublingual Nitroglycerin Protocol — MUST KNOW:**
1. Patient has chest pain → sit or lie the patient down first
2. Check blood pressure
3. Place **one tablet under the tongue** (or one spray)
4. Wait **5 minutes**
5. If pain continues and BP is okay → give **second dose**
6. Wait **5 more minutes**
7. If pain continues and BP is okay → give **third dose**
8. **If pain is NOT relieved after 3 doses (or after 15 minutes total) → activate emergency response / call 911**

> **Remember:** Up to 3 doses, 5 minutes apart. Not relieved? Call for help!

**After you give it — watch for:**
- **Headache** — this is EXPECTED and common (the blood vessels in the head also dilate). Treat with acetaminophen. A headache actually means the drug is working!
- **Hypotension** — blood pressure can drop significantly
- **Dizziness, lightheadedness**
- **Flushing** (face turns red)
- **Reflex tachycardia** — heart rate may increase as BP drops

**Key labs/assessments to monitor:**
| Assessment | Why It Matters |
|-----------|----------------|
| Blood pressure | Watch for hypotension |
| Heart rate | Watch for reflex tachycardia |
| Pain level | Assess if chest pain is relieved |
| Headache | Expected — reassure patient |

**Teach your patient:**
- Sit or lie down before taking the tablet to prevent falling if you get dizzy
- Place the tablet **under your tongue** — do NOT swallow it. Let it dissolve
- You should feel a slight tingling or burning under your tongue — this means the tablet is fresh and working
- If 3 tablets do not relieve your pain after 15 minutes, call 911
- **Store your nitroglycerin correctly:**
  - Keep in the **original dark glass bottle**
  - Keep away from **light, heat, and moisture**
  - Replace every **6 months** (it loses potency over time)
  - Do NOT store in the bathroom (too much humidity)
- **NEVER take nitroglycerin if you have taken Viagra (sildenafil), Cialis (tadalafil), or similar drugs in the last 24–48 hours** — this can cause a life-threatening drop in blood pressure
- Remove the nitroglycerin patch for **10–12 hours at night** (a "nitrate-free period") to prevent the drug from stopping working (tolerance)

**Interactions:**
- **🚨 PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil/Viagra, tadalafil/Cialis, vardenafil/Levitra)** — NEVER use together! Both lower blood pressure → severe, potentially fatal hypotension
- **Other blood pressure medications** — additive hypotensive effects
- **Alcohol** — increases the blood pressure-lowering effect

**Contraindications:**
- Use of PDE5 inhibitors within 24–48 hours
- Severe hypotension (SBP < 90 mmHg)
- Right ventricular infarction
- Increased intracranial pressure
- Severe anemia

## 6. Statins (HMG-CoA Reductase Inhibitors)

### 💛 Atorvastatin (Lipitor), Simvastatin (Zocor)

> **Memory tip:** Statins end in **-statin** — atorvastatin, simvastatin, rosuvastatin, pravastatin.

**What is it?**
- Statins lower cholesterol by blocking an enzyme in the liver that makes cholesterol

**Why do we give it?**
- **High cholesterol** (hyperlipidemia)
- **Prevention of heart attack and stroke** — statins reduce the buildup of plaque in blood vessels
- Patients with diabetes, heart disease, or high cardiovascular risk

**How does it work?**
- *Think of it this way:* Your liver is like a cholesterol factory. Statins turn down the factory's production. The liver then has to pull more cholesterol out of your blood to do its work → blood cholesterol goes down
- The enzyme they block is called HMG-CoA reductase (you do not need to memorize this for most questions, but you may see it)

**Before you give it:**
- ✅ Check **liver function tests** (ALT, AST) — baseline before starting
- ✅ Ask about **muscle pain** — any unexplained muscle aches?
- ✅ Check current **lipid panel** (total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides)

**🛑 When to HOLD or STOP:**
- Active liver disease or unexplained persistent elevation of liver enzymes
- **Unexplained severe muscle pain** — could be a sign of a dangerous condition (see below)
- **Pregnancy** — statins are contraindicated in pregnancy

**After you give it — watch for:**
- **Muscle pain (myalgia/myopathy)** — this is the BIG safety concern
  - Mild muscle aches can happen and may be okay
  - BUT: severe, unexplained muscle pain + dark-colored urine → think **rhabdomyolysis**!
  - Rhabdomyolysis = muscle breakdown → muscle contents flood the blood → can cause kidney failure
  - **If a patient reports muscle pain: check CK (creatine kinase) level**
- **Liver problems** — monitor liver enzymes (ALT, AST)
- **GI issues** — nausea, constipation, diarrhea (usually mild)

> *Think of it this way:* A patient on a statin says "My muscles are so sore and my urine looks like cola." That is an emergency — think rhabdomyolysis, check CK level, and notify the provider immediately!

**Key labs to monitor:**
| Lab | Normal Range | Why It Matters |
|-----|-------------|----------------|
| ALT (liver enzyme) | 7–56 U/L | Monitors for liver damage |
| AST (liver enzyme) | 10–40 U/L | Monitors for liver damage |
| CK (creatine kinase) | 22–198 U/L | Check if muscle pain occurs (elevated in rhabdomyolysis) |
| Lipid panel | Varies | Tracks if drug is working |

**Teach your patient:**
- **Some statins work best when taken at bedtime** (simvastatin, lovastatin) because the liver makes the most cholesterol at night. Atorvastatin and rosuvastatin can be taken at any time
- **Report any unexplained muscle pain, tenderness, or weakness right away** — especially if you also have dark urine or fever
- This is a long-term medication — do not stop it just because your cholesterol numbers improve
- Follow a heart-healthy diet and exercise along with the medication
- **Avoid grapefruit and grapefruit juice** if taking simvastatin, atorvastatin, or lovastatin — grapefruit blocks the enzyme that breaks down the drug, so more drug stays in your body → higher risk of side effects
- Keep all lab appointments for liver and cholesterol checks
- **Do NOT take if pregnant or planning to become pregnant**

**Interactions:**
- **Grapefruit juice** — increases levels of certain statins (simvastatin, atorvastatin, lovastatin)
- **Fibrates (gemfibrozil)** — increased risk of myopathy/rhabdomyolysis when combined
- **Niacin (high doses)** — increased risk of myopathy
- **Verapamil, diltiazem, amiodarone** — can increase statin levels
- **Warfarin** — some statins increase warfarin's effect (monitor INR)
- **Cyclosporine** — increases statin levels significantly

**Contraindications:**
- Active liver disease
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding** (can cause birth defects)
- Known hypersensitivity to the drug

## 7. Antiarrhythmics

### 💛 Amiodarone (Cordarone, Pacerone)

**What is it?**
- A powerful antiarrhythmic drug (a drug that treats abnormal heart rhythms)
- Often called a "dirty drug" in a good-natured way — because it affects many different ion channels in the heart (it does not fit neatly into one class)

**Why do we give it?**
- **Serious, life-threatening arrhythmias** — ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation
- **Atrial fibrillation** — when other drugs have not worked
- Used in ACLS (Advanced Cardiac Life Support) protocols during cardiac arrest

**How does it work?**
- *Think of it this way:* Amiodarone slows down the electrical signals in the heart. It gives the heart's electrical system a chance to "reset" and beat in a normal, organized way
- It has effects on sodium, potassium, and calcium channels, plus it blocks some adrenaline receptors

**Before you give it:**
- ✅ Check **heart rate and rhythm** (continuous cardiac monitoring)
- ✅ Check **blood pressure**
- ✅ Check baseline labs: **thyroid function** (TSH, T3, T4), **liver function** (ALT, AST), **pulmonary function tests** (lung function)
- ✅ Baseline **eye exam** (corneal deposits can develop)
- ✅ Check **potassium and magnesium** levels (low levels make arrhythmias worse)

**🛑 When to HOLD or STOP:**
- Severe sinus node disease or heart block (without pacemaker)
- Severe lung disease that worsens
- Signs of thyroid problems
- Signs of liver toxicity

**After you give it — watch for (MANY side effects!):**

> *Think of it this way:* Amiodarone affects almost every organ system. Remember the phrase **"Amiodarone affects the Lungs, Liver, Thyroid, Eyes, and Skin."**

- **🫁 Pulmonary toxicity** — the MOST DANGEROUS side effect
  - Can cause a serious lung condition (pulmonary fibrosis)
  - Watch for: new cough, shortness of breath, difficulty breathing
  - Get regular chest X-rays and pulmonary function tests
- **🦋 Thyroid problems** — amiodarone contains iodine
  - Can cause HYPOthyroidism (too little thyroid hormone) or HYPERthyroidism (too much)
  - Monitor TSH regularly
- **🫘 Liver toxicity**
  - Monitor liver enzymes (ALT, AST)
  - Watch for jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes)
- **👁️ Corneal microdeposits**
  - Tiny deposits in the eyes — most patients on long-term amiodarone develop these
  - Usually do not affect vision, but get regular eye exams
  - Can sometimes see halos around lights at night
- **☀️ Photosensitivity and skin changes**
  - Skin becomes very sensitive to sunlight
  - Can develop a blue-gray skin discoloration with long-term use
- **Bradycardia and hypotension** (especially with IV amiodarone)
- **GI symptoms** — nausea, vomiting, constipation
- **Peripheral neuropathy** — numbness or tingling in hands and feet

**Key labs and monitoring:**
| Test | How Often | Why |
|------|-----------|-----|
| Thyroid (TSH, T3, T4) | Every 6 months | Detects thyroid dysfunction |
| Liver (ALT, AST) | Every 6 months | Detects liver toxicity |
| Pulmonary function | Annually + if symptomatic | Detects lung toxicity |
| Eye exam | Annually | Monitors corneal deposits |
| Chest X-ray | Annually | Screens for pulmonary changes |
| ECG | Regularly | Monitors heart rhythm and QT interval |
| Potassium and magnesium | Regularly | Low levels worsen arrhythmias |

**Teach your patient:**
- **Wear sunscreen and protective clothing** when outside — your skin is very sensitive to the sun now
- Report any new cough, shortness of breath, or difficulty breathing right away — this could be a lung problem
- Report any weight changes, feeling too cold or too hot, or changes in energy — could be thyroid
- Report any yellowing of skin or eyes, dark urine, or belly pain — could be liver
- Report any vision changes
- Keep ALL follow-up appointments — you need regular blood tests, eye exams, and lung tests
- This drug stays in your body for a VERY long time (weeks to months) even after stopping — side effects can appear or continue even after stopping

**Interactions:**
- **Digoxin** — amiodarone increases digoxin levels (reduce digoxin dose by ~50%)
- **Warfarin** — amiodarone increases warfarin's effect (increase bleeding risk — monitor INR closely)
- **Simvastatin** — increased risk of muscle damage (limit simvastatin dose)
- **Beta-blockers, diltiazem, verapamil** — added heart rate slowing → risk of severe bradycardia
- **QT-prolonging drugs** — increased risk of dangerous arrhythmias

**Contraindications:**
- Severe sinus node disease or heart block (without pacemaker)
- Cardiogenic shock
- Known hypersensitivity
- Iodine sensitivity (amiodarone contains iodine)

## 🔑 Key Themes to Remember for the Exam

1. **Always check vital signs before giving cardiac drugs** — heart rate and blood pressure are your best friends
2. **Potassium is connected to EVERYTHING** — low K+ makes digoxin toxic, ACE inhibitors and ARBs raise K+
3. **Hold parameters matter** — know when NOT to give a drug (this keeps patients alive)
4. **Pregnancy is a red flag** — ACE inhibitors, ARBs, and statins are ALL contraindicated in pregnancy
5. **Teach patients to report danger signs** — angioedema with ACE inhibitors, muscle pain with statins, vision changes with digoxin
6. **Drug interactions can be deadly** — nitroglycerin + PDE5 inhibitors, digoxin + amiodarone, beta-blockers + non-DHP CCBs
