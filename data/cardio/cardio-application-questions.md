# Cardio Application Questions

Use these questions to practice clinical judgment for common cardiovascular problems. They focus on noticing important findings, choosing the safest action, and connecting basic heart concepts to bedside nursing care.

## How to use this file

- Try to answer the question before looking at the correct answer.
- After each question, ask yourself why the best answer is safest or most important.
- If a question feels hard, review the matching section in the cardio guide and flashcards, then try again.

## Review cardiovascular physiology

### Question 1 (Application)

**Question:** A client with pneumonia asks, "After blood picks up oxygen in the lungs, where does it go next?" Which structure should the nurse name?

A. Superior vena cava  
B. Pulmonary artery  
C. Pulmonary vein  
D. Right ventricle

**Correct answer:** C. Pulmonary vein

**Explanation:** The key clue is 'after blood picks up oxygen in the lungs.' At that point it travels through the pulmonary veins back to the left atrium, so C fits the path best. Pulmonary artery is the usual trap because it goes to the lungs, but it carries oxygen-poor blood there.

### Question 2 (Compare/Contrast)

**Question:** A student nurse asks why the AV node matters. Which explanation by the instructor is best?

A. It starts each heartbeat and sets the normal rhythm.  
B. It briefly delays the impulse so the ventricles can fill before they contract.  
C. It carries oxygen directly to the heart muscle.  
D. It pumps blood into the lungs.

**Correct answer:** B. It briefly delays the impulse so the ventricles can fill before they contract.

**Explanation:** Focus on the job of the AV node: it slows the impulse for a moment so the ventricles have time to fill. That short pause helps the heart pump in the right order. Choice A is tempting, but the SA node starts the normal heartbeat.

### Question 3 (Compare/Contrast)

**Question:** The nurse compares two clients: one is dehydrated after vomiting, and one has long-term uncontrolled hypertension. Which statement is correct?

A. Dehydration lowers preload, and hypertension raises afterload.  
B. Dehydration raises preload, and hypertension lowers afterload.  
C. Dehydration mainly slows the SA node, and hypertension mainly weakens the AV node.  
D. Dehydration increases contractility, and hypertension improves stroke volume.

**Correct answer:** A. Dehydration lowers preload, and hypertension raises afterload.

**Explanation:** Look at what each problem does to circulation. Dehydration leaves less blood returning to the heart, so preload drops, while long-term hypertension makes the ventricle pump against more resistance, so afterload rises. A quick NCLEX reminder: preload is filling, and afterload is resistance.

### Question 4 (Priority)

**Question:** Which finding most strongly suggests cardiac output is too low to perfuse organs well?

A. Warm hands and capillary refill of 1 second  
B. Urine output of 15 mL in 2 hours with new confusion  
C. Heart rate 88/min with regular rhythm  
D. Blood pressure 128/76 mm Hg after rest

**Correct answer:** B. Urine output of 15 mL in 2 hours with new confusion

**Explanation:** New confusion and almost no urine output are strong organ perfusion warnings. The brain and kidneys are very sensitive to low cardiac output, so this finding points to poor blood flow. The other options look stable and do not show the same danger.

## Assessing the cardiovascular system

### Question 5 (First/Best Action)

**Question:** A client with ankle edema says, "I feel more short of breath than yesterday." What should the nurse do first?

A. Ask about the client's usual salt intake  
B. Assess oxygen saturation, respiratory effort, and vital signs  
C. Teach ankle pump exercises  
D. Bring the client a pitcher of water

**Correct answer:** B. Assess oxygen saturation, respiratory effort, and vital signs

**Explanation:** Shortness of breath is the clue that needs first attention. It may mean fluid overload or another breathing problem, so the safest step is to check oxygen saturation, work of breathing, and vital signs right away. Teaching and routine questions can wait until the client is stable.

### Question 6 (Assessment)

**Question:** The nurse wants to inspect for jugular venous distention. How should the client be positioned?

A. Flat on the back  
B. Sitting straight up at 90 degrees  
C. With the head of bed at about 30 to 45 degrees  
D. Lying on the left side

**Correct answer:** C. With the head of bed at about 30 to 45 degrees

**Explanation:** This assessment works best when the client is partly upright. At about 30 to 45 degrees, the neck veins are easier to see without being too flat or too empty. Lying flat or sitting straight up can hide the true finding.

### Question 7 (Report to Provider)

**Question:** Which cardiovascular assessment finding should the nurse report promptly?

A. Capillary refill of 2 seconds in both hands  
B. New heart murmur heard during today's assessment  
C. Heart rate 76/min with regular rhythm  
D. Mild ankle swelling that improves after leg elevation

**Correct answer:** B. New heart murmur heard during today's assessment

**Explanation:** What matters most is that the murmur is new. A new heart sound can mean a valve problem or another change that needs prompt follow-up. The other findings are more expected and less urgent.

### Question 8 (Patient Teaching, SATA)

**Question:** The nurse teaches a client which symptoms to report because they can mean worsening cardiovascular function. Which symptoms should the nurse include? Select all that apply.

A. Chest pressure or chest pain  
B. Waking up suddenly short of breath at night  
C. Sudden weight gain and tighter shoes  
D. Palpitations with dizziness  
E. Hands feel warmer after exercise

**Correct answer:** A, B, C, D

**Explanation:** Each correct symptom points to a safety concern: chest pain can mean ischemia, waking up short of breath can mean fluid backing up, sudden weight gain suggests fluid retention, and palpitations with dizziness suggest poor perfusion. Those are the clues a nurse wants reported early. Warmer hands after exercise can happen normally, so it is not in the same risk group.

## Cardiac factors and impaired cardiovascular function

### Question 9 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** Which statement by a client shows correct understanding of ways to protect cardiovascular function?

A. "If I keep smoking but walk daily, my blood vessel risk is gone."  
B. "Controlling blood pressure and blood sugar, staying active, and limiting sodium can help my heart and blood vessels."  
C. "High blood pressure lowers the work of the left ventricle."  
D. "Poor diet affects weight, but not circulation."

**Correct answer:** B. "Controlling blood pressure and blood sugar, staying active, and limiting sodium can help my heart and blood vessels."

**Explanation:** This choice lists real risk-lowering steps: control blood pressure and blood sugar, stay active, and limit sodium. Those actions reduce strain on the heart and blood vessels over time. The other statements are test-day traps because smoking still damages vessels, hypertension increases ventricular workload, and diet does affect circulation.

### Question 10 (Priority)

**Question:** Which finding is most concerning for heart muscle damage and needs immediate follow-up?

A. Sudden chest pressure with shortness of breath and diaphoresis  
B. Mild fatigue after a long workday  
C. Heartburn after a spicy meal that resolves with antacid  
D. Trace edema in both ankles at bedtime

**Correct answer:** A. Sudden chest pressure with shortness of breath and diaphoresis

**Explanation:** Chest pressure with shortness of breath and diaphoresis is a classic danger pattern for myocardial ischemia or infarction. The combination matters more than any single symptom alone. Mild fatigue, heartburn relieved by antacid, and trace bedtime edema do not carry the same immediate risk.

### Question 11 (Compare/Contrast)

**Question:** Which finding is more consistent with impaired arterial blood flow to a leg than with venous stasis?

A. Brown discoloration near the ankles  
B. Bilateral pitting edema that improves with elevation  
C. Cool, pale foot with weak pedal pulse  
D. Aching calves after standing all day

**Correct answer:** C. Cool, pale foot with weak pedal pulse

**Explanation:** A cool, pale foot with a weak pedal pulse points to poor arterial flow. The key clue is weak delivery of oxygen-rich blood to the tissue. Brown skin changes and edema that improves with elevation fit venous problems more than arterial ones.

### Question 12 (Priority)

**Question:** Which client should the nurse assess first?

A. A client with 1+ ankle edema who says the legs feel better when elevated  
B. A client with blood pressure 138/84 mm Hg after walking to the bathroom  
C. A client with new confusion and urine output of 15 mL in 2 hours  
D. A client with fatigue when climbing one flight of stairs for the past month

**Correct answer:** C. A client with new confusion and urine output of 15 mL in 2 hours

**Explanation:** This client has two red flags at once: new confusion and very low urine output. Together they suggest worsening organ perfusion and low cardiac output, which can become serious quickly. The other findings are chronic, expected, or less urgent.

## Conduction problems and dysrhythmias

### Question 13 (Assessment)

**Question:** Which client with bradycardia needs immediate action?

A. A trained runner with a resting heart rate of 52/min and no symptoms  
B. A sleeping adult with a heart rate of 58/min who is easy to arouse  
C. A post-op client with a heart rate of 48/min, dizziness, and blood pressure 86/50 mm Hg  
D. A client taking a beta blocker with a heart rate of 60/min and no dizziness

**Correct answer:** C. A post-op client with a heart rate of 48/min, dizziness, and blood pressure 86/50 mm Hg

**Explanation:** The heart rate is not the only issue here; the symptoms tell the story. Dizziness and hypotension mean the slow rate is not supporting perfusion, so this client needs help now. A low rate without symptoms can be normal in sleep, fitness, or some medications.

### Question 14 (First/Best Action)

**Question:** Telemetry shows a wide, very fast rhythm. The client is awake but pale, has chest pain, and blood pressure 78/40 mm Hg. What is the nurse's best action?

A. Stay with the client and activate emergency or rapid-response help immediately  
B. Recheck the rhythm in 30 minutes  
C. Encourage slow deep breathing and reassess later  
D. Offer oral fluids for possible dehydration

**Correct answer:** A. Stay with the client and activate emergency or rapid-response help immediately

**Explanation:** The key clue is instability: chest pain, pallor, and a blood pressure of 78/40. That means the rhythm is already hurting perfusion, so the nurse should stay with the client and get emergency help right away. Waiting or trying simple comfort measures would waste critical time.

### Question 15 (Priority)

**Question:** A client suddenly becomes unresponsive. The monitor shows ventricular fibrillation, and no pulse is felt. What action should the nurse take first?

A. Start CPR and call for defibrillation or AED support  
B. Obtain a 12-lead ECG  
C. Give the client juice  
D. Place the client flat and recheck in 5 minutes

**Correct answer:** A. Start CPR and call for defibrillation or AED support

**Explanation:** No pulse with ventricular fibrillation means there is no effective circulation. CPR and defibrillation support are the fastest lifesaving steps. A 12-lead ECG or waiting to reassess delays treatment when seconds matter.

### Question 16 (SATA)

**Question:** Which findings can show a conduction problem is lowering perfusion? Select all that apply.

A. Dizziness  
B. Syncope  
C. Hypotension  
D. Shortness of breath  
E. Apical pulse 78/min with no symptoms

**Correct answer:** A, B, C, D

**Explanation:** These correct findings all reflect reduced perfusion from a rhythm problem: dizziness, syncope, hypotension, and shortness of breath. Think about how well blood is reaching the brain and body, not just what the monitor shows. A pulse of 78/min without symptoms does not suggest a perfusion problem.

## Venous stasis and edema

### Question 17 (Prevention, SATA)

**Question:** An older adult is on bed rest after surgery and is at risk for venous stasis. Which actions help prevent venous stasis? Select all that apply.

A. Encourage ankle pumps and leg exercises  
B. Reposition often and ambulate as soon as allowed  
C. Keep the legs crossed while sitting in bed  
D. Apply compression devices if ordered  
E. Encourage hydration if it is not contraindicated

**Correct answer:** A, B, D, E

**Explanation:** The helpful choices all improve venous return: leg exercises, early movement, compression, and hydration when allowed. That lowers blood pooling in the legs, which is the main stasis problem. Crossing the legs is the trap because it can slow venous flow instead of helping it.

### Question 18 (Compare/Contrast)

**Question:** Which situation is the best example of edema caused mainly by increased hydrostatic pressure?

A. A client with burns develops swollen tissue from leaky capillaries  
B. A client with heart failure has bilateral dependent leg edema  
C. A malnourished client has low albumin and generalized swelling  
D. A client with an allergic reaction has facial swelling from inflammation

**Correct answer:** B. A client with heart failure has bilateral dependent leg edema

**Explanation:** Heart failure is the best example because it increases pressure inside the vessels and pushes fluid into dependent tissue. That is the meaning of edema from increased hydrostatic pressure. Burns and allergy are more about capillary leak, while low albumin is about low oncotic pressure.

### Question 19 (First/Best Action)

**Question:** A client with worsening leg edema suddenly has crackles and shortness of breath. What is the nurse's first action?

A. Place the client upright and assess oxygenation  
B. Massage the swollen legs  
C. Encourage the client to drink more water quickly  
D. Apply heat to both calves

**Correct answer:** A. Place the client upright and assess oxygenation

**Explanation:** Crackles plus sudden shortness of breath point to fluid in the lungs. Using ABC thinking, the nurse should sit the client upright and assess oxygenation first. Massaging the legs, applying heat, or pushing fluids ignores the airway and breathing problem.

### Question 20 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** Which statement by a client with chronic leg edema shows correct understanding of home care?

A. "I will let my legs hang down when resting so the fluid can drain."  
B. "I will stop my diuretic when the swelling looks better."  
C. "I will elevate my legs when resting and call for help right away if swelling comes with shortness of breath."  
D. "I only need to limit salt on days when my shoes feel tight."

**Correct answer:** C. "I will elevate my legs when resting and call for help right away if swelling comes with shortness of breath."

**Explanation:** This statement shows safe home care because leg elevation can reduce dependent edema, and shortness of breath with swelling needs prompt help. The key clue is knowing when edema becomes more than a routine comfort issue. Stopping a diuretic or limiting salt only sometimes is unsafe self-management.

### Question 21 (Safety)

**Question:** Which leg assessment finding should the nurse report immediately because it may mean a serious circulation problem?

A. Mild swelling in both ankles after standing all day  
B. Shiny skin with long-term chronic edema  
C. One calf is warm, red, tender, and more swollen than the other  
D. Socks leave shallow marks on both ankles by evening

**Correct answer:** C. One calf is warm, red, tender, and more swollen than the other

**Explanation:** One calf that is warmer, redder, more tender, and more swollen than the other is a strong DVT warning. The one-sided change is the key clue. Mild swelling in both ankles after standing is much more common and less urgent.

### Question 22 (Who to See First)

**Question:** The nurse receives report on four telemetry clients. Which client should the nurse assess first?

A. A client with a heart rate of 52/min who is asleep and easy to arouse  
B. A client with palpitations, lightheadedness, and blood pressure 88/54 mm Hg  
C. A client with chronic ankle edema who asks for fresh water  
D. A client whose heart rate increased to 108/min after walking to the bathroom

**Correct answer:** B. A client with palpitations, lightheadedness, and blood pressure 88/54 mm Hg

**Explanation:** Symptoms plus hypotension make this client the priority. Palpitations with lightheadedness and a blood pressure of 88/54 suggest poor perfusion from a possible dysrhythmia. The other clients are stable, expected, or asking for routine care.

### Question 23 (First/Best Action)

**Question:** A client says, "My heart is racing." The pulse is irregular at 146/min, blood pressure 124/78 mm Hg, and the client denies chest pain or shortness of breath. What should the nurse do first?

A. Activate emergency response  
B. Assess for dizziness, syncope, oxygen saturation, and current symptoms while staying with the client  
C. Ask the client to walk to see if the rate comes down  
D. Offer a caffeinated drink

**Correct answer:** B. Assess for dizziness, syncope, oxygen saturation, and current symptoms while staying with the client

**Explanation:** The rhythm is fast and irregular, but there are no clear unstable signs yet. That is why bedside assessment comes first: stay with the client and check symptoms, perfusion, and oxygenation. NCLEX often tests this trap by making the monitor look alarming when the safest first move is still to assess the patient.

### Question 24 (Stable vs Unstable)

**Question:** Which finding most clearly shows a tachydysrhythmia is unstable rather than stable?

A. Heart rate 132/min after walking with blood pressure 126/74 mm Hg  
B. Feeling anxious during palpitations but speaking clearly  
C. New confusion and blood pressure 82/48 mm Hg during a fast rhythm  
D. Warm dry skin and oxygen saturation 97%

**Correct answer:** C. New confusion and blood pressure 82/48 mm Hg during a fast rhythm

**Explanation:** New confusion and a blood pressure of 82/48 show the fast rhythm is reducing blood flow to vital organs. That is what makes a tachydysrhythmia unstable. A high heart rate alone is not enough if perfusion is still good.

### Question 25 (Report Immediately)

**Question:** Which statement by a client with intermittent palpitations needs the most immediate follow-up?

A. "I notice it more after drinking coffee."  
B. "When it happens, I feel dizzy and like I might pass out."  
C. "It lasts a few seconds when I am stressed."  
D. "I can feel my heartbeat when I exercise hard."

**Correct answer:** B. "When it happens, I feel dizzy and like I might pass out."

**Explanation:** Dizziness and near-syncope are the key clues here. They suggest the rhythm may be dropping blood flow to the brain, so this needs prompt follow-up. Coffee, stress, or hard exercise can trigger palpitations, but they are less urgent when there are no perfusion symptoms.

### Question 26 (Assessment vs Action)

**Question:** A telemetry alarm sounds for a rapid irregular rhythm. What should the nurse do first?

A. Go to the bedside and assess the client, pulse, and symptoms  
B. Silence the alarm and check on the client after finishing medications  
C. Print the rhythm strip and place it in the chart before looking at the client  
D. Ask the UAP to decide whether the rhythm looks dangerous

**Correct answer:** A. Go to the bedside and assess the client, pulse, and symptoms

**Explanation:** Start with the client, not the monitor. The nurse needs to check the pulse, symptoms, and overall condition first to decide how urgent the rhythm really is. Printing a strip or silencing the alarm before assessment can delay care if the client is unstable.

### Question 27 (First/Best Action)

**Question:** A post-op client says one calf feels sore. The nurse sees that calf is more swollen and warm than the other. What is the nurse's best action?

A. Massage the calf to improve venous return  
B. Encourage the client to walk it off in the hall  
C. Assess both legs and notify the provider promptly  
D. Put a pillow under the knee and recheck in 4 hours

**Correct answer:** C. Assess both legs and notify the provider promptly

**Explanation:** A post-op calf that is sore, warm, and more swollen than the other leg raises concern for DVT. The safest response is to assess both legs and notify the provider promptly. Massage and walking are tempting, but they could worsen the situation if a clot is present.

### Question 28 (Who to See First)

**Question:** Which client should the nurse see first?

A. A client with chronic brown discoloration near the ankles and itching  
B. A client with bilateral ankle edema after sitting through a long car ride  
C. A client with suspected DVT who now has sudden shortness of breath and chest pain  
D. A client asking whether compression stockings should go on before getting out of bed

**Correct answer:** C. A client with suspected DVT who now has sudden shortness of breath and chest pain

**Explanation:** This pattern suggests a pulmonary embolism, which can quickly become life-threatening. Sudden shortness of breath and chest pain move this client to the top using ABC and safety thinking. Chronic skin changes and teaching questions can wait.

### Question 29 (Patient Teaching)

**Question:** Which statement by a client with chronic leg edema shows a need for more teaching?

A. "I will elevate my legs when I rest."  
B. "I will check for sudden weight gain or shoes getting tighter."  
C. "If one calf turns red and tender, I will massage it and keep walking."  
D. "I will call for help if swelling comes with trouble breathing."

**Correct answer:** C. "If one calf turns red and tender, I will massage it and keep walking."

**Explanation:** A red, tender calf is not something to massage or walk off. That clue can point to DVT, so the safer response is to stop and get medical help. This option shows unsafe teaching because movement or massage could dislodge a clot.

### Question 30 (Delegation)

**Question:** Which task is most appropriate for the RN to delegate to assistive personnel for a stable client with chronic bilateral leg edema?

A. Decide whether new calf pain suggests DVT  
B. Teach how to limit sodium at home  
C. Measure and record the client's daily weight and report it to the RN  
D. Evaluate how well the client's new diuretic is working

**Correct answer:** C. Measure and record the client's daily weight and report it to the RN

**Explanation:** Daily weight is routine data collection for a stable client, so it is appropriate for assistive personnel. The RN still interprets the finding and decides what it means. Deciding about DVT, teaching, and evaluating a medication all require nursing judgment.

### Question 31 (First/Best Action)

**Question:** A client with bilateral leg edema says, "I needed three pillows to sleep last night and woke up short of breath." What should the nurse do first?

A. Ask how much salt the client had for dinner  
B. Assess lung sounds, oxygenation, and work of breathing  
C. Measure calf circumference  
D. Teach the client to avoid crossing the legs

**Correct answer:** B. Assess lung sounds, oxygenation, and work of breathing

**Explanation:** Needing extra pillows and waking up short of breath suggest fluid may be backing up into the lungs. Breathing comes first, so lung sounds, oxygenation, and work of breathing should be assessed right away. Diet questions and leg teaching can wait until the respiratory status is clear.

### Question 32 (Report Immediately)

**Question:** The home health nurse reviews a client log. Which entry needs prompt follow-up?

A. Weight is up 3 lb since yesterday and shoes feel tighter today  
B. Ankles look mildly puffy after being on feet all afternoon  
C. Weight stayed the same for 4 days in a row  
D. Legs feel less swollen after elevation

**Correct answer:** A. Weight is up 3 lb since yesterday and shoes feel tighter today

**Explanation:** A 3-pound gain in one day with tighter shoes points to rapid fluid retention. That kind of change can signal worsening heart failure or overload and deserves prompt follow-up. Mild puffiness after a long day on the feet is much less concerning.

### Question 33 (Patient Teaching, SATA)

**Question:** The nurse teaches a client at risk for DVT which symptoms need emergency help because they can mean pulmonary embolism. Which symptoms should the nurse include? Select all that apply.

A. Sudden shortness of breath  
B. Chest pain that gets worse with a deep breath  
C. Coughing up blood  
D. Mild ankle swelling by evening  
E. Fainting or near-fainting

**Correct answer:** A, B, C, E

**Explanation:** These correct symptoms fit the pattern of a possible pulmonary embolism: sudden shortness of breath, pleuritic chest pain, coughing up blood, and fainting or near-fainting. The key clue is the sudden lung-related emergency picture. Mild ankle swelling alone is not enough to suggest PE.
